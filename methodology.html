Preface — What This Manual Is

This is a working manual for valuing a business, distilled from Warren Buffett's forty-eight annual letters to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders (1977–2024). It is not an anthology of quotations. The aim is a single, coherent method — the sequence of questions a disciplined analyst asks to move from "here is a company" to "here is what it is worth, and here is the price at which I would buy it."

The structure is built on an explicit analysis of the letters rather than impression. Each concept Buffett discusses was measured two ways: how broadly it recurs across the forty-eight letters, and how central it is to the act of valuation. These two measures diverge sharply, and the divergence is itself instructive. Operational subjects Buffett must report every year — insurance float, debt levels, dividends — appear in nearly every letter, while two of the most load-bearing principles of the method — the margin of safety and the circle of competence — are named in only four letters apiece, because he defined each one decisively and saw no need to repeat himself. Frequency therefore measures how often a subject arises, not how much it matters. This manual is ordered by the second measure. The full ranking, with its counting method, is reproduced in Appendix C.

The method is organized as a pipeline in four parts. Part I fixes the mental model: what you are buying, how price differs from value, and what risk actually is. Part II analyzes the business itself — can you understand it, is it protected, how good are its economics, how honest are its accounts and its managers — because the quality of a valuation is capped by the quality of these inputs. Part III does the arithmetic: discounting owner earnings, handling growth, choosing a discount rate, and converting an uncertain estimate into a decision through the margin of safety. Part IV applies the whole method to a sample business end to end, reduces it to a checklist, and — because an investment does not end at the purchase — closes with the discipline of holding and selling. Three appendices give the equations, a glossary, and the concept ranking.

Quotations are used sparingly and only where Buffett's own words fix a principle more precisely than a paraphrase could. The analysis carries the argument.

Part I — Foundations: The Mental Model

1. What You Are Actually Buying

A share of stock is a fractional ownership claim on a business, and the value of that claim is the cash the business will return to its owners over its remaining life. Every valuation technique in this manual is an attempt to estimate that cash and discount it to the present. This is the entire foundation, and it produces the master equation that the rest of the manual exists to make usable:

Intrinsic Value = Σ OEₜ / (1 + r)ᵗ

the discounted value of future owner earnings (OE), discounted at rate r

Three quantities drive everything: the stream of owner earnings the business will generate (how much, growing how fast, for how long — Parts II and III), and the discount rate r that converts future dollars into present ones (Part III). Buffett's definition, the cleanest in the literature, states the principle and immediately concedes its difficulty:

We define intrinsic value as the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life. Anyone calculating intrinsic value necessarily comes up with a highly subjective figure that will change both as estimates of future cash flows are revised and as interest rates move.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1994

That concession — the figure is subjective and moving — is not a weakness to be engineered away with more decimal places. It is the reason the margin of safety (Chapter 13) exists. The analyst's job is not to compute a precise number but to determine whether the business is worth conspicuously more than its price. These three moves — treating the stock as a business, holding value apart from price, and demanding a margin of safety — are one inherited framework, not three separate ideas:

[Graham's] three basic ideas: look at stocks as businesses; have a proper attitude toward the market; and operate with a margin of safety—they all come straight from Graham.

— Warren Buffett, Berkshire annual meeting, 1997

2. Value Is Not Price: Mr. Market

The market does not tell you what a business is worth; it tells you what someone will pay for it today. These are different numbers, and the gap between them is the only source of opportunity. Graham's allegory — which Buffett credits as describing the mental attitude toward market fluctuations most conducive to investment success — personifies the market as a manic-depressive partner who each day offers to buy or sell at a price driven by his mood:

Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1987

The operational consequence is that price volatility is raw material, not risk. A falling quote on a business whose value is intact is an improved buying opportunity. The analyst values the business independently, then consults the market only to find out whether today's price is foolish enough to act on. Price and value must be held as separate quantities at all times — captured in the line Buffett took from Graham:

Price is what you pay; value is what you get.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2008

3. What Risk Actually Is

Modern finance equates risk with volatility (beta) — how much a stock price bounces. For a business owner this is a category error. The real risk is the permanent loss of capital: the chance that the business's economics deteriorate, that you overpaid, or that you are forced to sell at the wrong time. Measured properly, the loss is one of purchasing power, not merely of nominal dollars:

the real risk that an investor must assess is whether his aggregate after-tax receipts from an investment … will, over his prospective holding period, give him at least as much purchasing power as he had to begin with, plus a modest rate of interest.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1993

A stock that fell 40% on a temporary panic, while its business strengthened, became less risky, not more — a conclusion beta-based theory gets exactly backward:

under beta-based theory, a stock that has dropped very sharply compared to the market — as had Washington Post when we bought it in 1973 — becomes 'riskier' at the lower price than it was at the higher price.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1993

Defining risk correctly has three direct consequences for the method:

Key points

  • Risk is reduced by understanding the business and buying below value — not by holding more, lower-conviction positions.
  • Volatility is opportunity, not danger; the discount rate should reflect the durability of the business's cash flows, not the wiggle of its share price.
  • Because the largest risk is permanent loss, the defenses are conservatism, a margin of safety, and never being forced to sell (no ruinous leverage).

This is why concentration in well-understood businesses, bought below value, is the opposite of recklessness:

The strategy we've adopted precludes our following standard diversification dogma. Many pundits would therefore say the strategy must be riskier … We disagree.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1993

Part II — Analyzing the Business

A valuation is only as good as its inputs, and the inputs come from understanding the business: whether you can forecast it at all, whether its returns are protected, how good those returns are, and whether the reported numbers and the people behind them can be trusted. Part II builds the inputs; Part III prices them.

4. The Circle of Competence — Can You Forecast It?

Discounting future owner earnings presupposes you can estimate them. For most businesses, for most people, you cannot — the future economics are genuinely unpredictable. The first filter is therefore not about price but about predictability: confine analysis to businesses whose long-term economics you can understand well enough to forecast within a sensible range.

You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1996

The discipline is honest self-assessment of the boundary. A business just outside the circle — understood approximately but not truly — is where the expensive errors occur, because the analyst applies the machinery of valuation to forecasts that are merely guesses. "Too hard — I cannot forecast this" is a complete and respectable answer, and using it often is a competitive advantage. What matters is not the reach of the circle but the discipline of operating within it:

The important thing is not how big the circle is … the important thing is staying inside the circle. If that circle only has 30 companies in it out of 1000s on the big board, as long as you know which 30 they are, you will be OK.

— Warren Buffett, University of Florida, October 1998

5. The Moat — Are the Returns Durable?

High returns attract competition that competes them away. A business worth owning must have a structural barrier that protects its returns — a moat. The moat is what lets you forecast that today's high profitability will persist, which is precisely the assumption a valuation rests on.

A truly great business must have an enduring 'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital. The dynamics of capitalism guarantee that competitors will repeatedly assault any business 'castle' that is earning high returns.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2007

Moats come from a limited set of sources: a sustainable low-cost position (GEICO, Costco), a powerful brand that confers pricing power (Coca-Cola, See's), high customer switching costs, a network effect, or scale and regulatory barriers. The diagnostic test is pricing power: a business that can raise prices materially without losing volume has a moat; one that cannot, does not.

The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by a tenth of a cent, then you've got a terrible business.

— Warren Buffett, Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission interview, May 26, 2010

Buffett's "economic franchise" is the same idea stated as three conditions:

An economic franchise arises from a product or service that: (1) is needed or desired; (2) is thought by its customers to have no close substitute; and (3) is not subject to price regulation.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1991

Two cautions for the analyst. Moats erode — many seemingly dominant businesses are "Roman candles" whose advantages prove temporary, so durability must be argued, not assumed. And the width of the moat governs the two inputs that matter most in Part III: how high the return on capital is and how long it can be sustained. Buffett's own screen makes the fad-versus-franchise distinction concrete:

Does the business have a durable competitive advantage? This is why I won't buy into a hula-hoop, pet rock, or Rubik's cube company. I will buy soft drinks and chewing gum.

— Warren Buffett, 2007

A third point completes the chapter, and it is the most consequential lesson of Buffett's own evolution — from Graham's statistical bargains ("cigar butts" with one free puff left) to durable franchises. Business quality is not a luxury on top of cheapness; over any long holding period it dominates cheapness, because a mediocre business consumes the bargain while you wait, and a great one keeps widening it:

It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1989

The same letter states the mechanism in one line: "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre." This does not repeal the margin of safety — price still decides the purchase (Chapters 13–14) — but it redirects the search: hunt first for the wonderful business, then wait for the fair price, rather than hunting for the lowest price and hoping the business is tolerable.

6. Return on Invested Capital — How Good Are the Economics?

The single best measure of business quality is the return it earns on the capital employed in it. A business that earns 20% on capital is worth far more, dollar of earnings for dollar of earnings, than one that earns 8%, because it can grow while consuming little capital and can return the rest to owners.

ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital

NOPAT = after-tax operating profit; invested capital = equity + debt funding the operations

The decisive comparison is ROIC against the discount rate r (the owner's opportunity cost). When ROIC exceeds r, growth creates value; when ROIC is below r, growth destroys it (Chapter 11). This single relationship separates businesses worth owning from value traps that look cheap. It also distinguishes the two kinds of business an analyst must tell apart:

Key points

  • Capital-light franchises (See's, branded consumer goods): high ROIC, little incremental capital needed to grow, so most earnings are free to distribute or redeploy. Worth a high multiple of earnings.
  • Capital-hungry businesses (the old textile mills, commodity manufacturers, many airlines): low ROIC, large reinvestment required merely to stand still, so reported earnings overstate the cash an owner can actually take out. Worth far less than the headline earnings suggest.

Case from the letters. See's Candy is the canonical capital-light franchise — "the prototype of a dream business" (2007 letter). Berkshire bought it in 1972 for $25 million, when pre-tax earnings were under $5 million and the capital required to run it was just $8 million; decades of growth followed while the business consumed almost no additional capital. The counter-case is Berkshire's own original textile operation: large and continuous reinvestment merely to stay competitive, at returns that never justified it. What high returns on essentially unleveraged capital look like — the letters' "Sainted Seven" earned roughly $100M after tax on some $175M of equity with only $2M of interest, a return near 57% — is genuinely rare:

You'll seldom see such a percentage anywhere, let alone at large, diversified companies with nominal leverage.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1987

7. Earnings Quality — Can You Trust the Numbers?

Reported (GAAP) earnings are an accounting construct, not the cash an owner can extract. The valuation must run on owner earnings — the cash that can be removed after funding everything the business needs to maintain its competitive position and unit volume.

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital

the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business

[Owner earnings represent] reported earnings plus depreciation, depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges less the average annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit volume.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1986

The hardest and most important term is maintenance capital expenditure — the portion of capital spending required just to stand still, as opposed to growth capex that funds expansion. Accounting does not separate the two; the analyst must. A business whose maintenance capex chronically exceeds its depreciation is quietly consuming more cash than it reports as profit.

Normalize before you capitalize. The owner-earnings figure that enters the valuation must represent normal earning power, not an unusually good or bad year — a distinction the letters return to repeatedly when appraising businesses. For a cyclical business, average through the cycle; for any business, strip windfalls and one-time items in both directions. Capitalizing a peak year into a perpetuity is how careful arithmetic manufactures a large, precise, and wrong intrinsic value. The margin of safety (Chapter 13) protects against honest error; it cannot protect an input that was optimistic by construction. The letters name two of the ways reported earnings are dressed up. A "one-time" restructuring charge frequently just relocates recurring costs into a single quarter already written off:

In this bit of legerdemain, a large chunk of costs that should properly be attributed to a number of years is dumped into a single quarter, typically one already fated to disappoint investors.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1998

Aggressive pension-return assumptions work the other way in time, lifting reported earnings now against a shortfall that lands only later:

What is no puzzle, however, is why CEOs opt for a high investment assumption: It lets them report higher earnings. And if they are wrong, as I believe they are, the chickens won't come home to roost until long after they retire.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2007

Partial stakes: look-through earnings. When the "business" being valued holds minority positions in other companies — or when you are valuing your own portfolio — reported earnings capture only the dividends received, not your share of what the investees earned and retained. The letters' correction (a fixture from 1989 through 2000) is look-through earnings: your proportional share of investees' earnings, whether distributed or not, less the tax due if they were paid out. The retained portion is not missing — it is compounding inside the investee, and it belongs in the owner-earnings stream being discounted.

Two further adjustments recur in the letters. First, economic versus accounting goodwill (the subject of Buffett's 1983 essay): a franchise like See's is worth vastly more than the tangible capital on its books, so book value understates a great business and overstates a poor one — never anchor value to book. Second, a short list of accounting red flags that signal management is obscuring the real economics:

Key points

  • EBITDA presented as if it were cash flow — it deletes the very real cost of maintaining the asset base.
  • Frequent "restructuring" or "one-time" charges that are in truth recurring costs of doing business.
  • Aggressive pension-return or revenue-recognition assumptions; stock options treated as free.
  • Vague, complex, or boastful disclosure — opacity usually hides something.

References to EBITDA make us shudder — does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures? We're very suspicious of accounting methodology that is vague or unclear, since too often that means management wishes to hide something.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2000

The same "a cost is a cost" logic disposes of the pretense that option grants are free:

If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it? And, if expenses shouldn't go into the calculation of earnings, where in the world should they go?

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1998

8. Financial Strength — Can It Survive?

The three preceding chapters asked whether a business earns well — whether its returns are durable, high on capital, and honestly reported. This one asks a prior question: can the business survive long enough to keep earning? A wonderful franchise financed recklessly can still be destroyed in a single bad year, while an ordinary business with a fortress balance sheet lives to compound another decade. The balance sheet is where that staying power is written, and it is the foundation the income statement rests on — earnings mean little if the enterprise that produces them can be forced to its knees by a maturing loan or a frozen credit market. The governing rule from the letters is to arrange affairs so that no outside force can ever interrupt the compounding: never become a forced seller, and never let leverage put a single zero at the end of a long line of good years.

Leverage is addictive. Once having profited from its wonders, very few people retreat to more conservative practices. … [A]ny series of positive numbers, however impressive the numbers may be, evaporates when multiplied by a single zero.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2010

Financial strength is read from the balance sheet against the cash flow that must service it. Five questions settle it, each answered with the company's own numbers rather than a rule of thumb:

Key points

  • Leverage — how much debt sits against the earning power? Measure net debt (total debt minus cash) against owner earnings or operating income: a conservative business could clear its debt from a few years of its own cash. Judge the burden by the company's own stability — a utility's steady stream safely carries debt that would sink a cyclical.
  • Coverage — does a normal year's operating income cover the interest bill several times over? Thin coverage means a routine downturn, not a catastrophe, is enough to threaten solvency.
  • Liquidity — can near-term obligations be met from current assets and cash without refinancing? Graham's discipline lived here: adequate working capital and a sound current ratio were his precondition for even considering a stock.
  • Refinancing and maturities — when does the debt come due, and must it be rolled over in markets that may be shut? A business that depends on the continued kindness of lenders does not control its own fate.
  • Hidden and off-balance-sheet obligations — operating leases, pension and post-retirement deficits, guarantees, and, for banks and insurers, the honesty of the reserves. The real leverage is often not on the face of the balance sheet; only when the tide goes out do you learn who was swimming naked.

That last image was written of over-leveraged financial institutions in particular:

You only learn who has been swimming naked when the tide goes out — and what we are witnessing at some of our largest financial institutions is an ugly sight.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2007

Interest Coverage = Operating Income (EBIT) ÷ Interest Expense

How many times over a normal year's earnings cover the interest bill; the more times, the wider the cushion.

Net Debt / Owner Earnings = (Total Debt − Cash) ÷ Owner Earnings

The years of owner earnings it would take to extinguish net debt — a rough gauge of staying power. The market's EBITDA version of this ratio flatters it, for the reasons of Chapter 7.

This is not a demand for zero debt. Modest, well-laddered borrowing against stable cash flows is prudent. The point is asymmetry: a stretched balance sheet offers only a marginal gain in good times and risks permanent loss of capital — the one real risk of Chapter 3 — in bad ones. So the balance sheet ties the whole method together: it is the denominator of the returns measured in Chapter 6, the guarantee that you are never the forced seller of Chapter 3, and the first thing to verify before you trust any stream of earnings to persist. When financial strength is in doubt, widen the margin of safety — or, just as respectably, pass.

Special cases: businesses that are leveraged by nature

Some businesses — regulated utilities, banks, insurers — carry large liabilities as a condition of their trade, and must be judged on different yardsticks: capital ratios, reserve quality, and funding stability, with their worth often read through book value rather than earnings. The letters' great worked example is insurance float: premiums an insurer holds between receipt and the payment of claims — money it does not own but may invest. Float is a liability on the balance sheet, yet if underwriting is disciplined it behaves like borrowed money at negative cost:

Cost of Float ≈ Underwriting Loss ÷ Average Float

When underwriting runs at a profit the cost is negative — the insurer is paid to hold investable funds.

The analytical lesson generalizes: for financial businesses, the quality of the liabilities is as decisive as the quantity. Reserves honestly stated and float that grows at negative cost are an asset in economic substance; the same balance sheet with careless underwriting behind it is a time bomb. If you cannot judge the liabilities, the business is outside the circle of competence (Chapter 4), whatever the ratios say.

A related special case is the company whose assets are largely a portfolio of securities. The letters value Berkshire itself this way — the two-column method: per-share investments taken at market value, plus the operating businesses' per-share earnings capitalized separately (a fixture of the letters from 1996 through 2015). Where a large portfolio sits inside an operating business, value the two parts on their own terms and sum them; a single consolidated multiple will misprice both.

9. Capital Allocation and Management

Once a business earns more cash than it needs, management must decide what to do with it, and this decision — capital allocation — determines how much of the business's earning power actually reaches owners. There are six uses of a dollar of free cash — five deployments, plus the decision to issue or retire the shares themselves; the analyst should judge management on its track record across all of them.

Key points

  • Reinvest in the existing business — best use when incremental ROIC is high.
  • Acquire other businesses — frequently value-destroying, because the "institutional imperative" pushes managers to expand for its own sake and overpay.
  • Pay down debt — raises safety, lowers the risk of forced selling.
  • Pay dividends — appropriate when reinvestment opportunities earn less than shareholders could earn elsewhere.
  • Repurchase shares — value-creating only when the stock is bought below intrinsic value, value-destroying above it. Buybacks are not automatically good; the price paid is everything.
  • Issue shares — or refuse to — the mirror image of the buyback. Intrinsic value is a per-share quantity, and issuing undervalued stock as acquisition currency, or streaming it out as option grants, gives away part of every owner's claim. Share count is capital allocation.

Case from the letters. Dexter Shoe (bought 1993 for $433 million of Berkshire stock) is Buffett's own confession on the sixth point: the moat he thought durable "vanished within a few years," but the deeper wound was the currency — "By using Berkshire stock, I compounded this error hugely," turning a $400 million mistake into a $3.5 billion one as the shares given away kept compounding (2007 letter). A bad purchase paid for in undervalued shares is two errors multiplied.

The governing principle is a market test for retained earnings — the "one-dollar test":

Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r (so that ≥ $1 of value is created per $1 retained)

otherwise the dollar should be returned to owners

[The test is whether] at least one dollar of market value will be created for owners. This will happen only if the capital retained produces incremental earnings equal to, or above, those generally available to investors.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1984

Management quality is assessed against this standard and two qualitative signals the letters stress: candor (does management report its mistakes as plainly as its successes?) and rational independence from the herd. The force working against rational allocation is structural, not personal:

Rationality frequently wilts when the institutional imperative comes into play.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1989

Part III — Valuing the Business

10. Intrinsic Value: Discounting Owner Earnings

With owner earnings estimated (Chapter 7) and their durability and growth argued from the moat and ROIC (Chapters 5–6), valuation is the mechanical step of discounting them to the present. In practice the infinite sum is handled as a two-stage model: an explicit forecast for a number of years in which the business grows at a rate the moat can support, followed by a terminal value capturing everything after, growing at a modest perpetual rate.

IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ + [ OEₙ₊₁ / (r − g) ] / (1+r)ⁿ

explicit-period value plus discounted terminal value (terminal growth g)

The model is only as good as its three inputs — starting owner earnings, the growth path, and the discount rate — and it is acutely sensitive to all three, which is why the output is a range, not a point. Buffett casts the same three inputs as Aesop's "a bird in the hand is worth two in the bush":

To flesh out this principle, you must answer only three questions. How certain are you that there are indeed birds in the bush? When will they emerge and how many will there be? What is the risk-free interest rate?

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 2000

How many birds and how certain is owner earnings; when they emerge is the growth path and horizon; and the risk-free rate anchors the discount rate to long-term bond yields. Four guardrails keep the machine honest:

Key points

  • The horizon n is the moat's horizon. Forecast explicitly only as far as the moat argument of Chapter 5 actually reaches — usually five to ten years. Beyond the point where you can defend the forecast, you are not forecasting; everything past it belongs in the terminal value on conservative terms.
  • Bound the terminal growth rate. g must be below r for the formula to mean anything, and should not exceed the long-run nominal growth of the economy — no business outgrows the economy forever. The terminal value is usually the largest piece of the estimate and the easiest place to smuggle in optimism; treat g as a cap, not a dial.
  • Sanity-check with the implied multiple. Divide the resulting intrinsic value by current owner earnings. If the machine says the business is worth 40 times its owner earnings, ask whether the moat and reinvestment economics genuinely justify what that multiple implies — the DCF and the multiple are the same statement in two languages, and translating catches errors.
  • Run the model backward on the market price. Ask what growth, for how long, today's quote silently assumes. When the price can only be justified by heroics, the market has done your negative analysis for you.

Chapter 14 works a complete example; Chapter 13 turns the resulting range into a decision.

11. Growth and Reinvestment — When Growth Adds Value

Growth is not automatically good. Growing earnings requires reinvesting capital, and whether that reinvestment creates or destroys value depends entirely on the return it earns. The growth a business can fund from within is governed by how much it reinvests and at what return:

g = Reinvestment Rate × ROIC

sustainable growth equals the fraction of earnings reinvested times the return on that capital

The value consequence follows directly: reinvested capital creates value only when ROIC exceeds the discount rate r. A business growing 10% while earning 6% on the capital that funds the growth is consuming value as it expands — growth makes it less valuable, not more. A business growing 10% at 25% ROIC is a compounding machine. This is why a low-ROIC business is not made attractive by a high growth rate, and why the capital-light, high-ROIC franchise of Chapter 6 is so prized: it can grow without much reinvestment, so nearly all of its earnings remain free for owners.

Key points

  • Good growth: ROIC > r — each reinvested dollar is worth more than a dollar; growth multiplies value.
  • Bad growth: ROIC < r — each reinvested dollar is worth less than a dollar; growth destroys value.
  • Neutral: ROIC = r — growth is a wash; the business is worth the same whether it grows or not.

Case from the letters. Berkshire's own textile business is the standing exhibit for bad growth: year after year, credible proposals to invest more capital promised to restore competitiveness, and the returns never came — "major additional investment in a terrible industry usually is about as rewarding as struggling in quicksand" (1983 letter). The mills were finally closed in 1985. Growth capital poured into a business without a moat does not build value; it buries it.

12. The Discount Rate: Interest Rates and Inflation

The discount rate r is the owner's opportunity cost — the return available on alternative investments of comparable risk — anchored to long-term interest rates. Interest rates act on asset values the way gravity acts on matter: when rates rise, the present value of every future dollar falls, and all asset prices are pulled down; when rates fall, they are lifted. (The gravity image is Buffett's, though from outside the letters — a 1999 Fortune essay; the letters state the substance whenever they link valuations to rates.) The analyst should therefore set r from prevailing long rates plus a premium for the uncertainty of the cash flows, and re-examine valuations when rates move materially.

Inflation — the dominant subject of Buffett's letters from 1979 to 1981 — sharpens the same point and explains why business quality determines survival. Inflation silently taxes owners by forcing ever more nominal capital into the business merely to maintain real output; it is hardest on capital-hungry, low-ROIC businesses, which must reinvest at rising replacement costs for no real gain. The businesses that withstand inflation are exactly the high-ROIC franchises with pricing power, which can raise prices to match costs while needing little incremental capital. Inflation thus does not change the method; it raises the premium on the very qualities Part II identifies.

13. From Estimate to Decision: The Margin of Safety

Because the inputs are uncertain, intrinsic value is a range, and the width of that range is large. The defense is to buy only at a price far enough below the conservative end of the range that errors in the estimate cannot produce a permanent loss. That gap is the margin of safety — engineered slack, not a forecast:

When you build a bridge, you insist it can carry 30,000 pounds, but you only drive 10,000-pound trucks across it. And that same principle works in investing.

— Warren Buffett, 2005

Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) / Intrinsic Value

require a large positive value; a small one offers no protection

We insist on a margin of safety in our purchase price. If we calculate the value of a common stock to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested in buying. We believe this margin-of-safety principle, so strongly emphasized by Ben Graham, to be the cornerstone of investment success.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1992

Two operating rules follow. Use conservative inputs — value the business on what it is likely to earn, not on its best case — so the margin of safety is not quietly spent on optimism. And concentrate: opportunities priced far below conservative value are rare, so act decisively and in size when one appears, and do nothing the rest of the time. The discipline is to wait for the rare, obvious mispricing rather than to force marginal decisions.

Paying up for quality

The margin of safety and the wonderful-company principle of Chapter 5 can seem to pull in opposite directions: truly great businesses almost never trade at huge headline discounts. The reconciliation is that the margin of safety is protection against error, and where the protection sits depends on the business. In a mediocre business, every dollar of safety must come from the purchase discount, and it is a melting cushion — time erodes the value while you wait to be proven right. In a durable, high-ROIC franchise, part of the safety lives in the quality itself: a conservatively projected earnings stream that grows makes today's estimate the floor of tomorrow's, so a fair price plus a real moat can offer more protection than a wonderful price on a fair business. What the principle never licenses is paying any price: quality justifies a fair price, not an assumption that quality is infinite. The worked example's rule stands — below the conservative range you buy in size; above it you admire the business and wait.

Part IV — Application

14. A Worked Valuation: "Sample Co."

This example runs the full pipeline on a hypothetical consumer-products business with a brand-based moat. The point is to show how the inputs from Part II feed the arithmetic of Part III, and how the conclusion is governed by the margin of safety rather than by the precision of the estimate.

Step 1 — Business quality (Part II). Sample Co. earns NOPAT of $150M on $500M of invested capital, an ROIC of 30% — well above any plausible discount rate, the signature of a real moat with pricing power. It is capital-light: it can grow while reinvesting only a minority of its earnings.

Step 2 — Owner earnings (Chapter 7). Reported earnings are $150M; add back non-cash charges of about $50M; subtract maintenance capital expenditure of about $45M and incremental working capital of about $15M. Normalized owner earnings are therefore roughly $140M — the cash an owner could actually withdraw this year.

Step 3 — Growth path (Chapter 11). With high ROIC and a durable moat, assume owner earnings grow 8% per year for 10 years, then a perpetual 3% thereafter.

Step 4 — Discount rate (Chapter 12). Use r = 9%, reflecting long-term rates plus a premium for forecast uncertainty.

Step 5 — Discount to present (Chapter 10). Applying the two-stage model to $140M of starting owner earnings produces the following (figures in $millions):

Two-stage DCF (selected years shown; sum uses all 10 explicit years)

Summing the present value of all ten explicit years ($1,331M) and the discounted terminal value ($2,192M) gives an intrinsic value of about $3,523M. On 100M fully diluted shares, that is roughly $35 per share. Note that a per-share value is only as durable as the share count behind it: option grants and stock-funded acquisitions dilute it, and buybacks below value concentrate it (Chapter 9). The estimate assumes the count holds.

Step 6 — Respect the uncertainty (Chapter 13). The estimate is acutely sensitive to the discount rate and terminal growth. Varying both shows how wide the true range is:

Intrinsic value per share under different assumptions

Discount rate r g = 2% g = 3% g = 4%
8% $38 $43 $50
9% $32 $35 $40
10% $28 $30 $33

The per-share estimate ranges from roughly $28 to $50 depending on assumptions that are all individually reasonable. This spread is the point, not a defect: it is why a precise "target price" is an illusion and why the decision must rest on a margin of safety.

Triangulation — cross-checks before deciding

The DCF, however carefully sensitized, is one machine running on one set of assumptions. Before deciding, run three cross-checks. Be clear about what they can and cannot do: all three run on the same owner-earnings figure, so none can validate that input — the input was validated in Chapter 7, before the arithmetic began. What they test is the model wrapped around the input, and each stresses a different joint. (These checks are the manual's synthesis rather than the letters' text, but each is derivable from doctrine already on the table: the no-growth floor is Chapter 11's neutral case, the yield check is Chapter 12's gravity, and the two-column method below is the letters' own procedure for Berkshire itself.)

1. The no-growth floor — checks the growth assumption. Value Sample Co. as if it never grows another dollar: $140M ÷ 0.09 = $1,556M, or about $15.60 per share. Everything above that floor is a payment for growth. At $23, $7.40 of the price — roughly a third — depends on growth materializing; at $38, well over half does. The floor tells you exactly how much of the price rests on the input most likely to be optimistic.

2. The implied multiple — checks the model's output. Divide the intrinsic-value estimate by current owner earnings: $3,523M ÷ $140M ≈ 25× owner earnings at the central case, and the $23 buy price is 16.4×. Now interrogate the translation: is 25× defensible for a 30%-ROIC franchise growing 8% and then 3%? If the machine had said 45×, this is where a runaway terminal value or double-counted growth would surface — errors invisible inside the DCF's own plumbing, because there they just look like a bigger sum.

3. The owner-earnings yield — checks the anchor to interest rates. At $23 the business yields $140M ÷ $2,300M = 6.1%, and the coupon grows 8% a year — against a long bond's static coupon, a clearly superior claim. At $38 the yield is 3.7%: below the bond, meaning the entire purchase leans on growth arriving as forecast. This is Chapter 12's gravity applied at the moment of decision.

Three further checks apply conditionally, when the business type calls for them: an asset or book-value floor where the business is a financial or the earnings have no moat behind them (Chapter 8's yardsticks); the two-column method — per-share investments at market plus separately capitalized operating earnings — where a company holds a large securities portfolio (the letters' own method for valuing Berkshire, stated repeatedly from 1996 to 2015); and the private-owner test — would a rational private buyer pay this for the entire company, in cash, with no exit? What stays excluded is valuation by peer multiples: pricing off comparable companies anchors the estimate to Mr. Market's opinions of other businesses, which is precisely what the method exists to avoid. The private-owner test is exactly how Buffett describes buying a real franchise — you value the cash stream, not a resale price:

we buy a private business like See's Candy for $25 million. We don't ever say if we ever get an offer of $50 million for this business we will sell it. That is not the way to look at a business.

— Warren Buffett, University of Florida, October 1998

The point of the checks is not more numbers; it is that the numbers arrive by roads that cannot all be wrong the same way. When they agree, act with confidence. When they disagree, the disagreement is information — find out which assumption is doing the arguing before any money moves.

Step 7 — Decide. Take the conservative end of the range and require a wide discount. If Mr. Market offers Sample Co. at about $23 per share — well below even the cautious estimates — the margin of safety against the central case of $35 is roughly 34%, ample protection against being wrong on growth or rates. At that price you buy, in size. At $38 or above, you do nothing and wait, however good the business — price, not quality alone, makes the investment. Even a business as good as See's gets a "no" above value:

If See's Candy had asked $100,000 more … Warren and I would have walked — that's how dumb we were.

— Charlie Munger, Berkshire annual meeting, 2003

15. The Valuation Checklist

The entire method reduces to a sequence of questions, to be answered in order. A "no" at any quality gate ends the analysis; the arithmetic is earned only after the business qualifies. The sequence below is an expansion of Buffett's own four filters:

Filter #1 – Can we understand the business? Filter #2 – Does the business have a durable competitive advantage? Filter #3 – Does it have management I can trust? Filter #4 – Does the price make sense?

— Warren Buffett, Berkshire annual meeting, 1997

Key points

  1. Competence: Can I genuinely understand and forecast this business? If not, stop.
  2. Moat: What protects its returns, and will the protection last? Is there pricing power?
  3. Returns: What is ROIC, and does it exceed the discount rate? Capital-light or capital-hungry?
  4. Owner earnings: What cash can actually be withdrawn after maintenance capex and working capital — in a normal year, not a peak one? Do the accounts hide anything?
  5. Financial strength: Can it survive a bad year and a shut credit market without becoming a forced seller?
  6. Capital allocation: Does management pass the one-dollar test, allocate rationally and candidly, and respect the share count?
  7. Intrinsic value: Discount conservative owner earnings at an opportunity-cost rate; produce a range, not a point — then triangulate it (no-growth floor, implied multiple, yield vs. bonds; Chapter 14).
  8. Growth check: Is growth funded at ROIC above r (value-creating) or below (value-destroying)?
  9. Margin of safety: Is the price far below the conservative end of the range? If yes, act decisively; if no, wait.
  10. Afterward: Monitor the business, not the quote — and sell only for the reasons of Chapter 16.

16. After the Purchase: Holding and Selling

The method does not end at the purchase, but its center of gravity shifts: once you own the business, the quote matters even less and the business even more. The default posture toward a great business, once bought well, is inaction:

In fact, when we own portions of outstanding businesses with outstanding managements, our favorite holding period is forever. We are just the opposite of those who hurry to sell and book profits when companies perform well but who tenaciously hang on to businesses that disappoint.

— Berkshire Hathaway Letter, 1988

The positive test for holding is a single recurring question about the owner-earnings stream:

The way to look at a business is this going to keep producing more and more money over time? And if the answer to that is yes, you don't need to ask any more questions.

— Warren Buffett, University of Florida, October 1998

That sentence contains both halves of the discipline. Do not sell a compounding franchise merely because it has gone up, because you have a profit to "book," or because the position has grown large — interrupting compounding is expensive, and taxes and reinvestment risk are real costs of every exit. Equally, do not cling to a disappointment out of loyalty or hope. The letters also warn against the opposite vice, portfolio churn dressed up as discipline — what the 1983 letter calls "gin rummy managerial behavior (discard your least promising business at each turn)." The refusal to sell on price extends back to the purchase: no exit target is set at the outset:

We never buy something with a price target in mind. We never buy something at 30 saying if it goes to 40 we'll sell it or 50 or 60 or 100. We just don't do it that way.

— Warren Buffett, University of Florida, October 1998

Within the logic of the method, only three reasons to sell survive:

Key points

  • The thesis broke. The moat is eroding, the economics have deteriorated, or you were wrong about them — the Dexter test (Chapter 9): what looked like a durable advantage vanished. Sell, because the owner-earnings stream you valued no longer exists; the current quote is irrelevant to that judgment.
  • The price has left the valuation far behind. When Mr. Market offers a price well above even the generous end of your range, the margin of safety has inverted — you now hold the overpriced side of the trade. For a truly wonderful business the bar should be high (Chapter 13's quality argument cuts both ways), but no business is worth an infinite multiple.
  • A decisively better use of the capital exists. Within your circle, at a wide margin of safety, and after taxes and the risk of being wrong twice. "Slightly better" is not enough to justify swapping a business you understand for one you understand less.

Everything else — market forecasts, macro anxiety, price wiggles, the itch to do something — is Mr. Market talking. The analyst who valued the business independently at purchase keeps valuing it independently afterward, and lets the business, not the quote, decide. Inaction, on this view, is not passivity but a measurable discipline:

I measure Berkshire by how little activity there is in it.

— Warren Buffett, University of Florida, October 1998

Appendix B — Glossary

Circle of competence. The set of businesses an investor can understand well enough to forecast. Its boundary matters more than its size.

Cost of float. The net cost of holding insurance float; negative when underwriting is profitable, so the insurer is paid to hold investable funds.

Discount rate (r). The owner's opportunity cost of capital, anchored to long-term interest rates plus a premium for cash-flow uncertainty; converts future dollars to present value.

Earning-power value (no-growth value). Normalized owner earnings divided by the discount rate — what the business is worth if it never grows again. The floor beneath a DCF, and the gauge of how much of a price is a bet on growth.

Economic vs accounting goodwill. Economic goodwill is the value of a franchise above its tangible capital; accounting goodwill is a balance-sheet figure. Book value understates great businesses and overstates poor ones.

Financial strength / solvency. The balance sheet's capacity to let a business survive a bad year without a forced sale — a function of modest leverage, ample interest coverage, and adequate liquidity. The precondition for trusting any earnings stream to persist.

Float. Money an insurer holds but does not own (premiums received before claims are paid), investable in the meantime.

Institutional imperative. The structural tendency of organizations to imitate peers and expand for its own sake, undermining rational capital allocation.

Interest coverage. Operating income (EBIT) divided by interest expense; how many times over current earnings cover the interest bill. Thin coverage turns a routine downturn into a solvency threat.

Intrinsic value. The discounted value of all the owner earnings a business will produce over its life; the true measure of worth, necessarily an estimate — and a per-share quantity, sensitive to changes in the share count.

Leverage. The use of borrowed money; it magnifies gains and losses alike, and any long run of good results is erased when multiplied by a single zero.

Look-through earnings. An owner's proportional share of investee earnings — dividends received plus retained earnings attributable to the stake, less the tax due on full payout. Corrects reported earnings, which capture only the dividends.

Maintenance capex. The capital spending required to preserve a business's competitive position and unit volume, as distinct from growth capex.

Margin of safety. The discount of price to a conservative estimate of intrinsic value; the cushion against error and bad luck. The cornerstone of the method.

Moat / economic franchise. A durable competitive advantage (low cost, brand, network, scale) that protects high returns; its signature is pricing power.

One-dollar test. Retain earnings only if each retained dollar produces at least a dollar of market value (i.e., incremental ROIC ≥ r).

Owner earnings. Reported earnings plus non-cash charges minus maintenance capex and incremental working capital — the cash an owner can actually withdraw.

Risk. The probability of a permanent loss of capital — not price volatility or beta.

ROIC. Return on invested capital (NOPAT ÷ invested capital); the central measure of business quality. Value is created only when ROIC exceeds r.

Triangulation. Testing one valuation with cross-checks that fail differently — the no-growth floor, the implied multiple, the owner-earnings yield against bonds, and, where the business type calls for it, an asset floor or the two-column method — so that errors cannot all hide in the same assumption.

Two-column method. Valuing a company that holds a large securities portfolio as per-share investments at market plus separately capitalized operating earnings; the letters' own procedure for Berkshire.
