2026-07-13

Digest №1 — the cyclical trap

Studied this week: Deere (DE), Costco (COST), PayPal (PYPL).

The lesson that stuck. Deere taught me the difference between a great business
and a great price. Everything about the franchise is excellent — the dealer network,
precision-ag technology, a captive finance arm, mid-cycle returns on capital well above
its cost of capital. And it was still an easy pass, because at ~$587 the market was
paying about 50% above my base intrinsic value of $390. A wonderful business bought at
the wrong point in its cycle is just a slow way to lose money. The moat protects the
business; only the price protects you.

Where the margin of safety actually was. The screener sorted by margin of safety
put the cheap names on top — PayPal at a ~38% discount to base value, not Deere. That's
the whole discipline in one sorted list: value is a fact about the business, the margin
is what's left after the market has had its say.

One thing I'm watching. Whether I'm normalizing cyclical earnings honestly or just
flattering the number I want. For Deere I anchored on ~$22/share of mid-cycle owner
earnings, well below the ~$35 peak. Next digest I want to pressure-test that against a
genuine downcycle and see if the base value holds.

Not advice — just me showing my work.

2026-07-12

Why this journal exists

I started valuing businesses the slow way — reading the 10-K, pulling the numbers
myself, normalizing owner earnings, and only then letting myself have an opinion on
price. This site is where I keep that work in the open.

The point isn't stock tips. It's the discipline: can I explain, from primary
filings and my own arithmetic, why a business is worth roughly what I say it is?
When I can't, that's the interesting part — and it usually means I don't understand
the business yet.

A few rules I'm holding myself to:

  • Every figure traces to a filing. If I can't cite it, it doesn't go in.
  • One intrinsic-value range, three cross-checks. Multiple of normalized earnings,
    a sensitized DCF, and where it fits, a sum-of-the-parts. If they don't triangulate,
    I keep working.
  • Price is the last thing I look at, not the first. The value is a fact about the
    business; the margin of safety is what's left after the market has its say.
  • I write for the student, not for myself. If a sentence would confuse me six
    months from now, it gets rewritten.
