Value Investing Seminar — Novo Nordisk A/S (NYSE: NVO)

A Graham–Buffett Case Study · June 30, 2026

In the Graham–Buffett tradition, grounded in the method manual "How to Value a Business," distilled from the Berkshire letters, 1970–2024. This is a teaching case, not investment advice.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Let me tell you what you would actually own. Novo Nordisk is a 100-year-old Danish pharmaceutical company that makes medicines for two conditions rooted in the same biology: diabetes and obesity. Together those two areas are roughly 99% of sales. It also has small legacy franchises in rare blood disorders (haemophilia) and hormone therapy, but do not be distracted — this is a diabetes-and-obesity company.

The engine is a molecule called semaglutide, sold as Ozempic (injectable, for diabetes), Wegovy (injectable, for weight loss), and now Rybelsus / an oral Wegovy (a pill). Semaglutide is a "GLP-1 receptor agonist" — it mimics a gut hormone that tells your brain you are full and helps the body regulate blood sugar. Alongside it sits the world's leading insulin franchise, where Novo has held a dominant share for decades. An independent research service pegs Novo's share of the global diabetes-care market at 34% — roughly one in three diabetes dollars, worldwide.

How it makes money: it discovers or licenses a molecule, runs it through years of clinical trials and regulatory approval, patents it, manufactures it at enormous scale (injector pens, and the hard-to-copy fill-and-finish sterile manufacturing), and sells it through prescribers and payers. 2025 sales by region: U.S. 55%, Europe/Canada 20%, China 10%, other emerging markets 9%, rest-of-APAC 6%. The United States is both the profit center and, as we will see, the source of the storm.

The one question that decides this case: Novo helped create the most valuable new drug market in a generation — GLP-1 weight-loss drugs — and then watched its stock fall 76% in eighteen months. Is that fall the market correctly repricing a franchise that is being out-innovated and commoditized (Eli Lilly pulling ahead, prices collapsing, Washington capping the rest)? Or is it Mr. Market, in a fit of disappointment, marking down the clear #2 player in a market on its way to $150 billion — to a price that already assumes almost no growth ever again? Everything below is built to answer that.

The Circle-of-Competence Gate (Manual Ch. 4)

Before we value a single dollar, the manual makes us stop and ask the honest question: can we forecast this business's owner earnings a decade out? Discounting owner earnings "presupposes you can estimate them; for most businesses and most people you cannot." The first filter is predictability, not price.

Let me be straight with you, because this is the most important admission in the whole seminar. Novo Nordisk is partly inside the circle and partly outside it.

  • Inside the circle: that people with diabetes and obesity need chronic, recurring, branded medicine; that the market is enormous and growing with global wealth and waistlines; that Novo has the scale, the manufacturing, and the regulatory track record to serve it; that these are high-margin, high-return economics. That much a diligent generalist can understand.
  • Outside the circle: which molecule wins. GLP-1 is a scientific arms race. This year Eli Lilly's next-generation drugs have shown better weight loss than Novo's much-hyped CagriSema. Novo's own oral Wegovy launched to 50,000 prescriptions in a month — a success — but at a sharply lower price that dampened revenue. Predicting the winner of a molecule-by-molecule clinical war three product cycles out is genuinely hard, and pretending otherwise is how you lose money "just outside the circle," which the manual warns is exactly where the expensive errors happen.

Where that leaves us. This is not a "too hard, pass" like a pre-revenue biotech — Novo has $17 billion of real profit and a 34% entrenched share. But it is not a Coca-Cola, whose product will be recognizably the same in 2045. So we will proceed, but we will carry the moat uncertainty explicitly into the discount rate and the margin of safety, and we will refuse to pay up. PRINCIPLE: a stock is a piece of a business, and you only value what you can understand — the honest move when part of the business sits outside your circle is not to fake precision, but to demand a bigger cushion for the part you cannot see.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

We have no 20-F footnotes to read, so we will read the evidence an independent research service gives us — the per-ADR array and the company-level financials — as the record of a real enterprise. (Each ADR = one common share; all dollar figures are translated from kroner.)

The income statement — a march down to a spectacular margin

Depreciation 2,337 5.0%

Walk down that ladder with me. Novo keeps 51 cents of operating profit on every sales dollar and 37.7 cents of net profit — after spending nearly 17 cents on research. Those are not normal margins; those are the margins of a franchise with pricing power (more on that below). For contrast, a typical industrial company keeps 8–12 cents.

Now the trend, because a single year lies and a decade tells the truth. Earnings per ADR:

EPS/ADR $1.06 $1.22 $1.49 $1.76 $2.77 $3.16 $3.95 $3.40 $3.60 $4.50

Two things jump out. First, the explosion from 2022 to 2025 — EPS more than doubled ($1.76 → $3.95) as Wegovy took off. Second, the crack in the estimate: An independent research service now models 2026 EPS falling 14% to $3.40. Management itself guided 2026 revenue down 5% to 13% and Danish-kroner earnings down ~16%. This is the first hard fact the value investor must respect: near-term earnings power is declining, not growing. We are not buying a company mid-ascent; we are buying one whose next year is guided down.

One-time items to strip out? The 2024 Catalent acquisition (below) and the currency swing (the dollar fell ~10% vs. the krone this past year, flattering dollar EPS and cushioning the kroner decline). We will normalize around these rather than take any single year's dollar figure as destiny.

The balance sheet — leverage arrived, but so did a fortress

Two judgment-laden features deserve a teacher's finger on them.

(1) Working capital is negative — and that is a compliment, not a warning. Current liabilities exceed current assets by ~$6.8 billion. In a weak company that signals a liquidity scare. In a franchise like this it signals the opposite: Novo collects and rebates on terms that let it fund its own operations with other people's money — a mild version of the "float" the manual prizes in insurers. A business that grows while its working capital stays negative is throwing off cash, not absorbing it.

(2) Debt appeared where there used to be almost none. Long-term debt jumped from ~$3.1 billion (2022) to $12.5 billion (2024) to $18.7 billion (2025). The cause is the ~$16.5 billion acquisition of Catalent — a contract manufacturer Novo bought to lock up fill-and-finish capacity for Wegovy pens. So the balance sheet is more leveraged than it was. But keep perspective: interest expense is ~$590 million against ~$24 billion of operating profit — coverage of roughly 40× — and an independent research service still grades Novo's Financial Strength "A+." This is a fortress that took on a mortgage to build a bigger factory, not a company borrowing to survive.

On book value — do not anchor to it. Shareholders' equity is $30.5 billion; the company is worth (at today's price) ~$215 billion. The manual is emphatic: "book value understates a great business and overstates a poor one." Novo's real asset — the semaglutide franchise, the manufacturing moat, the 34% share — is economic goodwill that barely appears on the balance sheet. Valuing Novo at book would be a category error.

The cash flow statement — is the profit real?

We do not have the full statement, but an independent research service's "Cash Flow per ADR" ($4.49 in 2025) is essentially net income per ADR ($3.95) plus depreciation (~$0.52). The reconciliation is clean: profit converts to cash. The one wrinkle is capital spending, which spiked to ~$3.07/ADR (~$13.6 billion) in 2025, up from $1.48/ADR in 2024. That is the Catalent build-out and a massive expansion of GLP-1 manufacturing — growth capex, not maintenance. We will separate the two in the next section, because it is the crux of owner earnings.

Earnings-Quality Scan (Manual Ch. 7)

Can we trust these numbers? Running the manual's red-flag checklist:
- EBITDA waved around as cash flow? No sign of it in the data we have; Novo's earnings are cash-backed and capex is disclosed, not hidden.
- Recurring "one-time" charges? None visible.
- Aggressive revenue recognition / pension games? Not detectable from this vantage — and here is exactly where the missing 20-F footnotes hurt us. Flag it as an open question.
- Vague or boastful disclosure? The opposite — an independent research service rates Earnings Predictability 90 (out of 100), among the highest in its universe. Historically, what Novo reports is what Novo earns.

The honest verdict: earnings quality looks high, but our confidence is capped by not reading the footnotes ourselves. PRINCIPLE: the statements are evidence and cash is the truth-teller — Novo's profit converts to cash and its capex is visible, but when you cannot read the footnotes, you cap your confidence rather than assume the best.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Owner earnings — separating the two kinds of capex

The manual's definition: Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital. The whole art is the maintenance-capex term, because "accounting does not separate" maintenance from growth spending, and Novo's 2025 capex is a dramatic case.

Owner-earnings build, 2025 ($mill.) Amount
Reported net profit 17,602
+ Depreciation & amortization (non-cash) +2,337
− Maintenance capex (judgment: ~ D&A level) −2,600
− Incremental working capital (negative → favorable, taken as ~0 conservatively) 0
≈ Owner earnings ≈ 17,340 (~$3.90 / ADR)

Notice what happened. Reported capex was $13.6 billion, but the vast majority of it is growth capex — building capacity for a franchise Novo expects to keep selling. Maintenance capex — the spend "just to hold position and unit volume" — is far smaller, historically ~$0.6–0.8/ADR in the pre-boom years, call it ~$2.6 billion now. So owner earnings (~$17.3 billion, ~$3.90/ADR) are essentially equal to reported earnings — the mark of a high-quality business whose profits are real.

The teaching subtlety: if you naïvely subtracted all $13.6 billion of capex, you would compute "free cash flow" of barely $1.40/ADR and conclude Novo is expensive. That would be the error the manual warns against from the other direction — treating growth investment as if it were a cost of standing still. The right move is to recognize that Novo is voluntarily spending ~$11 billion to grow, and that spending is optional; an owner could throttle it and harvest ~$3.90/ADR of cash.

Return on invested capital — the signature of a real moat (Manual Ch. 6)

The manual calls ROIC "the single best measure of business quality." Let me show you the arithmetic:

  • NOPAT = operating profit × (1 − tax rate) = $23,835M × (1 − 0.194) ≈ $19,215M
  • Invested capital = equity $30,512M + total debt $20,603M − cash $4,240M ≈ $46,875M
  • ROIC = 19,215 ÷ 46,875 ≈ 41%

Forty-one percent. Against a discount rate we will set at ~9%, that is a spread of ~32 points. And remember, invested capital is now inflated by ~$11 billion of Catalent goodwill; on the pre-acquisition capital base, returns were higher still. An independent research service's own figures agree from a different angle: Return on Total Capital 36.4%, Return on Equity 57.7% in 2025 (and an eye-watering 70.4% in 2024).

A word on that ROE, because leverage and buybacks flatter it. A 57–70% ROE is not "normal" profitability — it is what happens when a hugely profitable company also runs negative working capital and has spent years buying back stock, shrinking the equity base. Watch how an independent research service expects it to normalize toward 27.8% by 2029–31 as the company retains more earnings and equity rebuilds (book value per ADR is projected to more than double, $6.87 → $16.10). Even 28% ROE is elite. Use ROIC (~41%) as the cleaner quality read; use ROE only knowing leverage and buybacks are inflating it.

The moat — real, but with a crack in it

What protects a 41% return from "the dynamics of capitalism [that] guarantee that competitors will repeatedly assault any business castle"? Novo's moat has several genuine sources:
- Scale and manufacturing. Making GLP-1 injector pens at hundreds of millions of units, sterile fill-finish — this is hard, and supply has been the binding constraint on the whole category. The Catalent purchase widened this specific moat.
- Brand and prescriber habit. Ozempic is now a household word; that awareness and physician familiarity have real value.
- Regulatory barrier. Approvals, trial data, and IP are moats by law and by cost.
- Distribution and payer relationships, built over decades in insulin.

Now be skeptical, as the manual demands — what could erode this in ten years? Here is the crack, and it is a real one:
- Eli Lilly is out-innovating on efficacy. Lilly's Mounjaro/Zepbound combined for $12.8 billion in Q1 2026 sales, up 108% year-over-year, and its next-gen assets have beaten Novo's CagriSema on weight loss. The moat's most valuable input — pricing power — is the manual's decisive test of a moat, and Novo's pricing power is visibly weakening: its own oral launch had to come in cheap.
- The buyer is consolidating and negotiating. A U.S. deal will lower prices for many of Novo's weight-loss drugs for Medicare/Medicaid patients starting this year (the Inflation Reduction Act and "Most Favored Nation" pricing). Washington is now a price-setter, which directly attacks condition (3) of Buffett's "economic franchise" — not subject to price regulation.
- Litigation overhang. A rising wave of lawsuits alleging GLP-1 stomach injuries.

So the moat is real but narrowing — wide enough to defend a very profitable #2 position in a growing market, but no longer wide enough to guarantee the 20% growth of 2021–2024. PRINCIPLE: durable earning power proven by ROIC far above the cost of capital is what you are really buying — but a moat is only as good as its pricing power, and when a competitor out-innovates and the government sets prices, you must widen your margin of safety to match the narrowing moat.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Novo has an unusual and, on balance, reassuring ownership structure: the Novo Nordisk Foundation controls 77% of the votes while owning 28% of the shares (through high-vote A shares). This is the Danish long-term-steward model. The good: it insulates management from short-term Mr. Market pressure and the "institutional imperative" to chase quarterly fads; the Foundation's charter is to sustain the company for the very long run — exactly the horizon the manual prizes. The caution: public B-share holders like us are minority partners with limited votes, so we are riding with the Foundation's judgment, not steering.

The capital-allocation record, judged across the manual's five uses of a dollar:
- Reinvest: Novo is plowing ~$11 billion of growth capex into capacity. By the one-dollar test — retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r — this passes easily if the volume materializes: incremental returns in this business have been 30–40%+, far above the ~9% hurdle. The risk is not the return per dollar but whether the demand (and price) holds to fill the new capacity.
- Acquire: the $16.5 billion Catalent deal is the big test. It is vertical (securing supply), not empire-building, so it is not the "institutional imperative" the manual warns against — but it was expensive and debt-funded, and it must be judged over years, not headlines.
- Dividends: raised steadily; ~53% of profit now paid out, a ~3.5% yield at today's price. Dividend growth has been ~14% over a decade.
- Buybacks: Novo has repurchased stock for years, shrinking shares from ~5.7 billion (2010) to ~4.44 billion (2025). Here the manual is stern: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value. Much of Novo's past buying was done at 25–30× earnings, near the top — not obviously value-accretive. The encouraging news for a new buyer: if Novo keeps buying now, at ~11–12× earnings and below our intrinsic-value range, those repurchases finally work for remaining owners.
- Pay down debt: likely, as the Catalent leverage is digested.

Finally, candor and change. The 76% collapse "is driving a change in top management" — the CEO was replaced (Lars Fruergaard Jørgensen out; Maziar Mike Doustdar now President & CEO). A board that changes the CEO after a strategic stumble is behaving like an owner, not protecting a hired hand — a modest positive, though a new CEO is also a new unknown. PRINCIPLE: over time you ride with management's capital allocation — a long-term steward-owner and disciplined vertical M&A are in your favor, but past buybacks near the highs are a reminder that even good managers destroy value when they ignore price.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

The master equation is always true — IV = Σ OEₜ / (1 + r)ᵗ — but the manual insists we treat it as the definition of value, not the only estimator, "because the inputs are uncertain and the discounting is hypersensitive." Novo is a stable, cash-generative, high-return franchise with a contested growth outlook. That profile calls for a normalized-earnings multiple as the primary tool, cross-checked by a DCF, a reverse-DCF, and a dividend read. Let me triangulate.

The starting input — normalized owner earnings. Reported 2025 owner earnings were ~$3.90/ADR, but 2026 is guided down to ~$3.40. A conservative "normalized, mid-cycle" owner-earnings figure that respects the near-term decline but not permanent collapse is ~$3.75/ADR (with a low case of $3.50 and a recovery case of $4.00).

The discount rate — gravity (Manual Ch. 11). Set r = the prevailing long rate + a premium for uncertainty. The U.S. 10-year Treasury is 4.47%. A quality equity premium would put a stable franchise around 8%. But Novo's growth is genuinely contested (that narrowing moat), so we add uncertainty: r = 9% base, tested at 8–11%. (Teaching aside: in Novo's home currency the gravity is lighter — the German 10-year bund is only 2.94%, and Danish rates track it — so a European owner discounts Novo at a lower rate. As dollar investors in the ADR, we bear both the U.S. rate and the krone/dollar swing. That FX exposure is itself a source of the range.)

Method 1 — Normalized owner-earnings multiple (primary)

What multiple does this business deserve? Its own history is a median P/E of 23 and a range of 17–31. But that was earned in the 15–20% growth era. A franchise now growing ~4–5%, with a narrowing moat but 40% ROIC and a 34% share, does not deserve 23× — but it deserves far more than a no-growth 10×. A fair range is 15–20×.

Owner EPS \ Multiple 14× 17× 20× 23×
$3.50 (trough-weighted) $49 $60 $70 $80
$3.75 (base) $52 $64 $75 $86
$4.00 (recovery) $56 $68 $80 $92

Center of gravity (base EPS, 15–20×): ~$56–75.

Method 2 — Two-stage DCF (cross-check)

Starting owner earnings $3.80/ADR, Stage 1 growth 4%/yr for 10 years (modest — below an independent research service's history, respecting the moat crack), then a terminal rate. Varying the two inputs the manual says dominate everything:

r \ terminal g 1.5% 2.5% 3.5%
8% $72 $80 $91
9% $62 $67 $74
10% $54 $58 $63
11% $48 $51 $54

Look at that table and absorb the manual's warning made concrete: holding growth at 2.5%, moving the discount rate from 8% to 11% swings value from $80 to $51 — a 36% swing from a few points of an unknowable input. This is why we do not trust a single DCF point. The central band (r 9–10%, g 2.5–3.5%) is ~$58–74.

Method 3 — Reverse DCF (the sharpest lens)

Instead of forecasting, ask what today's price already assumes. Solving the Gordon form for the perpetual growth implied by $48.31:

Discount rate Implied perpetual growth in owner earnings
8.5% 0.6%
9.0% 1.1%
10.0% 2.0%

Sit with this. At a 9% discount rate, the market is pricing Novo to grow its owner earnings ~1% per year forever — below inflation, i.e., a real decline. This from a company that grew EPS ~14% annually for a decade, that an independent research service still models at +4.5%, and that operates in a market forecast to reach $150 billion by the early 2030s. The market is not pricing a slowdown; it is pricing near-permanent stagnation. That is the value-investor's opportunity — you do not need Novo to reclaim its former glory; you only need it to do better than ~1% growth forever.

Method 4 — Dividend cross-check

At $48.31 the yield is 3.5%, with projected dividend growth ~9%. A dividend-discount model at r = 9%, g = 4–6% yields ~$35–60 — supportive of the lower half of our range and a reminder that you are paid a real 3.5% to wait.

Triangulation — the range, not the point

Method Low Base High

I weight the normalized multiple and DCF most heavily (they best fit a stable-but-contested franchise) and let the dividend model pull the floor down as a conservatism check. Intrinsic value ≈ $55 (low) / $70 (base) / $85 (high) per ADR — and note this range is wide on purpose, because the moat is contested and the currency floats. PRINCIPLE: value is discounted owner earnings by definition, but because the arithmetic is hypersensitive, you estimate it with the method the business deserves, triangulate across several, and trust an honest range over a false point — and the reverse-DCF, which shows what price already assumes, is often the most honest lens of all.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Put the live price against the range. NVO trades at $48.31 (June 30, 2026).

Against Intrinsic value Margin of safety = (IV − P) / IV
Conservative low $55 (55 − 48.31)/55 = +12%
Base $70 (70 − 48.31)/70 = +31%
High $85 (85 − 48.31)/85 = +43%

So at today's price there is a meaningful margin of safety against the base case (~31%) and a comfortable one against the high case, but only a thin one (~12%) against the conservative low. The manual's rule: "if we calculate the value to be only slightly higher than price, we're not interested." Against the base and high cases we are well past "slightly." Against the pessimistic low case — the melting-ice-cube scenario where Lilly wins decisively and prices keep falling — the cushion is slim. A patient buyer wanting the manual's full protection against that scenario would want a price closer to $44–46 or below (a ~35% discount to the $70 base), which is exactly where the stock sat a few weeks ago (it bottomed near $35.80).

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational here? This is a beautiful split-screen. Look at the two an independent research service pages:

A year ago the same analyst, at the same firm, modeled 2026 EPS at $4.35 and a $102 target. Reality came in worse — and Mr. Market, who had bid the stock to $148 at the 2024 peak on euphoria, swung to despair and sold it down 76%. He is not depressed about the whole market — the S&P 500 is up 24.5% over twelve months, near all-time highs, and an independent research service's Asset Allocation Model actually raised stocks to 65%. Mr. Market is depressed specifically about Novo (and the Drug industry generally, ranked 61 of 94 for timeliness), while euphoric about Eli Lilly (at ~$1,129/share) on the other side of the same trade.

That is the classic setup the manual describes: a sound, profitable business whose price fell on a genuine but known problem, in a corner of the market everyone has given up on, while the broad market is greedy. The value investor's discipline is to be greedy exactly here — but only with a cushion, and only if the moat holds. A falling price on a business whose economics are intact has made it less risky, not more (Ch. 3). The open question is whether Novo's economics are truly intact or genuinely impaired — which is why the cushion matters. PRINCIPLE: insist on a cushion and use Mr. Market rather than obey him — his despair about one name inside a euphoric market is precisely where mispricings hide, but a discount is only a bargain if the business behind it is sound.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

What kind of business is this? A wonderful business with a suddenly-contested moat, offered at a fair-to-cheap price. The economics are extraordinary — 51% operating margins, ~41% ROIC, negative working capital, A+ balance sheet, a 34% share of a market growing toward $150 billion. What has changed is not the quality of the earnings but the certainty of their growth: a stronger competitor and a price-setting government have narrowed the moat from "wide" to "contested." So Novo has slipped from "wonderful and compounding" toward "wonderful but no longer guaranteed to grow" — and Mr. Market has repriced it from 20× a growth story to 11× a decline story.

Answering the one question we opened with: the 76% fall is partly justified and partly overdone. Justified: the growth premium of 2024 was fantasy, CagriSema did disappoint, pricing power is eroding, and 2026 earnings really are falling. Overdone: at $48 the market prices ~1% growth forever for the clear #2 in an enormous growing market with 40% returns on capital — a price that assumes the moat doesn't just narrow but effectively disappears. You do not have to believe Novo wins the GLP-1 war to find that too pessimistic; you only have to believe it remains a strong, profitable #2. On our numbers, that belief is worth a ~31% margin of safety to the base case.

The 2–3 things a student must understand better before getting truly comfortable (the open questions):
1. The molecule race. How durable is semaglutide's franchise against Lilly's next generation and the coming oral competitors? This is the outside-the-circle question, and it dominates the outcome. You would need to read the clinical pipeline the way an owner would.
2. U.S. price and volume. How far do Medicare/Medicaid and "Most Favored Nation" pricing cut the 55%-of-sales U.S. profit pool — and does lower price × much higher volume still grow the dollars?
3. The footnotes we could not read. With no 20-F text and empty XBRL, we are trusting an independent research service's translated figures. Before committing real capital, read the actual 20-F — the segment detail, the rebate accounting, the currency hedging, the Catalent purchase terms.

The universal lessons this case teaches most vividly:
- Growth is a premium the market grants and revokes violently. Novo's business shrank ~15% in value terms; its stock fell 76%. The extra 60 points were the growth premium evaporating. When you pay up for growth, you are the one holding that premium when it leaves. Buy so that you need little or no growth to be made whole — as you can, roughly, at $48.
- The reverse-DCF is the antidote to both greed and fear. At the 2024 top, running the numbers backward would have shown the price required permanent 15%+ growth — a warning. Today it shows the price requires ~1% growth forever — an opportunity. Same tool, opposite signals, no forecast required.
- A narrowing moat is a discount-rate problem, not a "no." You do not have to declare Novo either a fortress or a value trap. You carry the uncertainty into a higher r and a wider margin of safety, and let price decide — exactly as the manual's Sample Co. worked out to "buy at $23, wait at $38."

This is a teaching case built to demonstrate a repeatable way of thinking — not personalized investment advice, and emphatically not a recommendation to buy or sell Novo Nordisk. Do your own work, especially on the three open questions above.
