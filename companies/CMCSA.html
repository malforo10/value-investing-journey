Comcast Corp. (CMCSA) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition
Valuation date: 2026-07-04 · Live price: $23.79

Welcome. Today we are going to do something that looks easy and is not: value a company the market has decided is dying. Comcast trades at roughly 6.7 times next year's earnings and yields 5.5%. A number like that is a question wearing a price tag. Either the market has handed us a dollar for sixty-seven cents, or the business behind the dollar is melting and the market is early rather than wrong. That is the one question we are here to answer, and we are going to answer it with arithmetic, not adjectives. Let us begin the way the manual tells us to — with the sources, then the business, then the numbers, and only at the very end, the price.

A note on one number. Comcast's GAAP net income for 2025 was $19,998M, but that includes an $8,853M non-cash gain on investment activity — a windfall, not earning power. An independent research service's adjusted EPS of $4.31 (≈ $15.4B of net income) strips that out, and so do we. Normalize before you capitalize (Ch. 7): capitalizing a windfall into a perpetuity is how careful arithmetic manufactures a large, precise, wrong answer.

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

How Comcast makes money. Two businesses, five segments.

Connectivity & Platforms (64% of revenue, the profit engine). This is the cable business grown up. In 2025 it produced $80,940M of revenue and $32,377M of Adjusted EBITDA — a 40.0% margin. It has two parts:
- Residential Connectivity & Platforms — $70,704M revenue, $26,653M EBITDA, a 37.7% margin. This is residential broadband (the crown jewel), the declining pay-TV bundle, and Xfinity Mobile (an MVNO riding Verizon's network), plus Sky in the UK.
- Business Services Connectivity — $10,237M revenue, $5,725M EBITDA, a stunning 55.9% margin. Growing 5.5%. This is the quiet gem: broadband and network services sold to enterprises, and Comcast paid $1.3B for Nitel in 2025 to push deeper here.

Content & Experiences (36% of revenue, where cash is invested). Three segments — Media (NBC, Bravo, Peacock streaming), Studios (Universal, DreamWorks, Illumination), and Theme Parks (Universal Orlando, and the new Epic Universe park that opened May 2025). Peacock generated $5.4B of revenue against $6.5B of costs in 2025 — a ~$1.1B loss (narrowing from ~$1.8B), while adding 8M paid subscribers to 44M. Epic Universe consumed years of growth capital and is now open.

Where the cash is made vs. where it is spent. Read those two paragraphs together and the whole company snaps into focus. The residential and business broadband franchise throws off 40–56% EBITDA margins — that is the cash machine. Streaming and theme parks are where a large slice of that cash gets reinvested, some of it into a loss (Peacock) and some into a bet not yet proven (Epic Universe). A value investor's job is to see the cash engine clearly behind the noise of the content spend.

THE ONE QUESTION — bargain or value trap? At 6.7× forward earnings and a 5.5% yield, Comcast is priced like a melting ice cube. And there is real ice melting: domestic broadband customers fell 711,000 in 2025, to 31.3 million — the tenth straight quarter of losses. Fixed-wireless home internet from T-Mobile and Verizon, and fiber overbuilders, are attacking the once-unassailable broadband moat. So: is this a cigar butt with one cheap puff left, or a durable connectivity franchise the market has mistaken for a dying one? Everything below is built to answer that.

CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE gate (Ch. 4). Can we forecast this? Mostly yes. A broadband pipe into 60+ million homes, sold on a monthly subscription, is one of the more forecastable cash streams in the economy — we can see the subscriber trend, the pricing, and the margin. The content side (box-office hits, streaming economics) is genuinely harder, but it is a minority of value and largely a call option. The knowable part dominates. The business is inside the circle — with the honest caveat that the rate of broadband erosion is the one variable we must forecast and cannot forecast precisely. We pass the gate, and we mark that variable in red.

PRINCIPLE. A share is a fractional claim on a business's future cash (Ch. 1). Before touching the price, know how the business earns — and separate the engine that makes the cash from the projects that spend it.

3. READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The three statements are one story told three ways; make them articulate — agree with each other — or distrust them.

Income statement (FY2025). Revenue $123,707M; operating income $20,672M (16.7% operating margin, an independent research service's differently-defined operating margin runs ~27%); interest expense $4,409M; a $9,503M line of "investment and other income" (that is the windfall); income before tax $25,766M; tax $6,106M (23.7% rate); GAAP net income $19,998M. Adjusted for the windfall, earning power is ~$15.4B (EPS $4.31).

Cash flow statement (the honest one). Operating cash flow $33,643M — up sharply from $27,673M in 2024. Against it: capital expenditures $11,750M plus cash paid for intangibles (capitalized software, etc.) $2,658M = $14,408M of total capital spending. That leaves free cash flow of ~$19,235M (OCF − all capex). Note how OCF ($33.6B) dwarfs adjusted net income ($15.4B): the gap is $16,211M of depreciation and amortization — a capital-heavy business where non-cash charges are large and real.

Balance sheet. Assets $272,631M; stockholders' equity $96,903M; cash $9,481M; total debt $94,612M (3/31/26). The equity is thick with intangibles — cable franchise rights and goodwill — so book value overstates tangible backing (Ch. 7: never anchor value to book). An independent research service notes intangibles of $143.3B, or $39.77/share.

Ratios as arithmetic:
- FCF conversion: $19,235M FCF ÷ $15,396M adj. net income = 125% — cash earnings exceed reported earnings. A good sign; the accounts are not flattering cash.
- Operating margin (VL basis) ~27.5%, drifting down from 33% a decade ago as the mix shifts and video declines.

Earnings-quality scan (Ch. 7 red-flag list):
- EBITDA-as-cash-flow? Management leans on "Adjusted EBITDA" by segment — a caution flag in the manual, because it deletes the cost of maintaining a $200B+ asset base. We will not use EBITDA as our value input; we use owner earnings.
- One-time gains? Yes — the $8.9B investment gain, which we removed. To their credit, it is clearly disclosed, not buried.
- Revenue recognition? The auditor flagged bundled-services revenue as a Critical Audit Matter — inherent complexity, but tested and clean.
- Verdict: earnings quality is good. Cash flow exceeds reported profit, the windfall is transparent, and the accounts articulate.

PRINCIPLE. Reported earnings are an opinion; cash is a fact (Ch. 7). Here the cash flow statement is the hero — OCF of $33.6B is what a cable business really is.

4. EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The XBRL trend (2016 → 2025):

Year Revenue ($M) OCF ($M) Adj/Reported context
2016 80,403 19,691 pre-Sky
2019 108,942 25,697 Sky acquired
2021 116,385 29,146 pandemic broadband peak
2023 121,572 28,501 —
2024 123,731 27,673 —
2025 123,707 33,643 Epic opens; video declines

Revenue has plateaued (~$121–124B for four years); operating cash flow, remarkably, hit a record $33.6B in 2025. That divergence is the investment case in one line: the top line is flat while the cash engine still strengthens, because broadband and business services are higher-margin than the video they are replacing.

Owner earnings — the hard part (Ch. 7). The manual insists we split maintenance capex (what it costs to stand still) from growth capex (Epic Universe, network expansion). Comcast does not hand us the split, so we reason it out:

  • Total capital spending = $14,408M (PP&E $11,750M + intangibles $2,658M).
  • Connectivity & Platforms capex was $8.7B, mostly network upkeep and DOCSIS/fiber upgrades — largely maintenance.
  • Epic Universe and Universal Beijing construction, plus a slice of network expansion, are growth. We estimate growth capex ≈ $3.9B, and therefore maintenance capex ≈ $10.5B.

Owner earnings = adjusted net income + D&A − maintenance capex (ΔWC roughly neutral):
$15,396M + $16,211M − $10,500M = ≈ $21,107M, or $5.91 per share.

That is the generous read (it ignores intangible spend as maintenance). A conservative read — treating the whole $14.4B of capital spending as required, i.e. plain FCF — gives $19,235M, or $5.38/share. And because 2026 is a guided trough (an independent research service sees EPS falling >15% to $3.55 on the Versant spin-off, NBA-rights costs, and a broadband "reset"), we normalize below both: we carry normalized owner earnings of ~$13.5B (≈ $3.78/share) into the valuation. This deliberately haircuts for the trough and for the melting ice cube. If we are wrong, we are wrong on the safe side.

The moat — argued, not assumed (Ch. 5). Comcast's broadband franchise passes Buffett's three-part franchise test partially:
1. Needed or desired? Home internet is now a utility. Yes.
2. No close substitute? This is the cracking condition. For twenty years cable had a near-monopoly on high-speed home internet. That is eroding — fixed-wireless (T-Mobile/Verizon) and fiber overbuilders are real substitutes now. The 711,000 subscriber loss is the moat leaking.
3. Not price-regulated? Largely yes.

So the moat is narrowing, not gone. Comcast still has the fastest speeds for data-heavy households, a bundled-mobile lever (Xfinity Mobile), and a cost advantage in dense footprints. But it has lost the pricing power that defines a wide moat — note management is now cutting to simpler, cheaper plans and leaning on promotions. A business that must cut price to hold volume does not have a wide moat. Call it a narrowing moat around a still-essential product — durable enough to throw off cash for a long time, not durable enough to assume growth.

ROIC vs. cost of capital (Ch. 6) — the decisive comparison:
- NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax) = $20,672M × 0.76 = $15,711M.
- Invested capital = equity $96,903M + total debt $94,612M − cash $9,481M = $182,034M.
- ROIC = $15,711M ÷ $182,034M = 8.6%.
- Cost of capital r ≈ 9.5% (≈ 5% long bond + ~4.5% premium for a levered, slow-growth media franchise).

ROIC (8.6%) sits right at or just below r (9.5%). This is the whole verdict in one number. Comcast is not a capital-light 30%-ROIC franchise like See's. It is a capital-hungry business earning about its cost of capital — which means growth here creates little or no value (Ch. 11: ROIC ≈ r → growth is a wash). The value is in the existing cash stream, not in expansion. (The invested-capital base is inflated by decades of goodwill and franchise intangibles; on tangible capital the return is higher — but the honest, whole-enterprise figure is ~8.6%.)

ROE is more flattering: adjusted net income ÷ equity = $15,396M ÷ $96,903M = 15.9% — but that is ROIC levered up with $95B of debt. An independent research service's 13.5%. The leverage lifts ROE above ROIC; that is a warning, not a comfort.

PRINCIPLE. The single most important comparison in investing is ROIC vs. r (Ch. 6). At 8.6% vs. 9.5%, Comcast earns roughly its cost of capital: it is a cash cow, not a compounder. Value it for the cash it returns, not the growth it reinvests.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Can it survive a bad year and a shut credit market? (Ch. 8)

  • Leverage. Total debt $94,612M, cash $9,481M → net debt $85,131M.
  • Net debt ÷ OCF = $85,131M ÷ $33,643M = 2.53× — a little over two-and-a-half years of operating cash flow would clear it. For a stable, subscription cash stream, that is manageable.
  • Net debt ÷ owner earnings = $85,131M ÷ $21,107M = 4.0×.
  • Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest = $20,672M ÷ $4,409M = 4.7× (an independent research service: 5.7× on total interest coverage). A comfortable, not luxurious, cushion.
  • Cost & structure of debt. Weighted-average effective rate 4.0%; substantially all fixed-rate, senior unsecured; fair value ~$90.3B. Long-term debt $89.2B, current portion ~$5.4B.
  • Liquidity. Working capital is negative (current liabilities $33,308M > current assets $28,821M at 3/31/26) — normal and healthy for a subscription business that collects in advance and pays suppliers later; it is a source of float, not a red flag. Cash $9.5B plus undrawn revolver plus $33.6B annual OCF covers the ~$5.4B current maturity many times over.
  • Refi wall & hidden obligations. Debt is well-laddered and mostly long-dated at fixed 4% — Comcast does not depend on the kindness of lenders next quarter. Uncapitalized leases only $889M. No pension bomb flagged.

One-line verdict: Financial Strength A (an independent research service agrees). The leverage is large in absolute dollars but serviceable — and here is the tie-back the manual demands: leverage is safe only as long as the broadband cash is stable. 2.5× net-debt-to-OCF is fine for a utility-like stream and dangerous for a melting one. The debt verdict is hostage to the moat verdict. Today the cash is stable and even growing; that is why the balance sheet passes.

PRINCIPLE. Judge debt by the stability of the cash that services it, not by a rule of thumb (Ch. 8). Comcast's leverage is prudent if broadband holds — which makes Section 4's moat question the real solvency question.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

The six uses of a dollar (Ch. 9), scored on Comcast's record.

The decade share-count decline. an independent research service's common shares outstanding: 4,751.6M (2016) → 3,572M (2025) — a ~25% reduction in the share count in nine years. In 2025 alone Comcast bought back 205M shares for $6.8B and had $8.9B left on a $15B authorization. Cumulative buybacks 2016–2025 exceed $67B. Every remaining owner's slice of the broadband engine grew by a quarter without lifting a finger.

The dividend record. Dividends per share rose from $0.55 (2016) to $1.30 (2025), now $1.32 annualized — a long, unbroken record of annual increases (a Dividend-Aristocrat-like streak). Total dividends paid grew from $2.6B to $4.9B. Payout is a modest ~30% of net profit — abundant room to keep raising it.

The one-dollar test (Ch. 9) on the two growth bets:
- Peacock: still losing ~$1.1B (narrowing). By the strict test, a dollar retained here has produced less than a dollar of value so far — it fails today, though the loss is shrinking and it is defensible as a strategic hedge against cord-cutting. Watch it; do not credit it.
- Epic Universe: billions of growth capex, opened May 2025, already lifting theme-park revenue. Too early to score, but parks historically earn good returns on capital, and this one is now done spending and starting to earn — the capex headwind becomes a cash tailwind. Cautiously incomplete-but-promising.

Buybacks on price — the crucial judgment. The manual is emphatic: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value; the price paid is everything. Comcast is repurchasing stock at ~6.7× forward earnings and a 15%+ owner-earnings yield. If the business is not melting, these buybacks are powerfully value-accretive — management is retiring a 15% cash yield for shareholders. This is exactly the condition under which buybacks are a gift. The one caveat: buybacks funded while carrying $95B of debt are a choice to lever the equity; it works beautifully if broadband holds and stings if it doesn't. On balance, buying your own 15% yield at 6.7× is rational capital allocation — the single best thing management can do with the cash at this price.

Candor & control. Disclosure is detailed and the windfall was flagged plainly (candor: good). Governance caveat: Brian Roberts controls ~33% of the vote through Class B super-voting shares while owning a sliver of the economics — a permanent control structure investors must accept. It has, to be fair, delivered decades of disciplined allocation.

PRINCIPLE. Share count is capital allocation (Ch. 9). A 25% decade reduction in shares, bought at a mid-single-digit multiple, is management passing the one-dollar test on its own stock — provided the stock is truly cheap, which is precisely what we test next.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Method (Ch. 10). For a low-multiple, cash-generative, low-growth, levered franchise, the primary estimator is a normalized owner-earnings / FCF capitalization, cross-checked by a dividend-discount model and a reverse DCF. We are not pricing off peer multiples (the manual forbids anchoring to Mr. Market's opinion of other cable stocks).

The input. Normalized owner earnings ≈ $13.5B (conservatively below FY2025's $19.2B FCF and $21.1B owner earnings, to absorb the 2026 trough and broadband erosion), = $3.78/share on 3,572M shares.

Discount rate as gravity (Ch. 12). r = 9.5% = ~5% long bond + ~4.5% premium. With the 30-year Treasury at ~5%, the gravity is heavy — every future dollar is worth less than it was when bonds paid 2%. This is why Comcast's multiple compressed; it is not solely a business story.

The three cross-checks (Ch. 14 triangulation):

1. No-growth floor (checks the growth assumption).
OE ÷ r = $13,500M ÷ 0.095 = $142,105M, or $39.78/share.
Read this carefully. Assuming Comcast never grows another dollar, the business is worth ~$40/share — and it trades at $23.79. The entire market price sits far below the no-growth floor. In the manual's language, everything above the floor is a payment for growth; here the buyer is paying nothing for growth and getting a discount on the no-growth value itself. This single line is the strongest fact in the case.

2. Implied multiple / owner-earnings yield (checks output & anchor).
- Market cap = $23.79 × 3,572M = $84,983M.
- Price ÷ normalized OE = $84,983M ÷ $13,500M = 6.3× owner earnings.
- Owner-earnings yield = 15.9%. FCF yield (all-capex) = 22.6%. Against a 5% long bond, a 16% owner-earnings yield is an extraordinary spread — even if that coupon shrinks a couple percent a year, it still clears the bond. This is Chapter 12's gravity working for the buyer.

3. Reverse DCF (runs the model backward on the price — the sharpest test).
Solve for the perpetual growth rate the market's $23.79 implies, given OE $13.5B and r 9.5%:
$$g_{implied} = \frac{(MktCap \times r) - OE}{MktCap + OE} = \frac{(84{,}983 \times 0.095) - 13{,}500}{84{,}983 + 13{,}500} = \mathbf{-5.5\%}$$
At $23.79, the market is pricing Comcast to shrink its owner earnings by 5.5% every year, forever. That is not a slow melt — that is a business the market believes is in terminal, permanent decline. Is a 40%-broadband-margin franchise, with growing business services, record cash flow, and a controllable content-loss problem, really going to shrink 5.5% per year in perpetuity? That is the question the price is asking, and it is a heroic assumption of pessimism.

Dividend-discount cross-check (Ch. 12 gravity, income lens).
Gordon model on the $1.32 dividend, r = 9.5%:

Terminal dividend growth g DDM value
3% $20.92
4% $24.96
5% $30.80

The dividend alone — growing at just its recent trajectory (mid-single-digits) — justifies roughly $25–$31, above today's price, and the current 5.55% yield stands well above the stock's own historical 1.6–2.1% and above the 5% long bond. On dividends alone, Comcast is at least fairly valued to cheap.

Look at the entire table. Every cell — including the melting-ice-cube column at −1% growth and an 11% discount rate — sits above $31, comfortably above the $23.79 price. To justify $23.79, you must go off this table, to roughly −5.5% perpetual decline (the reverse-DCF result). The valuation is robust to pessimism.

Triangulated range (deliberately conservative, discounting the DCF outputs for the genuine erosion risk that the sensitivity table's mild cases may understate):
- Low ≈ $30 — the melting-ice-cube case: real, sustained broadband share loss, but a franchise that still throws off cash while it shrinks. (My −1% cells say $32–$36; I round down to $30 for a harder erosion path.)
- Base ≈ $37 — roughly the no-growth floor haircut for uncertainty; Comcast holds broadband roughly flat, business services and parks offset video decline, dividend keeps growing.
- High ≈ $48 — the modest-growth DCF (1–2% growth, r 9–9.5%) and an independent research service's own 18-month high.

Sanity vs. An independent research service. VL's 18-month target range is $21–$37 and its 2029–31 target is $50–$65. Our base ($37) lands at the top of VL's near-term range and our high ($48) sits just under its long-term low — consistent, and if anything our range is more conservative than an independent research service's. Good.

PRINCIPLE. Value is discounted owner earnings, and the multiple is that DCF in another language (Ch. 10). When the no-growth floor ($40), the dividend model ($25–31), and every sensitivity cell ($31+) all sit above the price, and the price implies −5.5% perpetual decline, the arithmetic is not ambiguous.

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

The arithmetic (Ch. 13), MoS = (IV − Price) / IV at $23.79:

Scenario IV Margin of Safety
Low (melting) $30 20.7%
Base $37 35.7%
High $48 50.4%

Even the melting-ice-cube case leaves a ~21% cushion. Against the base case, the discount is ~36% — the manual's threshold for "buy in size." The rare thing here is that the margin of safety is positive even when we assume the bear thesis is right about broadband, because the price already embeds a decline steeper than the bear thesis.

Where does the safety sit? The manual (Ch. 13) distinguishes a melting cushion (all the safety comes from the discount, and time erodes it) from quality safety (the earnings stream grows into the estimate). Comcast is a hybrid: the moat is narrowing, so some cushion is melting — but the melt is slow (record cash flow while losing subscribers), the dividend and buyback compound the owner's position while she waits, and the discount is so deep it more than pays for the erosion. This is not a wonderful business at a fair price; it is a decent-and-slowly-challenged business at a genuinely distressed price — the classic Graham situation, with a Buffett-grade balance sheet behind it.

Is Mr. Market depressed or rational? Both, partly. His fear is real — the 711,000 lost broadband subs are not imaginary, and rising long rates legitimately compressed the multiple. But he has extrapolated the fear to −5.5% forever, which is the depressive, not the rational, register. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, we find useful today (Ch. 2). He is offering us a 16% owner-earnings yield on a company still generating record cash. We should take the pocketbook and decline the wisdom.

PRINCIPLE. The margin of safety is protection against error (Ch. 13). When the price is cheap even on the bear's own assumptions, the errors that can still hurt you must be worse than the bear case — and that is a rare, favorable asymmetry.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Quality tier. Comcast is a Tier-2 business: a still-good, cash-rich franchise with a narrowing moat — decisively not a wide-moat compounder (ROIC 8.6% ≈ cost of capital tells us so), and decisively not a melting worthless shell (record $33.6B OCF, 40% broadband margins, A-rated balance sheet tell us so). It is a cash cow with a leak — and the market has priced the leak as a burst pipe.

THE ONE QUESTION, answered explicitly: bargain, not value trap. A value trap is a cheap business whose cheapness the business consumes faster than you're paid to wait. Comcast fails that definition on the facts: it is returning ~16% of its market cap in owner earnings annually, buying back 3–4% of shares a year at 6.7×, and growing both cash flow and the dividend while it "declines." The price implies terminal decline the numbers do not support. At $23.79, this is a cigar butt with a long fuse and a Buffett balance sheet — a bargain. The margin of safety is real even if the bears are largely right about broadband.

Open questions (honest ones):
1. How fast does broadband erode? If losses accelerate from ~700K/yr toward multi-million/yr, the low case moves down. This is the variable.
2. Does Peacock ever turn a profit, or become a permanent drain? Watch the loss trajectory.
3. Capital allocation discipline — will management resist a value-destroying mega-acquisition (Charter chatter, Warner assets)? Ch. 9's institutional imperative is the risk.
4. The Roberts control structure — permanent, and you are a passenger.

Universal lessons from this case:
- A low multiple is a question, not an answer. 6.7× forces you to ask what decline is priced in — and the reverse DCF turns that into a number (−5.5%) you can judge.
- The no-growth floor is decisive for a no-growth business. When you're not paying for growth, you don't have to forecast it — you only have to ask whether the existing stream survives.
- ROIC ≈ r means "value the cash, not the growth." Do not pay up for a capital-hungry business's expansion; prize its distributions.
- Cheapness plus a fortress balance sheet plus buybacks-at-a-discount is Graham's ideal setup — the discount compounds in your favor while you wait.

Teaching-case reminder. This is a seminar illustration, not investment advice. The numbers are as of 2026-07-04 and will move as broadband trends and interest rates move. Re-underwrite when they do.

11. GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (in order of appearance)

Adjusted (normalized) earnings. Reported earnings stripped of windfalls/one-offs. CMCSA: GAAP NI $19,998M − $8,853M investment gain ≈ $15,396M adjusted (EPS $4.31).

Adjusted EBITDA. Segment earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, amortization, and adjustments — a non-GAAP measure the manual treats warily. CMCSA: Connectivity & Platforms $32,377M (40.0% margin). Used for color, not as a value input.

Circle of competence. Businesses you can forecast. CMCSA: broadband subscription cash = inside; content hits = harder but minority. Verdict: inside, with erosion rate flagged.

Moat / economic franchise. Durable advantage; test = pricing power. CMCSA: essential product (✔), weakening "no substitute" (✘ — fixed-wireless/fiber), unregulated (✔). Narrowing moat; note management cutting price = eroding pricing power.

Owner earnings. = Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. CMCSA: $15,396M + $16,211M D&A − ~$10,500M maint. capex ≈ $21,107M ($5.91/sh); conservatively normalized to ~$13,500M ($3.78/sh).

Maintenance vs. growth capex. Standing-still spend vs. expansion spend. CMCSA: total capital spend $14,408M; est. maint. ~$10.5B, growth ~$3.9B (Epic Universe, network expansion).

Free cash flow (FCF). OCF − capex. CMCSA: $33,643M − $14,408M (all capex) = $19,235M.

ROIC. = NOPAT ÷ invested capital. CMCSA: $15,711M ÷ $182,034M = 8.6%.

Cost of capital / discount rate (r). Opportunity cost = long bond + risk premium. CMCSA: ~5% + ~4.5% = 9.5%.

ROE. = Net income ÷ equity. CMCSA: $15,396M ÷ $96,903M = 15.9% (ROIC levered by $95B debt; treat as caution).

Net debt. = Total debt − cash. CMCSA: $94,612M − $9,481M = $85,131M.

Net debt / OCF. Years of cash flow to clear debt. CMCSA: $85,131M ÷ $33,643M = 2.53×.

Interest coverage. = EBIT ÷ interest expense. CMCSA: $20,672M ÷ $4,409M = 4.7× (VL 5.7×).

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). CMCSA: buybacks at 6.7× pass; Peacock (−$1.1B) fails so far; Epic incomplete.

No-growth (earning-power) value. = OE ÷ r. CMCSA: $13,500M ÷ 0.095 = $142,105M = $39.78/sh. The floor — above the price.

Two-stage DCF. Explicit growth then terminal. CMCSA: base (2% then 1%, r 9.5%) ≈ $48/sh gross; discounted to $37 base for uncertainty.

Dividend-discount (Gordon) model. = D(1+g) ÷ (r−g). CMCSA: $1.32, r 9.5%, g 3–5% → $21–$31. Current yield 5.55%.

Reverse DCF. Solve for growth the price implies. CMCSA: $23.79 implies g = −5.5% perpetual decline — the sharpest fact in the case.

Owner-earnings yield. = OE ÷ market cap. CMCSA: $13,500M ÷ $84,983M = 15.9% vs. 5% bond.

Margin of safety. = (IV − Price) ÷ IV. CMCSA: vs. $37 base = 35.7%; vs. $30 melting case = 20.7%.
