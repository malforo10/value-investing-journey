Valuing Adobe Inc. (ADBE) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Co-taught by Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett
Date of analysis: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $219.72

Welcome. Pull up a chair. Today we are going to do something that sounds simple and is in fact the whole game: we are going to figure out what one business — Adobe — is actually worth, and then compare that to what a nervous fellow named Mr. Market will sell it to us for this morning. We will not be clever. We will define every term, show every division, and state the principle after each step, so that you leave with a method you can run again on any company, not a "price target" for one.

And Adobe hands us the single most instructive puzzle in the market right now. Here is a business an independent research service rates Financial Strength A, that earns a 47% return on equity, whose earnings have compounded at over 30% a year for a decade — and Mr. Market has marked it down to about 12.6 times earnings, against a historical median of 42. Two-thirds of the multiple has evaporated. Our entire seminar is organized around one question: Why has Mr. Market done that — and is he right?

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What are we buying? A share of Adobe is a fractional ownership claim on a software company that sells creative and document tools, and marketing software, almost entirely by subscription. In fiscal 2025, of $23,769M in total revenue, $22,904M — 96% — was subscription (10-K, Item 7). That single fact is the backbone of the business: recurring, ratably-recognized, high-visibility revenue. Remaining performance obligations (contracted-but-unrecognized revenue) stood at $22.52 billion, up 13% year over year. This is not a company that has to re-sell itself every quarter; it collects rent on installed workflows.

The three engines (fiscal 2025 revenue):

Segment FY25 revenue % of total YoY What it is
Digital Media (Creative Cloud + Document Cloud/Acrobat) $17,649M 74% +11% Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere, After Effects, Lightroom (Creative); Acrobat/PDF (Document)
Digital Experience $5,864M 25% +9% Analytics, Experience Platform, GenStudio — enterprise marketing software
Publishing & Advertising $256M 1% −7% Legacy

Digital Media is the crown jewel and holds two distinct moats inside it. Creative Cloud is the professional standard for imaging, design, and video — the software a working creative must know. Document Cloud rests on the PDF, a format Adobe itself created and which is now the global standard for electronic documents. Total Adobe Annualized Recurring Revenue (ARR) exited FY25 at $25.20 billion, +11.5% (Item 7).

THE ONE QUESTION

Is Creative Cloud's moat — professional lock-in, workflow switching costs, the PDF standard — durable against generative AI, or is generative AI a battering ram that will let "good enough" tools erode Adobe's pricing power and seat count?

Every dollar of Adobe's value depends on the answer. If the moat holds, a 47%-ROE franchise at 12x earnings is one of the great bargains of the decade. If generative image/video tools genuinely commoditize what Photoshop and Premiere do, then the historical 42x multiple was the fantasy and 12x may still be too generous. We will hold both possibilities open and let the numbers referee.

CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE — the gate

The manual's first filter is not price; it is predictability. Can we forecast this business within a sensible range? Two honest halves:

  • In the circle: The economics of a subscription software business with recurring revenue, near-zero unit cost, and a professional user base are understandable. We can read the ARR, the retention, the segment growth. The shape of the business is legible.
  • Straining the circle: The rate and direction of AI disruption over five to ten years is genuinely hard. Adobe's own 10-K names it plainly — the markets have "limited barriers to entry, short product lifecycles... and the frequent entry of new solutions or competitors," and lists "AI-first" and "cloud-native" companies as competitors (Item 1). This is not a business whose ten-year cash flows we can forecast with See's-Candy confidence.

Verdict on the gate: We pass — but narrowly, and the honest conclusion is that we cannot forecast a single point; we must forecast a range and let the margin of safety do the heavy lifting. That is exactly the discipline Adobe is built to teach.

PRINCIPLE. A share is a claim on a business's future owner earnings. The first question is never "how cheap?" but "can I forecast it at all?" Knowing the boundary of your circle is more valuable than expanding it. Adobe sits at the boundary — understandable in shape, uncertain in trajectory — so we forecast a range, not a number.

The three statements are one story told three ways. Read alone, each can mislead; read together — articulated — they cross-check one another. Here is fiscal 2025.

Gross margin = 21,218 / 23,769 = 89.3%. Operating margin = 8,706 / 23,769 = 36.6% (an independent research service reports 40%+ on its basis; the gap is classification, not substance). Effective tax rate = 1,604 / 8,734 = 18.4%. Note FY24 net income was depressed by a $1,000M Figma acquisition-termination fee — a one-time item we will strip when we normalize.

Balance sheet (as of 11/28/2025): Total assets $29,496M; total liabilities $17,873M; stockholders' equity $11,623M. Cash & equivalents $5,431M. Total debt $6,228M. Note that equity has fallen from $16,518M (FY23) to $11,623M despite booking $7.1B of profit — because buybacks ($11.28B in FY25 alone) return capital faster than earnings retain it. That is capital allocation, and we come to it in §6.

Cash-flow statement (FY2025) — where the truth lives:

Line FY25
Net income $7,130M
+ Depreciation, amortization & accretion $818M
+ Stock-based compensation $1,942M
+ Deferred taxes & other $(464)M
+ Change in working capital (incl. deferred revenue +$771M) ~$605M
Net cash from operations $10,031M

Free cash flow (OCF − capex) $9,852M

Articulation check: Operating cash flow ($10,031M) comfortably exceeds net income ($7,130M) — a 1.41x cash-conversion ratio. For most businesses that is a green flag. Here we must be careful why: a large chunk of the excess is the $1,942M of stock-based compensation added back as "non-cash." It is non-cash to the company's bank account, but it is very much a cost to you, the owner, because it is paid in the one currency you own — shares. We will not let it hide. Deferred revenue rose $771M, meaning customers are pre-paying — a mark of genuine demand, not channel-stuffing.

Earnings-quality scan:
- SBC ($1,942M, 8.2% of revenue): the single most important quality adjustment. Real cost, kept as a real cost throughout this seminar.
- No aggressive revenue recognition: subscription revenue recognized ratably; the 10-K's revenue-recognition policy is conservative and well-disclosed.
- Goodwill: large on the balance sheet (from past acquisitions), tested annually, no impairment in FY25. We will never anchor value to book — book understates a franchise like this.
- No "EBITDA-as-cash-flow" trickery in the filings; capex is genuinely tiny ($179M) because this is a capital-light business.
- One-timer: FY24's $1B Figma fee — flagged and excluded.

PRINCIPLE. Reported earnings are an accounting construct; the statements must be read together and reconciled to cash. The one number a software analyst must never wave through is stock-based compensation — it is non-cash to the company and a real, recurring cost to the owner. Add it back to compute cash flow if you must, but subtract it again before you call anything "owner earnings."

The ten-year trend (from XBRL)

Read those columns as a set. Revenue up ~50% in four years. Operating cash flow up ~39% in FY25 alone (partly because FY24 carried the $1B Figma fee). And the share count fell from 483M to 413M — down 14.5% — so every per-share figure grew faster than the totals. This is the signature of a capital-light compounder that returns its excess cash by shrinking itself.

The owner-earnings ladder (the honest number)

The manual's definition: Owner Earnings = Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. In practice, for a business with trivial capex, the cleanest path starts from operating cash flow (which already handles working capital) and makes SBC honest:

  Operating cash flow                    $10,031M
  − Capital expenditure                  $  (179)M   (maintenance ≈ all of it; capex < D&A)
  = Free cash flow                       $ 9,852M
  − Stock-based compensation             $(1,942)M   (real cost, paid in your shares)
  = OWNER EARNINGS                       $ 7,910M

Owner earnings ≈ $7,910M. Note the three rungs of the ladder the manual demands:

  • GAAP net income: $7,130M (EPS $16.70) — the conservative floor.
  • Owner earnings: $7,910M — cash flow, less capex, with SBC kept as a cost. Higher than GAAP because D&A ($818M) exceeds capex ($179M) by more than SBC-net offsets.
  • Non-GAAP (Adobe's preferred): ~$8,715M, ~$21.10/share — this adds SBC back, which is precisely the number we refuse to use as owner earnings. The $1.9B gap between non-GAAP and owner earnings is the dilution the company would like you to ignore.

Maintenance vs. growth capex: Adobe spends only $179M of capex against $818M of depreciation. A business whose capex is a quarter of its depreciation is not under-investing in physical plant — it simply has almost no physical plant. This is the capital-light franchise of the manual's Chapter 6, the "See's Candy" archetype. There is essentially no maintenance-capex trap here; the cash is real.

Normalization: FY25 owner earnings of $7,910M are broadly normal-to-slightly-favorable (FY25 had unusually strong 25% OCF growth off a Figma-depressed base). We will not capitalize a peak. For valuation we treat ~$7.9B as the starting owner-earnings figure and let the growth path, not the base, carry the optimism.

The moat — and the AI question, argued not assumed

Adobe's moat has four planks. Let us test each against generative AI with the skepticism the manual demands.

  1. Creative Cloud professional lock-in & switching costs. Photoshop/Illustrator/Premiere are the tools taught in schools and required in professional pipelines. Switching costs are behavioral (muscle memory, file formats, plugins) and organizational (team standardization). AI stress-test: this is the plank under attack. Text-to-image and text-to-video tools (Midjourney, Sora-class models, open-source diffusion) can now produce "good enough" output for many use cases without Photoshop skills. The risk is real and asymmetric: AI does not need to replace the professional workflow to hurt Adobe — it only needs to (a) reduce the number of seats (one AI-augmented designer does the work of two) or (b) cap price increases at renewal. Both compress the two levers of a subscription franchise.

  2. Document Cloud / the PDF standard. Adobe owns the PDF format; Acrobat is the reference implementation; billions of documents flow through it. This plank is the most durable. AI actually helps here — Acrobat AI Assistant (summarize/query PDFs) is an upsell, and the PDF standard is not something a generative model disrupts. This is quiet, defensible cash.

  3. Experience Cloud enterprise switching costs. Adobe Experience Platform is embedded in large enterprises' customer-data plumbing — deep integration, high switching cost, but only 25% of revenue and slower-growing (+9%).

  4. Adobe's AI counter-move — "commercially safe" Firefly. Adobe's differentiator is indemnification: Firefly is trained only on licensed/owned data, so enterprises get IP protection AI startups cannot offer. This converts the AI threat partly into an AI product — Firefly, Firefly Services, custom models, Content Credentials. Whether this offense outruns the erosion is the crux, and it is genuinely unresolved.

Honest read of the moat: Wide but no longer widening, and under live assault on its largest plank. Document Cloud and enterprise are durable; Creative Cloud is the battleground. This is precisely why we will adjust the historical 42x multiple sharply downward in §7 — not because the moat is gone, but because its durability discount is higher than it was three years ago.

ROIC — is the return real, and does it beat the cost of capital?

The manual's central quality test. NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate):

  NOPAT = $8,706M × (1 − 0.184) = $7,107M
  Invested capital (equity $11,623M + total debt $6,210M) = $17,833M
  ROIC = 7,107 / 17,833 = 39.9%
  ROIC (net of $5,431M cash) = 7,107 / 12,402 = 57.3%

ROIC ≈ 40% on total capital, ~57% net of cash. Against a cost of capital of ~9–10%, this is a ~30-point spread — the arithmetic signature of a genuine moat. On tangible capital (stripping goodwill) the return is effectively off the chart, because the business runs on software and people, not factories. ROE = 55% on average equity (an independent research service's 47% uses a different equity base; both say the same thing: extraordinary).

But hold the manual's warning in mind: a high ROIC is only valuable if it persists. The 40% is not in doubt today. Its durability over ten years is the AI question. ROIC tells us the castle is rich; the moat analysis tells us whether the walls hold.

PRINCIPLE. ROIC is the single best measure of business quality, and value is created only when ROIC exceeds the discount rate — here by ~30 points, the mark of a real franchise. But a moat is a claim about the future, not the past: a 40% return that competition (or AI) competes away in five years is worth a fraction of a 40% return that persists for twenty. Argue the durability; never assume it.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Before we trust any earnings stream to persist, we ask the prior question: can the business survive a bad year with the credit market shut? Adobe's answer is emphatic.

The manual's five questions, each with Adobe's own number:

  1. Leverage. Total debt $6,228M; cash $5,431M; net debt $797M. Against owner earnings of $7,910M, net debt is 0.10× owner earnings — the company could retire all its net debt from about five weeks of cash generation. Debt/OCF = 6,228 / 10,031 = 0.62. This is a fortress, not a balance sheet.
  2. Coverage. Interest coverage = operating income ÷ interest expense = 8,706 / 263 = 33×. A normal year covers the interest bill thirty-three times over. A catastrophe would have to erase 97% of operating income before interest was at risk.
  3. Liquidity. Current assets $10,386M (2/27/26) against current liabilities $11,390M — current ratio ~0.91. This looks thin, but it is a feature, not a bug: the large current liability is deferred revenue ($7.03B), which is not a cash obligation — it is revenue Adobe has already been paid for and will deliver as software, at ~90% margin. Strip deferred revenue and the liquidity picture is pristine.
  4. Refinancing/maturities. Long-term debt $5,379M, well-laddered senior notes (2030 and later), $250M annual interest. A $3B commercial-paper program exists but had zero outstanding at year-end. Adobe does not depend on the kindness of lenders.
  5. Hidden obligations. Uncapitalized operating leases ~$89M/year — trivial. No defined-benefit pension plan (an independent research service) — no hidden pension deficit. Nothing lurking.

One-line verdict: A fortress balance sheet — Financial Strength A is earned. This company cannot be forced to its knees; it will live to compound through any AI storm. That matters enormously for our valuation, because it removes the one risk the manual will not insure against — permanent loss from insolvency. Adobe's risk is competitive, not financial.

PRINCIPLE. Earnings mean nothing if the enterprise producing them can be forced to sell at the bottom. Modest, well-laddered debt against a torrent of recurring cash — net debt at one-tenth of a single year's owner earnings, interest covered 33×— is exactly the arrangement that lets a business survive to let its moat play out. Financial strength is the precondition for trusting every other number in this seminar.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Capital allocation is where a dollar of earnings either reaches you or is squandered. The manual lists six uses of a dollar; here is Adobe's scorecard.

  1. Reinvest in the business — R&D was $4,294M (18% of revenue), funding the Firefly AI counter-offensive. At ~40% incremental ROIC, this passes the one-dollar test decisively.
  2. Acquisitions — a genuine caution flag. Adobe tried to buy Figma for $20B (blocked by regulators, cost a $1B breakup fee in FY24) and has a pending purchase of Semrush. The Figma episode is a Dexter-Shoe warning: management was willing to pay a very rich price and, critically, part of the risk was that it revealed an appetite to spend. Watch this.
  3. Pay down debt — not a priority; the balance sheet is already a fortress.
  4. Dividends — none. Adobe pays no dividend, consistent with the software-industry norm of funding growth and buying back stock.
  5. Repurchase shares — the headline. Buybacks: $9,500M (FY24), $11,281M (FY25). Share count fell from 483M (2019) to 399M (latest quarter) — a ~17% reduction.
  6. Issue shares — the offsetting leak: SBC of $1,942M/year dilutes. The buyback is partly mopping up its own dilution.

The one-dollar test and buybacks-on-price

The manual is unambiguous: buybacks create value only when the stock trades below intrinsic value. Here is the crux. Adobe is buying back ~$11B/year of stock at ~12x earnings, well below every intrinsic-value estimate we will derive in §7. If the moat holds, this is one of the most value-creating buyback programs in the market — management shrinking the share count aggressively while the shares are cheap, so each remaining owner's slice of a 40%-ROIC franchise grows. If the moat is eroding, the same buyback is destroying value by buying a melting ice cube. The buyback's merit is entirely contingent on THE ONE QUESTION. That is the honest answer, and it is the same bet the whole case turns on.

Net share-count math: buying back 17% of shares while diluting ~2%/year via SBC nets to a real ~14.5% reduction over the decade — genuine per-share accretion, not a treadmill.

Candor: Management's disclosure is clear and detailed; the Figma failure was reported plainly. A CEO transition is underway — Shantanu Narayen (18 years) is moving off the CEO role to remain Chairman while a committee searches. Leadership continuity is a modest open question.

PRINCIPLE. Share count is capital allocation. A buyback is neither good nor bad in itself — the price paid is everything. Adobe repurchasing 17% of its shares below 12× earnings is brilliant if the earnings persist and folly if they don't; the buyback does not resolve the moat question, it leverages the answer. Judge management on whether each retained (or repurchased) dollar creates at least a dollar of value — and here that judgment is inseparable from the AI verdict.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Intrinsic value is the discounted value of the owner earnings the business will produce over its life. It is an estimate, a range, not a point. We will derive it three independent ways — a quality-justified owner-earnings multiple as the primary estimator, cross-checked by a two-stage DCF and a reverse-DCF — then triangulate.

The discount rate as gravity

The macro anchor (VL Selection & Opinion, July 10, 2026): the 30-year Treasury yields ~4.9–5.0%. Interest rates are gravity — every future dollar is discounted against them. We set:

  r = ~5% long bond + ~4.5–5% equity/uncertainty premium = 9.5% base

For a high-ROIC franchise this premium would normally be lower (~8–9%), but Adobe's AI-disruption exposure widens the uncertainty of the cash flows, so we push r up. We use r = 9.5% base, testing 9%–10%.

Deriving the multiple — and adjusting 42x downward, on purpose

Adobe's historical median P/E is 42. We must not simply apply it — that number was set in a world of (a) faster growth (10-yr revenue CAGR 19%, now decelerating to ~11–13%), (b) no AI disruption fear, and (c) near-zero interest rates. All three have reversed. We adjust it down deliberately, and we show the student the reasoning:

Factor Effect on multiple Adjustment
Growth decelerating (19% → ~12%) Lower −large
AI-disruption risk to the core moat Lower (higher discount, shorter horizon) −large
Long rates 5% vs. ~2% historically Lower (gravity) −meaningful
Fortress balance sheet, 40% ROIC Higher (quality) +some

Landing point: a durable, capital-light, high-ROIC grower facing real but survivable disruption deserves roughly 16–20× owner earnings — not 42×, and not the 12× panic price. On owner earnings of $7,910M and 399M shares (owner earnings ≈ $19.82/share):

  • 16× OE → $317/share
  • 18× OE → $357/share
  • 20× OE → $396/share

Cross-check 1 — the two-stage DCF

Master equation: IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. Three scenarios, all on owner earnings of $7,910M, 399M shares:

Scenario Explicit growth Term. g r IV/share
Low (AI erodes: moat cracks) 5% for 7y 2.5% 10% $311
Base (moat holds, growth slows) 9% for 10y 3.0% 9.5% $493
High (moat + AI upsell) 13% for 10y 3.5% 9.0% $778

Notice the DCF runs hot — even the AI-erosion low case says $311. That is the manual's guardrail at work: the implied multiple check. The base DCF ($493) implies ~25× owner earnings; the high case implies absurd multiples where terminal value is two-thirds of the answer. A DCF on a franchise this profitable always produces large numbers, which is exactly why we do not let it be the primary estimate — we anchor on the disciplined 16–20× multiple and use the DCF to confirm we are being conservative, not aggressive. The DCF says: even haircutting hard for AI, the math struggles to justify anything below ~$310.

Cross-check 2 — the no-growth floor

What is Adobe worth if it never grows another dollar? OE ÷ r:

  $7,910M ÷ 0.095 = $83,263M ÷ 399M shares = $209/share   (r = 9.5%)
  $7,910M ÷ 0.090 = $87,889M ÷ 399M shares = $220/share   (r = 9.0%)
  $7,910M ÷ 0.100 = $79,100M ÷ 399M shares = $198/share   (r = 10.0%)

Stop and look at this. The no-growth floor is $198–$220 per share. The stock trades at $219.72. Mr. Market is paying almost exactly the no-growth floor. He is pricing Adobe as if its owner earnings will never grow again — flat forever, from a 47%-ROE franchise that just grew cash flow 25%. Everything above the floor — all the growth this business is overwhelmingly likely to deliver — is being handed to the buyer for free.

Cross-check 3 — the owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond

  OE yield = $7,910M ÷ market cap ($219.72 × 399M = $87,668M) = 9.0%

A 9.0% owner-earnings yield, and the coupon grows, against a static ~5% long-bond coupon. This is a clearly superior claim — the equivalent of buying a bond yielding 9% whose coupon compounds at double digits. Gravity is with the buyer here, not against.

Reverse-DCF — the sharpest cut

Now run the machine backward: what growth does the $219.72 price silently assume? Solving the two-stage model for the growth rate that produces exactly $219.72 (term g 3%, r 9.5%):

  Price $219.72 implies a 10-year owner-earnings growth rate of ≈ −1.8% per year.

Read that again. At today's price, Mr. Market is pricing Adobe to shrink its owner earnings by roughly 2% a year for a decade. Against a 17% projected EPS growth rate (an independent research service), against 11.5% ARR growth just reported, against $22.5B of contracted backlog — the market has priced in an AI apocalypse. The gap between −1.8% and any plausible growth is the exact measure of how much pessimism is in the stock.

Triangulated intrinsic-value range

The estimators disagree in degree but agree emphatically in direction: every one lands far above $219.72. We weight the disciplined owner-earnings multiple most heavily (it is the least sensitive to terminal-value optimism), floor the range with the AI-erosion DCF/no-growth check, and cap it below the runaway DCF:

Per share Basis
Low IV $300 ~15× OE / AI-erosion DCF ($311) / near no-growth-plus-modest-growth
Base IV $360 ~18× owner earnings; the disciplined central estimate
High IV $430 ~22× OE; moat holds and AI becomes a net upsell

Intrinsic value ≈ $300–$430, base ~$360.

Sanity vs. An independent research service: VL's 18-month target range is $203–$417 (midpoint $310) and its 2029–31 target is $405–$610. Our base $360 sits squarely inside VL's near-term range and below its long-term target — we are, if anything, conservative. Good. We would rather be surprised upward.

PRINCIPLE. Value is discounted owner earnings, produced as a range and triangulated by roads that cannot all be wrong the same way. When a durable, high-ROIC franchise's no-growth floor ($209), owner-earnings yield (9% vs. a 5% bond), and disciplined multiple ($317–$396) all sit far above the market price — and the price only pencils out if the business SHRINKS for a decade — the market has done your negative analysis for you. Derive every multiple, adjust the historical one honestly downward for slower growth, AI risk, and higher rates, and still the arithmetic says cheap.

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value. At $219.72:

Against IV of Margin of safety
Low $300 (300 − 219.72)/300 = 27%
Base $360 (360 − 219.72)/360 = 39%
High $430 (430 − 219.72)/430 = 49%

Even against the conservative low end of our range, the margin of safety is 27%. Against the base case, ~39%. The manual's bar for buying "in size" is a wide discount to the conservative end — and we clear it against the low end.

Where does the safety sit? The manual teaches that in a great business, part of the safety lives in the quality itself — a growing owner-earnings stream makes today's estimate tomorrow's floor. But here we have something rarer: safety in both the quality and the price. We are not paying a fair price for a wonderful business (the usual best case); we are being offered a wonderful business at a distressed-multiple price — because Mr. Market is in a specific, nameable panic.

Mr. Market's mood: an independent research service's Computer Software industry report names it exactly — the "SaaS-pocalypse." His pocketbook, not his wisdom, is what we use.

PRINCIPLE. The margin of safety is the discount to a conservative value estimate — the cushion against being wrong. A 27–39% discount to a franchise this profitable, with a fortress balance sheet, is the rare situation where safety sits in both the price AND the quality. Mr. Market's AI fear is the source of the gift; his job is to serve you a price, not to tell you the truth about the business. Buy the business, not the mood.

11. GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with Adobe's numbers)

Owner earnings — Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital; the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. ADBE: OCF $10,031M − capex $179M − SBC $1,942M = $7,910M.

Stock-based compensation (SBC) — pay issued in shares; non-cash to the company, a real dilutive cost to the owner. ADBE: $1,942M (8.2% of revenue) — kept as a cost throughout.

GAAP vs. non-GAAP — GAAP net income is the reported floor; non-GAAP adds SBC back. ADBE: GAAP $7,130M (EPS $16.70) vs. non-GAAP ~$8,715M (~$21.10/sh). We use owner earnings ($7,910M), not non-GAAP.

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital — return on capital employed; the core quality measure. ADBE: NOPAT $8,706M × (1−0.184) = $7,107M; invested capital $17,833M → 39.9% (57.3% net of cash).

NOPAT — after-tax operating profit = Operating income × (1 − tax rate). ADBE: $8,706M × 0.816 = $7,107M.

ROE = Net income ÷ Average equity — ADBE: 7,130 ÷ 12,864 = 55% (VL reports 47% on its base).

Moat / economic franchise — durable advantage protecting high returns; its signature is pricing power. ADBE: Creative Cloud lock-in, PDF standard, enterprise switching costs — wide but under AI pressure.

Interest coverage = Operating income ÷ Interest expense — solvency cushion. ADBE: 8,706 ÷ 263 = 33×.

Net debt = Total debt − Cash — ADBE: 6,228 − 5,431 = $797M; Net debt ÷ OE = 0.10×; Debt/OCF = 0.62.

Discount rate (r) — opportunity cost = long bond + risk premium; the gravity on all asset values. ADBE: ~5% bond + ~4.5% premium = 9.5% (tested 9–10%).

Two-stage DCF = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ — explicit-period plus terminal value. ADBE base: 9% × 10y, term 3%, r 9.5% → $493/sh (runs hot; used as upside cross-check).

No-growth (earning-power) value = OE ÷ r — the floor if growth stops. ADBE: 7,910 ÷ 0.095 = $209/sh — essentially today's price.

Owner-earnings yield = OE ÷ Market cap — the earnings coupon vs. the bond. ADBE: 7,910 ÷ 87,668 = 9.0%, growing, vs. a static 5% bond.

Reverse-DCF — solve for the growth the price implies. ADBE: $219.72 implies ≈ −1.8% owner-earnings growth for a decade — the market prices in AI apocalypse.

Quality-justified multiple — the earnings multiple a durable franchise deserves, derived not borrowed. ADBE: historical 42× adjusted down to 16–20× OE for slower growth + AI risk + higher rates → $317–$396/sh.

Intrinsic value — discounted owner earnings; a range, a per-share quantity. ADBE: $300–$430, base $360.

Margin of Safety = (IV − Price) ÷ IV — the discount protecting against error. ADBE: at $219.72, 27% (low) to 39% (base).

One-dollar test — retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). ADBE: R&D and buybacks-below-IV pass; the Figma appetite is the caution.

Circle of competence — the businesses you can forecast; the boundary matters more than the size. ADBE: in the circle in shape, at its edge on AI trajectory.

Risk — the permanent loss of capital, not price volatility (Beta 1.20 is noise). ADBE: the real risk is competitive (AI erodes the moat), not financial (the balance sheet is a fortress).

Mr. Market — the manic-depressive partner who quotes a price, not a value. ADBE: mid-"SaaS-pocalypse" panic; his pocketbook is the opportunity, his wisdom is not.
