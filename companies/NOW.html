ServiceNow, Inc. (NYSE: NOW) — A Value-Investing Seminar

A teaching case in the Graham–Buffett tradition. I am the analyst here; the method is theirs, the numbers and the conclusion are mine. This is an educational case, not personalized investment advice.

Live price used throughout: $104.85 (equity_atlas.xlsx, as of 2026-07-14). All per-share figures are on the post-split basis (ServiceNow executed a 5-for-1 stock split effective December 17, 2025 — 10-K, Note 2), so they are directly comparable to that price.

Opening the case — the business

Let me start where an owner starts: what would I actually be buying?

ServiceNow sells subscriptions to a single cloud software platform that large organizations use to run their internal workflows. It began as the plumbing of corporate IT — the system a company's help desk uses to track a broken laptop or a request for new software — and has expanded outward from there into human resources, customer service, security operations, and now a layer of AI agents sitting on top of all of it. The company groups its applications into four areas — Technology, CRM and Industry, Core Business, and Creator and Other — but they all run on one platform with one data model (10-K, Item 1; Note 1). That "one platform" design is the whole commercial idea: once a customer runs three or four departments on ServiceNow, the platform becomes the connective tissue of the enterprise.

The way it makes money is beautifully simple and worth pausing on, because the revenue model is the moat. Of $13,278 million in total 2025 revenue, $12,883 million — 97% — was subscription, and just $395 million was professional services (10-K, Item 8, Consolidated Statements of Comprehensive Income). Customers sign multi-year, non-cancellable contracts, pay annually in advance, and the company recognizes the revenue ratably over the term (10-K, Note 2, Revenue Recognition). So the customer hands ServiceNow the cash up front, and ServiceNow delivers the service over the following year. That is why the balance sheet carries $8,314 million of current deferred revenue (10-K, Item 8, balance sheet) — that is money already collected for software not yet delivered. It is float, in the insurance sense: the customers finance the business.

The customers are the world's large enterprises and governments. One U.S. federal channel partner alone represented 11% of 2025 revenue (10-K, Note 2, Concentration of Credit Risk), and the company counted 603 customers with annual contract value above $5 million, up from 502 in 2024 and 420 in 2023 (10-K, Item 7, Key Business Metrics). These are deep, sticky, enterprise-wide deployments, not credit-card SaaS seats.

The one question that will decide this case. ServiceNow is, by almost every operating measure, an exceptional business — the renewal rate and the customer cohort will show that shortly. It is not in doubt that this is a high-quality franchise. The entire case turns on a single question: at $104.85, is Mr. Market asking me to pay for a decade of extraordinary growth that may or may not arrive — and is there any margin of safety left if it merely arrives on schedule rather than ahead of it? Quality is settled; price is the whole argument.

Circle of competence. Can I understand this well enough to forecast its economics a decade out? Partly. The revenue model — subscriptions, retention, prepaid float — is as clear as any business I could name, and the ten-year financial record is public and clean. What sits at the edge of my circle is the technology question: enterprise software is being reshaped by AI right now, and I cannot claim to know whether AI strengthens ServiceNow (more workflows to automate, a new product to sell) or erodes it (agents that make the seat-based platform less necessary, or AI-native rivals that rebuild the workflow layer from scratch). I can understand the business; I am honest that I cannot fully forecast the technology. That uncertainty does not disqualify the case — it tells me exactly where to demand a wider margin of safety. Principle: a stock is a piece of a business, and you value only what you can understand — and where the edge of your competence runs, the price cushion must widen to cover it.

The moat — is it durable?

Before I spend an hour in the statements, I make the judgment that can end the analysis early: is there a durable competitive advantage here, and can I name its source?

I can, and it is one of the strongest kinds: high switching costs reinforced by scale and a widening platform. Once a large enterprise has built its IT, HR, and security workflows on ServiceNow — with its data, its custom applications, its integrations, and its employees trained on it — ripping it out is a multi-year, high-risk project that touches every department. The evidence is in the single most important number in this whole case: the renewal rate was 98% in 2025, and 98% in each of 2024 and 2023 (10-K, Item 7, Key Business Metrics). A business that keeps 98 cents of every revenue dollar year after year, and then sells the retained customers more each year, has genuine pricing power and genuine stickiness. Net expansion shows up in the customer-cohort data: the count of $5-million-plus accounts grew 44% in two years (420 → 603) while the company kept essentially everyone.

The source of the advantage is worth stating precisely, because a moat you cannot name is a moat you do not have. It is not brand in the consumer sense, and it is not a patent. It is (1) switching costs — the platform becomes the system of action for the whole enterprise; (2) scale economies in R&D — ServiceNow spent $2,960 million on research and development in 2025 (10-K, Item 8), more than most rivals' entire revenue, and amortizes that across a huge installed base; and (3) a platform/ecosystem effect — each new module (HR, CRM, security, AI) makes the others more valuable and raises the cost of leaving.

Now the skeptic's question, which matters more than the admiring one: what erodes this over ten years? Management's own risk factors are unusually blunt — they describe a market "characterized by fragmentation, low barriers to entry, shifting customer needs and frequent introductions of new products" and name Microsoft, Oracle, SAP, Salesforce, and Workday as competitors, plus "new technologies and entrants... particularly those related to AI" (10-K, Item 1, Competition). The live threat — the one that took the stock from a split-adjusted high of $239.60 in 2025 to the low $100s (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) — is that AI changes the pricing model. ServiceNow's economics rest partly on selling seats to human users; if AI agents do the work of those users, a customer may need fewer seats, and the risk factors concede the company "may make significant investments in changing how we offer our products... such as incorporating more consumption-based pricing" (10-K, Item 1A). The other edge of the same sword: AI is also ServiceNow's biggest new product — its Now Assist line reportedly crossed $600 million in annual contract value at year-end 2025 and is guided toward $1 billion by the end of 2026 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01).

Verdict: the moat is wide today, but its ten-year durability carries a genuine, unusually visible question mark — not because the franchise is weak, but because the technology under it is in motion. I will call it a wide moat with a live AI question, and I will carry that qualifier into the price I am willing to pay. Principle: durable earning power must be defended by something real; name the moat's source, then be more interested in what could break it than in what built it.

Reading the statements together

Now the evidence. I read the three statements as the record of one enterprise and check that they articulate.

The balance sheet (10-K, Item 8) — a large cash pile, now spent. At December 31, 2025, ServiceNow held cash and cash equivalents of $3,726 million, short-term marketable securities of $2,558 million, and long-term marketable securities of $3,771 million — $10,055 million of cash and liquid securities — against just $1,491 million of long-term debt (a single tranche of 1.40% notes due 2030). Add $1,542 million of strategic investments (mostly private-company stakes, including a $750 million preferred position in Genesys — Note 3). Total assets were $26,038 million; total equity $12,964 million. Two judgment-laden items to flag: goodwill of $3,578 million and intangible assets of $1,121 million, which together (≈$4,700 million) are more than the entire equity base net of cash. That goodwill more than doubled in 2025 (from $1,273 million) because of a burst of acquisitions — Moveworks ($2.4 billion), Logik.io ($506 million), data.world, and others (Note 5). I will not anchor value to book here; a franchise like this carries enormous economic goodwill that the balance sheet does not show, and the accounting goodwill it does show is the price paid for growth, not a measure of worth.

But that year-end picture is already history, and the student must not stop there. The 2025 10-K described Armis and Veza as pending acquisitions. Both have since closed, and they were paid for in cash:

  • Veza closed March 2, 2026 for ≈$1.2 billion, substantially in cash — net assets acquired of $1,237 million (10-Q, Note 5, Business Combinations). Note this is the actual consideration, not the $1.25 billion "pending" figure the 10-K carried.
  • Armis closed April 20, 2026 for ≈$7.8 billion of cash consideration (10-Q, Note 19, Subsequent Events). Because it closed after the quarter end, Armis appears in no published balance sheet yet, and the purchase price allocation is still preliminary — ServiceNow expects to complete it in Q2 2026.

So roughly $9 billion of cash left the company, and the comforting net-cash cushion on the 2025 balance sheet is gone — not committed, spent. I will not credit the cash as a cushion in my valuation; it has been converted into goodwill and acquired revenue. The full accounting is in the next section.

The income statement (10-K, Item 8) — a decade of operating leverage, obscured by one tax event. Here is the multi-year march, which the long XBRL record lets me extend back:

A footnote on that 2019 line, because it teaches the seminar's central lesson twice over: An independent research service reports a 2019 "Operating Margin" of 8.5% on $3,460 million of revenue, which implies roughly $294 million of operating income — against GAAP's $42 million. That gap is not an error by either party; it is definitional. An independent research service is not measuring GAAP operating income. Whenever two respectable sources disagree by 7×, the first question is never "who is wrong?" but "what is each one actually counting?"

Revenue compounded at roughly 28% for a decade (an independent research service's own "past 10 years" figure — 2026-05-01) and grew 20.9% in 2025 alone (13,278 ÷ 10,984 − 1). GAAP operating income more than doubled — ≈2.4× (1,824 ÷ 762) — from $762 million (2023) to $1,824 million (2025) as the fixed-cost platform absorbed more revenue. Gross margin is superb: gross profit of $10,295 million on $13,278 million of revenue is 77.5% (10,295 ÷ 13,278), and subscription gross margin is higher still.

But look at the trap. GAAP net income was essentially flat — $1,731 million in 2023 versus $1,748 million in 2025 — while operating income more than doubled. A careless reader concludes earnings stalled. The truth is the opposite, and it is an earnings-quality lesson: in 2023 ServiceNow recorded a $723 million income-tax benefit because it released a $1.05 billion valuation allowance on deferred tax assets, having finally achieved sustained U.S. profitability (Note 17; the 2023 effective tax rate was negative 71.7%). That is a one-time, non-cash accounting event. Strip it out and 2023 net income on a normal tax rate would have been roughly $1,008 million pre-tax × (1 − 0.22) ≈ $786 million — so underlying earnings did not stall, they roughly doubled to 2025. Never read a single year's net income without checking what the tax line did.

The cash flow statement (10-K, Item 8) — cash confirms the profits, with a growth tailwind to strip. Operating cash flow was $5,444 million in 2025, up from $4,267 million (2024) and $3,398 million (2023). Capital expenditures were $868 million, so simple free cash flow was $4,576 million (5,444 − 868); ServiceNow's own non-GAAP free-cash-flow measure, which adds back deal and legal costs, was $4,636 million (10-K, Item 7). Notice that operating cash flow ($5,444M) towers over net income ($1,748M). Three things explain the gap, and a good analyst separates the honest from the flattering:

  • Stock-based compensation of $1,955 million (10-K, Item 8), added back as non-cash. Hold that thought — it is the hinge of the entire valuation, and I will not treat it as free money.
  • Depreciation and amortization of $738 million, genuinely non-cash.
  • A working-capital tailwind from growth: deferred revenue rose $1,179 million as prepayments flooded in (offset partly by −$758 million of new deferred commissions). That float is real cash, but it is a growth subsidy — a no-growth ServiceNow would not get the $1,179 million tailwind — so I will not capitalize it as if it were permanent earning power.

Earnings-quality scan. The accounts are clean and the auditor (PwC, since 2011) issued an unqualified opinion (10-K, Item 8). Revenue recognition is conservative — ratable over non-cancellable contracts — and was flagged only as a routine critical audit matter, not a concern. The one place management's optimism shows is the non-GAAP presentation, and I want the student to see exactly what it does. ServiceNow reports "non-GAAP income from operations" of $4,149 million against GAAP operating income of $1,824 million (10-K, Item 7). The single largest bridge item is adding back all $1,955 million of stock-based compensation. That one adjustment more than doubles reported operating profit. Principle: the statements are evidence and cash is the truth-teller — but when a company hands you a number that doubles its profit by waving away its largest real cost, your job is to put that cost back.

Earnings power & returns on capital — and the SBC question that decides everything

Is earning power growing? Unambiguously yes. Revenue, operating income, and operating cash flow have all compounded for a decade with 98% retention. This is not a cyclical to average through a cycle; it is a secular grower to normalize for quality of earnings, and the key normalization is stock-based compensation.

Building owner earnings — and why I keep SBC as a cost. Owner earnings, in Buffett's sense, are roughly the cash a passive owner could take out without harming the business: reported earnings, plus genuine non-cash charges, minus the maintenance capital the business needs to hold its position, minus incremental working capital. For a software company, one judgment dominates all the others: the treatment of stock-based compensation. ServiceNow's non-GAAP presentation adds back the entire $1,955 million of SBC as if it were free. It is not free. Those shares are a real transfer of ownership from existing holders to employees — the company even spent $1,840 million of cash in 2025 buying stock back largely to blunt the dilution (10-K, Note 14). SBC is a real cost of compensating the workforce; paying it in shares instead of cash does not make it disappear, it just moves it from the income statement to your ownership percentage. So I keep it as an expense. That single decision is the difference between "ServiceNow earns $4 billion" and "ServiceNow earns $1.75 billion," and it is the reason my valuation will look nothing like the bulls'.

Here is the ladder, laid out so a student can reproduce every step (FY2025):

Ladder rung Amount Note
GAAP net income $1,748M 10-K Item 8; already deducts SBC and full book tax
+ Depreciation & amortization (non-cash) +$738M 10-K Item 8 cash flow
− Maintenance capital expenditure −$738M set ≈ D&A (conservative; see below)
− (SBC — NOT added back) $0 kept as the real cost it is
= Owner earnings ≈ $1,748M ≈ $1.75B

Two judgments in that table deserve defense. First, maintenance capex: total capex was $868 million, of which a large part is growth — ServiceNow has committed $1.9 billion through 2028 to expand data centers (10-K, Note 18). I set maintenance capex equal to D&A ($738 million), which conservatively assumes the entire depreciation charge must be re-spent just to stand still; this makes the D&A add-back and the maintenance-capex subtraction cancel, so owner earnings collapse to net income. Read that equality for what it is: a conservative judgment I am imposing, not a fact I measured. Nobody discloses maintenance capex; I chose the harsh end of a defensible range. A less harsh reader — one who excluded the $120 million of acquired-intangible amortization from the "must be re-spent" bucket, since no one re-buys an intangible to stand still — would carry owner earnings toward $1.9–2.1 billion. I take the low end deliberately, but a student should know the number moved because I pushed it, and should be able to say by how much. Second, I do not add back the $120 million of acquired-intangible amortization (Note 6), even though non-GAAP does — because those intangibles were bought for growth, and adding back the amortization while ignoring the cash spent to acquire them would double-count. Both choices keep me conservative, which is exactly where a value investor wants to err.

So my single normalized owner-earnings figure is ≈ $1.75 billion, and I will use it, and nothing else, in every cross-check below. Now the denominator, stated explicitly — because "per share" is meaningless until you say which shares:

FY2025 owner earnings on Q1'26 diluted shares (1,039.9M) ≈ $1.68; on FY2025 diluted weighted-average shares (1,046.7M) it is $1.67.

I use the most recent count — $1,748M ÷ 1,039.9M ≈ $1.68 — throughout, on the reasoning that the share base I would be buying into today is the current one, not last year's average (10-K, Note 16; 10-Q). The choice moves the answer by one cent and is immaterial to the verdict; I state it only so the arithmetic is reproducible. (Shares outstanding had fallen further, to ~1,031 million by March 31, 2026, as buybacks outran issuance.)

That is essentially identical to GAAP diluted EPS of $1.67 (10-K, Note 16) — which is the honest answer for an asset-light software business where D&A ≈ maintenance capex and the big adjustment (SBC) is left in as a cost.

The share basis, reconciled. This matters, because a per-share valuation is worthless on the wrong denominator. ServiceNow executed a 5-for-1 split effective December 17, 2025, and retroactively restated all per-share history (10-K, Note 2). The $104.85 price, the $1.67 EPS, and my $1.68 owner earnings per share are all on the identical post-split basis. Share count is drifting down modestly — from 1,047 million outstanding at year-end 2025 to ~1,031 million by March 31, 2026 — because buybacks ($1,840 million in 2025, with a new $5 billion authorization in January 2026 plus a $2 billion accelerated repurchase — Note 14, an independent research service 2026-05-01) are now slightly outrunning the ~2%/year issuance from SBC and stock-funded acquisitions.

Now test the moat against returns on capital. A moat is a story until the returns on capital confirm it. Return on invested capital is NOPAT over the capital the business actually employs. Here is the FY2025 calculation:

  • NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate) = $1,824M × (1 − 0.227) = $1,410M (10-K Item 8; 22.7% effective rate from Note 17).
  • Invested capital = equity + total debt − excess cash = $12,964M + $1,491M − $10,055M = $4,400M.
  • ROIC = 1,410 ÷ 4,400 ≈ 32%.

Thirty-two percent against a cost of capital around 10% would be the quantitative fingerprint of a wide moat — returns three times the hurdle rate. The arithmetic is right. But I have to immediately qualify it, because that 32% is a pre-acquisition snapshot, and it is manufactured almost entirely by subtracting a $10 billion cash pile that no longer exists. Look at what the formula is doing: the denominator is small because excess cash is subtracted from it. Spend the cash, and the subtraction disappears while the goodwill it bought stays on the books.

So run it pro forma. Cash acquisitions add goodwill, not equity, so equity is roughly unchanged (~$11,728M at 3/31/26); debt goes to ~$7,591M once the term loan and commercial paper are in:

Basis Invested capital ROIC (on unchanged NOPAT of $1,410M)
FY2025, pre-spree (equity $12,964M + debt $1,491M − cash $10,055M) $4,400M ≈ 32%
Pro forma, no cash treated as excess (it is largely spoken for by the Oct 2026 term loan) ≈ $19.3B ≈ 7%
Pro forma, full ~$6.2B treated as excess ≈ $13.1B ≈ 11%

Pro-forma ROIC is therefore roughly 7–11%, against 32% before the spree — brushing the 10% hurdle rather than tripling it.

Now the honesty caveat, which matters more than the number. This is a transitional trough, not a steady state. The NOPAT in that table is a pre-deal figure: it excludes whatever Armis and Veza actually earn. I am dividing old profit by new capital, which is guaranteed to look ugly and would be a cheap trick if I let it stand as a verdict. So I will not say "ROIC collapsed." What I will say is this: the burden of proof has moved. Management has just converted roughly $9 billion of cash into roughly $9 billion of goodwill, and it now has to earn a return on it. Before the spree, the high ROIC was an observation. Today, it is a forecast — and forecasts are what get value investors killed.

Which means the moat evidence has to rest on the operating facts rather than on that ratio, and fortunately it can: the 98% renewal rate, held for three straight years, and the $5-million-plus customer cohort growing 420 → 502 → 603. Those are unaffected by the balance sheet and they are what actually demonstrate switching costs. Cite the 32% ROIC if you like, but cite it as pre-spree, and never as the load-bearing beam.

One thing the pre-spree figure did show that remains true and is worth keeping: that $4,400 million of invested capital was almost entirely goodwill and intangibles. On tangible invested capital, ServiceNow's invested capital was roughly negative — the $8,314 million of deferred revenue float funds the working capital. The customers' prepayments finance the business. That characteristic belongs to the operating model, not to the cash pile, and it survives the acquisitions intact.

Return on equity on the FY2025 books is more modest — ROE = 1,748 ÷ 12,964 = 13.5% (matching an independent research service's figure, 2026-05-01) — but that was not a knock at the time. ROE was dragged down by the $10 billion of low-yielding cash and securities parked in equity, and it was not flattered by leverage (there was almost none). Note the direction of travel, though: that cash is now gone into goodwill and the debt is real, so the two ratios are converging from opposite ends — ROE loses its idle-cash drag, ROIC loses its idle-cash flattery. Principle: persistently high ROIC far above the cost of capital is the moat made visible — but check what the denominator is doing before you believe it, because a ratio built on subtracting a cash pile only survives as long as the cash pile does.

Financial strength & solvency

This is the section that has changed most since the 10-K, and a student who reads only the annual report will get it badly wrong. The 2025 balance sheet showed ~$8.5 billion of net cash. That is no longer the company.

Start with the last audited-adjacent facts (10-Q balance sheet, March 31, 2026): cash $2,702M + marketable securities $2,480M + long-term marketable securities $2,724M = $7,906M of cash and securities; long-term debt $1,491M; total stockholders' equity $11,728M — down from $12,964M at year-end 2025, as buybacks outran earnings; goodwill $4,541M; intangibles $1,479M. Even this is a stale picture, because the big event came three weeks later.

Then add what the notes disclose (all from 10-Q, Note 19, Subsequent Events):

  • A $4.0 billion senior unsecured term loan, drawn in full on April 17, 2026, maturing October 16, 2026, with a six-month extension option.
  • A $3.0 billion unsecured revolving credit facility established April 1, 2026 — undrawn, maturing 2031.
  • A commercial paper program established April 1, 2026, with $2.1 billion outstanding as of April 22, 2026.
  • Armis closed April 20, 2026 for ≈$7.8 billion in cash.

Now the pro-forma estimate — and I label it an estimate deliberately, because the Armis purchase price allocation is still preliminary (expected to complete in Q2 2026), so precision here would be false precision:

Item Pro-forma estimate
Cash & securities: $7,906M − ~$7,800M (Armis) + $4,000M (term loan) + $2,100M (CP) ≈ $6.2B
Debt: $1,491M + $4,000M (term loan) + $2,100M (CP) ≈ $7.6B
Net position ≈ $1.4B NET DEBT

Two caveats on that table, in opposite directions. It ignores Q2 operating cash generation entirely, so the cash line is conservative — the real figure is higher. And it is lumpy in maturity: roughly $6.1 billion of the ~$7.6 billion is short-term — the October 2026 term loan plus the commercial paper — not the leisurely 2030 note the 10-K led with.

So: from ~$8.5 billion of net cash to ~$1.4 billion of net debt in one quarter. That is a genuine change in the character of this balance sheet, and I will not paper over it.

But I will not overstate it either, and the honest word is not distress. FY2025 operating cash flow was $5,444 million. A business generating that much cash services $6.1 billion of short-term paper comfortably — it can term it out, roll it, extend the loan six months, or simply pay it down out of one year's cash flow. The $3.0 billion revolver sits undrawn behind it. The remaining detail supports the same read:

  • Coverage. FY2025 operating income of $1,824 million against interest paid of $22 million (10-K, Item 8, supplemental cash flow) was ~83× — but that ratio belongs to the old capital structure and will fall materially as $6.1 billion of new borrowings carry real 2026 coupons rather than a 1.40% note from a zero-rate era. Do not quote the 83× forward.
  • Liquidity. Current assets of $10,471 million against current liabilities of $10,443 million was a current ratio of ~1.0 at year-end (10-K, Item 8) — misleading in the comforting direction, because $8,314 million of those liabilities is deferred revenue: not a cash bill, but a service to be delivered off an already-built platform.
  • Hidden obligations. Operating leases of $912 million present value and $7,946 million of non-cancellable purchase commitments — mostly $4.8 billion of cloud hosting and $1.9 billion of data-center capex through 2030 (Note 18). Real forward costs, but they are the cost of serving contracted revenue, backed by $28.2 billion of RPO. No pension deficit; no defined-benefit plan (an independent research service, 2026-05-01).

Verdict: strong, no longer pristine — and solvency is still not the risk. The company can pay. What changed is that a balance sheet which used to be an unambiguous asset is now an ordinary one, and the flexibility it represented has been converted into goodwill that must now perform.

Here is the lesson, and it is the most important one in this revision. Earlier I refused to credit the $10 billion cash pile as a valuation cushion, on the grounds that it was already spoken for. That refusal now looks less like conservatism and more like simple accuracy: the cash is gone. An analyst who had added ~$9 per share of "net cash" to intrinsic value in January would have spent the spring quietly deleting it. I do not have to delete anything — my intrinsic value does not change, because I never counted the cash in the first place.

That is what conservatism actually buys you. Not a lower number for its own sake — a number that does not need to be revised when the facts arrive. When you catch yourself adding a cushion to a valuation, ask the question that saved this case: is this asset free, or is it already promised to someone? Principle: solvency is the precondition that lets every other quality compound — and ServiceNow still clears it; but a cushion you have already committed was never a cushion, and the only way to find out cheaply is to refuse to count it before the facts force you to.

Management as partners

The capital-allocation record is a study in a fast-growing software company's temptations.

Reinvestment is the first and best use, and on the pre-spree record the one-dollar test passed with room to spare: a business reinvesting at ~32% ROIC creates far more than a dollar of value per dollar retained, so retaining everything (ServiceNow pays no dividend — an independent research service) was correct. The pro-forma ROIC of ~7–11% is a reminder that this test must be re-run, not assumed — it passes on the organic platform; whether it passes on the $9 billion just deployed is precisely the open question below. Buybacks: the company repurchased $1,840 million of stock in 2025 and $696 million in 2024 (Note 14). I judge buybacks on price, and here I raise an eyebrow — much of the 2025 repurchasing happened at split-adjusted prices well above $150 (the stock ranged to $239 in 2025), i.e. buying back near the highs, which destroys value if those prices exceeded intrinsic value. A new $5 billion authorization plus a $2 billion accelerated repurchase in early 2026 (Note 14; an independent research service) at least deploys into a much lower price (~$100).

The share count and the SBC give-away. This is the Dexter Shoe lesson to watch. Even with heavy buybacks, shares issued rose from 1,014 million (2022) to 1,066 million (2025) before treasury — because SBC ($1,955 million/year) and stock-funded acquisitions keep handing out ownership. On those stock-funded deals, be precise about which number you are quoting: ServiceNow issued ≈11.7 million shares for Moveworks and Logik.io specifically (≈9.6M and ≈2.1M respectively — 10-K, Note 5); the 13,235 thousand figure on the Statement of Stockholders' Equity is all 2025 business-combination issuances, which also includes data.world and other immaterial deals. Both counts are retroactively split-adjusted and sit on the same post-split basis as everything else in this seminar — you can check the tie: 9.6M × $153.04 ≈ $1,469M, against the $1,467M of consideration stated for Moveworks. Management is running hard on buybacks essentially to tread water against its own equity issuance. The net result is a roughly flat-to-slightly-declining diluted count — acceptable, but only because they are spending real cash to offset a real cost. This is precisely why I refused to add SBC back.

Acquisitions: the pace is aggressive, and it is no longer prospective — Moveworks, Logik.io, data.world, the Genesys stake, and now Veza (closed March 2, 2026, ≈$1.2 billion cash) and Armis (closed April 20, 2026, ≈$7.8 billion cash). The strategic logic (buying AI and security capability) is coherent, but ~$9 billion of cash M&A inside a year — funded with a six-month term loan and commercial paper — is the "institutional imperative" territory Buffett warns about. This is the single thing a student should now track, because the money is spent and the goodwill is booked: acquisition goodwill ran $1,273 million at the end of 2024, $3,578 million at the end of 2025, and $4,541 million at March 31, 2026, and once Armis is allocated it lands somewhere near $10 billion — roughly eight times where it stood eighteen months earlier (the Armis purchase price allocation is still preliminary, so treat that last figure as an estimate). The return on it is entirely unproven. Management communication (CEO Bill McDermott) is confident and promotional in the enterprise-software style; the disclosure itself is candid and complete. Principle: over time you ride with management's capital allocation — reinvestment at a high organic ROIC is a gift, but watch buybacks done near the highs and a $9 billion acquisition appetite, and judge them by the one-dollar test, not the press release.

What is it worth? — intrinsic value

Now the arithmetic — earned, because the business has passed every quality gate. The master equation is always true: intrinsic value is the discounted stream of future owner earnings, IV = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ. But because that discounting is hypersensitive to inputs, I will estimate it three independent ways, anchor everything on the same owner-earnings figure, and trust the range over any point.

The discount rate. Following the manual's doctrine, I use a normalized rate anchored to the long-term Treasury yield but floored — I will not accept a real expected return below ~10% whether short rates are high or low, and I add no beta and no company-specific premium (an independent research service's 1.25 beta is exactly the "mathematical gibberish" I ignore; I carry ServiceNow's real, technology-shift risk in the circle-of-competence gate and in the margin of safety, not in a fudged discount rate). I use r = 10%, held constant so this valuation stays comparable to every other case.

The anchor. Normalized owner earnings ≈ $1.75 billion, or $1.68 per share — SBC kept as a cost, as derived above. This one number feeds everything.

The three universal cross-checks (all on the same $1.68)

  1. No-growth (earning-power) floor = owner earnings ÷ r = $1.68 ÷ 0.10 = $16.80 per share. This is what ServiceNow is worth if it never grows again. The stock is $104.85. Therefore about 84% of today's price — ($104.85 − $16.80) ÷ $104.85 — is a bet on growth. Only sixteen cents on the dollar is covered by the business as it stands. That single line frames the entire decision.

  2. Implied multiple = price ÷ owner earnings = $104.85 ÷ $1.68 = 62.4× owner earnings. Is 62× defensible? For a no-growth business, never. For a high-quality ~15–20% grower with a wide moat, it is rich but not lunatic — the question is whether the growth is durable enough to earn back the multiple.

  3. Owner-earnings yield = owner earnings ÷ price = $1.68 ÷ $104.85 = 1.60%. Against a long Treasury yielding roughly 4.5%, ServiceNow hands the owner a 1.6% starting cash yield. Every dollar of the ~3-point gap must be made up by growth, and then some, before this beats a government bond. That is the price of admission.

Primary estimator: a two-stage discounted-owner-earnings model, with the sensitivity shown

ServiceNow's durability and growth are forecastable enough to attempt a DCF — but only if I show how violently the answer moves with the inputs. I grow owner earnings for ten years at a stage-one rate, then 4% in perpetuity, discounting at r. Here is the fair value per share across a grid (OE₀ = $1.68):

g₁ = 10% g₁ = 14% g₁ = 18% g₁ = 22%
r = 9% $55.96 $76.40 $103.93 $140.70
r = 10% $45.92 $62.18 $83.98 $113.03
r = 11% $38.79 $52.10 $69.89 $93.51

Read what this table teaches before you read any single cell. Holding growth at 18%, moving the discount rate from 9% to 11% cuts the value from $104 to $70 — a third of the value, gone, on a two-point change in one assumption. And moving growth from 14% to 22% at r = 10% swings value from $62 to $113. This is why a single "target price" is an illusion; the honest output is a range, and the range is wide because the business's value genuinely depends on unknowable inputs.

What I actually underwrite. As the analyst, I will not credit ServiceNow with the 21%+ decade-long owner-earnings growth the market is pricing (I derive that number next). An independent research service — which, note, uses GAAP earnings for ServiceNow, not the usual non-GAAP (footnote A, 2026-05-01), so its estimates are comparable to my owner-earnings basis — projects revenue growth decelerating to ~15% and GAAP EPS of $2.20 in 2026E, $2.55 in 2027E, and $4.00 by 2029–31 (2026-05-01). I will underwrite owner-earnings growth of roughly 12% (low) / 15% (base) / 18% (high) for the decade, reflecting a great but maturing franchise facing a real AI question.

Be clear about what that does: my 15% base sits below an independent research service's own near-term GAAP EPS path — their $1.67 → $2.20 → $2.55 implies growth in the high teens to low twenties out of the gate, and I am haircutting it from the first year. That is deliberate, not an oversight, and it is not a claim that an independent research service is wrong. It is the price I charge for two things I cannot resolve: a franchise that must mature off a $13 billion revenue base, and an AI question sitting at the edge of my circle of competence. If you disagree with the haircut, the sensitivity grid above tells you exactly what your disagreement is worth. At r = 10% my assumptions yield:

  • Low (g = 12%): ≈ $53
  • Base (g = 15%): ≈ $67
  • High (g = 18%): ≈ $84

A second, independent estimator: forward earnings discounted back

Cross-check with an independent research service's GAAP path. If ServiceNow earns $4.00 of GAAP EPS by 2030 (VL 2029–31 estimate) and by then trades at a still-premium 28–30× GAAP — appropriate for a business that by then grows slower — that is $112–$120 in 2030, discounted back five years at 10%: $112 ÷ 1.61 ≈ $70 to $120 ÷ 1.61 ≈ $74. At a more conservative 25× exit, ≈ $62. This lands squarely on my DCF base of ~$67, which gives me confidence in the center of the range.

Triangulated intrinsic value

The DCF base (~$67) and the forward-earnings check (~$70) agree; the no-growth floor ($16.80) and the reverse-DCF (below) bound the debate. I set my intrinsic-value range at:

Intrinsic value: low $52 — base $68 — high $88 per share.

My base of $68 implies a ~40× owner-earnings multiple ($68 ÷ $1.68) — a full price I am willing to name only because the moat is real, and never at any price.

An objection I have to answer: what about the acquired earnings?

A careful student should push back here, and the objection is fair. I removed the cash from the valuation — I refused to count $9 billion as a cushion. But Armis and Veza are now inside the company, and I have credited nothing for what they earn. That is conservative on the asset side and simply silent on the earnings side, which is not the same thing as conservative. It is one-sided. Let me fix it with arithmetic rather than adjectives.

Value the acquired businesses the same way I value everything else: capitalize their steady-state after-tax owner earnings at r = 10%, and divide by the ~1,040 million shares. Per-share value added = (acquired OE ÷ 0.10) ÷ 1,040M:

Steady-state acquired owner earnings (Armis + Veza combined) Value created Per share
$100M ~$1.0B ≈ $0.96
$200M ~$2.0B ≈ $1.92
$500M ~$5.0B ≈ $4.81

Now read the bottom row, because it settles the question. Even a generous $500 million of combined acquired owner earnings — on businesses bought for $9 billion, which would be a fine outcome — adds about $4.81 per share. The gap I am trying to close is roughly $37 ($104.85 − $68). Five dollars against thirty-seven.

The deals cannot close the valuation gap. Not at $100M, not at $500M, not at any acquired-earnings figure a sober person would underwrite. And notice which way this cuts: crediting the acquisitions strengthens the HOLD/WAIT conclusion rather than weakening it, because it demonstrates that the verdict does not depend on my having ignored them. A conclusion that survives its own strongest counter-argument is worth more than one that was never tested. This is also why the intrinsic-value range does not move: the deals are too small relative to the price to matter, and the cash that bought them was never in my number to begin with.

The reverse-DCF closer — what does $104.85 assume?

Now solve the problem backwards: at r = 10% and a 4% terminal rate, what ten-year owner-earnings growth rate must ServiceNow deliver to justify $104.85 today? The math solves to:

≈ 21% owner-earnings growth every year for a decade.

That is the bet embedded in the price. Is it impossible? No — ServiceNow grew revenue ~21% in 2025 and has a decade of ~28%. But 21% owner-earnings growth for ten more years, off a $13 billion revenue base, while keeping SBC as a real cost and while AI reshapes enterprise software — that is a demanding assumption with no room for disappointment built in. When the reverse-DCF hands you the company's best historical decade as the hurdle rather than the upside, Mr. Market has already paid himself for the growth. Principle: value is discounted owner earnings, but the inputs are uncertain and the discounting is hypersensitive — so triangulate, show the sensitivity, and let the reverse-DCF tell you exactly how heroic the market's assumptions have become.

Margin of safety & Mr. Market

Put the price next to the value:

  • Price $104.85 vs. base intrinsic value $68 → the stock trades at a ~54% premium to my base estimate ([$104.85 − $68] ÷ $68).
  • Even against my high estimate of $88, the price is a 19% premium — there is no margin of safety anywhere in my range.
  • To buy with a genuine ~30% margin of safety below my base $68, I would need a price near $48.

So where is the safety supposed to come from? In a high-return franchise, part of the margin of safety can live in the quality itself — a fair price plus a real moat can protect an owner better than a cheap price on a melting business. I accept that fully for ServiceNow. But quality justifies a fair price, never any price, and $104.85 is not a fair price for $1.68 of owner earnings — it is a price that already spends the next decade's growth and leaves the buyer with the downside if the AI question breaks the wrong way. The quality cushion is real; it is just already in the price and then some.

Is Mr. Market euphoric or depressed here? Interestingly, he is less euphoric than he was — the stock fell ~35% over the past year (an independent research service total return, 2026-05-01), from a split-adjusted $239 to the low $100s, on "increased fears from AI competition." So the fever has partly broken, and an independent research service itself now sees the stock trading below its 3–5-year target range and models 12–24% annual returns from here. I read it differently and more conservatively, because I refuse to add SBC back: even after a 35% fall, the price still embeds ~21% owner-earnings growth for a decade. Mr. Market has come down from euphoria to merely optimistic — not to fear, and nowhere near the pessimism that would hand a disciplined buyer a bargain. Principle: use Mr. Market, never obey him — a 35% decline is not automatically a buying opportunity if the starting point was extravagant; wait for him to offer the business at a price the owner earnings can defend.

The verdict & the lesson

Is this a wonderful, good, fair, or avoid-it business? Wonderful — full stop. A 98% renewal rate held for three straight years, a $5-million-plus customer cohort that grew 420 → 603 in two years, 77% gross margins, a decade of ~28% revenue growth, an operating model financed by its own customers' prepayments, and a widening platform. (Note what I did not put on that list: the 32% ROIC, which is a pre-spree figure, and the balance sheet, which is now an ordinary one. The quality case does not need either.) If I could buy this business at a fair price, I would want to own it for twenty years.

But return to the one question I framed at the open: at $104.85, is there any margin of safety if the extraordinary growth merely arrives on schedule rather than ahead of it? The answer is no. The price already assumes ~21% owner-earnings growth for a decade; my honest range values the business at $52–$88 with a base of $68; the stock trades ~54% above that base. This is the classic and instructive case of a wonderful business at the wrong price — not an "avoid the business," but an "avoid the price." The verdict is HOLD / WAIT: a great franchise to own, priced with no cushion; wait for Mr. Market to offer it below ~$68, ideally near $48–$52.

The 2–3 things a student must understand better before getting comfortable:
1. The AI question, both directions. Does AI expand ServiceNow's addressable work (more to automate, Now Assist toward $1 billion ACV) or erode the seat-based model that underpins pricing? This is the swing factor and it sits at the edge of my competence — resolve it and the growth assumption firms up in either direction.
2. The $9 billion acquisition spree (Armis + Veza), now closed and paid for in cash. Will these deals earn their cost of capital, or is this the institutional imperative converting a net-cash balance sheet into goodwill? Pro-forma ROIC of ~7–11% is a transitional trough, not a verdict — but the burden of proof now sits with management, and the sensitivity above shows that even $500M of acquired owner earnings adds only ~$4.81/share. Track the returns.
3. SBC and the true share count. If you ever find yourself tempted by the $4 billion "non-GAAP" earnings, re-read the ladder — the honest owner-earnings number is $1.75 billion, and the whole verdict flips on that discipline.

The universal lessons this case teaches most vividly:
- Great business ≠ good investment. Price is a separate decision from quality, and the surest way to lose money on a great company is to pay a price that already discounts a flawless decade.
- How you treat stock-based compensation determines your entire answer. Add it back and ServiceNow looks reasonably valued; keep it as the real cost it is and the price demands a flawless decade. The most consequential judgment in software valuation is a footnote most investors skip.
- A cushion you refuse to count is a cushion you never have to write off. I declined to add the $10 billion cash pile to intrinsic value because it was already committed. One quarter later it is spent, the balance sheet holds ~$1.4 billion of net debt, and my valuation needs no revision at all. Conservatism is not pessimism — it is building an estimate that survives contact with the facts.
- Check what a ratio's denominator is doing before you trust it. A 32% ROIC built by subtracting a cash pile becomes ~7–11% the moment the cash is spent, on identical profits. The moat did not change; the arithmetic did.
- The reverse-DCF is the antidote to a good story. When the price demands the company's best-ever decade just to break even, the story has already been paid for.

This is a teaching case, not personalized investment advice.

Glossary — terms & formulas

  • Owner earnings — the cash a passive owner could withdraw without harming the business = net income + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital. NOW: ≈ $1,748M + $738M D&A − $738M maint. capex ≈ $1.75B, or $1.68/share, with SBC deliberately kept as a cost.
  • Stock-based compensation (SBC) — employee pay issued as shares; a real cost that dilutes owners even though it uses no cash. NOW: $1,955M in 2025 — more than all of GAAP operating income; not added back here.
  • Free cash flow (FCF) — operating cash flow − capital expenditures. NOW: $5,444M − $868M = $4,576M.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex — the split between capex needed to hold position and capex to expand; a judgment call, never a disclosed fact. NOW: total capex $868M; I treat ≈ $738M (= D&A) as maintenance and the rest as growth (data-center build-out) — the conservative end; a less harsh read gives OE of ~$1.9–2.1B.
  • Return on invested capital (ROIC) = NOPAT ÷ invested capital. NOW: $1,410M ÷ $4,400M ≈ 32% pre-spree; pro forma for ~$9B of cash M&A, ≈ 7–11% on pre-deal NOPAT — a transitional trough, not a steady state.
  • NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate). NOW: $1,824M × (1 − 0.227) = $1,410M.
  • Invested capital = equity + total debt − excess cash. NOW: $12,964M + $1,491M − $10,055M = $4,400M pre-spree (nearly all goodwill/intangibles; tangible invested capital ~negative). Pro forma ≈ $13.1B–$19.3B depending on how much of the remaining ~$6.2B of cash you call excess. Note how much of the flattering 32% came from subtracting the cash.**
  • Return on equity (ROE) = net income ÷ shareholders' equity. NOW: $1,748M ÷ $12,964M = 13.5% at YE2025 (depressed by idle cash, not flattered by leverage); equity had fallen to $11,728M by 3/31/26 as buybacks outran earnings.
  • Interest coverage = operating income ÷ interest expense. NOW: $1,824M ÷ ~$22M ≈ 83× on the FY2025 structure — will fall materially now that ~$6.1B of new borrowings carry market coupons. Do not quote it forward.
  • Net cash / net debt = cash + securities − total debt. NOW: was $10,055M − $1,491M ≈ +$8.5B net cash at YE2025; pro forma after Armis/Veza ≈ $1.4B net debt (~$6.2B cash vs. ~$7.6B debt).
  • Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. NOW: $10,471M ÷ $10,443M ≈ 1.0 (but $8,314M of the liabilities is deferred revenue, not a cash bill).
  • Deferred revenue (float) — cash collected for services not yet delivered; a customer-funded, interest-free liability. NOW: $8,314M current.
  • Remaining performance obligations (RPO) — contracted revenue not yet recognized. NOW: $28.2B total, ~46% due within 12 months.
  • Renewal rate — 100% − attrition (by contract value). NOW: 98% in each of 2023–2025 — the moat's fingerprint.
  • Discount rate (r) — the hurdle/gravity on value; here normalized to 10%, no beta, floored per doctrine.
  • Intrinsic value = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ — the discounted stream of owner earnings. NOW: triangulated to $52–$88, base $68.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value = owner earnings ÷ r. NOW: $1.68 ÷ 0.10 = $16.80 — so ~84% of the $104.85 price is a growth bet.
  • Reverse DCF — solving for the growth rate the current price implies. NOW: $104.85 implies ~21% owner-earnings growth for a decade.
  • Two-stage DCF / terminal value / r − g spread — high growth for a stage, then perpetual growth g; terminal value = OE × (1+g) ÷ (r − g), which is hypersensitive to the r − g gap (see sensitivity grid).
  • Owner-earnings yield = owner earnings ÷ price. NOW: $1.68 ÷ $104.85 = 1.60%, vs. ~4.5% on the long bond.
  • Implied / justified P/E (owner-earnings multiple) = price ÷ owner earnings. NOW: 62.4× at $104.85; my base IV implies ~40×.
  • GAAP vs. non-GAAP earnings — GAAP follows accounting rules; non-GAAP adds back items management deems non-core. NOW: non-GAAP operating income $4,149M vs. GAAP $1,824M, the gap driven mainly by adding back SBC.
  • Economic goodwill — the value of a franchise above its tangible capital, invisible on the balance sheet (distinct from accounting goodwill, the price paid over fair value in acquisitions). NOW carries large economic goodwill; book value understates it.
  • Margin of safety = (intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value. NOW: negative at $104.85 (price is ~54% above base IV) — the case fails here.
  • Circle of competence — the set of businesses you can understand well enough to value; its edge matters more than its size.
  • One-dollar test — retain a dollar of earnings only if it creates at least a dollar of value (reinvest when incremental ROIC ≥ r). NOW passed clearly on organic reinvestment at ~32% pre-spree ROIC; on the ~$9B of cash M&A just deployed, the test is unresolved and must be re-run as the returns show up.
