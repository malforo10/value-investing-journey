Intuit Inc. (INTU) — A Value-Investing Case Study

Co-taught by Benjamin Graham & Warren Buffett · Seminar date: June 30, 2026

A teaching case, not investment advice. Every figure is drawn from Intuit's own filings and the an independent research service record; the arithmetic is shown so you can reproduce it. Our purpose is to teach a repeatable way of thinking, not to issue a verdict on the ticker.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Let me (Warren) start where an owner starts: what would I actually be buying?

Intuit is a financial-software company. It sells four things, and you already know most of the brands:

Segment (FY2025) What it sells Revenue % of total YoY growth Segment op. margin
Global Business Solutions (QuickBooks, Mailchimp, payroll, payments, Intuit Enterprise Suite) Accounting, payroll, marketing & money tools for small and mid-market businesses $11,077M 59% +16% 76%
Consumer (TurboTax) DIY and assisted tax preparation for U.S. & Canadian consumers $4,870M 26% +10% 78%
Credit Karma Free credit scores; a marketplace paid by lenders/insurers on a per-action basis $2,263M 12% +32% 37%
ProTax (Lacerte, ProSeries, ProConnect) Professional tax software for accountants $621M 3% +4% 86%
Total $18,831M 100% +16%

(Source: 10-K Item 8, segment footnote; Item 7 MD&A. Effective Aug 1, 2025 Intuit collapsed Consumer + Credit Karma + ProTax into one "Consumer" segment, so the current 10-Q reports only two segments — GBS and Consumer.)

How it makes money. Overwhelmingly by subscription and recurring services — 87% of revenue ($16.4B) is "service" revenue, and that share is rising. QuickBooks Online is a monthly subscription that a business, once it runs its books and payroll on it, essentially never leaves. TurboTax is a once-a-year purchase, highly seasonal — the April quarter (fiscal Q3) alone carried $4.36B of TurboTax's $5.14B nine-month revenue, roughly 85% (10-Q). Credit Karma is different in kind: it is a lead-generation marketplace paid by lenders and insurers per click or per funded loan — transactional, rate-sensitive, and less sticky.

The one question that will decide this case. Intuit is a wonderful business by almost every historical measure — but its stock has fallen from $404.83 on May 1 to $263.66 today, about -35%, and is down ~39% year-to-date. The market is not confused about the past; it is frightened about the future. The whole case reduces to one question:

Will generative AI erode Intuit's moat — commoditizing tax prep and bookkeeping and collapsing its pricing power — or will Intuit harness AI to deepen that moat? And at $263.66, how much decay is the market already charging you to assume?

Circle of competence (Manual ch. 4). Can we understand this business and forecast it a decade out? The business is squarely inside the circle — subscription software with clear customers, clear pricing, transparent segment economics. But be honest about the second half: forecasting its economics ten years out is genuinely harder in 2026 than it was in 2020, precisely because AI is a live threat to the core product. So this sits inside the circle to understand, but near the edge on forecastability. A thoughtful student is entitled to say "the ten-year forecast is now too hard, I pass" — and the manual reminds us that "too hard" is a complete and respectable answer. We will not pretend the AI question away; we will price it.

PRINCIPLE. A stock is a piece of a business, and you value only what you can understand. Knowing the boundary of your circle matters more than its size — and the boundary here runs right through the ten-year forecast.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Now let me (Ben) do what I always do: read the three statements as evidence, and check the earnings against the cash.

The income statement — a march from revenue to profit

(10-K Item 8, part 1; $ millions)

FY2023 FY2024 FY2025
Total net revenue 14,368 16,285 18,831
Cost of revenue 3,143 3,465 3,848
Implied gross profit 11,225 12,820 14,983
Gross margin 78.1% 78.7% 79.6%
Operating income 3,141 3,630 4,923
Operating margin 21.9% 22.3% 26.1%
Net income 2,384 2,963 3,869
Diluted EPS $8.42 $10.43 $13.67

Read down the layers. A ~80% gross margin tells you the marginal cost of serving one more QuickBooks subscriber is almost nothing — the signature of a software franchise. The gap between the 80% gross margin and the 26% operating margin is where the money goes: selling & marketing $5,035M (27% of revenue — this is a company that spends heavily to acquire customers) and R&D $2,928M (16%). Revenue compounded 16% a year and operating income grew even faster (+36% in FY2025) — operating leverage is real and improving.

One-time items to strip out: a restructuring charge of $15M in FY2025 (down from $223M in FY2024). Hold that thought — we will come back to whether "restructuring" is truly one-time.

The balance sheet — what it owns and owes

(10-K Item 8, part 1; July 31, 2025)

  • Liquidity: cash & investments $4.6B; current liabilities look large ($10.4B) but $7.1B of that is customer funds — money Intuit holds temporarily for payroll/payments customers, offset dollar-for-dollar by a matching $7.1B asset. Ignore it; it nets to zero.
  • Leverage: long-term debt $5,973M against $19,710M of equity and $4.9B of operating income — interest coverage is roughly 14–15×. An independent research service grades Financial Strength "A." This is a conservatively financed company; debt cannot force a sale. Good.
  • The judgment-laden items — read this carefully. Goodwill is $13,980M and acquired intangibles are $5,302M. Together, $19,282M — about 98% of the $19,710M of equity. In plain English: tangible book value is essentially zero. Nearly all of Intuit's accounting net worth is the price it paid for two acquisitions — Credit Karma (~$8.1B, 2020) and Mailchimp (~$12B, 2021).

This is the first place the manual's warning bites: never anchor value to book. For Intuit, book value is almost meaningless — it is a monument to two purchase prices, not a measure of the earning power of QuickBooks and TurboTax. A great franchise carries enormous economic goodwill that never appears on the balance sheet at all. Price-to-book is the wrong tool here; we will not use it.

The cash flow statement — the truth-teller

(10-K Item 8, part 1; $ millions)

FY2023 FY2024 FY2025
Net income 2,384 2,963 3,869
+ Depreciation 160 159 172
+ Amortization of acquired intangibles 646 630 637
+ Share-based compensation 1,712 1,940 1,968
± Working capital & other 144 (808) (439)
Operating cash flow 5,046 4,884 6,207
− Capex + capitalized software (260) (250) (124)
"Free cash flow" 4,786 4,634 6,083

Here is where the three statements articulate, and where the crucial lesson lives. Net income of $3,869M becomes operating cash of $6,207M — a $2.3B gap. Trace it: the biggest single reconciling item is stock-based compensation of $1,968M, added back because it is non-cash. Capital spending is trivial — just $124M, less than depreciation — because this business needs almost no physical capital to grow. So the headline "free cash flow" is a gorgeous $6.1B.

But cash is only the truth-teller if you interpret it honestly. That $1,968M of stock-based compensation is not free money. It is real compensation paid to employees in shares — a genuine economic cost that dilutes you, the owner. The accountants add it back because no cash left the building; but Intuit then spent $2,772M of actual cash buying back stock largely to mop up that dilution. Add SBC back to get to cash flow, and you must remember that the company is quietly spending nearly all of it, in cash, to stand still on the share count. A dollar of stock-based comp is a dollar of cost, whether or not it flows through the cash-flow statement. We will refuse to treat it as free — that refusal is the difference between owner earnings and salesmanship.

Earnings-quality scan (Manual ch. 7) — can we trust these numbers?

  • Non-GAAP vs. GAAP — the flag to watch. Intuit (and an independent research service, following it) headlines an EPS of $20.15 for FY2025. GAAP diluted EPS was $13.67. That is a 47% markup, achieved mainly by excluding stock-based compensation and acquired-intangible amortization. Amortization of purchased intangibles is a fair thing to exclude (it is non-cash and non-economic — see below). Stock-based compensation is not — and it is by far the larger add-back. Whenever a company leans this hard on a number that is half-again its GAAP earnings, the manual says slow down. We will build our own figure between the two.
  • "Restructuring" that recurs. $223M (FY2024) → $15M (FY2025) → and the 10-Q discloses a new "2026 Plan," $300–340M, mostly in Q4 FY2026. Two reorganizations in two fiscal years. The manual names "frequent 'one-time' charges that are in truth recurring" as a classic yellow flag. Not alarming here — Intuit is genuinely reshaping around AI — but a student should mentally treat some restructuring as an ordinary cost of this business, not a free pass.
  • What is clean. No goodwill impairments; deferred revenue is straightforward subscription timing; the effective tax rate (~20%) is transparent; disclosure is detailed and not evasive. This is an honestly-kept set of books — the only real judgment call is how you treat SBC, and we have made ours.

PRINCIPLE. The statements are evidence, and cash is the truth-teller — but only if you refuse to let a non-cash add-back like stock comp masquerade as free cash. Owner earnings, not the promoted non-GAAP number, is what you value.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Is earning power growing? (the long record)

Operating cash flow is the cleanest continuous signal we have, and it is a thing of beauty:

Fiscal year FY16 FY19 FY21 FY23 FY25
Revenue ~5.2B 6,784 9,633 14,368 18,831
Operating cash flow 1,460 2,324 3,250 5,046 6,207

Operating cash flow quadrupled in nine years — a 17.4% CAGR — on almost no incremental physical capital. Revenue compounded ~15–18%. An independent research service's per-share array tells the same story: earnings per share rose from $2.11 (2010) to $20.15 (2025). This is, on the historical evidence, a capital-light compounding machine.

Owner earnings (Manual ch. 7)

Let me build the number the manual actually asks for — the cash an owner could withdraw without weakening the business.

Owner earnings (FY2025):
  GAAP net income                                   3,869
  + Amortization of acquired intangibles × (1−0.20)  +510   (non-cash, non-economic; tax-effected)
  + Depreciation 172 − maintenance capex ~172          ~0   (asset-light; capex ≈ depreciation)
  − Stock-based compensation add-back                   0   (already expensed; a REAL cost, kept)
  ≈ Owner earnings                              ≈  $4,400M   (~$16.1 per share on 274M shares)

Why add back the intangible amortization? Because the $637M is the accounting unwind of the purchase prices of Credit Karma and Mailchimp — Intuit is not spending that cash again, and it already funds ongoing product work through the $2.9B R&D line. Why not add back SBC? Because it is genuine compensation that dilutes owners; GAAP already (correctly) charged it, and we leave it charged.

Look at the ladder of "earnings," lowest to highest — the whole point of the exercise:

Measure FY2025 Per share What it is
GAAP net income $3,869M $13.67 The accountant's number
Owner earnings (ours) ~$4,400M ~$16.1 What an owner can withdraw
Non-GAAP / an independent research service $5,702M $20.15 GAAP + SBC + amortization added back
"Free cash flow" (OCF−capex) $6,083M $22.20 Treats $2.0B SBC as free

Our owner-earnings figure sits deliberately below the promoted $20.15 and the $22 "FCF," and modestly above GAAP. That is the honest middle, and it is what we will value.

Return on invested capital (Manual ch. 6) — the central quality test

NOPAT = (Operating income 4,923 + intangible amort 637) × (1 − 0.20 tax) = $4,448M

ROIC (gross, incl. all goodwill) = 4,448 / (debt 5,973 + equity 19,710)         = 17.3%
ROIC (net of excess cash)        = 4,448 / (25,683 − 4,646)                       = 21.1%
ROIC on TANGIBLE capital         = 4,448 / (25,683 − 19,282 goodwill/intangibles) = 69.5%
ROE (GAAP)                       = 3,869 / 19,710                                 = 19.6%

Every one of these clears a ~9% cost of capital with room to spare — the mark of a real moat. But the spread between them is the lesson. On tangible capital the underlying business earns an astonishing ~70% — QuickBooks and TurboTax need almost no capital to run. Yet the reported ROIC is only ~17–21%, because $19.3B of capital is tied up in the prices Intuit paid for Credit Karma and Mailchimp. So:

The operating franchise is extraordinary; the capital allocation via two mega-acquisitions diluted the return on capital from ~70% toward ~20%. Both facts are true at once, and a good analyst holds them together.

What actually protects the earnings?

  • Switching costs (the strongest moat). Once a small business runs its accounting, payroll, and payments on QuickBooks, moving off it means re-entering years of financial data and retraining — most never do. QuickBooks Online revenue grew +20% with average revenue per customer up 14% (10-K MD&A): the textbook signature of pricing power, which the manual calls the single best diagnostic of a moat.
  • Brand + habit. TurboTax and QuickBooks are default choices; the brands are worth more than anything on the balance sheet.
  • Data + scale + distribution. ~100 million customers, and a $2.1B/year advertising budget that a new entrant cannot match.

Be skeptical — what could erode it in ten years?

This is where honesty earns its keep, because the market's fear is not baseless:
- TurboTax federal units are already declining (10-Q) — growth is coming from price and assisted ("Full Service") mix, not more filers. The DIY tax franchise is showing the first cracks the bears predicted: if AI makes filing trivially easy and free, the $5B DIY tax pool shrinks.
- AI lowers the cost of building software. an independent research service cut its 3-to-5-year P/E from 32× to 28× and wrote plainly: "AI coding may encroach on some of Intuit's software functions" and "the defensive valuation premium that INTU garnered for decades will likely take years to reflate."
- Credit Karma (12%) is transactional and cyclical — a rate-sensitive lead-gen business, not a moated one.

The bull's rejoinder, also on the evidence: Intuit is layering AI in, not being disrupted from outside — GenOS, AI agents in Intuit Enterprise Suite, and AI-enabled human experts; QuickBooks and payments are still compounding at ~20%; and switching costs on business accounting are exactly the kind of moat AI struggles to dissolve.

PRINCIPLE. Durable earning power, defended by a moat and proven by returns on capital far above the cost of capital, is what you are buying. But a moat must be argued, not assumed — and here the argument is genuinely contested at the DIY-tax edge, which is precisely why the price fell.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Over time you ride with how management allocates each dollar of free cash. Intuit's record across the five uses (10-K/10-Q):

  • Reinvest in the business: the best use, because incremental ROIC on the core is enormous. Passes the one-dollar test easily — a dollar retained and plowed into QuickBooks/payments/AI has been producing far more than a dollar of value. Retention is justified.
  • Dividends: grown every single year, from $3.12 (FY23) to $4.16 (FY25) to a $4.80 forward run-rate — a ~16% ten-year growth rate. Disciplined and shareholder-friendly.
  • Buybacks: $2.8B in FY2025, $3.4B in the first nine months of FY2026, and a fresh $8B authorization added in May 2026. Here the manual makes us grade on price, not volume. Much of the 2021–2024 buying happened at $400–$700 — at or above intrinsic value, which destroys value. Buying today near $264 is a far better use of the same dollar. So the buyback program is not automatically virtuous; its wisdom depends entirely on the price paid, and the recent price makes it more attractive, not less.
  • Acquisitions: the debatable chapter. ~$20B on Credit Karma and Mailchimp, funded substantially in stock (share count rose ~8% over the decade to a FY2022 peak before recent buybacks). Credit Karma now grows 32% and looks like a hit; Mailchimp's contribution is harder to see. This is exactly the "institutional imperative" the manual warns about — the pull to expand via big deals. The verdict is mixed: superb organic reinvestment and dividend discipline, set against two large, richly-priced, stock-funded acquisitions whose returns are still being proven.
  • Debt: conservative, well-laddered, "A" financial strength. No risk of a forced sale.

One candor note: the heavy promotion of a $20.15 non-GAAP EPS over the $13.67 GAAP figure is the kind of thing partners should be a little more even-handed about.

PRINCIPLE. Judge management by the one-dollar test and by the price it pays for its own stock — not by the size of the buyback or the boldness of the deal. Intuit passes on organic reinvestment and dividends, and earns an "improving" grade on buybacks now that the price has fallen.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Value is, by definition, the discounted stream of future owner earnings (Manual master equation). But because Intuit's ten-year owner earnings are exactly what AI has thrown into doubt, a literal DCF here is treacherously sensitive — so we lead with the steadier tool the manual recommends for a mature, profitable franchise (a normalized-earnings multiple) and use the DCF to bound the range and reveal the sensitivity, not to manufacture a false point.

The discount rate (Manual ch. 11 — "gravity"). The 10-year Treasury is 4.47% and the 30-year is 4.97% (Selection & Opinion, Selected Yields, 6/15/26). To that risk-free anchor we add an equity premium, and — because the durability of these cash flows is now genuinely uncertain — a little extra for that uncertainty. We use r = 9% as our base, and sensitize 8%–10%.

Method 1 — Normalized owner-earnings multiple (our primary anchor)

Owner earnings ≈ $16.1/share. What multiple does a franchise with 20%+ ROIC, ~80% gross margins, and (haircut) high-single-digit growth deserve? An independent research service's median historical P/E for INTU is 33× — but that was on the non-GAAP number and in an era before the AI question. We deliberately use a lower, more defensible multiple on our more conservative earnings figure:

Multiple on owner earnings ($16.1) 18× 22× 26×
Value per share $290 $354 $419

Cross-check on an independent research service's forward FY2026 non-GAAP EPS of $23.10, applying multiples well below the 33× median to reflect AI risk:

Multiple on FY26 non-GAAP ($23.10) 14× 17× 20×
Value per share $323 $393 $462

Method 2 — Two-stage DCF (to bound the range and show the sensitivity)

Starting owner earnings $4,500M; 9% growth for 10 years (well below the ~17% history and below an independent research service's ~13% EPS projection — a deliberate AI haircut); then a terminal rate. Per share (274M shares):

r ＼ terminal g 2.5% 3.0% 4.0%
8% $508 $544 $641
9% $423 $446 $506
10% $361 $377 $416

And the effect of the growth assumption alone (r = 9%, g = 3%): 6% → $355 · 9% → $446 · 12% → $561.

Notice what the manual promised: reasonable inputs, individually defensible, swing the answer from ~$360 to ~$640. The DCF does not give you a price; it gives you a warning about false precision. Its central cluster (~$400–450) sits above every multiple-based figure — which tells us the DCF is the more optimistic tool here and should be weighted less, exactly because its terminal value (two-thirds of the total) rests on the ten-year durability that is in question.

Triangulation → an honest range

Weighting the normalized-earnings multiple most heavily (the manual's prescription for a mature franchise), cross-checked by the forward multiple and bounded by the DCF:

Intrinsic value range ≈ $330 (conservative) — $400 (central) — $470 (optimistic) per share.

The reasonable disagreement between the methods is the range, and its width honestly reflects the AI uncertainty.

Growth check (Manual ch. 10). Is the growth worth anything? ROIC (17–21%) is far above r (9%), so Intuit's growth creates value rather than consuming it — the good kind. And because the business is capital-light, it grows while returning most of its cash. That is why a franchise like this deserves a real multiple.

A sanity check against an independent research service: VL's 18-month target midpoint is $506 and its 2029–31 range is $800–$1,200 — far above our numbers. VL is explicitly using the pre-AI 28× multiple on rising non-GAAP EPS. We think that is too generous given the very risk that just repriced the stock; we would rather be conservative and let the margin of safety do the work.

PRINCIPLE. Value is discounted owner earnings by definition — but because the inputs are uncertain and the discounting is hypersensitive, estimate with the method the business makes most reliable (here, a conservative earnings multiple), triangulate, and trust an honest range over a false point.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Now put the price next to the value.

Live price (equity_atlas.xlsx, 6/30/2026):   $263.66
Intrinsic value range:                        $330 – $470  (central ~$400)

Discount to central value:  (400 − 263.66) / 400  =  34%
Discount to conservative low: (330 − 263.66) / 330 =  20%

At $263.66 you are paying roughly two-thirds of our central estimate, and you sit below even the conservative end of the range. That is a meaningful margin of safety — for a business with an "A" balance sheet, ~80% gross margins, 20%+ returns on capital, and a two-decade compounding record.

But let Mr. Market speak, and listen to what he's actually saying. Run the DCF in reverse: at r = 9% and 3% terminal growth, today's $263.66 is consistent with only about 2% perpetual owner-earnings growth. Even raising the discount rate to 10% (an extra AI-risk premium), the price implies roughly 5% growth. Against a 17% historical CAGR and an independent research service's ~13% projection, Mr. Market is pricing Intuit as if its franchise will barely outgrow inflation, forever. He is not offering this discount out of generosity — he is genuinely fearful that AI permanently decays the moat.

That is the whole investment decision, stated cleanly: the price is attractive if you believe the moat survives with even modest growth; the price is roughly fair only if you believe the DIY-tax and bookkeeping franchises are structurally impaired. Mr. Market has taken the pessimistic side. Your job is not to obey him or defy him, but to decide — from the business evidence — whether ~2% forever is too gloomy. The 20% QuickBooks growth and 14% pricing power in the most recent filings suggest it may well be. The declining TurboTax federal units suggest the fear is not pure fantasy.

Where would the margin of safety become ample rather than merely meaningful? Against a conservative $330 value, a 30% cushion implies roughly $230 or below — a price at which even a fairly pessimistic AI scenario would be well protected.

PRINCIPLE. Insist on a cushion, and use Mr. Market rather than obey him. A falling price on an intact franchise is opportunity; a falling price on a decaying one is a warning. Reverse-engineering the price into an implied growth rate tells you which fear you are being asked to buy.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

What kind of business is this? On its economics — capital-light, 80% gross margins, ~70% returns on tangible capital, deep switching costs, two decades of 15%+ compounding — Intuit is a wonderful business, full stop. The only thing standing between "wonderful business, obvious buy" and "genuinely hard call" is the durability question that the AI era has forced open.

The one question, answered. Will AI erode the moat, and how much decay is priced in? Our read of the evidence: the core (QuickBooks, payments, professional tax) shows a moat that is still widening — 20% growth, 14% pricing power, switching costs AI does not easily dissolve. The DIY-tax edge (TurboTax units, Credit Karma) is where the fear is legitimate. At $263.66 the market is pricing in ~2% perpetual growth — a scenario closer to broad impairment than to the "widening core, softening edge" the filings actually show. On that reading, the pessimism looks overdone, and a meaningful (though not yet ample) margin of safety exists — which is why this is a good-to-wonderful business at a fair-to-attractive price, not an "avoid." But we hold that conclusion humbly, because the forecast sits near the edge of the circle.

Two or three things a student must understand better before getting truly comfortable (the open questions):
1. The AI trajectory of DIY tax. Are declining TurboTax federal units a temporary mix-shift toward "Full Service," or the leading edge of structural decay? This single fork governs the terminal value.
2. Whether Intuit's own AI (GenOS, agents) deepens or merely defends the moat — does it raise pricing power and attach rate, or just prevent share loss?
3. Capital-allocation discipline going forward — will the $8B buyback authorization be spent at these lower prices (value-creating) or chase the stock back up, and will management resist another mega-acquisition?

The universal lessons this case teaches most vividly:
- Owner earnings, not the promoted number. The distance from $13.67 (GAAP) to $16 (owner earnings) to $20.15 (non-GAAP) to $22 ("FCF") is the whole game. Stock-based compensation is a real cost; refusing to let it hide is what separates an owner from a promoter.
- A great operating business and mediocre capital allocation can live in one company. ~70% ROIC on the core, dragged to ~20% by $20B of acquisitions — hold both truths at once.
- Price is the thing that changed, not the business — so read the price as a question. A 35% drop turned a perennially-expensive compounder into one trading at ~11× forward earnings and an implied ~2% growth. Reverse the DCF, find the fear the market is charging you to assume, and decide whether it is justified. That is Mr. Market as servant.

This is a teaching case built from Intuit's filings and an independent research service as of June 30, 2026. It is designed to demonstrate a method of reasoning, not to recommend that anyone buy, hold, or sell Intuit stock. Do your own work, inside your own circle.
