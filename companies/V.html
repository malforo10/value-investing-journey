Visa Inc. (NYSE: V) — A Value Investing Seminar

Taught from the filings, the an independent research service record, and the Berkshire method manual

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Strip away the technology jargon and Visa is a toll bridge. When you tap a card at a coffee shop, Visa does not lend you money, does not issue your card, and does not decide your credit limit — it just clears the message between your bank (the "issuer") and the merchant's bank (the "acquirer") and takes a small toll for making the message move safely and instantly. The company itself says so in plain language: "Visa is not a financial institution. We do not issue cards, extend credit or set rates and fees for account holders of Visa products nor do we earn revenue from or bear credit risk with respect to any of these activities" (10-K, Item 1, Business). That single sentence is the whole business model. Visa owns the rails (VisaNet); the banks own the customers and the credit risk.

Visa earns net revenue from four buckets — service revenue (for standing ready to process), data processing revenue (for the actual authorization/clearing/settlement), international transaction revenue (cross-border and currency conversion), and a growing "other" revenue from value-added services (fraud/risk tools, advisory, issuing/acceptance solutions) — all reduced by client incentives, the payments Visa makes to banks and merchants to win and keep their business (10-K, Item 1 and Item 7, MD&A). In fiscal 2025 (year ended September 30, 2025) those four buckets summed to $40.0 billion of net revenue, up 11% from fiscal 2024's $35.9 billion (10-K, Item 7, MD&A financial overview table). The company processed 258 billion transactions on its own network and touched 329 billion payment and cash transactions in total across nearly 14,500 financial-institution clients, almost 5 billion payment credentials, and more than 175 million merchant locations in over 200 countries (10-K, Item 1, Business, "Fiscal 2025 Key Statistics" and overview).

International business is now the larger half: $24.4 billion of net revenue came from outside the U.S. in fiscal 2025 versus $15.6 billion domestically, and international revenue grew 15% year over year against 6% domestically (10-K, Item 7, MD&A, "Net Revenue" table) — a business still digitizing consumer spending across the globe, not a mature domestic story.

The one question that will decide this case: Visa's economics — a network that gets stronger every time a new cardholder or a new merchant joins it — are about as good as economics get in corporate America. The question is not "is this a good business?" (it plainly is) but "at today's price, is the market still paying a fair toll, or is it paying for a decade of flawless execution that has already been priced in?"

The circle-of-competence gate. Can an ordinary investor genuinely forecast Visa's economics ten years out? I believe the core engine is understandable: global electronic-payment volume grows a bit faster than global GDP as cash and checks keep disappearing, Visa takes a small, fairly stable cut of that volume, and the moat (discussed below) is structural rather than fashionable. What is not fully forecastable in fine detail is the pace and shape of interchange regulation around the world, and the ultimate size of the U.S. interchange litigation liability — both are legal and political processes, not economic ones, and I do not pretend to call those with precision. That is a real limitation, but it is a limitation on the size of a few cost lines, not on whether the core toll-bridge model survives. On balance, this sits inside my circle: I can forecast the trajectory of volumes and margins with reasonable confidence, while being honest that the litigation and regulatory tail is a genuine, irreducible uncertainty I will price into the discount rate and the margin of safety rather than pretend to know precisely.

Principle: A stock is a claim on a real business, and the first job is to describe in one paragraph, without jargon, how that business turns a customer's action into a dollar of profit. If you cannot do that, you are not ready to value it — and if the honest answer is "I can forecast the core engine but not a specific liability," say so explicitly rather than average it away.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The balance sheet: what Visa owns and owes

As of September 30, 2025 (10-K, Item 8, Consolidated Balance Sheets), Visa's balance sheet is unusual for an industrial-minded investor because it is dominated by settlement mechanics rather than plant and inventory. Total assets were $99,627 million. On the asset side, cash and cash equivalents were $17,164 million, plus a separate $2,990 million of restricted cash held in a U.S. litigation escrow account — money set aside specifically to fund settlements, not available for general corporate use. Goodwill was $19,879 million and other intangible assets were $27,646 million — together $47.5 billion, or nearly half of total assets, almost entirely the residue of acquisitions (Visa Europe, Plaid-adjacent fintech deals, CyberSource, Tink, and smaller bolt-ons like Featurespace). That is worth pausing on: the accounting goodwill on Visa's books is large, but the manual's lesson from Chapter 7 cuts the other way here — economic goodwill (the value of the network moat) is worth far more than the accounting goodwill on the balance sheet. Book value understates this business badly; I will not anchor its value to book.

On the liability side, total liabilities were $61,718 million. Total debt was $25,171 million — $19,602 million of long-term debt (10-K, Item 8, Balance Sheet) plus $5,569 million of current maturities coming due within fiscal 2026. A large share of both current assets and current liabilities is settlement plumbing that offsets itself: settlement receivable/payable ($4,191M / $4,568M) and customer collateral ($3,625M held, $3,625M owed) are pass-through balances tied to daily transaction clearing, not true obligations of the business. Total equity was $37,909 million.

Current ratio = Current assets ÷ Current liabilities = $37,766M ÷ $35,048M = 1.08× (10-K, Item 8, Balance Sheet). By Graham's classic industrial yardstick of 2:1 that looks thin, but it is the wrong yardstick here: once you net out the offsetting settlement and customer-collateral balances, Visa's true near-term obligations are a fraction of the reported current liabilities, and cash alone ($17.2 billion) covers the current portion of long-term debt more than three times over ($17,164M ÷ $5,569M = 3.1×).

The income statement: the march from revenue to profit

Fiscal Year (ended Sept 30) Net Revenue Operating Income Operating Margin Net Income Diluted EPS
2023 $32,653M $21,000M 64.3% $17,273M $8.28
2024 $35,926M $23,595M 65.7% $19,743M $9.73
2025 $40,000M $23,994M 60.0% $20,058M $10.20

(10-K, Item 8, Consolidated Statements of Operations; 10-K Item 7, MD&A financial overview.)

Operating margin arithmetic for FY2025: $23,994M ÷ $40,000M = 60.0%. That margin actually fell from 65.7% in FY2024, and the reason is visible in one line: total operating expenses rose 30% year over year — from $12,331M to $16,006M — "primarily driven by higher litigation provision and personnel expenses" (10-K, Item 7, MD&A). The litigation provision alone jumped from $462M (FY2024) to $2,562M (FY2025), a $2.1 billion swing that by itself explains most of the margin compression. That is a real cost that hit real operating income — not a rounding error, and not something I will wave away.

Visa also reports a non-GAAP net income and EPS that strip out gains/losses on equity investments, amortization of acquired intangibles, acquisition costs, severance, lease consolidation costs, and — the big one — the entire litigation provision. On that basis, FY2025 non-GAAP net income was $22,542M and non-GAAP diluted EPS was $11.47, versus GAAP's $20,058M and $10.20 (10-K, Item 7, MD&A, non-GAAP reconciliation table). The gap between GAAP and non-GAAP EPS is $1.27/share, of which $1.00/share — the majority — is the litigation add-back alone.

This is the single most important earnings-quality judgment in the case. Visa's own non-GAAP framing treats litigation as if it were a one-time item that will not recur. It has appeared, in some size, in every one of the last several fiscal years: $927M (FY2023, pretax on the income statement), $462M (FY2024), $2,562M (FY2025) (10-K, Item 8, Statements of Operations, "Litigation provision" line; 10-K Item 7 non-GAAP reconciliation tables for the after-tax figures of $705M, $337M, and $1,964M respectively). A cost that shows up three years running, even if the size is lumpy, is not a one-time item — it is a recurring cost of running the world's dominant card network, a cost that will keep recurring as long as merchants keep suing over interchange. The manual's earnings-quality chapter warns exactly about this: "Frequent 'restructuring' or 'one-time' charges that are in truth recurring costs of doing business" is a listed red flag (manual, Ch. 7). I am not accusing Visa of bad faith — the disclosure is full and clear — but I will not adopt their non-GAAP number uncritically. Below, in Earnings Power, I normalize this cost to a multi-year average rather than deleting it or accepting the worst single year.

One more genuinely distinctive wrinkle, worth flagging because it is exactly the kind of footnote detail the manual tells us to hunt for: under Visa's "U.S. retrospective responsibility plan," the monetary cost of the U.S. covered litigation is recovered not from Class A (public) shareholders' per-share earnings directly, but through a downward adjustment to the rate at which the banks' Class B and B-2 shares eventually convert into Class A shares (10-K, Item 7, MD&A; Note 5, U.S. and Europe Retrospective Responsibility Plans). In fiscal 2025 this mechanism meant Class A diluted EPS was essentially unchanged by the conversion-rate adjustment even as consolidated net income absorbed the $2.56 billion charge. In plain English: the banks that created Visa, not the public shareholders who bought it at IPO, bear much of the litigation bill, through a smaller eventual share count rather than a smaller check. That is a real structural protection for a Class A owner, and it is a good example of a footnote that meaningfully changes how conservative you need to be about a headline number.

The cash flow statement: does the cash agree with the profit?

FY2025 operating cash flow was $23,059 million against GAAP net income of $20,058 million (10-K, Item 8, Statements of Cash Flows) — cash flow ran ahead of net income, the reassuring direction. The reconciliation shows why: net income is adjusted upward for client-incentive amortization ($15,751M), share-based compensation ($897M), and depreciation & amortization ($1,220M), then reduced by the actual cash paid out for client incentives that period (a $15,314M working-capital outflow) and other smaller working-capital swings. Netting those out, the client-incentive mechanics essentially wash through cash flow close to dollar-for-dollar with their effect on revenue — which tells you the accrual accounting for client incentives is not distorting the picture; the cash follows the accounting reasonably closely.

Capital expenditures — "purchases of property, equipment and technology" — were $1,482 million in FY2025, just 3.7% of net revenue ($1,482M ÷ $40,000M). Free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capex) was therefore $23,059M − $1,482M = $21,577 million for FY2025, versus $18,693M in FY2024 ($19,950M − $1,257M) and $19,696M in FY2023 ($20,755M − $1,059M). Financing activities show where that cash actually went: $18,316 million of share repurchases and $4,634 million of dividends in FY2025 (10-K, Item 8, Statements of Cash Flows) — together $22,950 million returned to owners against $20,058 million of net income, a payout of more than 100% of earnings, funded by the gap between cash generation and reported profit plus some new debt issuance.

Principle: the three statements must articulate with each other — net income flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet and into the top of the cash flow statement, and the change in cash on the balance sheet must tie to the sum of operating, investing, and financing cash flows. When they do, and when the gaps between accrual profit and cash are explained by identifiable, sensible items (as here), you can trust the numbers enough to build a valuation on them — provided you make your own judgment about which "non-recurring" items are truly non-recurring, rather than accepting management's non-GAAP framing wholesale.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The moat: why this toll bridge is defensible

Visa's moat rests on the oldest and most durable structure in payments — the four-party network effect. Every bank that issues Visa cards makes Visa more useful to every merchant that accepts them, and every merchant that accepts Visa makes a Visa card more useful to every consumer who carries one. That flywheel, built over nearly seven decades, is why Visa's global network (VisaNet) now touches roughly 12 billion cards, bank accounts, and digital wallets and 175+ million merchant locations (10-K, Item 1, Business). A new entrant cannot simply build a better algorithm; it has to convince banks, merchants, and consumers to switch simultaneously, which almost never happens at scale.

Layered on top of the network effect: a globally trusted brand; deep, sticky technical integration into thousands of banks' core systems (switching costs for an issuer are enormous — you do not re-platform your national card business lightly); and, somewhat unusually, a degree of regulatory protection — Visa has been designated a systemically important or "prominent" payment system in multiple jurisdictions (10-K, Item 1A, Risk Factors), which functions as a barrier to new entrants even as it invites more regulatory scrutiny of pricing.

Applying the manual's diagnostic test directly (Ch. 5): does Visa have pricing power? The honest answer is nuanced. Visa does not set the interchange reimbursement fees that flow between banks (those are largely between issuers and acquirers, and increasingly capped by regulators); what Visa does control is its own network access, data-processing, and value-added-service fees, and it has raised those steadily while growing volume — net revenue per dollar of payments volume has held up even as regulators squeeze interchange. Value-added services revenue (fraud tools, advisory, issuing/acceptance solutions) grew 24% in FY2025 to $10.9 billion (10-K, Item 7, MD&A) — a genuinely different, faster-growing, higher-margin business layered on top of the toll bridge, sold because banks and merchants want it, not because they are compelled to buy it. That is real pricing power, just exercised on a different fee line than the one most investors watch.

What could erode this moat in ten years? Three real threats, all disclosed at length in Item 1A: (1) interchange and network-fee regulation keeps expanding — the EU caps consumer interchange outright, the U.S. Federal Reserve's Regulation II is under active litigation that could push debit caps lower, and a UK regulator is reviewing Visa's own network fees; (2) domestic and government-sponsored rails — UnionPay in China, RuPay in India, Pix in Brazil, FedNow and real-time-payment schemes in the U.S. and Europe — are explicitly being built to reduce reliance on international card networks, sometimes with direct government backing; (3) new payment rails (account-to-account, stablecoins, agentic AI-driven commerce) could someday let money move without touching a card network at all, though Visa is trying to make itself the connective tissue for those rails too (Visa Pay, Visa Direct, Visa+, its stablecoin and tokenization efforts). None of these looks like an existential threat over the next five years; all of them are legitimate long-term risks to the size of the toll, which is exactly why I will not assume the historical growth rate continues forever in the terminal value below.

Principle: a moat built on a two-sided network effect, reinforced by switching costs and a trusted brand, is one of the most durable structures in business — but "durable" does not mean "static." The threats to Visa's moat are not competitors doing the same thing better; they are governments and central banks trying to build around the toll bridge entirely. That is a different, slower-moving, but real kind of competitive risk, and the manual's instruction to argue durability rather than assume it applies with full force here.

Return on invested capital: how good are the economics, really?

NOPAT = Operating income × (1 − effective tax rate) = $23,994M × (1 − 0.171) = $23,994M × 0.829 = $19,893 million (10-K, Item 8, Statement of Operations for operating income; 10-K Item 7 for the 17.1% effective tax rate).

Invested capital = Total debt + Total equity − Cash = $25,171M + $37,909M − $17,164M = $45,916 million (10-K, Item 8, Balance Sheet).

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested capital = $19,893M ÷ $45,916M = 43.3%.

For comparison, Return on equity = Net income ÷ Shareholders' equity = $20,058M ÷ $37,909M = 52.9% (my calculation from the FY2025 year-end balance sheet), close to an independent research service's reported 59.5% for fiscal 2025, which likely uses average rather than year-end equity (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, per-share array, "Return on Shr. Equity").

Either measure tells the same story: Visa earns a return on capital several multiples of any reasonable cost of capital. This is not a business that needs heavy reinvestment to grow — it is the manual's "capital-light franchise" (Ch. 6) in its purest form, the See's Candy case study scaled to a global network. Note, too, that a meaningful share of that $45.9 billion of invested capital is acquired goodwill and intangibles from Visa Europe and various fintech deals, not organically-built capital — and even loading that acquisition cost fully into the denominator, ROIC still clears 40%. That is about as clean a "yes" as this test ever gives.

Principle: the single best measure of business quality is how much profit a dollar of invested capital produces, because a business earning 43% on capital can fund enormous growth from its own cash while returning the rest to owners — precisely the dynamic that makes a modest reinvestment rate compatible with double-digit earnings growth, as we will see next.

Owner earnings: normalizing FY2025

Start from GAAP net income and make three explicit adjustments, using the single owner-earnings figure that will be used consistently everywhere below.

Step 1 — normalize the litigation charge, rather than deleting it. The after-tax litigation charges Visa itself disclosed in its non-GAAP reconciliations were $705M (FY2023), $337M (FY2024), and $1,964M (FY2025) (10-K, Item 7, MD&A, non-GAAP reconciliation tables). Three-year average = ($705M + $337M + $1,964M) ÷ 3 = $3,006M ÷ 3 = $1,002 million — call this the "normal" annual cost of Visa's litigation exposure. FY2025's actual charge of $1,964M was $1,964M − $1,002M = $962 million above that normal run rate. I add back only that excess, not the whole charge (unlike Visa's non-GAAP measure, which adds back the entire $1,964M as if the cost were zero in a normal year):

Normalized net income = $20,058M (GAAP) + $962M (excess-litigation add-back) = $21,020 million

Step 2 — add back depreciation & amortization (non-cash) and subtract capital expenditure (real cash outlay). Visa does not separately disclose a maintenance-versus-growth split of its capex; given how small capex is relative to the business (3.7% of revenue) and that nearly all of it goes to sustaining and upgrading the existing processing network rather than building new physical capacity, I treat the full amount as maintenance capex — a conservative choice, since if any portion is truly growth capex, this slightly understates owner earnings.

Owner earnings (aggregate) = $21,020M + $1,220M (D&A) − $1,482M (capex) = $20,758 million

Step 3 — convert to a per-share figure using the diluted, as-converted share count Visa itself uses for EPS (1,966 million weighted-average diluted shares, matching the GAAP/non-GAAP EPS calculations in 10-K Note 16, Earnings Per Share):

Owner earnings per share = $20,758M ÷ 1,966M shares = $10.56/share, which I round to $10.60/share for clean arithmetic in the valuation section below. This is the single figure I will use in every cross-check that follows.

As a sanity check, FY2025 free cash flow per share was $21,577M ÷ 1,966M = $10.97/share — modestly higher than my $10.60 normalized figure, which gives me confidence that $10.60 is a conservative, not an aggressive, estimate of what a passive owner could withdraw from this business in a normal year.

For context on the trend, GAAP diluted EPS has compounded from $8.28 (FY2023) to $9.73 (FY2024) to $10.20 (FY2025) — roughly 11%/year — while non-GAAP EPS (Visa's own, litigation-excluded measure) has compounded from $8.77 to $10.05 to $11.47, roughly 14%/year (10-K, Item 7, MD&A). An independent research service projects non-GAAP EPS of $18.55 by the 2029-31 period (an independent research service, 2026-05-01), implying roughly 10%/year growth in Visa's own preferred earnings measure over the next five years.

Principle: normalize before you capitalize. The number that enters a valuation should represent a normal year's earning power, neither an unusually bad year (which understates the business) nor a management-selected non-GAAP figure that deletes a real, recurring cost entirely (which overstates it). Splitting the difference honestly — keeping a "normal" amount of the recurring cost, excess only above that — is itself the discipline the manual calls normalization.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

  • Leverage. Total debt = long-term debt $19,602M + current maturities $5,569M = $25,171 million; cash and equivalents = $17,164 million (10-K, Item 8, Balance Sheet). Net debt = $25,171M − $17,164M = $8,007 million. Net debt ÷ owner earnings = $8,007M ÷ $20,758M = 0.39 years — Visa could retire all of its net debt from less than five months of a single year's normalized owner earnings. This is about as light a leverage burden as a large-cap company carries.

  • Coverage. Interest expense FY2025 was $589 million (10-K, Item 8, Statement of Operations). Interest coverage = Operating income ÷ Interest expense = $23,994M ÷ $589M = 40.7×. A routine downturn would have to be almost unimaginably severe to threaten Visa's ability to service its debt.

  • Liquidity. Current ratio = $37,766M ÷ $35,048M = 1.08× — nominally thin by Graham's classic 2:1 rule, but, as discussed above, most of both sides of that ratio are offsetting settlement and customer-collateral pass-throughs specific to a payments network's business model, not true working-capital risk. Cash alone covers the entire current portion of long-term debt ($17,164M ÷ $5,569M = 3.1×) with room to spare.

  • Refinancing wall. $5,569 million of debt matures within fiscal 2026. Visa comfortably has the cash on hand to retire it without touching the capital markets, and it demonstrated continued easy access to those markets anyway — issuing €3.5 billion (~$3.9 billion) of new senior notes in May 2025 and a further $3.0 billion in February 2026 (10-K, Item 7, MD&A; 10-Q, Item 2, MD&A). A company with an A++ an independent research service financial-strength rating borrowing fresh money at will is not a company worried about a shut credit market.

  • Hidden obligations. The main "off-balance-sheet-flavored" risk is the interchange litigation itself: an accrued litigation liability of $2,698 million as of FY2025 (10-K, Item 8, Note 20, cited in the auditor's critical-audit-matter discussion) against estimated unresolved U.S. covered-litigation claims that stood at roughly $39.4 billion as of October 1, 2025 (down from $49.6 billion two years earlier) (10-K, Item 7, MD&A). Some of this is pre-funded through the $2,990 million U.S. litigation escrow account, and — as discussed above — much of the ultimate economic cost is structurally directed at Class B/C bank shareholders through the conversion-rate mechanism rather than at Class A (public) EPS dollar-for-dollar. Operating lease commitments are small ($163 million of annual rentals per the 10-K capital structure summary); there is no defined-benefit pension plan to worry about.

Verdict: Fortress. Visa carries the balance sheet of a company that essentially never needs to worry about surviving a bad year — the leverage is trivial relative to cash generation, coverage is enormous, and even the one real "soft" liability (litigation) is partially pre-funded and structurally shared with the banks that created the company. An independent research service's own Financial Strength grade of A++ and Safety rank of 1 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) agree with this read.

Principle: solvency is the precondition that lets every other quality compound. A wonderful moat and a high ROIC mean nothing if the company can be forced to sell assets or dilute owners in a bad year — and Visa's fortress balance sheet means none of the analysis above needs to be discounted for survival risk; the entire discount, if any, belongs at the price you pay, not at the quality of the business you are buying.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Visa's capital-allocation record over the last several years is simple to describe: modest reinvestment in the core business, disciplined small bolt-on acquisitions, growing dividends, and very large, very consistent share buybacks.

  • Reinvestment: capex has run 3.5%–4% of revenue for years ($1,059M FY2023, $1,257M FY2024, $1,482M FY2025 — 10-K, Item 8, Statement of Cash Flows) — modest by design, because the network scales with little incremental capital, exactly the pattern a capital-light franchise should show.

  • Acquisitions: disciplined and small relative to Visa's size. Featurespace (fraud-detection AI) for $946 million in December 2024; Prisma and Newpay in Argentina for $1.5 billion in February 2026 (10-K, Item 7, MD&A; 10-Q, Item 2, MD&A). These are bolt-on capability and geographic purchases, not empire-building — there is no sign here of the "institutional imperative" the manual warns against (Ch. 9).

  • Dividends: grown from $0.59/share (FY2016) to $2.36/share declared (FY2025), roughly a 16.5%/year compound rate over the past five years (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, per-share array, "Div'ds Decl'd persh"), while the payout ratio has stayed low and steady at about 21% of net profit (an independent research service, same source, "All Div'ds to Net Prof"). This is a dividend policy consistent with a business that has more cash than it needs for reinvestment — exactly the condition under which the manual says dividends are the appropriate use of a dollar (Ch. 9).

  • Buybacks: the dominant use of cash. $12,101M (FY2023), $16,713M (FY2024), $18,316M (FY2025) of cash spent on repurchases (10-K, Item 8, Statement of Cash Flows), retiring 55 million, 64 million, and 54 million shares respectively at average prices of $222.27, $266.24, and $335.44 (10-K, Item 8, Note 15, Stockholders' Equity). An independent research service's per-share array shows total shares outstanding falling from 2,133.0 million (2016) to 1,825.0 million (2025) — a reduction of (2,133.0 − 1,825.0) ÷ 2,133.0 = 14.4% over the decade (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, per-share array). On the manual's one-dollar test (Ch. 9), Visa's ROIC of 43% comfortably clears any reasonable hurdle rate, so retaining and redeploying cash rather than paying it all out is easily justified in principle.

A caution, not a condemnation. The one-dollar test is about the return on retained capital reinvested in the business; whether a buyback specifically creates value is a separate question that depends on the price paid relative to intrinsic value (manual, Ch. 9: "buybacks create value only below intrinsic value and destroy it above"). Visa's FY2025 buybacks were executed at an average price of $335.44/share. Against my base-case intrinsic value of roughly $300/share (developed below), that means a meaningful share of the FY2025 repurchase program was executed above my own conservative estimate of value — not a disaster, since it still sits within my high-case estimate, but a real data point that management, like the market, is treating the stock as fairly-to-fully valued rather than cheap. This is worth watching, not alarm: a management that buys back stock reflexively regardless of price is not being disciplined about capital allocation, even when the underlying business easily passes the one-dollar test on reinvestment.

Principle: judge capital allocation by the one-dollar test on reinvestment, and separately by price discipline on buybacks — a business can pass the first test easily (Visa's ROIC dwarfs its cost of capital) while its managers still occasionally buy back stock at prices that offer little or no margin of safety to the company doing the buying.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Visa is exactly the kind of business the manual says deserves a normalized-earnings multiple as the primary tool (Ch. "Valuing the Business," multiple methods) — stable, mature-but-still-growing, extraordinarily profitable, with earnings quality I have already normalized above. I will triangulate that multiple-based estimate against a sensitized two-stage DCF as a cross-check, then run the manual's three universal checks.

Anchor 1 — Visa's own historical multiple

An independent research service reports Visa's median P/E over its own multi-year history at 28.0×, with a current (as reported) P/E of 24.4× and a trailing P/E of 26.8× (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, header block). That 28.0× median has been remarkably stable across the last three an independent research service reports I read (28.0× in the 2025-08-01, 2026-01-30, and 2026-05-01 editions), which tells me it is not an artifact of a single report but Visa's genuine long-run valuation home.

Anchor 2 — the peer multiple

Visa sits in an independent research service's "Financial Services (Diversified)" industry, specifically its "Credit Card Companies" sub-segment alongside Mastercard and American Express (an independent research service industry report, Financial Services (Diversified), 2026-05-01: "This corner of the industry consists of such household names as Visa, American Express, and MasterCard"). From equity_atlas.xlsx: Mastercard's median P/E is 32× with a live P/E of 30.5×; American Express's median P/E is 15× with a live P/E of 21.9×. Mastercard is the closer comparable — a pure network model like Visa's, without the credit risk American Express carries as both issuer and network — and its slightly higher historical multiple (32× vs. Visa's 28×) likely reflects Mastercard's somewhat smaller size and historically faster growth rate. I read this as evidence that a multiple in the high-20s to low-30s is the market's settled opinion of what this category of business is worth, with Visa reasonably priced at a modest discount to Mastercard given its larger scale and correspondingly slightly slower growth ceiling.

Anchor 3 — a quality-and-growth-justified multiple from first principles

A business earning 43% ROIC, growing owner earnings at a an independent research service-projected ~10%/year for the intermediate term, financed with essentially no leverage risk, and defended by a network-effect moat, deserves a P/E meaningfully above the market's, but not an unbounded one. The DCF cross-check, fully sensitized

Two-stage model: owner earnings of $10.60/share, growing at g₁ = 10%/year for 10 explicit years (roughly an independent research service's projected growth), then a terminal growth rate g₂, discounted at rate r. The three universal cross-checks (same $10.60/share owner earnings, every time)

1. No-growth floor. Value Visa as if owner earnings never grow another dollar: $10.60 ÷ 0.09 = $117.78/share (using r = 9%). At the current live price of $348.97 (equity_atlas.xlsx), ($348.97 − $117.78) ÷ $348.97 = 66.2% of today's price is a pure bet that growth continues for years to come. That is a large fraction to be betting on a single assumption, even for a business whose moat argument is as strong as Visa's.

2. The implied multiple. At my base-case intrinsic value: $300 ÷ $10.60 = 28.3× — by construction, essentially identical to Visa's own historical median (28.0×), a clean internal consistency check. At the current price: $348.97 ÷ $10.60 = 32.9× — noticeably above Visa's own history and closer to Mastercard's peer multiple (32×). In other words, the market today is pricing Visa as if it deserved Mastercard's historically richer growth multiple, not its own long-run home multiple. That may prove justified if Visa's value-added-services growth (up 24% in FY2025) keeps accelerating — but it is a real, visible stretch versus Visa's own trading history, not an assumption to wave past.

3. Owner-earnings yield. At the current price: $10.60 ÷ $348.97 = 3.04%. The 30-year Treasury yield is roughly 4.9% (an independent research service Selection & Opinion, 2026-07-10). Visa's owner-earnings yield today sits below the risk-free long bond — the manual's Chapter 12 "gravity" check flagged directly: at this price, the entire investment case rests on owner earnings growing meaningfully faster than the bond's fixed coupon for a long time, which is plausible given the moat, but is not a free lunch — it is a specific, and not small, bet on continued execution.

All three checks point the same direction: the current price is a growth bet, not a value bet. That is not a disqualifying finding for a business this good — but it does mean the checks validate what the model already implied, not something new; the underlying assumption (continued double-digit growth) is doing essentially all of the work in all three checks, which is exactly the kind of "same assumption showing up three ways" the manual tells us to watch for.

Principle: value is discounted owner earnings, by definition — but because a small change in the discount rate or the terminal growth assumption can swing that discounted value by a third or more, a single point estimate is a false comfort. Triangulating a normalized-earnings multiple (anchored to the company's own history and its closest peer) against a fully sensitized DCF, and then running the same owner-earnings figure through the no-growth floor, the implied multiple, and the bond-yield comparison, converts an uncertain arithmetic exercise into an honest range you can actually reason about.

The live price, from equity_atlas.xlsx (priced July 12, 2026), is $348.97. Putting that price against the triangulated range:

  • Against IV base ($300): ($300 − $348.97) ÷ $300 = −16.3% — price carries a roughly 16% premium to my central estimate, not a discount.
  • Against IV high ($355): ($355 − $348.97) ÷ $355 = +1.7% — price is essentially even with, marginally below, the optimistic case.

There is no margin of safety at today's price under my base-case assumptions. The stock is priced for something close to my most optimistic, growth-justified scenario, not my central one.

Where should the safety come from here? Per the manual's Chapter 13, in a mediocre business every dollar of protection has to come from the discount in the purchase price. Visa is not a mediocre business — it is close to a "wonderful franchise," and part of its safety genuinely does live in the quality itself: a 43%-ROIC network moat with a fortress balance sheet is far less likely to suffer a permanent impairment of value than a cyclical or commodity business trading at the same discount to a DCF estimate. But the manual is equally explicit that quality justifies a fair price, never any price — and $348.97 is not a fair price relative to my $300 base case; it is a price that requires the high end of my range, and possibly a scenario even more optimistic than that, to be proven right.

Is Mr. Market euphoric or rational here? I would call this closer to quietly confident than euphoric — an independent research service's own Timeliness rank of 2 and Safety rank of 1 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) reflect a market that has bid this stock steadily upward on genuinely strong, consistent execution (double-digit revenue and EPS growth every year, expanding value-added-services revenue, disciplined buybacks), not a speculative mania. But "quietly confident" prices still leave no room for the litigation docket to worsen, for interchange regulation to bite harder than expected, or for a consumer-spending slowdown to interrupt the growth run — any one of which would leave a buyer at today's price with a real, not merely theoretical, downside.

Principle: use Mr. Market's prices, never take orders from them. A price close to the top of an honest valuation range on a wonderful business is not a reason to panic-sell if you already own it and the thesis is intact — but it is every reason to decline to buy more today, and to wait, patiently, for a price that leaves room for you to be wrong.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Returning to the one question I opened with: is the market still paying a fair toll for Visa, or has it already paid for a decade of flawless execution? The evidence says the latter. The business itself is not in question — ROIC north of 40%, a fortress balance sheet, a moat built on a two-sided network effect that has survived nearly seven decades of competitive assault, and a management team that reinvests lightly, acquires modestly, and returns the great bulk of its cash to owners. But at $348.97 against a triangulated range of $235–$355 anchored on $300, the price has already collected almost all of the reward this business can plausibly offer over the medium term, leaving very little room for anything to go wrong.

This is a wonderful business, not currently available at a fair price. It clears every quality gate in the manual's checklist with ease, and fails only the last one — the margin of safety — which is the gate that ultimately governs the buy decision, not the hold decision.

Three things a student should understand better before getting fully comfortable:

  1. The size and trajectory of interchange and network-fee regulation. I treated this as a manageable, priced-in risk, but the U.S. Regulation II litigation, the EU's ongoing IFR review, and a growing list of country-level interchange caps could compress Visa's net-revenue-per-dollar-of-volume more than my base case assumes. This is a genuinely hard-to-forecast legal and political process, and it deserves closer, ongoing tracking rather than a one-time judgment.

  2. Whether value-added-services growth (24% in FY2025) is a durable second engine or a temporary catch-up. If it keeps compounding at anywhere near that pace, my "high" case understates the business; if it decelerates toward Visa's core payments-volume growth rate, my "base" case may already be generous.

  3. The ultimate size of the U.S. interchange multidistrict litigation liability (estimated unresolved claims of roughly $39.4 billion as of October 2025) and how much of that is genuinely absorbed by Class B/C conversion-rate adjustments versus consolidated earnings — a mechanism I described but whose long-run magnitude is hard to model precisely.

The two universal lessons this case teaches most vividly:

First, a wonderful business and a wonderful price are two different questions, and the manual's warning that "quality justifies a fair price, never any price" is not a hedge — it is the entire discipline. It would be easy to talk yourself into paying up for Visa because the business is genuinely excellent; the seminar's job is to show, in real arithmetic, exactly how much of today's price is already a bet on the future, so that enthusiasm for the business does not substitute for judgment about the price.

Second, normalize before you capitalize, and do not let a company's own non-GAAP presentation make that judgment for you. Visa's non-GAAP EPS treats a recurring litigation cost as if it were zero; simply averaging that cost over a few years — rather than deleting it, and rather than being frightened by its worst single year — produced a materially different, and I believe more honest, owner-earnings figure than either the GAAP or the non-GAAP number alone.

This is a teaching case, built to illustrate a method — not personalized investment advice.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

  • Owner earnings — the cash a passive owner could withdraw from the business in a normal year without weakening it: Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. For Visa (FY2025, normalized): $20,058M (GAAP NI) + $962M (excess-litigation add-back) + $1,220M (D&A) − $1,482M (capex) = $20,758M, or $10.56/share (rounded to $10.60).
  • Free cash flow (FCF) — operating cash flow minus capital expenditures. Visa FY2025: $23,059M − $1,482M = $21,577M, used here as a sanity check on owner earnings, not as the primary figure.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex — the split between capital spending needed just to hold today's business steady versus spending to expand it; a judgment call the accounts do not make for you. For Visa, capex is small (3.7% of revenue) and largely network/technology upkeep, so I treated the full amount as maintenance capex — a conservative choice.
  • Return on invested capital (ROIC) = NOPAT ÷ Invested capital. Visa FY2025: $19,893M ÷ $45,916M = 43.3%.
  • NOPAT (net operating profit after tax) = Operating income × (1 − tax rate). Visa FY2025: $23,994M × (1 − 0.171) = $19,893M.
  • Invested capital = Debt + Equity − Cash. Visa FY2025: $25,171M + $37,909M − $17,164M = $45,916M.
  • Return on equity (ROE) = Net income ÷ Shareholders' equity. Visa FY2025: $20,058M ÷ $37,909M = 52.9% (an independent research service reports 59.5% using average equity).
  • Interest coverage = Operating income (EBIT) ÷ Interest expense. Visa FY2025: $23,994M ÷ $589M = 40.7×.
  • Net debt = Total debt − Cash. Visa FY2025: $25,171M − $17,164M = $8,007M. Net debt ÷ owner earnings = $8,007M ÷ $20,758M = 0.39 years.
  • Current ratio = Current assets ÷ Current liabilities. Visa FY2025: $37,766M ÷ $35,048M = 1.08×.
  • Discount rate (r) — the owner's opportunity cost of capital, anchored to long-term interest rates plus a premium for uncertainty; acts as "gravity" on value because a higher r shrinks the present value of every future dollar. Used here at 8%–10%, base case 9%, against a 30-year Treasury yield of ~4.9%.
  • Intrinsic value (master equation) = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1+r)ᵗ — the present value of every future dollar of owner earnings the business will produce. The conceptual anchor for every method used in this case.
  • Two-stage DCF / terminal value — an explicit forecast period (here, 10 years at 10%/year growth) followed by a terminal value capturing everything after [OE₁₁ ÷ (r − g₂)], discounted back to the present. The r − g₂ "spread" is the most sensitive input in the whole model.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value = Owner earnings ÷ r. Visa: $10.60 ÷ 0.09 = $117.78/share — the floor beneath the DCF, and the measure of how much of today's price ($348.97) is a bet on growth (66.2% of it, here).
  • Implied multiple = Intrinsic value ÷ owner earnings. Base case: $300 ÷ $10.60 = 28.3×; at the current price: $348.97 ÷ $10.60 = 32.9×.
  • Owner-earnings yield = Owner earnings ÷ Price. At today's price: $10.60 ÷ $348.97 = 3.04%, compared with a ~4.9% 30-year Treasury yield.
  • Margin of safety = (Intrinsic value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic value. Against the base case: ($300 − $348.97) ÷ $300 = −16.3% — a negative margin, meaning a premium, not a discount.
  • P/E ratio = Price ÷ EPS. Visa's own 10+ year median (per an independent research service) is 28.0×; the live P/E (equity_atlas.xlsx) is 30.4×.
  • Justified / quality-adjusted multiple — a P/E derived from first principles (moat strength, ROIC, growth rate) rather than asserted; reconciled here against Visa's own history (28×) and Mastercard's peer multiple (32×) to land on a triangulated range of 22×–33.5×.
  • Economic moat — a structural competitive advantage (here, a two-sided network effect, brand trust, and switching costs) that protects high returns from being competed away.
  • Economic vs. accounting goodwill — the value of a franchise above its tangible capital (economic goodwill) versus the number actually on the balance sheet (accounting goodwill, $19,879M for Visa). Book value badly understates a franchise like this one.
  • Circle of competence — the set of businesses an investor can genuinely understand well enough to forecast; here, the core payments-toll model is inside the circle, while the precise size of regulatory and litigation risk is an acknowledged limitation.
  • One-dollar test — retain a dollar of earnings only if it produces at least a dollar of incremental market value (i.e., incremental ROIC ≥ r); otherwise return it to owners. Visa's reinvestment easily passes; its buyback price discipline is the softer spot.
  • EBITDA / non-GAAP earnings — earnings measures that add back certain charges; useful as a data point but not to be substituted uncritically for owner earnings, especially when they delete a genuinely recurring cost (as Visa's non-GAAP EPS does with litigation).
  • Working capital — current assets minus current liabilities; for Visa, heavily influenced by offsetting settlement and customer-collateral balances specific to its business model.
