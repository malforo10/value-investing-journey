2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What it does. PayPal operates a two-sided digital-payments network: 439 million active accounts across ~200 markets, connecting consumers who want to pay and merchants who want to get paid. In 2025 roughly $1.79 trillion of Total Payment Volume (TPV) — the dollar value of payments completed on the platform — flowed across it. PayPal takes a small slice of each of those dollars.

How it makes money — two revenue lines. The 10-K classifies revenue into exactly two buckets, and understanding the split is the case:

  • Transaction revenues — $29,798M in 2025 (90% of the total). Fees charged per transaction, based on TPV, plus currency conversion, cross-border, instant-transfer, and crypto fees.
  • Revenues from other value-added services — $3,374M (10%). Partnerships, referral and subscription fees, gateway fees, interest and fees on the loan book, and interest earned on the assets underlying customer balances (this last piece falls when interest rates fall — a live headwind).

The two engines inside "transaction revenue." This is the fork in the road:

  • Branded checkout — the yellow PayPal button and Venmo at online checkout. The consumer chooses PayPal; PayPal owns the customer relationship on both sides. This is the high-margin franchise and the two-sided network's crown jewel.
  • Unbranded processing (Braintree) — PayPal processes cards for a big merchant (think a large marketplace) without the PayPal brand ever appearing. It is a commodity card-processing utility: enormous volume, low margin, higher expense rates (the 10-K says Braintree "generally [has] higher expense rates than our other products and services").

Warren: One company, two economically opposite businesses. One is See's Candy; the other is close to a toll bridge that has to hand most of the toll to the road crew. If you value the whole thing on a single blended multiple, you will misprice both — the manual's warning in Chapter 8 about businesses that must be valued in parts.

Segments. PayPal reports as one operating segment, so we get no clean sub-P&L for branded vs. unbranded — we must reconstruct the economics from take-rate arithmetic (Section 4).

Circle-of-competence gate (Chapter 4). Ben: Can we forecast this business within a sensible range? Partly. Payments are a durable human need; PayPal's scale, its float, and its 20-year brand are real and understandable. But the decisive variables — will branded checkout hold its share against Apple Pay, Shop Pay, and stablecoins? will the network effect keep two-sidedness intact? — are genuinely contestable. This sits at the edge of the circle: understandable as a business, but with a moat whose durability is the open question. That is precisely where the manual says expensive errors live, so we proceed with humility and a wide margin of safety, not false precision.

THE ONE QUESTION THAT DECIDES THE CASE:

Is branded checkout — the PayPal button and Venmo — a durable, defended franchise, or a slowly eroding brand whose owner-earnings are being quietly hollowed out even as headline volume grows?

Everything in the valuation turns on the answer. If the franchise holds, $45.47 is a gift. If it erodes, the low multiple is the market being right.

PRINCIPLE. Separate volume from profit, and separate the parts of a mixed business. A single revenue line can hide two businesses with opposite economics; find where the profit actually comes from before you value a dollar of it.

The balance sheet (12/31/2025, $M) — and the customer-funds trap

This is where PYPL's balance sheet fools the careless. Total assets are $80,173M and total liabilities $59,917M — but $40,198M of those liabilities is "Funds payable and amounts due to customers," matched by $38,198M of "Funds receivable and customer accounts" on the asset side. That is customer money PayPal merely custodies, not corporate leverage. You must strip both sides out to see the real balance sheet:

  • Corporate cash + short-term investments: 8,049 + 2,373 = $10,422M
  • Corporate debt (long-term notes 9,987 + commercial paper 200): $10,187M
  • Net corporate position: 10,422 − 10,187 = +$235M — essentially net-cash-neutral.
  • Total equity: $20,256M (down slightly from $20,417M — buybacks are shrinking book equity even as retained earnings rise; see Section 6).

Ben: The float (customer balances PayPal invests in the meantime) is real and valuable — it even earns interest income. But it is liabilities of high quality, not a war chest. Judge the fortress on the corporate columns alone.

Earnings-quality scan (Chapter 7 red-flag checklist)

  • SBC treated as free? No — and this is the crucial quality point. SBC ($1,002M) is expensed in GAAP net income. An independent research service's "adjusted" $5.31 EPS and the company's non-GAAP figures add SBC back, which the manual forbids: SBC is a real cost — it dilutes owners (see the share-count discipline in Section 6). We will keep it as a cost.
  • "Restructuring / one-time" charges? Recurring: $331M in 2025, $438M in 2024. When a charge shows up every year it is a cost of doing business, not a one-timer. We normalize through it.
  • EBITDA-as-cash-flow? Management does not lean on it here. Good.
  • Aggressive assumptions / opacity? Disclosure is clear and detailed. No pension plan. The one soft spot is reliance on non-operating tailwinds (tax discretes, investment gains) to flatter 2025 net income — we discount those.

PRINCIPLE. The three statements must articulate — reconcile net income to the cash that actually appeared, and strip the balance sheet down to what the corporation truly owns and owes. A payments company's customer float and a lender's loan-book flows can make a healthy business look levered or a good year look weak; do the subtraction yourself.

The owner-earnings ladder (Chapter 7) — the key earnings-quality lesson

The manual defines Owner Earnings = Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital, keeping every real cost. Watch how the "earnings" number balloons as you climb the wrong direction of the ladder:

Warren: Look at the last two rungs. The distance between honest owner earnings ($5.81) and the polished non-GAAP number ($6.62) is almost entirely stock-based compensation — a billion dollars of real cost, paid in shares instead of cash, that the adjusted figure pretends is free. The tooth fairy does not pay for stock comp any more than she pays for capex. We value PayPal on ~$5.3–5.4B of owner earnings, not on $6.4B of dressed-up "earnings."

We normalize down slightly to ~$5,300M owner earnings, because 2025 leaned on a low tax rate and investment gains that won't repeat, and because interest income on customer balances is falling as rates drop (Section 5). Call normalized OE ≈ $5.76 per share.

Maintenance vs. growth capex

Capex $852M sits below D&A $963M, and the business grew anyway. That tells us maintenance capex is modest and most of reported capex is arguably discretionary/growth. We conservatively treated all capex as maintenance in the ladder — if anything that understates owner earnings. This is the mark of a capital-light franchise (Chapter 6): it can grow while consuming little incremental capital.

The moat — the heart of the case

The bull evidence (a real moat exists):
- Two-sided network — 439M consumers and tens of millions of merchants; each side makes the other more valuable. Genuine network effect.
- Brand & trust — 20+ years; the PayPal button converts because buyers trust it. Branded checkout is the high-margin core.
- Capital-light, high returns — the signature of pricing power and a franchise.

The bear evidence (the moat is being tested) — and this is the sharpest number in the case:

Compare the freshest quarter, Q1 2026, from the 10-Q:
- TPV grew +11% (to $463,955M).
- But transaction revenue grew only +7%, and — the killer — transaction-margin dollars were essentially flat. Do the arithmetic the company won't hand you on one line:
- Q1 2026 transaction margin $ = transaction revenue 7,501 − transaction expense 4,165 − transaction & credit losses 378 = $2,958M
- Q1 2025 transaction margin $ = 7,016 − 3,704 − 371 = $2,941M
- Growth = 2,958 ÷ 2,941 − 1 = +0.6% — profit stood still while volume rose 11%.

Why? The 10-Q is explicit: of the ~$620M gross rise in transaction revenue, ~$410M came from Braintree — the low-margin, unbranded processing. The company is moving oceans of volume and keeping almost none of the incremental profit. Meanwhile, for the full year 2025, PayPal deliberately shed low-margin Braintree volume ("profitable growth"): full-year payment transactions actually fell 4% and transactions-per-account fell 5%, even as TPV rose 7%.

Ben: This is the two-faced number I warned about. In the quarter, growth comes from the wrong (low-margin) engine; over the year, they starve that engine to protect margins — and branded checkout is not visibly accelerating to fill the gap. The franchise is not obviously shrinking, but it is not obviously widening either, while Apple Pay, Shop Pay and stablecoins press on the branded button. The moat is narrow and contested, not wide and secure. We will call it a good business, not a wonderful one.

ROIC vs. cost of capital (Chapter 6) — the arithmetic

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital.
- NOPAT = operating income × (1 − normalized tax rate) = 6,065 × (1 − 0.20) = $4,852M
- Invested capital, gross (equity + corporate debt) = 20,256 + 10,187 = $30,443M → ROIC = 4,852 ÷ 30,443 = 15.9%
- Invested capital, net of the ~$10.4B corporate cash it doesn't need = 20,256 + 10,187 − 10,422 = $20,021M → ROIC = 4,852 ÷ 20,021 = 24.2%

Either way, ROIC (16–24%) comfortably exceeds our cost of capital r ≈ 10.5% (Section 7). By the manual's decisive test, growth here creates value — if it is branded, high-margin growth. The catch, again: unbranded Braintree growth earns close to r or below, so mix is everything.

ROE = 5,233 ÷ 20,256 = 25.8% (an independent research service shows 25.4%). A leverage caveat: buybacks have shrunk equity, which mechanically inflates ROE — so don't read 25.8% as pure operating brilliance; part of it is a smaller denominator bought with cash. ROIC is the cleaner read.

PRINCIPLE. Owner earnings, not "adjusted" earnings — and a moat must be argued from margin dollars, not headline volume. The test of a payments moat is whether transaction-margin dollars per unit of volume hold; growing TPV while margin dollars stagnate is a moat leaking, not a moat working.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Judged on the corporate balance sheet (customer float excluded), each answer with its arithmetic and citation.

  • Leverage. Corporate debt = notes $9,987M + commercial paper $200M = $10,187M. Corporate cash + ST investments = $10,422M. Net debt = −$235M — the company is essentially net-cash. Net debt ÷ owner earnings ≈ 0. (10-K balance sheet & Note 12.)
  • Coverage. Interest coverage = operating income ÷ interest expense = 6,065 ÷ 441 = 13.8×. A normal year covers the interest bill nearly fourteen times over — a wide cushion. (10-K income statement; Note 7.)
  • Liquidity. Current assets $59,759M vs. current liabilities $46,443M (mostly customer funds payable, matched by customer-fund assets). Corporate liquidity is ample: $10.4B cash/ST + an undrawn $5.0B revolving credit facility (matures 2028). (10-K balance sheet; Note 12.)
  • Refinancing wall. Well laddered. Notes mature $1,250M (Oct 2026), $500M (2027), then staggered out to 2028, 2029, 2030, 2032, 2034, 2035, and 30–40-yr maturities to 2050–2062, at coupons of 1.65%–5.50%. No cliff. The nearest $1.25B 2026 note is trivially covered by $10.4B of cash. (Note 12.)
  • Hidden obligations. No defined-benefit pension. Uncapitalized operating-lease rentals only ~$174M/yr (an independent research service). The real "hidden leverage" in a payments firm is the customer float and the loan book — and here the float is high-quality (invested in cash and AFS debt securities), and the loan book is largely sold forward. Legal reserves are "not material." (10-K Notes 12, 13.)

Verdict: FORTRESS. Net-cash, 13.8× interest coverage, an undrawn $5B revolver, no pension, no near-term wall. An independent research service's B++ Financial Strength is, if anything, slightly conservative — the corporate balance sheet is very strong. This matters enormously: a fortress balance sheet means PayPal can never be forced to sell while it fights the moat battle. It buys time to be right.

PRINCIPLE. Survivability is the precondition for every earnings forecast — and for a financial-adjacent business you must first strip out the money that isn't yours. Strength is measured on the corporate columns, against the cash that must service the debt.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Capital allocation across the manual's six uses, judged on the record:

  1. Reinvest in the business — modest and capital-light (capex $852M < D&A). Tech & development $3.1B, sales & marketing $2.3B ("PayPal Everywhere," "Venmo Everything"). Appropriate given high ROIC — if it defends the branded franchise.
  2. Acquisitions — restrained lately; no large, value-destroying deals in the period. A welcome contrast to the industry's institutional imperative.
  3. Pay down debt — net-cash already; no urgency. Fine.
  4. Dividends — first-ever dividend initiated December 2025 ($0.14/quarter, ~$130M/yr; ~1.24% yield today). A signal of maturity and confidence in cash generation.
  5. Repurchase shares — the headline act. This is where management has been most aggressive, and we judge it on price (Chapter 9):

The decade share-count trend (XBRL):
| 2016 | 2018 | 2020 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1,207M | 1,174M | 1,172M | 1,136M | 1,072M | 993M | 920M |

That is a 24% reduction in shares — a real, owner-friendly shrinking of the pie into fewer slices. In 2025 alone PayPal spent $6,052M on buybacks (net share reduction 993→920M ≈ 73M shares).

Judged on price — the manual's only test that matters: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value. PayPal repurchased through 2024–2025 at an average in the $60–$90s, and now trades at $45.47. Against our intrinsic-value estimate of $60–$90 (Section 7), buying back stock in the $50s–$80s was value-neutral to modestly accretive, and buying at today's $45.47 would be decisively value-creating — the company is retiring dollars of owner earnings at ~8× while they are worth ~11–15×. This is exactly the capital allocation the manual applauds: retiring undervalued shares.

  1. Share issuance discipline (the sixth use). The counterweight: SBC of $1,002M/yr streams shares out the back door even as buybacks pull them in the front. Gross buybacks of $6B bought only ~73M net shares partly because option/RSU grants dilute. The net count still fell sharply, so management is winning the tug-of-war — but owners should keep score on the net number and treat SBC as the real cost it is.

The one-dollar test (Chapter 9). With ROIC (16–24%) above r (10.5%), a dollar retained should create more than a dollar — provided it funds branded, high-margin growth. The initiation of a dividend and heavy buybacks says management itself judges that not every dollar can be reinvested at those returns, and is returning the surplus. That is rational.

Candor. Management is plain about the hard truths: it disclosed deliberately shrinking Braintree volume for "profitable growth," and an independent research service notes the board conceded "the pace of change and execution was not in line with expectations," replacing CEO Alex Chriss with Enrique Lores (effective March 1, 2026). Owning the miss and changing the driver is candor, not spin.

PRINCIPLE. Share count is capital allocation, and buybacks are only as good as the price paid. Retiring 24% of the shares is wonderful if done below value and merely expensive if done above it — always score buybacks against your own intrinsic-value estimate, and always net out the shares handed back through stock comp.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Choosing the method

Intrinsic value is the discounted value of future owner earnings (Chapter 10) — that is the definition, always. But we choose the estimator the business deserves. PayPal is a stable, cash-generative, capital-light franchise trading at a distressed multiple with a genuine moat question. For such a business the manual points to a normalized owner-earnings approach, cross-checked hard. So:

  • Primary: a normalized owner-earnings multiple, with the multiple derived, not borrowed.
  • Cross-checks: a reverse-DCF (what is the market assuming?), the no-growth floor, and the owner-earnings yield vs. the 5% long bond.
  • Sensitivity table to make the false-precision point.

The discount rate as gravity (Chapter 12)

Rates are the gravity that pulls on every asset value. The 30-year Treasury is ~5.0% (Selection & Opinion, July 2026). We add an equity-and-uncertainty premium — larger than usual because the moat is contested and the business carries a 1.35 beta and real disruption risk:

r = 5.0% long bond + ~5.5% premium = 10.5%.

Deriving the multiple (never borrow one blindly)

Three independent reads of the fair multiple for ~$5.3B of normalized owner earnings:
1. Quality-justified. A capital-light business with 16–24% ROIC and low-single-digit defensible growth, discounted at 10.5%, is worth roughly the no-growth floor (≈9.5×, see below) plus a few turns for modest growth → ~11–13× owner earnings.
2. Historical. an independent research service's Median P/E is 41 and recent-year average P/Es ran 13–47×. Those reflect PayPal's growth-stock past — clearly too rich for today's decelerating reality. We discard the exuberant end and anchor to the low-teens, matching where a mature, contested franchise belongs.
3. Reverse-engineered from the floor. No-growth value ÷ owner earnings = ($5,300M ÷ 0.105) ÷ $5,300M = 9.5× — the multiple if PayPal never grows again. Any growth at all lifts it above that.

Reconciling the three, a defensible multiple is ~11–15× owner earnings (11× ≈ no-growth-plus-a-little; 15× ≈ modest durable growth).

Triangulating the value

Normalized owner earnings ≈ $5,300M, or $5.76 per share (÷920M shares).

  • LOW (~11× OE): 11 × $5.76 = ~$63/sh → round down to ~$60 honoring the moat risk — the barely-any-growth case.
  • BASE (~13× OE): 13 × $5.76 = ~$75/sh — modest 3–4% durable owner-earnings growth.
  • HIGH (~15× OE): 15 × $5.76 = ~$86/sh → ~$90 — branded checkout holds and re-accelerates.

Cross-checked with an explicit two-stage DCF (10-yr explicit, r = 10.5%): even a cautious DCF (4% growth then 2.5% terminal) produces ~$82/sh, and a 6%/3% base produces ~$98/sh. The DCF sits above our multiple range. Warren: When the DCF runs hotter than the multiple, trust the more conservative machine — the DCF is capitalizing owner earnings that the transaction-margin evidence tells us may not compound as smoothly as a formula assumes. We deliberately haircut to the multiple range and set:

Intrinsic value range: LOW ~$60 · BASE ~$75 · HIGH ~$90 per share.

The three universal cross-checks (as arithmetic)

  1. No-growth floor (checks the growth assumption). OE ÷ r = $5,300M ÷ 0.105 = $50,476M → $54.87/sh. Read this carefully: the no-growth floor alone is $54.87 — already above the $45.47 price. The market is paying less than PayPal is worth even if it never grows another dollar. Every dollar of future growth is being handed to the buyer for free.
  2. Implied multiple (checks the output). Base IV ÷ OE = $75 ÷ $5.76 = ~13× owner earnings — eminently defensible for a 16–24% ROIC, net-cash, capital-light franchise. No runaway terminal value hiding here.
  3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the bond (checks the anchor to rates). At $45.47, OE yield = $5,300M ÷ (45.47 × 920M) = $5,300M ÷ $41,832M = 12.7% — versus a 5.0% long bond. You are buying a growing 12.7% coupon against a static 5% coupon. Free-cash-flow yield is a similar 13.3%. This is Chapter 12's gravity working for the buyer.

The reverse-DCF closer — what is the market actually assuming?

Solve for the owner-earnings growth rate that today's $45.47 implies (r = 10.5%, 2.5% terminal): the price can only be justified if owner earnings shrink about 4–5% per year, forever. Ben: Sit with that. At 8.5× earnings, Mr. Market is not pricing slow growth — he is pricing terminal, permanent decline of the whole franchise. That is a bet that Apple Pay and stablecoins don't just contest the moat but destroy it. Possible — but that is the negative analysis the market has done for us, and it is a demanding assumption to bake into perpetuity.

Sensitivity table (the false-precision point)

Intrinsic value per share — two-stage DCF, 6% explicit growth for 10 yrs, varying r and terminal g:

The spread ($81–$114) is the point: precision is an illusion, but every reasonable cell of the machine says the same direction — the stock is cheap. When the whole grid clears the price by a wide margin, you don't need the decimal places. (We nonetheless anchor our headline range to the more conservative multiple method — $60–$90 — precisely because that DCF assumes a smoother 6% compounding than the margin-dollar evidence yet supports.)

Sanity check vs. An independent research service & the live multiple

  • An independent research service 18-month target range: $38–$83 (midpoint $61); 2029–31 target $70–$105. Our $60–$90 range sits squarely inside VL's own longer-run band.
  • Live P/E ≈ 8.5×; historical/median P/E far higher. Our implied ~13× base is a deliberately humble re-rating, not a return to growth-stock froth.

PRINCIPLE. Choose the estimator the business deserves, derive every multiple, and let the cross-checks fail differently. When the no-growth floor, the yield-vs-bond, the DCF, and a conservative multiple all point the same way, the conclusion is robust to your ignorance of the exact number — which is the only kind of conclusion worth acting on.

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

The arithmetic (Chapter 13). Margin of safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value.

  • Against BASE $75: (75 − 45.47) ÷ 75 = 39%.
  • Against the conservative LOW $60: (60 − 45.47) ÷ 60 = 24%.
  • Against the no-growth floor $54.87 (the harshest honest yardstick): (54.87 − 45.47) ÷ 54.87 = 17% — you still have a margin of safety even assuming the business never grows again.

Where the safety sits. The manual distinguishes safety bought with price discount from safety that lives in quality. Here it is unusually stacked in the price — a 24–39% discount to conservative value — and buttressed by the fortress balance sheet (can't be forced to sell) and the capital-light, high-ROIC economics. The one place safety does not live is in certainty about the moat — that risk is real and undiscounted. So the price discount is doing the heavy lifting, which is exactly right for a good (not wonderful) business at the edge of the circle: when quality is uncertain, demand the discount in cash.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational? On PayPal specifically, depressed — pricing perpetual decline (the reverse-DCF's −4% to −5% forever) into a net-cash, 13.8×-covered, 16–24%-ROIC franchise still generating $5.5B of free cash. The broad market is the opposite: An independent research service's median P/E is 18.2 and the market is a tech/AI-capex bull. PayPal is a distinctly out-of-favor name in an in-favor market — the classic setup where Mr. Market's pocketbook, not his wisdom, is useful to us.

PRINCIPLE. The margin of safety is a discount in price, not a hope in the story. When a good business is priced for terminal decline, the depressed quote is the raw material — but you still demand the discount in cash precisely because the moat question is not settled.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: GOOD (not wonderful, not fair). Capital-light, high-ROIC, fortress balance sheet, real two-sided network and brand — but a contested, narrowing moat where headline volume growth is not converting to transaction-margin-dollar growth. That single fact is what keeps it out of the "wonderful" tier.

Verdict: a GOOD business at a conspicuously CHEAP price — attractive on the numbers, with an honest, unresolved moat risk. Intrinsic value $60–$90 (base $75) against a $45.47 price is a 24–39% margin of safety, and the stock is priced for perpetual decline that the fundamentals do not yet show. In the manual's language: this is closer to a good company at a wonderful price than a wonderful company at a fair price — the Graham end of the spectrum, where the discount must carry the thesis.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION — is branded checkout a durable franchise or an eroding brand? — the honest answer is "contested, and not yet resolved either way." The bull case (network effect, brand, high ROIC, aggressive buybacks below value) and the bear case (flat transaction-margin dollars, −4% payment transactions, Apple Pay / Shop Pay / stablecoin pressure) both have real evidence. The price, however, has resolved the question pessimistically — and that gap between an open question and a settled-pessimistic price is the entire opportunity.

Two or three open questions to monitor (the thesis stands or falls here):
1. Transaction-margin dollars — do they resume growing? This, not TPV, is the moat's vital sign. (Flat in Q1 2026 is the yellow flag.)
2. Branded checkout share — does the PayPal button hold against Apple Pay, Shop Pay, and stablecoin rails? Any disclosure that isolates branded volume is gold.
3. Interest income on customer balances — how much owner earnings erodes as rates fall (already down: interest income $662M→$517M year over year).

Two or three universal lessons from this case:
- Volume is not profit. A payments business can grow TPV 11% while transaction-margin dollars grow 0.6%. Always find the margin dollars.
- "Adjusted earnings" often just means adding stock-comp back. The $5.81 owner-earnings vs. $6.62 non-GAAP gap here is SBC. Keep it as a cost.
- A cheap price can carry a merely-good business — Graham's insight — but only with a fortress balance sheet (so you're never forced to sell) and a real margin of safety in cash (so an honest error can't ruin you). PayPal has both.

This is a teaching case built to demonstrate a method — not investment advice, and not a recommendation to buy or sell PayPal.

11. GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with PYPL's own numbers)

Total Payment Volume (TPV). Dollar value of payments completed on the platform. PYPL: ~$1.79 trillion (FY2025); +11% YoY in Q1 2026 — but growth concentrated in low-margin Braintree.

Transaction margin dollars. Transaction revenue − transaction expense − transaction & credit losses; the profit actually kept per unit of volume. Formula: TR − TE − losses. PYPL Q1'26: 7,501 − 4,165 − 378 = $2,958M, vs $2,941M a year prior = +0.6% while TPV +11%. The moat's vital sign.

Take rate. Revenue as a % of TPV. PYPL: transaction revenue $29,798M ÷ TPV ~$1,790,000M ≈ 1.66%, drifting down as unbranded Braintree grows.

Branded vs. unbranded. Branded = the PayPal/Venmo button (high margin, owns the customer). Unbranded = Braintree card processing (low margin, commodity). PYPL: ~$410M of Q1'26's ~$620M transaction-revenue rise came from Braintree — volume without much profit.

Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can forecast within a sensible range; its boundary matters more than its size. PYPL: at the edge — understandable business, contestable moat.

Moat / economic franchise. A durable advantage (network, brand, low cost, scale) protecting high returns; its signature is pricing power. PYPL: two-sided network + brand, but narrow and contested.

Owner earnings. Formula: Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. The cash an owner can withdraw. PYPL: 5,233 + 963 − 852 − 0 ≈ $5,344M (~$5.81/sh); normalized to ~$5,300M. SBC ($1,002M) is kept as a real cost, not added back.

Stock-based compensation (SBC). Pay in shares; a real, dilutive cost. PYPL: $1,002M FY2025 — the entire gap between honest owner earnings ($5.81/sh) and non-GAAP "adjusted" earnings ($6.62/sh).

Maintenance capex. Capital spending to preserve competitive position/unit volume, vs. growth capex. PYPL: capex $852M < D&A $963M — capital-light; we treated all capex as maintenance (conservative).

Free cash flow. Formula: Operating cash flow − capex. PYPL: 6,416 − 852 = $5,564M (~$6.05/sh); FCF yield at $45.47 = 13.3%.

ROIC (Return on Invested Capital). Formula: NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital; NOPAT = op income × (1 − tax). PYPL: 6,065 × 0.80 = $4,852M; ÷ $30,443M (gross) = 15.9%, ÷ $20,021M (net of corporate cash) = 24.2%. Above r → value-creating.

NOPAT. After-tax operating profit. PYPL: $6,065M × (1 − 0.20) = $4,852M.

ROE (Return on Equity). Formula: Net income ÷ equity. PYPL: 5,233 ÷ 20,256 = 25.8% — but flattered by buyback-shrunk equity; read ROIC instead.

Discount rate (r). Owner's opportunity cost = long bond + uncertainty premium. PYPL: 5.0% (30-yr Treasury) + 5.5% = 10.5%.

No-growth (earning-power) value. Formula: Owner earnings ÷ r. The value if growth stopped today. PYPL: 5,300 ÷ 0.105 = $50,476M → $54.87/sh — already above the $45.47 price.

Intrinsic value (two-stage DCF). Formula: Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. PYPL: even a cautious 4%/2.5% DCF ≈ $82/sh; we haircut to a multiple-based $60–$90 range.

Implied multiple. IV ÷ owner earnings — the DCF restated in one number. PYPL: $75 ÷ $5.76 = ~13× owner earnings at base.

Owner-earnings yield. Formula: Owner earnings ÷ market cap. PYPL: 5,300 ÷ 41,832 = 12.7% vs. a 5.0% long bond.

Reverse DCF. Solve for the growth the current price implies. PYPL: at $45.47 (r 10.5%), implied owner-earnings growth ≈ −4% to −5% forever — the market pricing terminal decline.

Interest coverage. Formula: Operating income (EBIT) ÷ interest expense. PYPL: 6,065 ÷ 441 = 13.8×.

Net debt / owner earnings. Formula: (Total corporate debt − corporate cash) ÷ owner earnings. PYPL: (10,187 − 10,422) ÷ 5,300 = ≈ 0 (net-cash).

Customer float / funds payable. Customer money PayPal custodies and invests; a high-quality liability, not corporate leverage. PYPL: funds payable & due to customers $40,198M, matched by funds receivable & customer accounts $38,198M — stripped out before judging solvency.

Margin of safety. Formula: (Intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value. PYPL: (75 − 45.47) ÷ 75 = 39% at base; 17% even against the no-growth floor.

Mr. Market. The manic-depressive partner whose price, not wisdom, you use. PYPL: currently depressed on this name — pricing perpetual decline into a net-cash, high-ROIC franchise.

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r. PYPL: ROIC 16–24% > r 10.5% → retention justified for branded growth; surplus rightly returned via buybacks + new dividend.
