NIKE, Inc. (NYSE: NKE) — A Value-Investing Case Study

In the Graham–Buffett tradition · Date of analysis: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $44.09

A teaching case, not a recommendation. We are going to think out loud together, show every number as arithmetic, cite where each figure comes from, and after each step name the principle you can carry to the next company you study. The whole exercise is about learning a repeatable way to think — not about NIKE.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Ben speaking. Let us begin where the manual insists we begin: not with the price, but with the business — "a share of stock is a fractional ownership claim on a business." What would we be buying a piece of?

NIKE is the largest seller of athletic footwear and apparel in the world (Item 1, Business). It designs and markets footwear, apparel, equipment and accessories under three brands: the NIKE Brand (the Swoosh), the Jordan Brand (the Jumpman), and Converse. It reports in five segments — four NIKE Brand geographies (North America; Europe, Middle East & Africa; Greater China; Asia Pacific & Latin America) plus Converse standalone.

A few facts fix the shape of the enterprise (all from the FY2025 10-K, Item 1 and Item 7):

  • Revenue FY2025: $46,309 million across roughly 190 jurisdictions where the trademarks are registered.
  • Asset-light manufacturing. "Nearly all of our products are manufactured by independent contractors." NIKE owns almost no factories: 97 finished-goods footwear factories in 11 countries; 303 apparel factories in 34 countries. Footwear sourcing is concentrated in Vietnam (~51%), Indonesia (~28%), China (~17%). NIKE owns the brand and the design; others own the plants.
  • Channels. Two: NIKE Direct (owned stores + digital, ~42% of NIKE Brand revenue) and wholesale (~58%).
  • ~77,800 employees. The Swoosh and Jordan trademarks are described as "among our most valuable assets."

The circle-of-competence gate

Warren here. The manual's first filter is not "is it cheap?" but "Can I genuinely understand and forecast this business? If not, stop." (Checklist, item 1). So: do we understand NIKE?

Yes — and this is the honest kind of yes. NIKE sells sneakers and sweatshirts. You have worn them. You understand why a teenager pays $180 for a shoe with a Swoosh he could buy unbranded for $40. The product is inside any layman's circle of competence. The revenues are diversified across geographies, price points, and tens of thousands of SKUs — no single customer is 10% of sales.

But notice the trap the manual warns of: "A business just outside the circle — understood approximately but not truly — is where the expensive errors occur." The easy part is understanding what NIKE sells. The hard part — and the whole game here — is forecasting NIKE's normalized earning power through a turnaround. This year's reported earnings are depressed by a deliberate, self-inflicted cleanup. To value NIKE you must estimate what it earns in a normal year, not a trough year. That judgment is right at the edge of the circle, and we will treat it with the humility the edge deserves.

THE ONE QUESTION

Everything that follows serves a single question, and we want you to hold it in your mind the whole way through:

Is NIKE a temporarily-troubled great business — a wonderful brand in a self-made trough, worth buying while it is unloved — or a structurally-impaired one whose moat is quietly narrowing, a value trap dressed up as a bargain?

The stock at $44 is either a gift or a warning. The arithmetic cannot answer that alone; the business analysis must.

PRINCIPLE. Understand the business before you price it, and be honest about which part of it strains your circle of competence. For a cyclical or turnaround company, the competence you need is not "do I know the product?" but "can I estimate normalized earning power?"

  • Operating margin FY2025 (EBIT ≈ revenue − COGS − SG&A) = (46,309 − 26,519 − 16,088) ÷ 46,309 = 3,702 ÷ 46,309 = 8.0%. (NIKE's own segment table reports total EBIT of $3,778M, i.e. 8.2%, after other segment items — either way, single digits.)

Warren here — hold those numbers, because in the next section we will show you that an 8% operating margin is a trough, not a normal, for this business.

Two observations. First, this is a strong balance sheet: cash + short-term investments of 7,464 + 1,687 = $9,151 million against long-term debt of $7,961 million — NIKE is essentially net cash. Debt to operating cash flow = 7,961 ÷ 3,698 = 2.2×, comfortable. An independent research service rates Financial Strength B++. A company that will not be forced to sell — the manual's cardinal defense against permanent loss — has staying power to fund a turnaround.

Second, a footnote that would alarm a novice and shouldn't: retained earnings are negative — $(727) million (Statement of Shareholders' Equity). Did NIKE lose its accumulated profits? No. It has bought back so much stock that repurchases have driven retained earnings below zero. Over FY2023–FY2025 NIKE repurchased 5,480 + 4,250 + 2,985 = $12,715 million of its own shares — more than three years of net income. The negative retained earnings line is an artifact of capital return, not of losses. Which brings us to the manual's warning:

"Book value understates a great business and overstates a poor one — never anchor value to book."

NIKE's book equity is $13.2 billion. Its market capitalization is roughly $65 billion. The gap is the economic goodwill of the Swoosh — a brand worth vastly more than the tangible capital on the books. If you tried to value NIKE at book, you would conclude a global franchise is worth 5× its tangible net worth "too much." That is exactly the error the manual forbids. We will value the earning power, not the balance sheet.

The cash flow statement — articulation and earnings quality (Item 8)

Does profit become cash? Let us check FY2025:

  • Net income 3,219 → Cash from operations 3,698. Operating cash exceeded reported profit, which is healthy. The bridge: add back D&A of 775 and stock-based comp of 709, and note working capital was roughly neutral (inventories actually released $120M of cash as the cleanup drew stock down).
  • Capital expenditure (additions to PP&E) was just $430 million — strikingly low, and below depreciation of $775M. An asset-light brand does not need to spend much to stand still.
  • Free cash flow ≈ 3,698 − 430 = $3,268 million, even in a trough year.

Earnings-quality scan (the manual's red-flag list): no EBITDA-as-cash-flow games in the statements; the FY2024 restructuring charge of $443 million was disclosed plainly and did not recur in FY2025 (Note 18) — a one-timer that behaved like one, which is the honest kind. Stock-based comp of $709M is a real cost and is expensed. The critical audit matter was income taxes (routine for a global company). Auditor: PwC, clean opinion, relationship since 1974. Nothing here smells of concealment. The accounts appear trustworthy.

One thing to watch, not to fear: inventory. For an apparel company inventory is where trouble hides — unsold product becomes markdowns becomes gross-margin destruction. NIKE's inventory is flat at ~$7.5 billion, and the whole turnaround is about clearing it. We will return to this.

PRINCIPLE. Read the three statements as one interlocking system. Profit must become cash (articulation); ratios you compute by hand you truly own; and never anchor a branded franchise to book value — the economic goodwill lives off the balance sheet, in the earning power.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Warren here. This is the heart of the case. A trough year's EPS of $2.16 tells you almost nothing about what NIKE is worth, any more than a farmer's income in a drought tells you the value of his land. We must find normalized earning power — and to do that we must first establish that the high margins of the past were real and repeatable (a moat), not a fluke (a Roman candle).

The long trend: peak → trough

Here is the margin history, verified against the XBRL facts (FY2016–FY2025) and the 10-K income statements. Watch the arc.

Fiscal year Revenue $M Gross margin Operating margin (EBIT) Net margin Diluted EPS
2017 34,350 44.6% ~13% 12.3% $2.51
2019 39,117 44.7% ~12% 10.3% $2.49
2021 44,538 44.8% ~15% 12.9% $3.56
2022 (peak) 46,710 46.0% 14.3% 12.9% $3.75
2023 51,217 43.5% 11.6% 9.9% $3.23
2024 51,362 44.6% 12.3% 11.1% $3.73
2025 (trough) 46,309 42.7% 8.2% 7.0% $2.16

Read that arc. For most of a decade NIKE earned a gross margin of 44–46% and an operating margin of 12–15%. FY2022 was the peak: 46.0% gross, 14.3% operating, $6,046M net income. Then margins round-tripped down to the FY2025 trough of 42.7% gross and ~8% operating — net income nearly halved, from $6.0 billion to $3.2 billion.

The essential question: was the ~12–14% operating margin the real NIKE, temporarily lost, or was it a bubble that has now normalized lower? Two facts argue it was real and largely recoverable:

  1. The trough is substantially self-inflicted and explained. MD&A (Item 7) lays out the FY2025 gross-margin bridge almost to the basis point: lower average selling price (higher discounts + channel mix) cost ~180 bps; higher inventory-obsolescence reserves cost ~90 bps; Converse and FX another ~30 bps — partly offset by lower product costs (+80 bps). In plain English: NIKE is deliberately liquidating old inventory at a markdown and cutting the supply of its own best-selling classic sneakers to make room for new product. Management calls this repositioning NIKE Digital as a "full-price platform" and "reinvesting in wholesale." That is a choice that depresses margin now, not a permanent loss of pricing power.

  2. The freshest quarter shows the top line stabilizing. From the Q3 FY2026 10-Q (nine months ended Feb 28, 2026): revenue of $35,426M is essentially flat against $35,212M a year earlier, and Q3 revenue of $11,279M was flat versus $11,269M. The bleeding on the top line appears to have stopped even as the cleanup continues. (Nine-month net income is still down, $2,039M vs $3,008M — margin recovery lags revenue recovery, exactly as you'd expect.)

Owner earnings — the cash an owner can actually withdraw

Ben here. Reported earnings are an accounting construct. The manual insists we run the valuation on owner earnings:

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital

Let us split maintenance from growth capex, the manual's "hardest and most important term." NIKE's total capex has run $758M (FY2022), $969M (FY2023), $812M (FY2024), and just $430M (FY2025) against depreciation of ~$775–800M every year. Because the business is asset-light — it owns brand and design, not factories — maintenance capex is modest, roughly $500 million (below D&A). The rest, in good years, is growth capex (distribution centers, digital, retail).

Owner earnings on normalized profit (base case, derived below at ~$4,860M net income):

  • Normalized net income ≈ 4,860
    • Depreciation & amortization ≈ 780
  • − Maintenance capex ≈ 500
  • − Incremental working capital ≈ 150
  • = Owner earnings ≈ $4,990 million, or $3.37 per share on 1,481M shares.

Notice owner earnings exceed normalized net income — the signature of a capital-light franchise. NIKE converts profit into distributable cash better than a factory-heavy manufacturer ever could.

ROIC — how good are the economics?

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital; compare against the discount rate r.

Invested capital = equity + debt funding operations = 13,213 + 7,961 = $21,174 million.

  • Trough (FY2025): NOPAT ≈ EBIT 3,778 × (1 − 0.19 tax) = 3,060. ROIC = 3,060 ÷ 21,174 = 14.5%. Even in its worst year in a decade, NIKE earns ~14.5% on capital.
  • Peak (FY2022): invested capital ≈ 15,281 + 8,920 = 24,201; NOPAT at a normalized 19% tax ≈ 6,675 × 0.81 = 5,407. ROIC = 5,407 ÷ 24,201 = 22.3%. (an independent research service's own return-on-total-capital series shows the high-20s to low-30s in the boom years.)
  • Normalized: NOPAT ≈ 4,860 → ROIC = 4,860 ÷ 21,174 = 23.0%.

Set these against a discount rate of ~9% (justified below). NIKE earns roughly 14% on capital at the bottom and low-20s normalized — comfortably above its ~9% cost of capital. By the manual's central test — "value is created only when ROIC exceeds r" — NIKE is a genuine value-creator, not a value-consumer. This is a capital-light, high-ROIC franchise: "worth a high multiple of earnings."

Is the moat narrowing? Be skeptical and specific.

Warren here, and now we play the skeptic, because the manual warns that moats "erode" and that many dominant businesses are "Roman candles." NIKE's moat has always rested on four pillars — a globally dominant brand, unmatched scale, a marketing and athlete/endorsement ecosystem ($16.2 billion of endorsement commitments as of May 2025; $4,689M of demand-creation spend in FY2025, up 9%), and shelf space. Let us stress-test each honestly:

  • The DTC strategy reversal is an admitted misstep. For years NIKE pushed direct-to-consumer and pulled back from wholesalers. Under returning CEO Elliott Hill (rejoined October 2024), NIKE is reversing this — rebuilding relationships with Foot Locker and Dick's, re-seeding wholesale. Management is candid that the prior strategy cost the company shelf space and let competitors in. A moat you have to repair is a moat that was scratched.
  • Competition is real and rising. On (the Swiss running brand), Hoka (Deckers), New Balance, and lululemon have taken share, especially in performance running — a category NIKE once owned. The MD&A lists adidas, Anta, ASICS, Deckers, Li Ning, lululemon, New Balance, On, Puma, Under Armour and VF as competitors.
  • China is the deepest wound. Greater China revenue fell −13% to $6,586M in FY2025, with segment EBIT down −31% and gross margin down 420 bps. In Q3 FY2026 (10-Q), China sales fell a further ~7%, and management warned of a potential 20% top-line decline in the fiscal fourth quarter as local brands (Anta, Li Ning) press their advantage. China is where the "structural impairment" thesis has the most evidence.
  • Jordan Brand and classic franchises are being deliberately throttled. Jordan revenue fell −16% to $7,270M — but partly by design, as NIKE cuts the supply of its own classics to restore scarcity and pricing power. That is a moat-management action, not a moat failure — if it works.

Our honest read: the moat is real but narrower than it was, and its width is now an open question rather than a settled fact. The brand's pricing power is intact in North America (three consecutive quarters of growth by Q3 FY2026) and damaged in China. A moat you can still see but must argue for — that is precisely the manual's instruction: "durability must be argued, not assumed."

PRINCIPLE. For a business in a trough, normalize the earnings: establish from a long margin history what a normal year looks like, strip the self-inflicted and cyclical charges, and value that. A single high-ROIC year proves nothing; a decade of 12–15% margins interrupted by an explained, self-inflicted dip is evidence of a real, if tested, moat. And always argue the moat's durability against specific threats — here, China and the rise of On/Hoka — rather than assuming yesterday's dominance persists.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Ben speaking. We now judge management as we would a business partner, against the manual's five uses of a dollar and the one-dollar test.

NIKE's free cash flow in FY2025 (~$3.3 billion) went to two places:

  1. Dividends: $2,300 million. NIKE has raised its dividend for over two decades — the FY2025 rate was $1.57/share, up from $1.45 (FY2024) and $1.325 (FY2022). An independent research service shows dividends compounding ~12% over ten years. A growing, well-covered dividend from a strong balance sheet is the appropriate return of cash from a mature franchise whose reinvestment needs are modest — squarely what the manual endorses when "reinvestment opportunities earn less than shareholders could earn elsewhere."

  2. Buybacks: $2,985 million in FY2025 — but here we must apply the manual's discipline. "Repurchase shares — value-creating only when the stock is bought below intrinsic value... the price paid is everything."

Judging the buybacks on price

NIKE bought back $12.7 billion of stock across FY2023–FY2025. The uncomfortable truth: much of that was purchased at prices well above today's $44 — during FY2021–2022 NIKE's stock traded as high as $179 and averaged in the $100–150 range. Buying your own stock at $130 that now trades at $44 fails the one-dollar test — those dollars did not create a dollar of lasting value; they destroyed value. This is the single sharpest criticism we would level at prior management: they repurchased aggressively at the top of the cycle.

But watch what the current management is doing — and this is genuinely encouraging. From the Q3 FY2026 10-Q, nine-month buybacks were slashed to just $146 million (versus $2,786M in the prior nine months). Management has stopped buying back stock heavily during the trough — precisely when the balance sheet needs preserving and the strategy is unproven. Whether this is deliberate price discipline or simply cash conservation, the effect is right: they are no longer overpaying.

Candor

The manual prizes managers who "report mistakes as plainly as successes." On this count, Elliott Hill's team scores well. The MD&A openly states that the turnaround actions "have had, and in the future could have, a negative impact on our Revenues and gross margin" — management is telling shareholders the medicine will taste bad before it helps. Naming the DTC-versus-wholesale reversal as a correction of a prior strategy is an admission of error. That candor is worth something.

PRINCIPLE. Judge capital allocation on the price paid, not the act. Buybacks at a premium to intrinsic value destroy value no matter how "shareholder-friendly" they sound; the same buyback below intrinsic value creates it. And weigh management candor heavily — a team that names its own strategic mistake is more trustworthy than one that only reports good news.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Warren here. Now the arithmetic — and remember the manual's warning: the output is a range, not a point. We value NIKE the way a mature franchise in a trough deserves: on normalized earning power, cross-checked two ways — a normalized-earnings multiple and a two-stage discounted-owner-earnings model.

Step 1 — Normalized mid-cycle earnings (the whole ballgame)

We must pick a defensible mid-cycle operating margin, stated as a range, from the peak-to-trough history:

  • Peak (FY2022): 14.3% operating margin.
  • Decade norm: 12–15%.
  • Trough (FY2025): ~8.2%.
  • An independent research service's own 2029–31 estimate: 12.0% operating margin.

We will not assume a return to the 14%+ peak — that reflected a boom and a fuller China. We take a cautious mid-cycle band of 11%–14%, base case 12.5%, on a normalized revenue that recovers from the trough but stays below the old $51B peak:

Case Norm. revenue Op. margin EBIT ×(1−19% tax) = net ÷ 1,481M sh = EPS
Low $46,000M 11.0% 5,060 4,099 $2.77
Base $48,000M 12.5% 6,000 4,860 $3.28
High $50,000M 14.0% 7,000 5,670 $3.83

So normalized EPS is roughly $2.75–$3.85, base ~$3.25 — versus the reported trough EPS of $2.16 and an independent research service's FY2026 estimate of $1.50. This is the entire reframing: the honest earning power is 50–75% above the trough number the market is staring at.

Step 2 — Where does the multiple come from? (and reconcile)

A multiple is not pulled from air; it must be justified. Three anchors:

  1. NIKE's own history — with a haircut. an independent research service's 10-year median P/E is 32, and the stock averaged 25–35× in the boom. But — and this is critical — that multiple reflected 10%+ annual growth that no longer exists. an independent research service now projects long-run EPS growth of just 1%. A 32× multiple on a 10%-grower does not transfer to a 1–4% grower. Honesty requires a lower multiple applied to higher normalized EPS.
  2. Blue-chip consumer comps. From Selection & Opinion (June 2026): the Dow trades ~23× forward earnings; McDonald's — a comparable global consumer brand with a moat — trades 21.6×; the broad an independent research service universe median is 18.1×. The Shoe industry review names NIKE the industry leader and lone Dow-30 member but prints no peer P/E table, so we lean on these blue-chip anchors.
  3. A quality/growth-justified multiple. A high-ROIC (low-20s) franchise growing low-to-mid single digits with a 3.6% dividend justifies something in the 16–20× range — above the market median for quality, below the old boom multiple because growth has slowed.

Reconciled multiple: 16× (low) to 20× (high), base 18×. Applying it:

Low EPS $2.77 Base EPS $3.28 High EPS $3.83
16× $44 $52 $61
18× $50 $59 $69
20× $55 $66 $77

The normalized-multiple method centers around $50–$60 per share.

Step 3 — Two-stage DCF on normalized owner earnings

Now the manual's master equation, IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + terminal. Inputs:

  • Starting owner earnings: base ~$4,900 million (from the owner-earnings build above).
  • Discount rate r: the 10-year Treasury is 4.47% and the 30-year ~5.0% (Selection & Opinion, June 2026). NIKE's beta is 1.1. A cost of equity of long-rate + ~4.5% equity premium ≈ 9% is reasonable; we use 9% base, and stress 8%–10%.
  • Growth: recovery of 5%/yr for 10 years (turnaround plus modest real growth), then terminal 3%. We keep this deliberately modest — NIKE grows value only because ROIC (~20%) exceeds r (~9%), the manual's value-creating growth condition.

Sensitivity — intrinsic value per share (base OE = $4,900M, 5% growth for 10 years):

gT = 2.0% gT = 2.5% gT = 3.0% gT = 3.5%
r = 8.0% $71 $75 $80 $86
r = 8.5% $65 $68 $72 $77
r = 9.0% $60 $63 $66 $70
r = 9.5% $56 $58 $61 $64
r = 10.0% $52 $54 $56 $59

The DCF base case (r = 9%, gT = 3%) lands at ~$66. Flexing the owner-earnings input too: a low case (11% margin, r = 9.5%, gT = 2.5%) gives ~$43; a high case (14% margin, r = 8.5%, gT = 3.5%) gives ~$99. The DCF is more generous than the multiple method — which is the manual's whole point about the estimate's sensitivity: "a precise target price is an illusion."

Value-creating vs value-consuming growth

The manual: growth adds value only when ROIC > r. NIKE's normalized ROIC (~20–23%) sits far above its ~9% discount rate, so every dollar NIKE reinvests to grow is worth more than a dollar. This is why NIKE deserves a premium multiple and why its modest projected growth still counts for it, not against it. Contrast a low-ROIC retailer growing at the same rate — there growth would destroy value. Same growth rate, opposite verdict; ROIC is the hinge.

Triangulate — and be conservative

Warren here. The multiple method says ~$50–60; the DCF says ~$43 (low) to $66 (base) to ~$99 (high). Reconciling — and deliberately weighting the cautious end, because the turnaround is unproven and China is a real risk — we settle on:

  • Low (conservative): $42 — trough margins barely recover, China stays weak, higher discount rate.
  • Base: $54 — mid-cycle 12.5% margin, 18× / 9% DCF blend.
  • High: $68 — margins approach the old norm, turnaround succeeds, lower discount rate.

Sanity check. an independent research service's 18-month target midpoint is ~$40–56 and its 2029–31 target is $60–90 — our range brackets the near-term VL figure and sits below its bullish long-term target, which we are comfortable with (VL is more optimistic on the recovery than we choose to be). The live P/E of 21× on trough EPS looks expensive; on normalized $3.25 EPS the same $44 price is only 13.5× — cheap for this quality. That divergence is the case.

PRINCIPLE. Value a franchise in a trough on normalized earnings, and build the multiple from evidence — the company's own history (haircut for lost growth), quality comps, and a growth-justified band — then reconcile the multiple method against a DCF. When two methods disagree, that spread is information about your uncertainty; lean toward the conservative end and let the margin of safety, not false precision, carry the decision.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Ben speaking. We have a business worth, conservatively, $42–$68 per share, base ~$54. Mr. Market is offering it at $44.09. Now the manual's cornerstone:

Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value

  • Against base $54: (54 − 44.09) ÷ 54 = 18.4% margin of safety.
  • Against high $68: (68 − 44.09) ÷ 68 = 35.2%.
  • Against low $42: (42 − 44.09) ÷ 42 = −5.0% — at the conservative floor, the stock is slightly above fair value.

So the honest picture: at $44, NIKE offers a modest margin of safety against the base case (~18%) and a comfortable one against the optimistic case (~35%) — but essentially none against the pessimistic case where the turnaround stalls and China keeps bleeding. The manual is demanding: "if we calculate the value to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested." An 18% cushion is real but not the conspicuous discount Buffett insists on before acting in size. A price in the mid-$30s would open the wide margin the manual wants; at $44 the discount is thinner.

Mr. Market's mood — overdone or warranted?

Warren here. Is the pessimism at $44 overdone or earned? Both, honestly:

  • The pessimism looks partly overdone because the market is capitalizing trough earnings — pricing a self-inflicted, explained, and (on the latest quarter) stabilizing downturn as if it were permanent. The stock has fallen ~65% over five years and below $50 "for the first time in a decade" (an independent research service). Mr. Market is in one of his depressive moods, and the manual teaches that "a falling quote on a business whose value is intact is an improved buying opportunity."
  • The pessimism is partly warranted because the impairment is not purely cyclical. China is structurally harder, On and Hoka have permanently taken some running share, and the DTC misstep cost real ground. This is not a pure drought; some of the farm's soil has genuinely thinned.

That tension — is this a drought (buy) or thinning soil (beware) — is unresolved, and that is exactly why the margin of safety at $44 is merely adequate rather than compelling.

PRINCIPLE. The margin of safety is the whole defense against being wrong. Compute it against the conservative end of your range, not the base. When the cushion is thin (here ~18% base, negative at the floor), the disciplined answer is patience — wait for Mr. Market to offer a wider discount — not to reach.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Ben and Warren, together.

Business quality: GOOD — a genuinely wonderful brand whose moat has been scratched. NIKE is the world's leading athletic brand, capital-light, high-ROIC (low-20s normalized, ~14% even at the trough), net-cash, with a candid new-old CEO reversing a real strategic error. But the moat is narrower than it was — China is a structural wound and On/Hoka have taken share — so we stop short of "wonderful."

Verdict: GOOD business at a FAIR price. At $44 the stock is cheap on normalized earnings (~13.5× our $3.25 base EPS) and expensive on trough earnings (21× reported). The margin of safety is adequate against our base value (~18%) but not the conspicuous, act-in-size discount the manual demands. This is a "watch closely, and act decisively only if Mr. Market offers the mid-$30s or the turnaround visibly takes hold" situation — not a table-pounding bargain, not a value trap to avoid.

THE ONE QUESTION, answered

Temporarily-troubled great business, or structurally-impaired one? On the weight of evidence — a decade of 12–15% margins, an explained and self-inflicted trough, a stabilizing top line in the latest quarter, a fortress balance sheet, and ~14% ROIC at the very bottom — we lean toward temporarily-troubled, with genuine and unresolved structural erosion in China and running. It is mostly a drought, with some thinned soil. That "mostly" is why we say Good/Fair, not Wonderful/Buy.

Two or three open questions we cannot yet answer

  1. China: is the ~7–20% decline a cyclical trough or a permanent ceding of the market to Anta and Li Ning? This single question moves the base value by $10+ per share.
  2. The running franchise: can NIKE's 2027 product pivot win back share from On and Hoka, or has the performance-runner permanently diversified away from the Swoosh?
  3. Normalized margin: does NIKE settle at an independent research service's 12% or reclaim its old 14%+? The whole valuation pivots on this one assumption.

Two or three universal lessons

  1. Normalize through the trough. The single most important skill this case teaches: a cyclical or turnaround company's reported EPS is nearly useless for valuation. NIKE at 21× trough EPS looks expensive; at 13.5× normalized EPS it looks cheap. Same price, same day — the difference is entirely whether you value the drought year or the normal year. Always ask "what does this business earn in a normal year?" before you ask "what is the P/E?"
  2. Value trap vs. turnaround — the test is the moat and the balance sheet. A cheap stock with a deteriorating business and a weak balance sheet is a value trap; a cheap stock with a scratched-but-real moat and a fortress balance sheet that cannot be forced to sell is a turnaround candidate. NIKE's net-cash balance sheet and ~14% trough ROIC are what separate it from a true trap — it has the staying power to fund its own repair.
  3. The price paid is everything, in buybacks as in purchases. NIKE's own history is the lesson: $12.7B of buybacks at $100–150 destroyed value; the same discipline that should have stopped those buybacks is the discipline that should make you wait for a wider margin of safety before buying at $44.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (in order of appearance)

Intrinsic value. The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. Formula: IV = Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ. NIKE: our estimate $42–$68/share, base $54.

Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can understand well enough to forecast; its boundary matters more than its size. NIKE: the product is inside it; forecasting normalized turnaround earnings sits at its edge.

Mr. Market. Graham's manic-depressive partner who quotes a price daily driven by mood, not wisdom; "price is what you pay, value is what you get." NIKE: offering $44.09 in a depressive mood after a ~65% five-year fall.

Owner earnings. The cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Formula: Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. NIKE: ≈ 4,860 + 780 − 500 − 150 = $4,990M normalized (~$3.37/share).

Maintenance vs. growth capex. Maintenance capex preserves competitive position and unit volume; growth capex funds expansion. Accounting does not split them; the analyst must. NIKE: maintenance ≈ $500M (asset-light) of total capex that ran $430M–$969M.

Economic vs. accounting goodwill. Economic goodwill is a franchise's worth above its tangible capital; book value understates great businesses. NIKE: ~$65B market cap vs. $13.2B book equity — the gap is the Swoosh's economic goodwill. Never anchor to book.

Gross / operating / net margin. Gross profit, operating income (EBIT), and net income each ÷ revenue. NIKE FY2025: 42.7% / 8.2% / 7.0% (trough) vs. FY2022 peak 46.0% / 14.3% / 12.9%.

ROIC (return on invested capital). Formula: NOPAT ÷ (equity + debt funding operations). The central measure of quality; value is created only when ROIC > r. NIKE: 3,060 ÷ 21,174 = 14.5% trough; ~23% normalized; vs. r ≈ 9%.

NOPAT. After-tax operating profit = EBIT × (1 − tax rate). NIKE FY2025: 3,778 × (1 − 0.19) = $3,060M.

Moat / economic franchise. A durable competitive advantage (brand, scale, network, low cost) whose signature is pricing power; moats erode and must be argued. NIKE: brand + scale + $16.2B endorsement ecosystem; narrowed by China and On/Hoka.

Discount rate (r). The owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long-term rates plus a premium for uncertainty; "interest rates are gravity" on asset values. NIKE: ~9% (10-yr Treasury 4.47% + ~4.5% premium; beta 1.1).

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth. Growth adds value only when ROIC > r; below r it destroys value. Formula: g = reinvestment rate × ROIC. NIKE: ROIC ~20% >> r ~9%, so growth creates value.

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it produces ≥ $1 of market value (incremental ROIC ≥ r); otherwise distribute it. NIKE: FY2021–22 buybacks at $100–150 failed it; dividend and reduced current buybacks pass it.

Two-stage DCF. Formula: IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. NIKE: base OE $4,900M, 5% for 10 yrs, terminal 3%, r 9% → ~$66/share; sensitivity $52–$86 across reasonable r and g.

Margin of safety. Formula: (Intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value; the cushion against error — the cornerstone. NIKE: (54 − 44.09) ÷ 54 = 18.4% vs. base; negative vs. the $42 floor.

Risk. The probability of permanent loss of capital — not price volatility. NIKE: mitigated by a net-cash balance sheet (won't be forced to sell); elevated by the unresolved China/competition impairment.
