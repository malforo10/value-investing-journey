A Value-Investing Seminar: Intuitive Surgical, Inc. (ISRG)

In the Graham–Buffett tradition— a teaching case, not a recommendation

Date: July 4, 2026 · Ticker: ISRG (Nasdaq) · Live price: $426.01 · Market cap: ~$151–167 billion

A quick word before we begin, from the two of us. We are going to spend this session on one of the finest businesses in the world — a razor-and-blade robotic-surgery franchise with a fortress balance sheet, returns most companies can only dream of, and a moat you can see from orbit. And we are going to conclude, with some reluctance, that at today's price it is not something we would buy. Hold both of those thoughts. Learning to admire a business and decline the stock in the same breath is most of the discipline. — W.B. & B.G.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What it is. Intuitive Surgical sells the da Vinci surgical robot and, increasingly, the Ion robotic lung-biopsy platform. But do not think of it as a machine company. Read the revenue and the business declares itself (10-K, Item 7, "Recurring Revenue"):

FY2025 revenue ($M) Amount Share
Instruments & accessories 6,018.9 60%
Service 1,572.1 16%
Operating-lease revenue 874.3 9%
Total recurring 8,465.3 84%
Systems (up-front) ~1,599 16%
Total revenue 10,064.7 100%

Eighty-four cents of every revenue dollar is recurring — instruments that "expire or wear out as they are used in surgery" (Item 7), plus multi-year service contracts, plus lease payments. This is the razor-and-blade model in its purest surgical form. The company earns "between $900 and $3,700 of instruments and accessories revenue per surgical procedure" (Item 7). The system is the razor; every one of the ~3,153,000 da Vinci procedures performed in 2025 (up 18% year over year) pulls blades.

The installed base is the annuity. As of March 31, 2026: ~11,395 da Vinci systems and ~1,041 Ion systems installed, da Vinci up 12% year over year (10-Q). Each system is a $0.7–$3.1 million capital sale that then meters recurring revenue for a decade. Utilization rose 3% in 2025 — the base is not just growing, it is being used harder.

The customers are hospitals and surgeons; the demand is driven by "patient value = procedure efficacy ÷ invasiveness" (Item 7) — less trauma, faster recovery. Revenue is 64% U.S., 36% outside the U.S. (Q1 2026), so there is a long international runway.

The circle-of-competence gate (Manual Ch. 4). Can we forecast this business? Mostly, yes — and that itself is unusual for a device maker. The recurring 84% is tied to a procedure count that has compounded for two decades and to an installed base that cannot be quickly displaced. We can forecast the shape of the next five to seven years with more confidence than for almost any other high-growth name. What we cannot forecast precisely is the pace of the newest competitive threats and how far international and general-surgery adoption ultimately runs. So the business is inside the circle for its economics and partly inside for its terminal growth — which, as you will see, is exactly where the whole argument lives.

THE ONE QUESTION. For ISRG the question is never "is this a good business?" — it plainly is. The one question is Chapter 5's tension made concrete: Is a genuinely wonderful business worth this price? Everything below is built to answer it, and the honest answer will turn on how much of a 40-times-plus multiple the growth can actually justify.

Principle. When a business is obviously wonderful, the analyst's job shifts entirely from quality to price. The temptation is to let the quality do the deciding. It cannot. "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price" (Manual Ch. 5) — note the words fair price. Quality earns a fair price, never any price.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The balance sheet — a fortress with a war chest (10-K, Consolidated Balance Sheets, 12/31/2025):

  • Cash & equivalents $3,368.0M + short-term investments $2,566.9M + long-term investments $3,099.2M = $9,034M of cash and marketable securities. That is ~$25 per diluted share of the $426 price sitting in liquid instruments.
  • Total debt: none. an independent research service: "Total Debt None." The only fixed obligations are $44.5M of uncapitalized leases — a rounding error.
  • Inventory $1,840.0M (razor-blade stock, up from $1,487M — a deliberate build), PP&E net $5,342.4M.
  • Total assets $20,458.7M; total liabilities only $2,517.0M; equity $17,824.0M.
  • Current ratio = current assets $9,779.5M ÷ current liabilities $2,006.2M = 4.9×. Graham would have smiled: this is the kind of liquidity that lets a business laugh at a shut credit market.

Do not anchor to book (Manual Ch. 7). Equity is $17.8B; the market pays ~$151–167B. Book value understates this franchise by an order of magnitude — the economic goodwill of a 20-year installed base, surgeon training, and switching costs never appears on the balance sheet. Book is a floor of tangible capital, not a measure of the business.

The income statement — margins and trend (10-K, Statements of Income):

Revenue compounded ~19% a year over this stretch; operating income grew faster still as the margin widened. Gross margin dipped slightly in 2025 — the 10-K attributes ~$63M of added cost of revenue to tariffs, plus da Vinci 5 launch and mix. Nothing broken; a wonderful margin structure holding up under a launch year.

Cash flow vs. net income (10-K, Statements of Cash Flows, FY2025):

  • Operating cash flow $3,030.5M vs. net income $2,876.6M — OCF exceeds NI, a mark of high earnings quality (depreciation and SBC are non-cash add-backs, only partly offset by a large inventory build of −$1,063.4M for razor-blade stocking and da Vinci 5).
  • Capital expenditure $539.8M in 2025 — but note this is abnormally low: 2024 was $1,111.2M and 2023 $1,064.2M, reflecting a manufacturing build-out that has now eased. Depreciation was $614.7M. So a normalized maintenance-capex figure is roughly depreciation, ~$615M, not the $540M reported this year.
  • FCF (as reported) = $3,030.5M − $539.8M = $2,491M; normalized against ~$615M maintenance capex, ~$2,416M.

The three statements articulate. Rich margins on the income statement convert to more than their reported profit in cash (OCF > NI), because a genuinely capital-light franchise throws off cash; the balance sheet then swells with that cash because management retains far more than it can reinvest. That is the fingerprint of a See's-type business (Manual Ch. 6): high returns, low capital intensity, cash piling up.

Earnings-quality scan — and the one honest asterisk: stock-based compensation. SBC in 2025 was $788.2M (10-K cash-flow statement; Item 7 note 1: "non-cash charges of $1.43 billion, consisting primarily of share-based compensation of $788 million and depreciation... of $615 million"). Two things must be said plainly:

  1. SBC is a real cost. The manual is emphatic — "stock options treated as free" is a red flag (Ch. 7). $788M is roughly 28% of net income. It is compensation the company chose to pay in ownership instead of cash, and it dilutes every existing owner. We will not add it back to owner earnings, and we treat an independent research service's non-GAAP $8.93 EPS (which adds back ~$1.06 of items) with suspicion for exactly this reason. GAAP $7.87 is the more honest anchor.
  2. But the buyback offsets it. In 2025 the company repurchased $2,295M of stock (4.8M shares) while issuing ~4.0M shares through employee plans (net of settlement). Diluted share count was 362.7M in 2025 vs. 362.0M in 2024 — essentially flat. So the dilution from SBC is being paid for out of real cash, which is honest, if expensive. We will judge that trade in Management as Partners.

No EBITDA games, no serial "restructuring" charges, clean PwC audit (auditor since 2014), one sensible critical-audit-matter (allocating multi-element system revenue). Disclosure is detailed and candid. Earnings quality: high, with SBC as the single real cost to keep honest.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The long earning-power record (XBRL facts + an independent research service, split-adjusted):

Revenue ($M) 2,704 4,479 6,222 7,124 8,352 10,065
Operating income ($M) 950 1,375 1,577 1,767 2,349 2,946
Net income parent ($M) — — 1,322 1,798 2,323 2,856

An independent research service's per-share history shows EPS of $2.08 (2016) → $3.85 (2019) → $5.03 (2023) → $7.87 (2025 GAAP) — roughly a 15.5% annualized earnings growth over ten years (VL "Annual Rates"). This is a durable, compounding earning-power record, not a one-year windfall. The 2020–2022 wobble (COVID delaying elective surgery, then a soft 2022) is visible and instructive: procedures are somewhat deferrable, but they came back. We normalize around the trend, not off the 2025 peak.

Owner earnings (Manual Ch. 7): Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital, with SBC left in as a cost.

  • Net income (parent): $2,856M
    • Depreciation $614.7M + amortization $62.4M = +$677M non-cash (SBC deliberately not added back)
  • − Maintenance capex ≈ depreciation ≈ $615M
  • − Normalized incremental working capital (the recurring portion of inventory/receivable growth needed to support volume) ≈ $300M (the 2025 inventory build of $1,063M is largely growth stocking; we take a conservative recurring slice)
  • Owner earnings ≈ $2,900M ≈ $8.00 per diluted share.

That is close to GAAP EPS, which makes sense: for a capital-light franchise where maintenance capex ≈ depreciation, owner earnings and reported earnings converge. We will anchor the valuation on ~$2.9B / ~$8.00 per share of normalized owner earnings.

ROIC — the signature of the moat (Manual Ch. 6, ROIC = NOPAT ÷ invested capital):

  • NOPAT = operating income $2,945.5M × (1 − 13.1% effective tax) = $2,559M.
  • The effective tax rate is unusually low (13.1%) because of $246M of excess tax benefits on employee equity — a recurring but stock-price-dependent item. On a normalized ~21% rate NOPAT would be ~$2,327M; we flag the tax tailwind.
  • Invested capital is where the two-column logic matters. On total equity ($17,824M, which includes $9,034M of idle cash and securities), ROIC ≈ 14.4% — respectable but not spectacular, because a third of the equity is cash earning bond yields. Strip the cash and measure the operating business: invested capital ≈ $17,824M − $9,034M = $8,790M, and operating ROIC ≈ $2,559M ÷ $8,790M = ~29%.

~29% return on the capital actually employed in the business, against a ~9% cost of capital. That is the number that matters (Manual Ch. 6). The franchise is capital-light and high-return; the reported 14–16% ROE (an independent research service: 17.5% est.) is dragged down by the cash hoard, not by weak economics. This is precisely the case the two-column method (Ch. 8) was built for: value the ~29%-ROIC operating engine separately from the $25/share of cash.

The moat, specifically:
- Installed base + switching costs. 11,395 systems, each a $1–3M sunk investment plus surgeon retraining. A hospital that has standardized on da Vinci, trained its surgeons, and stocked its instruments does not casually switch platforms.
- Recurring razor-blade revenue. 84% recurring; instruments are consumed every procedure and are proprietary to the system.
- Regulatory + clinical barriers. Every indication requires FDA/EU clearance backed by clinical data. The 10-K lists dozens of clearances built up over years — an accumulated regulatory estate a competitor must replicate one procedure at a time.
- Surgeon training / network. Intuitive Learning, SimNow, proctoring — the company owns the training pipeline that produces da Vinci-fluent surgeons.
- Data + scale. Connected systems feed Case Insights; scale funds $1.31B of annual R&D (13% of revenue) that a subscale rival cannot match.

This is a textbook economic franchise (Manual Ch. 5): needed, no close substitute for the trained surgeon, and largely price-un-regulated in the U.S. Pricing power is real — the diagnostic test of a moat.

What could erode it in ten years — argue durability, don't assume it (Manual Ch. 5). This is the crux, and it is no longer hypothetical (live web check, 2026):
- Medtronic Hugo filed 510(k)s in June 2026 to expand from urology into general and gynecologic surgery — the two largest da Vinci categories.
- J&J Ottava was submitted to the FDA (January 2026) and met its pivotal-study endpoints.
- Others (CMR Versius, Stryker/Mako in ortho) round out a field that, as one industry source put it, "in 2026 finally looks like a market, not a monopoly."
- China provincial price caps on robotic surgery are already pressuring instrument pricing and procedure counts (10-K, Item 7).

None of these has yet dented da Vinci's economics, and the installed base + switching costs give Intuitive years of defense. But the terminal-growth assumption — the single largest driver of value, as we will see — is exactly what a credible second and third entrant threatens. The moat is wide; the question is whether it is widening or slowly narrowing, and honest analysts will disagree.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Run the five questions of Manual Ch. 8, with the company's own numbers:

  • Leverage. Total debt = $0. Net debt = $0 − $9,034M cash/investments = negative $9.0 billion. Net debt / owner earnings is not just low, it is negative. There is nothing to extinguish.
  • Coverage. Interest coverage (EBIT ÷ interest expense) is undefined in the useful direction — the company is a net receiver of interest: "Interest and other income, net" was +$365.9M in 2025. It earns money on its cash; it pays essentially none.
  • Liquidity. Current ratio 4.9× (above). Graham's precondition — ample working capital, sound current ratio — is met many times over.
  • Refinancing / maturities. None. There is no debt to roll, so the business "controls its own fate" (Ch. 8) absolutely. It can never be a forced seller.
  • Hidden / off-balance-sheet obligations. $44.5M uncapitalized leases; no defined-benefit pension; a consolidated Fosun joint venture with a $117.7M noncontrolling interest (immaterial). Nothing lurking.

One-line verdict: This is one of the strongest balance sheets in corporate America — a genuine fortress. Solvency is a non-issue; the $9B of net cash is itself a valuation asset, not just a cushion. Financial Strength A+ (an independent research service). The manual's whole concern — surviving a bad year without a forced sale — is answered before it is asked.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Capital allocation (Manual Ch. 9), across the six uses of a dollar:

  1. Reinvest in the business. $1.31B of R&D (13% of revenue) and heavy capex through 2024 to build manufacturing. Given ~29% operating ROIC, reinvestment easily clears the one-dollar test — every retained dollar spent inside the franchise creates well more than a dollar (incremental ROIC ≫ r). This is textbook good growth (Ch. 11).
  2. Acquisitions. Minimal ($14M in 2025) — no empire-building, no institutional-imperative overreach. Good.
  3. Pay down debt. N/A — there is none.
  4. Dividends. None. Defensible only if reinvestment beats what shareholders could earn elsewhere — and at ~29% incremental ROIC, it does. No dividend is the right call here, not a red flag.
  5. Buybacks. $2,295M in 2025 (and $2,607M in 2022, $416M in 2023). The manual's rule: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value (Ch. 9). At 40–50× earnings, ISRG is buying back stock that is arguably at or above our intrinsic-value estimate — so these repurchases are, at best, a wash and possibly value-neutral-to-destructive on a pure IV basis. They function mainly to offset SBC dilution rather than to concentrate ownership cheaply. This is the one place where a superb management is doing something the manual would question — not out of empire-building, but out of the near-universal habit of buying one's own richly priced shares to mop up option dilution.
  6. Share issuance. SBC issues ~4M shares/year; buybacks retire a similar amount. Net diluted share count: 357.4M (2023) → 362.0M (2024) → 362.7M (2025) — essentially flat over a decade once you adjust for the 3-for-1 splits. Owners are not being quietly diluted; they are being held roughly whole, at the cost of ~$2.3B of cash a year.

The one-dollar test: retained earnings inside the operating business pass comfortably (incremental ROIC ~29% ≫ r ~9%). Retained earnings parked in the cash pile earn only bond yields — which is why that cash is a drag on blended ROE and arguably should, at some point, be returned. Management has chosen to keep the powder dry.

Candor. The 10-K is detailed and forthright: it quantifies tariffs ($63M), names competitive and China-pricing risks explicitly, and discloses the tax-benefit tailwind. Chairman Gary Guthart (long-tenured) and CEO David Rosa; officers/directors own <1% (an independent research service). Verdict: rational, candid, owner-aligned capital allocators — with the honest caveat that buying back stock at 40–50× earnings is the least defensible line item.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Anchor (Manual Ch. 10): normalized owner earnings ≈ $2,900M ≈ $8.00 per diluted share (362.7M shares), plus $9,034M ≈ $25/share of net cash and investments valued separately (two-column method, Ch. 8 — the operating engine and the cash portfolio are different animals and must be priced on their own terms).

Because ISRG is a durable, high-ROIC grower, it deserves a growth-based method — a two-stage DCF — not a no-growth capitalization. We discount the operating owner-earnings stream, then add the net cash.

The two-stage DCF (operating value), r = 9%

Discount rate r = 9% — long bond ~4.3% plus a premium for the forecast uncertainty around competition and terminal growth (Manual Ch. 12: r is gravity). Ten explicit years, then a terminal perpetuity.

Scenario Stage-1 growth (10 yr) Terminal g Operating IV/sh + Net cash IV/share
Low 8% 2.5% ~$191 $25 ~$216
Base 10% 3.0% ~$234 $25 ~$259
High 13% 3.5% ~$314 $25 ~$339

(Owner earnings ~$8.00/sh growing at stage-1 rate for 10 years, terminal value on year-10 OE at (r−g), all discounted at 9%, plus $25/sh cash.)

Sensitivity — watch the hypersensitivity (Manual Ch. 10, 13)

Vary r and terminal g (stage-1 fixed at 10%), IV per share including net cash:

8% $277 $311 $363

Move r by one point or g by one point and the answer swings ~$40–70 a share. The estimate is a range, not a point — and the entire range sits below the $426 price.

Where the multiples come from, and reconciling them

  • Historical median P/E (an independent research service): median P/E of 58 (recent years, on a rich franchise), and avg annual P/E estimated at 45.0 for 2029–31. On our GAAP owner earnings of $8.00, the base IV of $259 implies ~32× owner earnings; the high case ~42×. So even our generous case sits at or below ISRG's own historical multiple — the market's historical willingness to pay 45–58× is richer than a first-principles DCF supports.
  • Quality-justified multiple, from first principles: a business growing owner earnings ~10% for a decade then fading to 3%, at 29% operating ROIC and 9% cost of capital, is worth roughly 28–35× those owner earnings. That is where the base case's ~32× comes from — reasoned, not borrowed.
  • Peer multiple (named): medtech peers — Medtronic, Boston Scientific, Stryker — trade at far lower multiples (mid-teens to ~30× at most for the fastest growers). ISRG's premium is deserved for its superior growth and returns, but 45–58× is at the extreme end of what any peer commands. Reconciling: the DCF and the quality-justified multiple agree at ~30–35×; the market and historical multiples are higher, which tells us the market is pricing terminal growth we are not willing to underwrite.

The three cross-checks (Manual Ch. 14) — as arithmetic

1. No-growth floor (checks the growth assumption). Value the operating business as if it never grows again: $2,900M ÷ 0.09 = $32,222M ≈ $89/share of operating value, + $25 cash = ~$114/share. At a $426 price, the no-growth floor supports only $114 — meaning ~$312/share, or 73% of the price, is a pure bet on growth. Three-quarters of what you pay today is a promise about the future. That is the single most important number in this seminar.

2. Implied multiple (checks the model's output). Base IV $259 ÷ owner earnings $8.00 = ~32× owner earnings. Interrogate it: is 32× defensible for a 29%-ROIC franchise growing ~10% then 3%? Yes — comfortably. But the market's $426 ÷ $8.00 = ~53× owner earnings (and ~48× on an independent research service's adjusted $8.93). That multiple requires either much faster growth for much longer, or a lower discount rate, than we are willing to assume.

3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond (checks the anchor to rates). At $426, owner-earnings yield = $8.00 ÷ $426 = 1.9% — versus a ~4.3% long bond. Even stripping out the $25 of cash (operating price ~$401), the operating yield is ~2.0%. The coupon grows ~10%, so in time it overtakes the bond — but you are starting more than two points below the risk-free rate and paying entirely for future growth. Contrast the manual's Sample Co., where even the rich case yielded 3.7%. ISRG at today's price is a starker growth bet than that.

Triangulate

  • Low: ~$216 · Base: ~$259 · High: ~$339 per share.
  • Sanity vs. An independent research service targets: an independent research service's 18-month target is $797 and its 2029–31 target $795 — but those rest on paying ~45× 2029–31 estimated EPS of $14.70, i.e., on the multiple staying at historical-premium levels. Our DCF deliberately does not assume the multiple persists; it asks what the cash stream is worth. The gap between our ~$259 base and an independent research service's ~$795 target is the price-vs-quality tension, quantified.
  • Sanity vs. live multiple: at $426 the stock trades at ~48× adjusted / ~53× GAAP owner earnings — above even our high case's implied multiple.

Conclusion of the valuation: a defensible intrinsic-value range of roughly $215–$340 per share, base ~$260. The live price of $426 sits above the entire range.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Set price beside value (Manual Ch. 13):

Value/price
IV low $216
IV base $259
IV high $339
Live price $426
  • Discount/(premium) to base: ($259 − $426) ÷ $259 = −64%. The price is 64% above our central estimate.
  • Even against the high case ($339): premium of ($426 − $339) ÷ $339 = +26%. The market is paying a premium to our most generous defensible value.
  • Margin of safety = negative. There is none. The manual's formula, (IV − Price)/IV, returns a negative number at every point in our range.

Is part of the safety in the quality? The manual concedes (Ch. 13, "Paying up for quality") that in a durable high-ROIC franchise, part of the protection lives in the quality itself — a conservatively projected, growing earnings stream makes today's estimate the floor of tomorrow's. That argues for paying a fair price, and it means ISRG is far less dangerous as a business than a cigar-butt at the same headline discount. But — and the manual is explicit — "quality justifies a fair price, not an assumption that quality is infinite." A 64% premium to base value, with 73% of the price resting on growth, is not a fair price; it is the market pricing the quality as if it were infinite.

Is Mr. Market euphoric? Somewhat. The stock rose 5.9% in a single session (July 2, 2026) merely positioning ahead of an earnings print — that is mood, not value (Manual Ch. 2: "It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful"). An independent research service rates Timeliness a middling 3 and notes the stock "has long traded at a premium valuation, which tends to keep those with a more conservative investment style on the sidelines." Mr. Market is offering to sell us a wonderful business at an exuberant price. Chapter 2's discipline: we value independently, then consult him — and today he is not foolish in our favor.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: WONDERFUL — a razor-and-blade robotic-surgery franchise with ~84% recurring revenue, ~29% operating ROIC, a fortress net-cash balance sheet, a wide and (for now) durable moat, and rational, candid management. On business quality alone, this is a top-decile enterprise.

The investment call at $426: AVOID / DO NOT BUY at today's price — not because anything is wrong with the company, but because the price sits ~64% above our base intrinsic value and above even our high case. There is no margin of safety; there is a margin of danger.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION — is a wonderful business worth this price? No. The business is worth roughly $215–$340 a share on defensible assumptions; the market asks $426. Wonderful, yes. Worth $426, no. If Mr. Market ever offers it in the low-$200s or below — a ~40%+ decline — the quality argument and a fair price would finally line up, and we would look hard. Until then we admire and wait (Manual Ch. 13, 16).

Two or three open questions to keep watching:
1. Does the moat widen or narrow? Medtronic Hugo (expanding into general/gyn surgery, 2026) and J&J Ottava (FDA-submitted, 2026) are now real. If they take even modest share, the terminal growth that justifies most of the price is at risk.
2. How long can ~10% owner-earnings growth run? U.S. urology/gyn is maturing; the bull case rests on international, general surgery, and Ion. Watch procedure growth (18% in 2025) and utilization for the first sign of deceleration.
3. What does management do with $9B of net cash — reinvest at 29%, keep buying back stock at 50×, or eventually return it? The answer changes the ROE story.

Two or three universal lessons:
- A great business can be a poor investment at the wrong price. This is the whole seminar. Quality does not repeal the margin of safety; it only tells you where to look. Price still decides the purchase (Manual Ch. 5, 13).
- Separate the cash from the operating engine. Blended ROE (14–16%) understated how good this business is; the operating ROIC (~29%) revealed it. Where a large portfolio sits inside an operating company, use the two-column method or you will misprice both (Ch. 8).
- The no-growth floor tells you how much of a price is a promise. When 73% of the price is a growth bet, you are not buying a business — you are buying a forecast. Know that before you act (Ch. 14).

Teaching-case disclaimer. This seminar is a teaching exercise in method, not investment advice. The numbers are drawn from the cited filings and an independent research service as of mid-2026 and will change; owner earnings, growth, and discount rates are estimates; reasonable analysts will land on different ranges. Do your own work and mind your own circle of competence.

Post-purchase hold/sell discipline (Manual Ch. 16), for a hypothetical owner who bought lower:

GLOSSARY (definition · formula · ISRG's numbers)

Intrinsic value. The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life; a per-share quantity. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. ISRG: ~$215–$340/sh (base ~$259), operating DCF + $25/sh net cash.

Owner earnings. Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital; the cash an owner can withdraw. ISRG: $2,856M NI + $677M D&A − $615M maint capex − ~$300M WC ≈ $2,900M ≈ $8.00/sh (SBC left in as a real cost).

Maintenance capex. Capital spending to preserve competitive position/unit volume, vs. growth capex. ISRG: ~$615M (≈ depreciation); reported 2025 capex of $540M is abnormally low after the 2024 build-out ($1,111M).

ROIC. NOPAT ÷ invested capital; the measure of business quality; create value only when ROIC > r. ISRG: NOPAT $2,559M ÷ operating capital $8,790M ≈ 29% (14% on total equity, dragged by the cash pile).

Discount rate (r). Opportunity cost of capital, anchored to long rates plus an uncertainty premium; gravity on asset values. ISRG: 9% (≈4.3% bond + ~4.7% premium for competitive/terminal uncertainty).

No-growth (earning-power) value. Normalized OE ÷ r — the value if growth stopped; the floor, and the gauge of how much of a price is a growth bet. ISRG: $2,900M ÷ 0.09 = ~$89/sh operating + $25 cash = ~$114/sh; so 73% of the $426 price is a growth bet.

Two-column method. Per-share investments at market + separately capitalized operating earnings; for companies holding large securities portfolios. ISRG: $25/sh cash & investments valued at face + the ~29%-ROIC operating engine valued by DCF.

Margin of safety. (IV − Price)/IV; the discount of price to conservative IV; the cornerstone. ISRG: negative — price $426 vs base IV $259 = −64% (a premium, not a discount).

Moat / economic franchise. A durable competitive advantage whose signature is pricing power; needed, no close substitute, price-unregulated. ISRG: installed base (11,395 systems), switching costs, 84% recurring instruments, regulatory/clinical barriers, surgeon-training network.

Recurring revenue. Revenue from instruments & accessories, service, and operating leases — the razor-blade annuity. ISRG: $8,465M = 84% of 2025 revenue.

Financial strength / solvency. The balance sheet's capacity to survive a bad year without a forced sale. ISRG: zero debt, $9,034M net cash, current ratio 4.9×, A+ — a fortress; net debt is negative.

Stock-based compensation (SBC). Non-cash pay in equity; a real cost that dilutes owners — never "free." ISRG: $788M (2025), ~28% of net income; offset in share count by ~$2.3B of buybacks, so diluted shares ~flat.

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r (≥ $1 of value created per $1 retained). ISRG: passes for operating reinvestment (~29% ≫ 9%); the cash pile earns only bond yields and is the weak point.

Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest expense. ISRG: not meaningful — the company is a net earner of interest (+$365.9M "interest and other income, net," 2025), with no debt to cover.

Mr. Market. Graham's manic-depressive partner who quotes a price by his mood, there to serve not guide. ISRG: currently exuberant — +5.9% in one session on positioning; offering a wonderful business at ~64% above base value.

Risk. The probability of permanent loss of capital — not volatility/beta. ISRG: business risk is low (fortress, moat); the real risk here is overpaying — buying a superb business at a price that already discounts a decade of heroics.
