COSTCO WHOLESALE — Value Investing Seminar

A Teaching Case in the Graham–Buffett Method

Date: 2026-07-12 · Ticker: COST · Live price: $916.25 (equity_atlas.xlsx, prices as of July 12, 2026)

This is my own valuation of the business, worked end to end in the Graham–Buffett tradition — Graham's discipline on price and the statements, Buffett's eye for the business. It is not a buy or sell recommendation; it is me showing the repeatable way of thinking, applied to a real company.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Costco is not a supermarket with a membership card stapled on. It is a membership warehouse club: members pay an annual fee for the right to shop a deliberately limited assortment — fewer than 4,000 active SKUs per warehouse in the core business — of high-quality national brands and Costco’s private-label Kirkland Signature, sold in bulk, in no-frills buildings, at prices that competitors struggle to match (10-K, Item 1).

At fiscal year-end August 31, 2025 the company operated 914 warehouses worldwide (629 U.S., 110 Canada, 42 Mexico, and the rest across Japan, the U.K., Korea, Australia, Taiwan, China, Spain, France, Sweden, Iceland, and New Zealand). By May 10, 2026 that count had risen to 928 (10-Q). Paid memberships stood at 81.0 million at FY2025 year-end and 82.9 million by Q3 FY2026, with 145–148 million total cardholders including household cards (10-K Item 1; 10-Q). The U.S./Canada renewal rate was 92.3% at FY2025 year-end — a trailing measure that captures renewals seven to eighteen months prior (10-K Item 7).

How the money is made — two engines, one flywheel.

  1. Merchandise sales (FY2025 net sales $269.9 billion): foods and sundries ~41% of the mix historically, gasoline ~10%, fresh foods, non-foods, pharmacy, optical, e-commerce (~7% of sales), and travel. Merchandise is deliberately run at thin gross margins — 11.12% of net sales in FY2025 (10-K Item 7) — because volume and rapid inventory turns, combined with buying direct and routing through depots, produce the low prices that make the membership worth paying for.
  2. Membership fees (FY2025 $5.32 billion, up 10%): Gold Star and Business memberships at $65 in the U.S., with Executive upgrades (another $65) that earn a 2% reward. Executive members drove ~73.6% of worldwide net sales in 2025 (10-K Item 1). Fee revenue is almost pure profit once the base is built, and it is recognized ratably over the membership year.

The flywheel is simple and powerful: low prices → more members and more trips → more volume → more buying power and lower unit costs → still lower prices → higher renewal rates and more Executive upgrades → more fee income. That is the castle. The moat is the low-cost position reinforced by the membership model, the brand, and the scale that no new entrant can easily replicate.

Segments. The company reports three geographic segments — United States, Canada, Other International — with the same operating model in each (10-K Note 11). U.S. total revenue was $200.0 billion of the $275.2 billion consolidated total in FY2025; Canada $36.9 billion; Other International $38.3 billion. Operating income was $6.88B / $1.85B / $1.66B respectively.

The one question that decides this case.

Costco is almost certainly a wonderful business. The question is not quality. The question is:

At roughly $916 a share — nearly 50× trailing earnings and well above the company’s own historical median multiple — does price leave any margin of safety for a fallible analyst, or has Mr. Market already capitalized every good thing we can see and then some?

Circle of competence gate (manual ch. 4). Can we forecast Costco’s economics a decade out? Yes — with humility about the rate, not about the model. The membership warehouse format has been stable for forty years. Renewal rates, fee income, and the low-cost flywheel are understandable. We are not forecasting a biotech pipeline or a fashion cycle. Costco sits inside the circle for a generalist business owner. That does not mean the stock is cheap; it means we are allowed to value it.

PRINCIPLE: A stock is a piece of a business, and you only value what you can understand. “Too hard” is a complete answer — but Costco is not too hard. The hard part will be the price.

Liquidity. Current ratio = 38,380 ÷ 37,108 = 1.03×. That looks thin by industrial standards, but for Costco it is structural and favorable: the company turns inventory so fast that it often sells goods before it pays suppliers, and deferred membership fees are cash already collected. Cash + short-term investments were $15.3 billion at year-end and had grown to ~$20.0 billion by May 10, 2026 (10-Q). Graham’s precondition — can it pay its bills without refinancing? — is met with room to spare.

Leverage. Total long-term debt face was about $5.8 billion (Note 4); cash far exceeds it. Net debt = 5,805 − 15,284 = −$9.5 billion — i.e., a net cash position. There is no meaningful refinancing wall: $75M due in 2026, then $2.25B in 2027 (the 3.000% and 1.375% Senior Notes), then longer-dated paper (Note 4). Interest expense was only $154 million against operating income of $10,383 million → interest coverage = 10,383 ÷ 154 = 67×.

Judgment-laden items. Goodwill is modest — $994 million across segments (Note 1) — because Costco grows almost entirely by opening its own warehouses, not by acquiring other retailers. No defined-benefit pension plan (an independent research service). Self-insurance liabilities of ~$1.9 billion are estimated actuarially (Note 1) — a normal retail item, not a red flag. Operating lease ROU assets $2.7 billion and lease liabilities are disclosed cleanly (Note 5).

How the three articulate. Net income flows into retained earnings (equity statement: retained earnings rose from $17,619 to $22,650 after $8,099 of income, $2,183 of dividends, and buyback charges). Net income is the starting point of the cash flow statement; adding back D&A and working through inventory/payables produces the $13.3 billion of operating cash. The change in cash on the cash flow statement ($4,255 million) ties the two balance sheets ($9,906 → $14,161). Profit and cash are close cousins here: Costco’s earnings convert cleanly into cash because inventory turns are fast and the membership model pre-collects fees.

Earnings-quality scan (manual ch. 7)

  • EBITDA as cash flow? Management does not wave EBITDA around as the measure of owner cash. Good.
  • Recurring “one-times”? A LIFO charge of $142 million in 2025 for higher merchandise costs is an accounting consequence of inflation, not a disguise (Item 8 Note 1). No serial restructuring charges.
  • Revenue recognition. Membership fees deferred and recognized ratably — the conservative and correct treatment. Executive 2% rewards reduce sales, with breakage estimated from data (Note 1).
  • Opacity? Disclosure is plain. Segment data, warehouse counts, renewal rates, and fee economics are all laid out.
  • Book value? Equity is $29.2 billion; the franchise is worth far more than tangible capital. Never anchor Costco’s value to book — economic goodwill (the membership base, the brand, the low-cost system) dwarfs accounting equity. Book understates a great business.

PRINCIPLE: The statements are evidence, and cash is the truth-teller. Costco’s earnings convert into cash; the balance sheet is a fortress of net cash; and book value is a distraction, not a floor.

Share count is already diluted; the economic cost is reflected in the slow grind of RSU grants against modest buybacks. Treating SBC as a non-cash add-back would overstate owner earnings.

Maintenance vs. growth capex — the hard judgment.

Total capex FY2025: $5,498 million (cash flow statement). The company opened 27 warehouses (24 net of relocations) and plans up to 35 (including 5 relocations) in FY2026, with intended spend of $6.0–6.5 billion (MD&A). Property and equipment net rose from $29,032 to $31,909 — a $2,877 increase — while D&A was $2,426; the gap is net growth investment.

We estimate maintenance capex ≈ $2.7 billion — roughly D&A of $2.4 billion plus a modest inflation/refresh premium to keep existing warehouses competitive. That implies growth capex ≈ $2.8 billion (5,498 − 2,700). Growth capex funds new boxes that historically earn high returns; it is optional in the sense that the existing fleet would still produce today’s earnings without it. That is why we subtract only maintenance.

Normalized owner earnings for valuation (the single anchor):

FY2025 OE of ~$7.8 billion is a solid base, but membership fee increases (effective September 1, 2024) are still phasing into the P&L, comparable sales remain mid-single-digit to high-single-digit, and FY2026 EPS is estimated at $20.45. We set:

Normalized owner earnings = $8.5 billion ≈ $19.10 per share
(on ~445 million diluted shares)

Bridge: start from FY2025 OE $7.825B; add ~$0.4B for full-year fee-hike run-rate still embedding; add ~$0.3B for organic volume/margin into a normal year → $8.5B. This is below a naive capitalization of peak-feeling FY2026e net income (~$9.1B) and above a pure trough. It is the single number we will use in the no-growth floor, the implied multiple, and the owner-earnings yield.

Free cash flow cross-check: OCF $13,335 − total capex $5,498 = $7,837 million. FCF is close to our OE because growth capex and the working-capital benefit of growth roughly offset in the arithmetic. Over a decade, Costco’s cumulative cash generation has been excellent.

The moat — what protects those earnings?

  1. Sustainable low-cost position. Limited SKUs, depot cross-docking, high volume, low shrinkage (membership-controlled entrances), shorter hours, higher full-time mix, and merchandise often sold before it is paid for. This is Buffett’s GEICO/Costco archetype of cost advantage (manual ch. 5).
  2. Membership model as switching-cost / habit moat. A prepaid annual fee plus 92%+ renewal rates create a committed customer base. Executive 2% rewards deepen the habit. Paid members grew from 71M (2023) to 81M (2025) to 82.9M (Q3 2026).
  3. Brand and trust — especially Kirkland Signature. Private label is both a margin lever and a pricing tool against national brands (VL commentary). Members trust Costco on quality at a low price; that trust is earned over decades and is hard to fake.
  4. Scale in buying and real estate. 900+ warehouses and enormous volume give Costco terms and site selection advantages rivals cannot match overnight. Sam’s Club and BJ’s compete, but Costco’s sales per warehouse and membership economics remain the industry benchmark.
  5. Culture as an economic asset. “Take Care of Our Employees” is not a slogan: U.S. average hourly wage ~$32; starting wage raised to at least $20; ~94% retention for employees past one year in the U.S./Canada (Item 1). Low turnover is a cost advantage dressed as a values statement.

What could erode the moat in ten years?
- A sustained price war by Amazon/Walmart that collapses the perceived value gap (Costco’s whole thesis is that the gap is wide and widening under inflation/tariff stress — VL 2026-04-17).
- Membership fatigue if fee hikes outrun the value delivered (the Sept 2024 hike is the live test; so far fee income and renewals hold).
- E-commerce disruption of bulk warehouse trips — though digitally-enabled sales are growing 20%+ and Costco is investing in app personalization (VL; 10-Q).
- International execution risk in newer markets where renewal rates run lower.
- Regulatory or tariff shocks that Costco cannot fully offset without raising prices and dulling the value perception.

We judge the moat wide and durable, not eternal. Durability is an argument, not an assumption.

Return on invested capital — the central quality measure

NOPAT = Operating income × (1 − tax rate)
= 10,383 × (1 − 0.251) = 10,383 × 0.749 = $7,777 million (10-K).

Invested capital (year-end, simple): Equity + interest-bearing debt − cash & ST investments
= 29,164 + 5,805 − 15,284 = $19,685 million.

ROIC = 7,777 ÷ 19,685 = 39.5%.

Even if we average beginning/ending invested capital or add operating leases into capital, ROIC remains well above 25% — and far above any plausible cost of capital (we will use ~8–10%). A moat is real only when high returns persist rather than being competed away. Costco has earned mid-to-high-20s returns on equity and very high returns on tangible capital for decades (VL: ROE 27.8% recently; return on total capital 23.4%).

ROE = 8,099 ÷ 29,164 = 27.8% — not flattered by leverage, because net debt is negative. This is pure operating excellence.

Capital-light franchise character. Costco needs warehouses, so it is not See’s Candy. But relative to the earnings stream, incremental capital is modest and earns far above the cost of capital. Growth is value-creating (manual ch. 11): g ≈ reinvestment rate × ROIC, and ROIC ≫ r.

PRINCIPLE: Durable earning power, defended by a moat and proven by returns on capital far above the cost of capital, is what you are really buying. Costco passes this gate decisively.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Conceptual north star

Intrinsic value = Σ OEₜ / (1 + r)ᵗ
(manual ch. 1, 10 — the discounted value of future owner earnings)

That identity is always true. Because the inputs are uncertain and discounting is hypersensitive, we will not pretend a single DCF point is “the” answer. Costco is a stable, mature, high-ROIC franchise — the business type for which a normalized-earnings multiple, triangulated and cross-checked, is the steadiest tool (manual Part III). We will still run a sensitized two-stage DCF as one leg of the triangle.

Anchor: normalized owner earnings

$8.5 billion, or ≈ $19.10 per share on ~445 million diluted shares. This single figure feeds every cross-check below.

Method 1 — Justified P/E on normalized owner earnings

We derive the multiple three ways and reconcile (never assert).

(a) Company’s own historical median P/E.
An independent research service median P/E: 33.0. Average annual P/E over the last decade has ranged roughly mid-20s to low-40s, with a clear upward drift as the market re-rated quality compounders (VL array: 26.8 → 29.1 → 34.9 → 33.9 → 39.0 → 35.7 → 42.8). Using the median 33× as the historical center of gravity:

33 × $19.10 = $630 per share.

(b) Peer / industry context.
Retail Store / warehouse club comps: Walmart trades at a live P/E ~40× (equity_atlas); traditional grocers far lower. Costco has historically earned a premium to broad retail for growth, returns, and safety. A peer-informed band of 30–38× on normalized earnings is the range in which Costco has spent most of the last decade when sentiment was neither panicked nor euphoric.

(c) Quality-and-growth-justified multiple (first principles).
A business that can grow owner earnings ~8–10% for a long stretch while earning 30%+ ROIC and returning surplus cash, with fortress finances and 100 earnings predictability, deserves a premium to a no-growth multiple of 1/r.

  • No-growth multiple at r = 9%: 1 / 0.09 ≈ 11.1×
  • Value of growth: with ROIC ≫ r, most of the reinvestment creates value. A simple two-stage intuition (8–9% growth for a decade, then 3%) supports mid-20s to mid-30s earnings multiples for this quality — consistent with history.
  • We set a quality-justified base multiple of 35× normalized OE — a modest premium to the 33× median for durability and net-cash fortress, not a blank check.

35 × $19.10 = $669 per share.

Reconciliation of multiple anchors:
| Anchor | Multiple | Value/share |
|---|---:|---:|
| Historical median | 33× | $630 |
| Peer-informed band midpoint | ~34× | $649 |
| Quality/growth justified | 35× | $669 |
| Optimistic quality (still below today’s 46–49×) | 40× | $764 |
| Conservative (recession / re-rating) | 28× | $535 |

We weight the quality-justified 35× as the base, with a range spanning the conservative 28× to a still-disciplined 40× high. We explicitly reject the live multiple of ~46–49× (equity_atlas live P/E 46.1; VL trailing 53 / current 49.1) as a valuation input — that is Mr. Market’s mood, not a justified earning power multiple.

Method 2 — Two-stage DCF (sensitized)

Assumptions for the central case:
- Starting OE = $8.5B
- Explicit growth g = 8% for 10 years (below recent history, near VL’s 9.5% EPS projection — slightly conservative)
- Terminal growth g_t = 3% (at or below long-run nominal GDP)
- Discount rate r = 9% (long bond ~4.4% + premium for equity uncertainty; fortress quality argues against a higher r, but gravity is gravity — manual ch. 12)

Three universal cross-checks (manual ch. 14) — all on the same $8.5B OE

(1) No-growth floor
OE ÷ r = 8,500 ÷ 0.09 = $94.4 billion ÷ 445M = ≈ $212 per share.

At the live price of $916, the no-growth floor is only 23% of the price:
($916 − $212) / $916 = 77% of the price is a bet on growth.
That is an honest statement of what you are buying — not a reason to avoid a compounder, but a reason to demand either a lower price or very high confidence in the growth path.

(2) Implied multiple at our IV and at market
- Our base IV $700 ÷ $19.10 OE = 36.6× normalized OE — defensible for this quality if growth continues.
- Market price $916 ÷ $19.10 = 48.0× normalized OE — a multiple Costco has rarely sustained as a median, and one that leaves little room for disappointment.
- Market ÷ FY2025 EPS $18.21 ≈ 50.3×; live P/E on VL’s framing ≈ 46× (equity_atlas).

(3) Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond
- At our base IV $700: yield = 19.10 ÷ 700 = 2.73%, growing ~8–10%/yr — compared to a ~4.4% static 10-year Treasury (Selection & Opinion / market context). Acceptable only because of growth and quality.
- At live $916: yield = 19.10 ÷ 916 = 2.08% — less than half the long bond, with the entire investment thesis resting on growth arriving as hoped. Gravity (manual ch. 12) is not on the buyer’s side at this price.

Growth check (manual ch. 11): Costco reinvests at ROIC ≫ r → growth creates value. We are not dealing with a value-destroying grower. The issue is solely what we pay for that growth.

Business grade: WONDERFUL

The one question — answered

At ~$916, does price leave a margin of safety?

No. Our triangulated range is $550 – $700 – $850. The live price sits above the high end and ~31% above base. There is no margin of safety. The correct posture is admire and wait — own it in the mind, not necessarily in the portfolio, until Mr. Market has one of his gloomy days about a business whose economics have not changed.

Open questions before true comfort (even at a lower price)

  1. Fee-hike durability. Does the September 2024 membership increase hold renewal rates through a full cycle, or does elasticity appear with a lag?
  2. Growth capex productivity. As easy sites get scarcer, do new warehouses and the Mission Viejo-style experiments keep earning high incremental ROIC?
  3. E-commerce and competitive response. Can digitally-enabled growth stay accretive without eroding the warehouse cost advantage that is the whole moat?

Universal lessons this case teaches most vividly

  1. Wonderful ≠ buy. The 1989 line — “It’s far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price” — is completed by its twin: quality justifies a fair price, not an infinite multiple. Costco is the living exam question.
  2. The no-growth floor reveals the bet. When 77% of the price is payment for growth, you must be right about growth — and still leave room for error. That is what margin of safety is.
  3. ROIC and the share count tell the truth about quality and partners. ~40% ROIC with a flat share count for a decade is the signature of a business and a management worth waiting for.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS USED IN THIS CASE

Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can understand well enough to forecast. Costco: inside — stable membership model, transparent unit economics.

Economic moat. A durable competitive advantage protecting high returns. Costco’s: low-cost warehouse system + membership habit + brand/trust + scale.

Economic goodwill. The franchise value above tangible capital. Costco’s market value far exceeds book equity of $29.2B precisely because of economic goodwill — which is why we never value it on book.

Owner earnings. Cash a passive owner can withdraw without harming competitive position.
OE ≈ NI + D&A − maintenance capex − maintenance ΔWC.
Costco FY2025: 8,099 + 2,426 − 2,700 − 0 ≈ $7.8B; normalized $8.5B ($19.10/sh).

Free cash flow (FCF). OCF − capital expenditures. Costco FY2025: 13,335 − 5,498 = ~$7.8B.

Maintenance vs. growth capex. Maintenance keeps today’s earnings power intact; growth expands it. Accounts do not split them — the analyst must. Costco: maint ~$2.7B of $5.5B total.

NOPAT. Net operating profit after tax = Operating income × (1 − tax rate).
Costco: 10,383 × (1 − 0.251) = $7,777M.

Invested capital. Capital used in operations ≈ equity + interest-bearing debt − excess cash.
Costco: 29,164 + 5,805 − 15,284 = $19,685M.

ROIC. Return on invested capital = NOPAT ÷ invested capital.
Costco: 7,777 ÷ 19,685 = ~39.5%.

ROE. Net income ÷ equity. Costco: 8,099 ÷ 29,164 = ~27.8% (unlevered in substance — net cash).

Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest expense. Costco: 10,383 ÷ 154 = ~67×.

Net debt. Total debt − cash & equivalents (and ST investments). Costco: 5,805 − 15,284 = −$9.5B (net cash).

Current ratio. Current assets ÷ current liabilities. Costco: 38,380 ÷ 37,108 = 1.03×.

Discount rate (r). Opportunity cost of capital; “gravity” on present value. We used 8–10%, central 9%, lower bound 8% for fortress quality.

Intrinsic value. Discounted value of future owner earnings: IV = Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ. Estimated here as a range $550–$850, base $700.

Margin of safety. (IV − Price) / IV. At $916 vs $700 base: (700 − 916) / 700 = −31% (negative — no safety).

P/E ratio. Price ÷ earnings per share. Live ~46×; VL median 33×; our justified base ~35× on normalized OE.

Normalized / mid-cycle earnings. Earning power in a normal year — not peak, not trough. Our anchor: $8.5B OE.

No-growth (earning-power) value. OE ÷ r = 8,500 ÷ 0.09 = $94.4B ≈ $212/sh. The floor; everything above is payment for growth.

Owner-earnings yield. OE / Price. At $916: 19.10 / 916 = 2.08% (vs. ~4.4% long bond).

One-dollar test. Retain earnings only if incremental ROIC ≥ r (so ≥$1 of value per $1 kept). Costco’s warehouse reinvestment passes; surplus is dividendable.

Two-stage DCF. Explicit forecast of OE for n years, then terminal value OEₙ₊₁ / (r − g), all discounted. We sensitized r and g rather than trusting one point.

Terminal value / r − g spread. The perpetuity formula’s sensitivity: small changes in (r − g) swing TV sharply — hence the grid.

Mr. Market. Graham’s allegory: the market is a manic partner quoting prices daily; use his pocketbook, not his wisdom.

Risk (in this method). Permanent loss of capital — not price volatility (beta). Costco’s low beta (0.80) is incidental; the real risk at $916 is overpaying.
