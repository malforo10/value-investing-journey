EOG Resources, Inc. (NYSE: EOG) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition
Seminar date: July 4, 2026
A teaching case in the method of How to Value a Business — A Security-Analysis Manual. This is education, not investment advice.

Welcome. Sit down, pour a coffee, and let us think together about an oil and gas producer.

We want to say at the outset what kind of company this is, because it changes the whole seminar. Most of our worked examples — See's Candy, Coca-Cola — are price-makers: businesses with a brand or a franchise that lets them raise prices. EOG is the opposite. EOG is a price-taker. It sells crude oil, natural gas liquids, and natural gas into world markets at prices it does not control and cannot influence. When oil is $80, EOG prints money; when oil is $27 — as it was in 2020 — EOG loses money. No amount of cleverness in Houston changes the price of a barrel in Cushing.

So the great question of this seminar is one the Manual poses in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6: can a business with no pricing power still create durable value for its owners? The answer, as you will see, is a qualified yes — but every dollar of that answer depends on two things you must supply yourself: the mid-cycle oil price you assume, and the price you pay for the stock. Keep those two variables in front of you the whole way through.

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What EOG does. EOG Resources is one of the largest independent (non-integrated) exploration-and-production companies in the United States. "Independent" means it does the upstream — it finds the hydrocarbons and lifts them out of the ground — and does not own refineries or gas stations downstream. It drills horizontal wells into shale rock and fractures ("fracs") them to release oil and gas. Its foundational acreage is in the Delaware Basin (West Texas / New Mexico — its crown jewel, 262 MMBoe of 2025 production), the Eagle Ford play in South Texas, and — new since 2025 — the Utica play in Ohio's Appalachian Basin, plus the Dorado gas play, the Rocky Mountain basins (Powder River, Williston), Trinidad, and now exploratory ventures in Bahrain and the UAE.

In 2025 EOG produced 449.8 million barrels of oil equivalent (MMBoe) — about 1.23 million barrels of oil equivalent per day — of which roughly 42% was crude oil, 23% NGLs, and the rest natural gas (10-K, ITEM 1). At year-end 2025 it held 5,514 MMBoe of proved reserves (1,905 MMBbl crude, 1,510 MMBbl NGLs, and 12,592 Bcf of gas). Its reserve life is on the order of a dozen years at current production rates.

How it makes money. EOG sells its production at market prices tied to benchmarks like NYMEX WTI crude and Henry Hub gas. In 2025 it realized $65.63/Bbl on crude, $22.58/Bbl on NGLs, and $3.02/Mcf on gas (10-K, ITEM 1 & 7). Multiply price by volume, subtract the cost of getting it out of the ground, and you have EOG's economics. Because price is the multiplier and EOG doesn't set it, revenue swings violently with the commodity cycle: $29.6 billion in 2022, then $24.2B, $23.7B, and $22.6B as oil softened.

The one question that decides the case. Can a price-taker — a business with no classic moat — create durable per-share value over a full cycle? EOG's own answer, stated in ITEM 1 of every 10-K, is its thesis: "EOG is focused on being among the highest return and lowest cost producers." That is the whole argument in one sentence. EOG concedes it cannot control price, so it competes on cost and on the quality of its rock. Our job is to decide whether that is a real, durable edge — a moat — or just being the best horse in a hard race.

The Circle of Competence gate (Manual, Ch. 4). Before any arithmetic, the honest question: can you forecast this business a decade out? Chapter 4 says the boundary of your circle matters more than its size, and that "too hard" is a respectable, complete answer. For EOG, be candid about what you cannot know: you cannot forecast the price of oil in 2031. Anyone who tells you they can is guessing. What you can forecast, within a range, is EOG's cost structure, its reserve base, its balance sheet, and its capital discipline — the things management controls. So the circle-of-competence verdict for EOG is nuanced: the company is understandable; the commodity price is not. That means EOG can only ever be valued as a range whose width is set by the oil price — never as a point. If that uncertainty makes you uncomfortable, "too hard" is the correct and disciplined answer, and no one should talk you out of it.

PRINCIPLE. A share is a fractional claim on a business (Ch. 1). For a commodity price-taker, half of what drives the owner's cash — the selling price — sits permanently outside the circle of competence. You may still invest, but only with humility: value it as a range, and let the width of that range, not false precision, govern the decision.

3. READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The Manual (Ch. 7) insists we read the three statements as one articulated whole. Let us walk EOG's.

The balance sheet (at 12/31/2025, from the 10-K). Total assets $51.8 billion. The overwhelming majority is oil and gas properties — the wells, the leaseholds, the facilities — carried at historical cost less accumulated DD&A. Against that:
- Cash: $3.4 billion (down from $7.1B a year earlier — they spent it on Encino).
- Total debt: ~$7.9 billion of senior notes (debt-to-total-cap 21%, up from 14%, because they issued notes to fund Encino).
- Stockholders' equity: $29.8 billion; book value ~$55/share.

This is a low-debt balance sheet for a cyclical producer — a deliberate strength we will return to in Section 5. Note the Manual's warning (Ch. 7): book value understates or overstates a business; never anchor value to it. Here it is especially misleading — the properties are at historical cost, but their economic worth depends on today's oil price and today's reserves. The reserve-value cross-check (the standardized measure) is the honest asset figure, not book.

The income statement (FY2025 vs. FY2024, 10-K). Total operating revenues $22,632M (down 4%). Walk down the layers:
- Revenue from EOG's own crude/NGL/gas production: $17,668M.
- Subtract operating costs. The per-Boe cost card (ITEM 7) is worth memorizing because it is EOG's competitive story: Lease & well $3.72, Gathering/Processing/Transport $4.74, DD&A (depletion) $9.34, G&A $1.82, interest $0.52 — total ~$20.72/Boe.
- Operating income: $6,385M (down from $8,082M — pure price effect).
- Net income: $4,980M, down from $6,403M in 2024 (10-K, MD&A). Diluted EPS $9.12 (or $10.16 on an independent research service's adjusted, ex-nonrecurring basis).

Notice the single largest cost is DD&A — depletion — at $9.34/Boe, roughly $4.5 billion for the year. This is the accountant's estimate of how much reserve EOG consumed to produce this year's oil. Hold that number. It is the ghost at this feast, and we will meet it again.

Impairments appear in down-price years, exactly as the Manual predicts (Ch. 7). In 2025 EOG took $843 million of impairments (up from $391M), mostly writing down Barnett Shale and Woodford gas assets "driven by play-specific economics" (MD&A). Impairments are the accounting confession that yesterday's drilling economics no longer hold at today's prices — a recurring feature of E&P, not a one-time event. Do not let anyone tell you these are "non-recurring."

The cash flow statement — the treadmill (10-K). Here is where the E&P truth lives.
- Net cash from operations 2025: $10,044M. Huge — far above net income, because you add back that $4.5B of DD&A (a non-cash charge) and other items.
- But now look at investing: exploration & development expenditures of $13,078M, of which $6,703M was the one-time Encino acquisition. Strip that out and organic capital spending was roughly $6.3–6.5 billion (dev drilling $4,885M + facilities $622M + leasehold $197M + capitalized interest $86M ≈ $5.8B subtotal, plus other PP&E), and 2026 organic capex is guided to $6.3–$6.7 billion (MD&A).

Here is the lesson that separates E&P from a normal business. That ~$6.5B of annual organic capex is not growth spending — it is mostly the price of standing still. Shale wells decline steeply: a well can lose 60–70% of its output in year one. To keep production merely flat, EOG must drill hundreds of new wells every year. The 10-K is explicit that ~$6.3–6.7B is the recurring level. So of the $10B of operating cash flow, a large chunk — call it most of it — is a mandatory reinvestment just to replace the reserves depletion consumed. Free cash flow in 2025 was roughly $10,044M − $6,500M ≈ $3.5 billion, or about $6.59/share — well below the ~$10 of reported EPS. That gap between reported earnings and withdrawable cash is the depletion treadmill, and it is the most important thing to understand about valuing an oil producer.

Articulation. The three statements tie together cleanly: net income ($4,980M) + DD&A ($4,461M) + other non-cash items, adjusted for working capital, = operating cash flow ($10,044M); operating cash flow minus capex = free cash flow; free cash flow funds the dividends ($2,161M) and buybacks ($2,564M) you see in financing. The articulation is honest here — EOG's cash flow is not a fiction. What must be interrogated is not whether the cash exists, but how much of it is truly free versus committed to the treadmill.

Earnings-quality scan (Ch. 7). Run the checklist:
- Successful-efforts vs. full-cost accounting. EOG uses the successful-efforts method — the more conservative of the two — expensing dry holes and unsuccessful exploration rather than capitalizing everything (10-K). Good sign.
- Depletion assumptions. DD&A runs on the unit-of-production method off proved reserves. If reserves are revised up, the DD&A rate per barrel falls and reported earnings rise — a lever worth watching. In 2025 unit rates actually fell in the U.S. (a favorable driver of the DD&A change), which is legitimate but flatters the comparison.
- Impairment timing. Taken in down-price years (as above) — normal for the industry.
- EBITDA / "EBITDAX" misuse. The Manual (Ch. 7) shudders at EBITDA presented as cash flow: "does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" For an E&P, "EBITDAX" (EBITDA before exploration expense) is worse — it strips out the very costs of finding the next barrel. Never value EOG on EBITDAX as if it were owner cash. For this business the abuse is not academic — it deletes the entire treadmill.

PRINCIPLE. For a depleting-asset business, the cash flow statement is the truth-teller and DD&A is the tell. Operating cash flow flatters you because it adds back depletion; but depletion is real — it is reserves you must spend billions to replace. Owner earnings must subtract that replacement cost. Book value and EBITDAX are both traps here.

4. EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Earning power across the cycle (from the ten-year XBRL history and an independent research service). Look at EOG's adjusted EPS across a full commodity cycle — this is the single most instructive table in the seminar:

2015 $0.06 oil crash begins
2016 ($1.61) trough — a LOSS
2017 $1.12 recovery

2019 $4.98 strong

Read that column and the whole case leaps out. EOG earned a loss in 2016 and barely broke even in 2020 — then earned $13.76 in 2022. That is a swing of roughly $15 per share driven almost entirely by the oil price, which EOG does not control. This is why the Manual (Ch. 7) is emphatic: normalize before you capitalize. Capitalizing the $13.76 of 2022 into a perpetuity would manufacture a large, precise, and wrong intrinsic value. Capitalizing the ($1.61) of 2016 would tell you the business is worthless. Both are absurd. The truth is in the middle.

Normalizing to mid-cycle. How to find the middle? Average across a full cycle that includes at least one boom and one bust. Over 2021–2025, adjusted EPS averaged ~$11.2. But 2021–24 were unusually strong (post-COVID boom); include the busts and weight toward a mid-$60s to low-$70s oil environment, and a defensible mid-cycle earning power sits around $9.00–$10.50 per share — below the recent boom, above the trough. We will carry ~$9.50–$10.00 of normalized earnings power as our anchor, and treat owner earnings (below) as somewhat lower still.

Owner earnings — the hard number (Ch. 7). Start from operating cash flow and subtract the maintenance capex needed to hold production flat:
- Operating cash flow (2025): $10,044M
- Less maintenance capex — the drilling required just to replace depletion and hold volume: this is the crux. EOG's total organic capex (~$6.5B) is mostly maintenance, because shale decline is so steep. A defensible split: of ~$6.5B, perhaps $5.0–5.5B is maintenance (replacing decline) and the balance funds modest growth. Being conservative, treat ~$5.5B as maintenance.
- Owner earnings ≈ $10,044M − $5,500M ≈ $4.5 billion, or roughly $8.4/share on 537.7M diluted shares.

Note this is below reported EPS of ~$10, and the gap is deliberate: it reflects the depletion treadmill the income statement obscures. This ~$8–9/share of mid-cycle owner earnings is the number that will drive the valuation. (In a higher-oil year owner earnings are much larger; in a bust they vanish. The single figure is a mid-cycle stand-in.)

The "moat" — is the low-cost position durable? EOG has no brand, no switching costs, no network effect, no pricing power. Its only candidate for a moat is being a low-cost producer with premium acreage (Ch. 5 lists "sustainable low-cost position" as a legitimate moat source — GEICO, Costco). Does EOG qualify? The evidence for:
- A genuinely low cost structure (~$20.72/Boe all-in cash-plus-DD&A card), and a culture obsessed with rate-of-return that predates the industry's recent discipline. EOG pioneered "premium drilling" — only sanctioning wells that clear a high hurdle rate.
- A large multi-basin inventory of "premium" locations, extended by the 2025 Encino/Utica acquisition (675,000 net Utica acres) and Dorado gas.

The evidence against durability — and it is serious:
- Geology depletes. an independent research service's February 2026 note flags "signs of deteriorating well productivity at EOG's primary legacy assets in the Eagle Ford and Delaware Basin." The best rock gets drilled first; what remains is, on average, worse. A low-cost position built on premium acreage is a wasting advantage unless continually replenished.
- Competitors catch up. Fracking technology diffuses. What EOG pioneered, Devon, Diamondback, ConocoPhillips, and others now do too.
- The Manual's own warning (Ch. 5): many advantages are "Roman candles" — temporary. A low-cost edge in a commodity is closer to a Roman candle than to Coca-Cola's brand.

Verdict on the moat: narrow and wasting, not wide and durable. EOG is a best-in-class operator in a no-moat industry. Its cost edge is real and worth something — it lets EOG stay profitable at oil prices that push weaker producers into losses — but it does not confer pricing power and it must be re-earned every year through drilling and acquisition. That is the opposite of See's Candy, which widens its moat while sitting still.

ROIC vs. cost of capital, through the cycle (Ch. 6). EOG prides itself on returns — let us verify.
- Invested capital ≈ equity + debt ≈ $29.8B + $8.7B ≈ $38.5B (end 2025).
- 2024 (strong year): NOPAT ≈ operating income $8,082M × (1 − 0.22 tax) ≈ $6.3B; ROIC ≈ ~19%. Excellent — well above any plausible discount rate.
- 2025 (softer): NOPAT ≈ $4,980M on ~$38.5B ≈ ~13%. Still above cost of capital.
- Trough years (2016, 2020): ROIC near zero or negative.

So ROIC through the cycle averages comfortably above the ~9–10% discount rate in good and normal years — the signature of a quality operator — but collapses below it in busts. An independent research service's "Return on Total Capital" line confirms the pattern: ~20% in 2022–24, falling to ~15% estimated for 2025–26. On a cycle-average basis EOG creates value; in any given trough it destroys it. The moat, such as it is, shows up precisely here: EOG's trough returns are less negative than a high-cost competitor's, which is why it survives to compound in the next upcycle.

ROE watch. an independent research service shows return on shareholders' equity of 22–25% in the boom (2022–24), guided to ~18.5% for 2025–26 — the Equity Atlas live figure is 18.5%. Strong, but again cycle-dependent.

PRINCIPLE. A low-cost position in a commodity is a moat of a kind — but a wasting one, defended by drilling and geology rather than by brand or switching costs. It shows up not as pricing power (there is none) but as surviving the trough and earning above-average returns across the cycle. Value it as such: real, but re-earned annually, and never capitalize a boom-year ROIC into perpetuity.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

For a commodity producer, this chapter is not a formality — it is close to the whole game. The Manual (Ch. 8) asks: can the business survive a bad year and a shut credit market without becoming a forced seller? For an E&P, "a bad year" means $30 oil, and forced sellers get destroyed at the trough. EOG's answer is a deliberate, decades-old strategy: run low debt so you never have to sell into a crash.

Walk the five questions (Ch. 8), each with EOG's own numbers (10-K):

  • Leverage. Total debt ~$7.9B (year-end 2025), $8.3B at 3/31/26; cash $3.4–3.9B; net debt ≈ $4.5 billion. Against mid-cycle operating cash flow of ~$8–10B, that is net debt of well under 1× cash flow — clearable from under a single normal year's cash generation. Even against conservative mid-cycle owner earnings of ~$4.5B, it is about one year. Debt-to-total-cap is 21% (management's stated policy: "consistently below average" versus peers). This is a fortress posture for the sector.
  • Coverage. Interest expense net was $235M in 2025 against operating income of $6,385M — interest coverage of ~27×. Even in a trough with operating income cut by two-thirds, coverage stays comfortably in double digits. No solvency threat from the interest bill.
  • Liquidity. Current assets $8,963M vs. current liabilities $5,224M at 3/31/26 — current ratio ~1.7 — plus $3.0B of undrawn revolver (10-K). Graham's precondition (adequate working capital, sound current ratio) is met.
  • Refinancing wall. EOG terms out its debt as fixed-rate senior notes laddered to 2028, 2031, 2032, 2036, 2054, 2055. It has no senior notes maturing in 2026 (MD&A). It does not depend on the kindness of lenders at any single date.
  • Hidden obligations. Asset-retirement obligations (plugging wells) and operating leases (~$515M annual rentals; ~$1.0B/yr transportation & storage commitments) exist but are modest against cash flow. No pension hole (pension assets ~ obligations). Nothing lurking.

Verdict: fortress — Financial Strength "A" (an independent research service), and deservedly. Now teach why this matters so enormously for a commodity producer. In 2020, when oil went briefly negative, over-levered shale companies were forced to sell assets, cut dividends, dilute shareholders with emergency equity, or file for bankruptcy — permanent losses of capital, the one real risk (Ch. 3). EOG's low debt meant it did none of those things; it kept its dividend, kept its acreage, and lived to buy Encino at the bottom of a soft patch. The balance sheet is what converts a brutal, no-moat industry into one a patient owner can survive. As the Manual puts it (Ch. 8): "any series of positive numbers … evaporates when multiplied by a single zero." EOG's balance sheet is built to make sure there is never a zero.

PRINCIPLE. In a price-taking cyclical, the balance sheet is not a footnote — it is the survival mechanism. Low debt earns little in good times and everything in a crash, because it guarantees you are never the forced seller of Chapter 3. Carry this strength directly into the margin-of-safety judgment: EOG's fortress balance sheet lets you assume it survives to the next upcycle, which is precisely the assumption a cyclical valuation depends on.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

The Manual (Ch. 9) judges management on capital allocation across six uses of a dollar, and on candor. EOG's model is unusually disciplined for its industry — and its industry's history is the perfect foil.

The industry's original sin (the institutional imperative, Ch. 9). For two decades U.S. shale pursued growth at any cost — outspending cash flow, drilling to grow volume regardless of returns, diluting and levering to do it. This is the "institutional imperative" in its purest form: everyone did it, so everyone did it. It destroyed enormous shareholder capital.

EOG's answer. EOG built its identity in explicit opposition to that. Its "premium" and later "double-premium" drilling standards sanction only wells clearing a high after-tax rate of return. Since 2024 it operates a formal Cash Return Framework: a commitment to return "a minimum of 70 percent of annual [operating cash flow] … less total capital expenditures" to shareholders (MD&A). Walk the six uses:

  • Reinvest in the business: disciplined — organic capex held to ~$6.3–6.7B, funded from internal cash flow, aimed at the highest-return plays (Delaware, Eagle Ford, Dorado, Utica).
  • Acquisitions: EOG is historically reluctant — it prefers organic, internally-generated prospects. The 2025 Encino/Utica deal ($5.7B including debt) is the notable exception; the test (Ch. 9) is whether it clears the return hurdle and whether the currency was cash (it was — funded with debt and cash, not dilutive stock, unlike Buffett's Dexter Shoe blunder). Early signs (an independent research service, May 2026) call the acquired inventory "high-return."
  • Pay down debt / keep it low: a core discipline, as Section 5 showed.
  • Regular dividends: grown relentlessly — from $0.67/share (2016) to $3.99 declared (2025), a ~21% ten-year growth rate. Raised again to $1.02/quarter in 2025.
  • Special dividends: EOG's signature. It has paid large variable special dividends when cash is abundant — e.g., $1.00 + $2.00 (2021), $1.00 + $1.80 + $1.50 + $1.50 (2022), $1.00 + $1.50 (2023). These are the release valve that returns windfall cash without over-committing the regular dividend. Teach students that specials are variable by design — you cannot count them in a forward yield.
  • Buybacks: ramped up recently — $3,246M (2024) and $2,564M (2025), versus almost nothing before 2023. Here apply the Manual's test (Ch. 9): buybacks create value only below intrinsic value. EOG bought heavily in 2024–25 when the stock languished at $100–115 — plausibly below our estimated value (Section 7). That is the disciplined kind of buyback. Watch that they don't keep buying if the stock runs well past value.

Share count over the decade. This is the acid test of whether the returns reach owners per share. Diluted shares: ~508M (2010) rising to ~588M (2022) as the company grew, then falling to ~538M (2025) and ~533M (mid-2026) as buybacks took hold. So EOG diluted modestly through the growth years and is now shrinking the count — a shareholder-friendly turn. The Encino deal was cash-funded, not stock-funded, preserving per-share value.

The one-dollar test (Ch. 9). Has each retained dollar produced at least a dollar of value? Over the cycle, with cycle-average ROIC comfortably above the discount rate and a stock that has compounded (5-year total return ~108% per an independent research service, May 2026), the answer through the upcycle is yes. Through the 2015–16 and 2020 troughs, retained capital earned poorly — but that is the commodity, not the management.

Candor. EOG's disclosure is clear and consistent; it reports its cost card, its impairments, and its reserve revisions plainly. It does not lean on EBITDAX rhetoric in its MD&A. Management (Chairman & CEO Ezra Yacob) frames strategy around returns through cycles, not volume. This is honest, herd-independent allocation.

PRINCIPLE. In an industry whose default setting is growth-at-any-cost (the institutional imperative), a management that imposes a return hurdle, keeps debt low, funds acquisitions with cash not undervalued stock, buys back shares only when cheap, and returns windfalls via variable specials is doing exactly what Chapter 9 asks. EOG passes the management gate — the rarest thing to find in a commodity business.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Now the arithmetic. The master equation (Ch. 1, App. A) is always the definition: IV = Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ. But for a price-taking, depleting-asset cyclical, we do not run a proud ten-year DCF off a single year — that would violate every guardrail in Chapter 7 and Chapter 10. Instead we anchor on normalized mid-cycle owner earnings and triangulate across multiple methods, letting the width of the range express the oil-price uncertainty.

The anchor. From Section 4: mid-cycle owner earnings ≈ $8.4/share (conservative, treadmill-adjusted) and mid-cycle earnings power ≈ $9.5–10.0/share (an independent research service adjusted basis). We will use a normalized owner-earnings figure of ~$9.0/share as the central anchor, and a discount rate r = 10% (long bond plus a premium for the cash-flow uncertainty of a commodity — the Manual's Chapter 12 method; a commodity's cash flows deserve a higher premium than a franchise's).

Method (a): Normalized earnings multiple — derived from EOG's own history

The Manual (Ch. 10) says the DCF and the multiple are the same statement in two languages. What multiple does EOG deserve, and what has it historically commanded? An independent research service reports EOG's median P/E of 15 and an average annual P/E that has ranged from ~8.7 (2022–23, at the boom) to ~20 (2018–19). Note the cyclical P/E trap (Ch. 7): at the 2022 peak earnings, EOG traded at only ~8.7× — the low multiple was a warning, not a bargain, because peak earnings were unsustainable. Conversely a high P/E on trough earnings can be normal.

The right approach: apply a normalized multiple to normalized earnings, not a trough multiple to peak earnings. A best-in-class, fortress-balance-sheet operator in a no-moat industry deserves roughly a market-average-to-modest-premium multiple on mid-cycle earnings — call it 12–15× normalized owner earnings, below an independent research service's 15 median (which reflects some boom-era optimism) but respecting EOG's quality:
- $9.0 OE × 13× ≈ $117/share (central)
- $9.0 × 12× = $108; $9.0 × 15× = $135

Method (b): Peer multiple from the industry

An independent research service places EOG in the Natural Gas (Diversified) grouping (Equity Atlas), alongside large independent E&Ps — peers include Devon (DVN), Diamondback (FANG), ConocoPhillips (COP) (which absorbed Pioneer/PXD), and others. Large-cap independents typically trade at ~5–7× EV/EBITDAX or roughly 10–14× normalized earnings, depending on oil-price sentiment. EOG merits a premium within the peer set — for its balance sheet (A / 21% debt, the lowest-levered of the majors independents), its returns-focused culture, and its inventory depth. A justified premium of a turn or two on the multiple supports the $115–135 zone from Method (a).

Method (c): Asset-value cross-check — the standardized measure (PV-10 floor)

This is the E&P analyst's special tool (Ch. 8's asset-floor idea applied to reserves). EOG's audited after-tax standardized measure of discounted future net cash flows from proved reserves (10-K supplemental disclosure):
- 2025: $41,317M — on ~533M shares ≈ $77.6/share
- 2024: $43,626M (~$78/sh); 2023: $42,992M (~$74/sh)

This is a floor-ish asset value: the after-tax, 10%-discounted value of only the proved reserves at a trailing-12-month average price (~$65 oil), assuming EOG produced them and drilled no new wells ever. Two cautions: (i) it uses SEC-prescribed backward-looking prices, not a forecast; (ii) it counts only proved reserves — it gives zero credit for EOG's vast unproved drilling inventory, its exploration upside (Utica, Bahrain, UAE), or its operating skill. So ~$77/share is a conservative asset floor. The going concern — a company that keeps finding and developing new reserves at high returns — is worth meaningfully more than its proved-reserve liquidation value, which is why our earnings-based estimate ($115–135) sits above the PV-10 floor ($77). The gap between them ($77 floor vs. ~$117 central) is the market paying for EOG's ability to keep replacing reserves profitably — i.e., for the operator quality and the inventory runway.

For a commodity price-taker, intrinsic value is a wide range anchored on normalized mid-cycle owner earnings, floored by the after-tax standardized measure of proved reserves, and cross-checked with the no-growth value and the owner-earnings yield. The width of the range is the analysis — it tells you that your margin of safety must be large, because your central estimate rests on an oil price you cannot forecast.

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

The arithmetic (Ch. 13). Live price (Equity Atlas, July 4, 2026): $130.78. Our range: low $90, base $115, high $145.

  • Against the base ($115), the price is a ~14% premium — you are paying slightly above the central estimate. Margin of safety = (115 − 130.78)/115 ≈ −14%. Negative.
  • Against the high ($145), there is a ~11% discount — but you should never buy against the optimistic end of your own range.
  • Against the no-growth floor ($90) and asset floor (~$77), the price sits comfortably above both — meaning a meaningful chunk of today's price is a bet on mid-cycle-or-better oil and continued growth, not just proved reserves.

So at $130.78, there is no margin of safety on our base case. The Manual (Ch. 13) is categorical: "If we calculate the value of a common stock to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested in buying." Here it is not even slightly higher — the price is at or slightly above our central value.

For a commodity price-taker this matters doubly. The Manual (Ch. 13) teaches that in a mediocre or no-moat business, every dollar of safety must come from the purchase discount — the quality (low-cost position) helps you survive but does not substitute for a cheap price the way a durable franchise's compounding can. EOG's operating quality is real, but it cannot override paying up. Safety here lives in the discount and the asset floor, and today there is little of the former.

Where was the margin of safety? Look back at the an independent research service reports we read: in November 2025 the stock was $106.57, and in August 2025 $116.84. At $106 — near our base and above the floors but with real discount to the high case, a 3.9% yield, and Encino about to add inventory — the risk/reward was far more favorable. This is the lesson in real time: the business barely changed between November's $106 and July's $131; only the price did. That price change is the entire difference between an interesting entry and an expensive one.

Is Mr. Market euphoric or depressed on energy? (Ch. 2.) The May 2026 an independent research service notes the stock "moved lower amid a broader pullback in the sector," with Wall Street worried about natural-gas oversupply and Venezuelan production — some fear in the tape. Yet EOG has rallied ~23% off its November low (from $106 to $131) on a strong Q1 2026 ($3.70 diluted EPS) and Encino optimism. So Mr. Market is neither despairing nor euphoric on EOG specifically — he is modestly optimistic, pricing in a decent oil environment and the Encino payoff. That is precisely the mood in which a disciplined owner waits, because the discount has closed.

PRINCIPLE. Mr. Market is a servant, not a guide (Ch. 2). For a no-moat commodity producer, the servant's usefulness is entirely in his pocketbook — the price he quotes — because you cannot lean on a widening moat to bail out an over-payment. At $131, against a $115 base, the pocketbook is not offering a bargain. Value the business independently; consult the price only to see if it is foolish enough to act on. Today it is not.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Verdict: a good business at the level of the operator, run inside a fair-to-hard commodity industry* — and, at today's price, one to admire and wait on rather than buy.

In the Manual's four-way sort (wonderful / good / fair / avoid): EOG is not "wonderful" — it has no durable moat, no pricing power, and earnings that swing with a price it cannot control. But it is decisively not "avoid" either: it is a best-in-class, low-cost, fortress-balance-sheet operator with disciplined, candid management and a deep inventory. Call it "a great operator in a tough business" — a good company whose value depends entirely on the oil price you assume and the price you pay. The quality is genuine and it earns EOG the right to survive and compound across cycles where weaker peers cannot. But quality cannot be capitalized into an infinite multiple, and it cannot rescue an entry made above intrinsic value.

Answering the one question ("can a price-taker create durable per-share value?"): Yes — but conditionally. EOG has created real per-share value over the cycle through low costs, a fortress balance sheet, and shareholder-friendly capital allocation. That value is durable in the operator but not durable in the earnings, which the commodity will keep whipping around. The durability lives in surviving the troughs to harvest the peaks — which is a real, if humble, form of moat.

On price: at $130.78 versus a $90–$145 range (base $115), there is no margin of safety. The verdict is therefore: a fine business to own at the right price; this is not that price. The disciplined action is the one from Chapter 13 — admire it, and wait for Mr. Market to offer it below the conservative end of the range (as he did near $106 only months ago).

Two or three open questions to keep honest:
1. What is the true mid-cycle oil price? Our whole valuation pivots on ~$65–70 oil. If the world settles at $55, EOG is worth much less; at $85, much more. You cannot know this — which is exactly why the range is wide and the required discount large.
2. What is the inventory runway / reserve life? an independent research service's warning about deteriorating well productivity in the legacy Eagle Ford and Delaware is the central long-term risk. If premium inventory depletes faster than Encino/Utica/international replaces it, the treadmill speeds up and owner earnings fall. Watch reserve replacement and finding-and-development costs each year.
3. The energy transition. Over a decade-plus horizon, does demand for EOG's product plateau or decline? This is a genuine terminal-value risk for any depleting hydrocarbon asset and argues for conservative terminal assumptions.

Two or three universal lessons — the reason this case is worth teaching:
- Normalizing a cyclical. Never capitalize a boom year ($13.76 EPS in 2022) or a bust year (a loss in 2016) into a perpetuity. Average across a full cycle that contains at least one of each. This single discipline separates careful analysts from the crowd that extrapolates the last good year.
- The maintenance-capex / depletion treadmill. Reported earnings and even operating cash flow overstate the withdrawable cash of a depleting-asset business, because a large fraction of capex is mandatory just to replace what depletion consumed. Owner earnings — cash flow minus real maintenance capex — is far lower than headline earnings, and it is the only honest anchor. Anyone valuing EOG on EBITDAX has deleted the treadmill and will overpay.
- A great operator in a bad industry, and the balance sheet as survival. Business quality (Ch. 5) usually dominates cheapness over long holding periods — but only when the quality is a durable moat. In a no-moat commodity, that logic weakens: the operator's excellence buys survival and cycle-average outperformance, not a widening moat, so the purchase discount must do the heavy lifting for your margin of safety. And the fortress balance sheet is what lets a good operator survive the trough to see the next peak — in this industry, not going bankrupt in 2020 was itself a form of alpha.

This is a teaching case, not advice. The point is the method, not the ticker.

11. GLOSSARY

Each term: (1) definition, (2) formula, (3) EOG's number.

  • Owner earnings. (1) The cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. (2) Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. (3) EOG 2025: net income $4,980M + DD&A $4,461M + other non-cash, less ~$5.5B maintenance capex ≈ ~$4.5B, ~$8.4/share — below reported EPS because of depletion.
  • Free cash flow (FCF). (1) Operating cash flow after all capex. (2) Operating cash flow − total capex. (3) 2025: $10,044M − ~$6,500M organic ≈ $3.5B (~$6.59/sh); note this treats all organic capex as a use, which for a depleting E&P is nearly right.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex. (1) Maintenance = spending to hold production/reserves flat; growth = spending to expand. (2) Judgmental split of total capex. (3) EOG's ~$6.5B organic capex is mostly maintenance (~$5.0–5.5B) because shale wells decline 60–70% in year one — the "treadmill."
  • DD&A / depletion. (1) The non-cash charge writing off the cost of reserves as they are produced. (2) Unit-of-production: (cost) × (units produced ÷ proved reserves). (3) 2025: $4,461M, or $9.34/Boe — EOG's single largest cost.
  • Reserve replacement. (1) Whether new reserves added ≥ reserves produced. (2) (Reserves added ÷ production produced). (3) 2025 proved reserves rose 766 MMBoe to 5,514 MMBoe (aided by Encino) against 449.8 MMBoe produced — replacement well above 100% this year.
  • Standardized measure / PV-10. (1) SEC-prescribed present value of future net cash flows from proved reserves. (2) Future cash inflows − production costs − development costs − income taxes, discounted at 10%. (3) EOG 2025 after-tax: $41,317M (~$77.6/sh); an independent research service's pre-tax PV-10 is $51.8B. An asset-value floor.
  • ROIC. (1) Return on invested capital — business quality. (2) NOPAT ÷ invested capital. (3) 2024 ~19%; 2025 ~13%; troughs ~0. Above r in normal/good years.
  • NOPAT. (1) After-tax operating profit. (2) Operating income × (1 − tax rate). (3) 2025: $6,385M × 0.78 ≈ $4.98B.
  • Invested capital. (1) Capital funding operations. (2) Equity + debt. (3) ~$38.5B (end 2025: $29.8B equity + $8.7B debt).
  • ROE. (1) Return on shareholders' equity. (2) Net income ÷ equity. (3) 2025: $4,980M ÷ $29,833M ≈ 16.7% (an independent research service/Atlas: 18.5%).
  • Interest coverage. (1) How many times earnings cover interest. (2) EBIT ÷ interest expense. (3) 2025: $6,385M ÷ $235M ≈ 27×.
  • Net debt. (1) Debt net of cash. (2) Total debt − cash. (3) ~$8.3B − ~$3.9B ≈ $4.5B (<1× mid-cycle cash flow).
  • Current ratio. (1) Short-term liquidity. (2) Current assets ÷ current liabilities. (3) 3/31/26: $8,963M ÷ $5,224M ≈ 1.7.
  • Cost of capital / WACC / discount rate (r). (1) Owner's opportunity cost, long rate + risk premium. (2) Judgmental. (3) We use r = 10% — long bond plus an above-average premium for commodity cash-flow uncertainty.
  • Intrinsic value. (1) Discounted value of all future owner earnings. (2) IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. (3) Triangulated $90 / $115 / $145 per share.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value. (1) Worth if it never grows again. (2) OE ÷ r. (3) $9.0 ÷ 0.10 = $90/share.
  • Owner-earnings yield. (1) Owner earnings relative to price. (2) OE ÷ price. (3) $9.0 ÷ $130.78 ≈ 6.9% at the live price.
  • Margin of safety. (1) Discount of price to conservative value — the cornerstone. (2) (IV − Price) ÷ IV. (3) At $130.78 vs. base $115: ≈ −14% (a premium, no safety).
  • P/E ratio. (1) Price per dollar of earnings. (2) Price ÷ EPS. (3) Live ~12.9×; an independent research service median 15×; only ~8.7× at the 2022 peak (the cyclical-P/E trap).
  • EV/EBITDAX. (1) Enterprise value to EBITDA-before-exploration — an E&P multiple. (2) EV ÷ (EBITDA + exploration expense). (3) Cited to be criticized: it deletes the treadmill; large-cap E&Ps ~5–7×. Do not treat EBITDAX as owner cash.
  • Dividend yield (regular + special). (1) Cash returned per dollar of price. (2) Dividend ÷ price. (3) Regular: $4.08 ÷ $130.78 ≈ 3.1%; specials are variable (e.g., $6.30/sh across 2021–23) and must not be counted forward.
  • Economic moat. (1) Durable advantage protecting returns. (2) Qualitative; signature is pricing power. (3) EOG: none of the classic type — narrow, wasting low-cost edge only.
  • Low-cost producer. (1) The one moat source available to a commodity business. (2) Lowest cost per unit. (3) EOG all-in ~$20.72/Boe; the edge that lets it survive troughs.
  • Circle of competence. (1) What you can forecast; boundary matters most. (2) Qualitative. (3) EOG's operations are inside; the oil price is outside — hence the wide range.
  • One-dollar test. (1) Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥$1 of value. (2) Retain ⇔ incremental ROIC ≥ r. (3) Passes over the cycle; fails in troughs (commodity, not management).
  • Normalized / mid-cycle earnings. (1) Earning power averaged across a full cycle, not a peak or trough. (2) Cycle-average, windfalls stripped. (3) EOG: ~$9.5–10.0/sh earnings power, ~$9.0/sh owner earnings (vs. $13.76 peak 2022 and a loss in 2016).
  • Risk. (1) Permanent loss of capital — not volatility. (2) Qualitative. (3) For EOG: over-paying, or a structural collapse in oil / reserve base — not the stock's beta of ~1.0.
