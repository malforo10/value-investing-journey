Phillips 66 (PSX) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition— a teaching case, not investment advice.
Date: July 4, 2026. Live price: $176.42.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What it is. Phillips 66 is a large integrated downstream energy company, spun out of ConocoPhillips in 2012. It takes crude oil and other feedstocks and turns them into fuels, chemicals, and lubricants, and it owns the pipes and terminals that move all of it. Five segments (10-K MD&A, item7_mda_part1, and Note 30):

  1. Midstream — pipelines, terminals, NGL gathering/processing/fractionation/export. Largely fee-based and stable. FY2025 pretax income $2,817M (Transportation $918M + NGL $1,899M). Segment assets $30.2B — the largest asset base.
  2. Chemicals — a 50% equity stake in CPChem (JV with Chevron). Commodity petrochemicals (ethylene, polyethylene). FY2025 equity pretax $297M (down from $876M — a cyclical trough for polyethylene margins, 7.1¢/lb vs 17.7¢/lb in 2024).
  3. Refining — 10 refineries in the U.S. and Europe, ~1.87 MMbbl/d crude capacity. The cyclical heart. FY2025 pretax −$274M (a loss — trough), vs +$5,340M in 2023 (peak). This swing of ~$5.6B in two years is the case.
  4. Marketing & Specialties (M&S) — buys and re-sells refined product, plus lubricants. FY2025 pretax $4,500M — but $2.9B of that was one-time disposition gains (Germany/Austria retail $1.9B + Coop $1.0B). Recurring M&S is roughly $1.3–1.9B.
  5. Renewable Fuels — the Rodeo Complex and Humber; FY2025 pretax −$380M (ramp-up losses).

How it makes money. Refining earns the crack spread (the gap between product prices and crude), which is set by the market, not by PSX. Midstream earns tolls. Chemicals earns chain margins. So four of the five segments are price-takers on commodity spreads; only Midstream and the fee parts of M&S produce earnings you can forecast with a straight face.

THE ONE QUESTION. Through a full cycle, what does Phillips 66 actually earn — and is that normalized earning power worth conspicuously more than the $176 the market is asking today? Everything else is detail.

The Circle of Competence gate. Can we forecast this? Partly. Midstream tolls and marketing margins are forecastable within a range. Refining crack spreads and CPChem chain margins are not forecastable year to year — no one can tell you the 2028 Gulf Coast 3:2:1 crack. The manual's discipline (Ch. 4) says: confine the forecast to what you can defend, and handle the rest by normalizing through the cycle (Ch. 7) rather than by pretending to predict a peak or a trough. That is exactly what a cyclical demands, and it is the spine of this seminar. PSX sits inside the circle — but only if we value it on mid-cycle power, never on a single year.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Balance sheet (10-K item8_part1, at 12/31/2025):
- Liquidity. Current assets $17,271M vs current liabilities $13,326M → current ratio 1.30 ($17,271 ÷ $13,326). Cash $1,116M is thin, but $5.7B of committed credit capacity backs it, and by Q1'26 cash jumped to $5,150M after a $2B junior-sub issuance. Graham's working-capital precondition is met, not lavish.
- Leverage. Total debt $19,716M, total equity $30,241M → debt/capital 39% (10-K "Financial Indicators"). Net debt ≈ $18.6B.
- Goodwill/intangibles. Goodwill $1,433M + intangibles $978M = $2.4B — only ~3% of $73.7B assets. This is a hard-asset business (net PP&E $39,097M), not a goodwill tower. Good: little acquisition air in the book.
- Book value is $72.53/sh (VL). The manual says never anchor to book (Ch. 7) — for a hard-asset refiner book is closer to replacement cost than for a franchise, but it still tells us little about earning power, which is what we discount.

Income statement (10-K item8_part1, three-year):
- Revenue is falling ($147.4B → $143.2B → $132.4B) — mostly lower crude prices, not lost volume (throughput steady at ~1.76 MMbbl/d).
- Margins, layer by layer (2025): the business runs on razor-thin net margins — net income $4,403M ÷ $132,376M revenue = 3.3%. That is the nature of a commodity refiner: enormous revenue, tiny spread. Watch dollars of segment profit, not revenue.
- The multi-year trend is the whole story: net income to PSX $7,015M (2023) → $2,117M (2024) → $4,403M (2025). But 2025 is flattered — see one-timers below.
- One-timers (earnings-quality scan, Ch. 7): 2025 net income includes +$2,984M net gain on dispositions (Germany/Austria + Coop), offset by $1,060M impairments (mostly a $948M WRB write-down) and accelerated LA-refinery depreciation. Strip the ~$2.9B of net one-time gains and 2025's operating earnings are far lower — which is exactly why an independent research service reports adjusted EPS of $6.44 vs GAAP $10.79. We anchor on the adjusted/normalized figure, not the GAAP headline.

Cash flow (10-K item8_part1):
- OCF $4,962M vs net income $4,528M — OCF exceeds NI, good. But note the reconciliation: it subtracts the $2,984M disposition gain (non-operating) and adds back $3,251M D&A and $1,060M impairments. So OCF is cleaner than GAAP NI here.
- Capex & investments $2,233M; sustaining (maintenance) capex is explicitly ~$1.1B for 2026 ($590M Refining + $400M Midstream + the rest), with $1.3B of growth capex on top (10-K "Capital Spending"). This maintenance/growth split is gold for owner earnings.
- FCF (OCF − total capex) = $4,962 − $2,233 = $2,729M in a soft year; much of the extra cash came from $3.5B of asset sales.

How the three articulate. Falling crude prices shrink both revenue and cost of goods (income statement); working capital swings with commodity prices (cash flow — a −$2,264M Q1'26 OCF was almost entirely a WC build); the balance sheet absorbs it via the revolver and receivables facility. The engine that matters is segment dollars of profit, normalized.

Earnings-quality verdict: clean auditor opinion (E&Y, unqualified; effective internal controls), no EBITDA games, one-timers are disclosed and quantified, and management points you to adjusted numbers. Trustworthy — provided you do the normalizing yourself. Principle: in a cyclical, GAAP net income is a snapshot of where you are in the cycle, not a measure of earning power; the analyst's job is to average through it and strip the windfalls in both directions.

The lesson leaps off the page: EPS ranged from −$9 to +$18 in the space of a few years. Capitalize the 2022 peak and you "prove" the stock is worth $250; capitalize the 2024–25 trough and you "prove" it's worth $50. Both are wrong. Normalized mid-cycle EPS — averaging 2018–2025 ex-COVID gives ≈$9.5; the 2021–2025 full-cycle average is ≈$9.6; VL's own 2026/2027 path ($7.25/$8.30) plus the 2029–31 target ($11.35) brackets a mid-cycle of roughly $9 per share. We carry normalized EPS ≈ $8.5–$9.5 forward.

Owner earnings (Ch. 7). Build it on normalized figures ($ millions, ≈401M diluted shares):
- Normalized net income ≈ $3,500M (mid-cycle, ≈$8.7/sh).
- + non-cash D&A (steady-state, ex-accelerated LA charge) ≈ $3,000M.
- − maintenance capex. This is the hard, decisive term. Sustaining capex is ~$1.1B, but a refiner also spends on recurring turnarounds and asset-retirement/environmental work; we use a conservative $1,700M of true maintenance capex (above the stated $1.1B, below full D&A).
- − incremental working capital ≈ $0 mid-cycle (it swings violently quarter to quarter but nets to roughly zero through a cycle).

Owner earnings ≈ $3,500 + $3,000 − $1,700 = $4,800M ≈ $12.0 per share. (A stricter reading that treats all of D&A as maintenance would put OE closer to normalized NI, ~$8.7/sh — we keep that stricter figure in mind as the conservative floor.)

ROIC vs cost of capital (Ch. 6). Invested capital ≈ total debt $19.7B + equity $30.2B = ~$50B (less ~$1.1B cash → ~$49B). Normalized NOPAT ≈ normalized pretax operating income (~$5.0B mid-cycle, ex-one-timers) × (1 − 23%) ≈ $3.85B. ROIC ≈ $3.85B ÷ $49B ≈ 7.9%. Against a discount rate of ~9% (below), ROIC ≈ cost of capital through the cycle — the classic profile of a capital-hungry commodity business. In the peak year (2023) ROIC was far above cost; in the trough (2024–25) far below. An independent research service's own "Return on Total Capital" line tells the same story: 5.6% (2024), 6.6% (2025), rising to a projected 10.5% by 2029–31. ROE runs higher (VL 7.7%/9.1%, projected 15%) — but that is leverage at work (debt/capital 39%), so read it alongside, not instead of, ROIC.

The moat — and what erodes it. PSX's advantages are real but modest: (1) scale and integration — a wellhead-to-market NGL chain and 1.9 MMbbl/d of refining give cost and logistics advantages; (2) irreplaceable Midstream assets — pipelines and export terminals are hard to permit and duplicate, a genuine toll-road moat; (3) CPChem's low-cost U.S./Middle-East ethane feedstock position. What it is not: a pricing-power franchise. Refining has no pricing power — it takes the crack spread the market hands it (the diagnostic test of Ch. 5, pricing power, fails for the biggest segment). What erodes it in 10 years: the energy transition (PSX is already idling the LA refinery and converting San Francisco to renewables), demand for gasoline peaking, and permanent margin compression. Principle: integration and irreplaceable midstream assets create a narrow, asset-based moat around the stable pieces; the refining core is a cost-competitive commodity operation with a cyclical, not durable, advantage. The stable Midstream + M&S core is what deserves a multiple; the refining/chemicals cycle is what you must normalize.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

  • Leverage. Net debt ≈ $18.6B ÷ normalized owner earnings ≈ $4.8B = ~3.9× owner earnings ($18,600 ÷ $4,800). Against normalized EBIT (~$5.0B) net debt is ~3.7×. For a cyclical, that is moderate, not alarming — but it is real leverage that a trough year strains (in 2024–25 the refining segment lost money while carrying this debt).
  • Coverage. Normalized EBIT (~$5.0–5.5B) ÷ interest expense $1,039M = ~5× ($5,000 ÷ $1,039). In the trough, coverage compressed toward 3× — adequate, and never threatened, but not a fortress cushion.
  • Liquidity. Current ratio 1.30; cash $1.1B (YE'25) → $5.2B (Q1'26) + $5.7B committed credit. Next-year debt maturity is only $1,038M, easily covered.
  • Refinancing wall — none. Note 15 maturities: 2026 $1,038M, 2027 $1,282M, 2028 $1,319M, 2029 $1,217M, 2030 $1,192M. Beautifully laddered — roughly $1.0–1.3B a year, with the junior subordinated notes pushed out to 2056. No year forces PSX into a shut credit market. This is the Ch. 8 test passed cleanly.
  • Hidden obligations. Pension modestly underfunded (assets $2,872M vs obligation $3,183M, ~$311M gap — small). Operating-lease residual guarantees ~$579M. The Dakota Access litigation is a genuine tail risk (25% CECU share ~$215M plus potential shutdown of a key pipeline). The Propel Fuels judgment (~$867M accrued, on appeal, with possible exposure above the accrual) is a real but bounded liability. None threatens solvency.
  • Ratings: Moody's Baa1 (downgraded from A3 in Sept 2025), S&P BBB+ — solidly investment grade, but the downgrade is a reminder the balance sheet loosened with the WRB/Coastal Bend acquisitions.

One-line verdict: ADEQUATE — a well-laddered, investment-grade balance sheet that survives a bad year, but carries enough cyclical leverage (~3.9× net debt/owner earnings) that it is "adequate," not "fortress." an independent research service's Financial Strength A reflects the laddering and access; our owner-earnings lens keeps us honest that a deep, prolonged refining trough would pressure it. This carries into the margin of safety: cyclical leverage means we demand a wider discount, not a narrower one.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Capital allocation across the six uses (10-K MD&A + cash-flow statement):
1. Reinvest — $2.2B/yr, tilted to Midstream growth (higher-ROIC than refining). Sensible: they are steering capital toward the stable, higher-return segment. Good.
2. Acquire — 2025 was busy: WRB (remaining 50%, $1.3B), Coastal Bend NGL ($2.2B), Dos Picos. Funded largely by $3.5B of asset sales (Germany/Austria, Coop, GCX). This is portfolio high-grading — selling lower-return international retail to fund integrated NGL. Directionally rational, though the $948M WRB impairment taken the same year they bought the other half is an uncomfortable signal on price paid.
3. Pay down debt — targeting total debt to $17B by end-2027 (from $19.7B). Good discipline.
4. Dividends — raised again to $1.27/qtr (Feb 2026), a 15% CAGR since the 2012 spin; VL projects a ~52% payout by 2029–31. Secure and growing.
5. Buybacks — since 2012, $22.7B spent repurchasing 248M shares at ~$91.5 average cost. Share count fell from ~518M (2016) to ~401M (2025) — a ~23% reduction. Judged on the manual's rule (Ch. 9, price is everything): buying at an average ~$91 when normalized value is ~$95 was value-neutral-to-accretive historically; but repurchasing at today's $176 — well above our normalized IV — would destroy per-share value. The 2025 pace already slowed to $1.2B (from $3.5–4.0B). Watch this: buybacks here are only friendly if paused at these prices.
6. Share issuance — minimal (only ~2.4M shares/yr for comp). Disciplined; no stock-funded empire-building.

One-dollar test (Ch. 9). Retained earnings should create ≥$1 of value each. With mid-cycle ROIC ≈ cost of capital, PSX's retained dollars are roughly break-even on value creation — which argues for returning cash (dividends + buybacks below IV) over reinvesting in low-return refining, and for the Midstream tilt where incremental ROIC is higher. Management's stated policy — return >50% of OCF (ex-WC) to shareholders — is the right instinct for a business whose reinvestment economics are only average.

Candor. The 10-K discloses the WRB impairment, the Propel loss, and the LA-refinery wind-down plainly. CEO Mark Lashier's strategic targets (world-class ops, $17B debt, >50% payout) are specific and measurable. Verdict: a competent, shareholder-oriented steward — the concern is not honesty but the temptation to keep buying back stock at cyclical-high prices.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

A cyclical commodity refiner does not deserve a single-point DCF — the inputs (crack spreads) are unforecastable. The manual (Ch. 7, 14) is emphatic: normalize, then capitalize a mid-cycle figure, and cross-check. For a business holding large operating segments with different economics, we also run a sum-of-the-parts. We reconcile the two.

Method 1 — Normalized mid-cycle earnings × multiple (primary)

Anchor: normalized EPS ≈ $9.00 (the 2018–2025 ex-COVID and 2021–25 averages both cluster near $9.5; VL's 2026/2027 estimates of $7.25/$8.30 sit below mid-cycle because refining is still in a trough; the 2029–31 target of $11.35 sits above). We use $9.00 as base, $8.5 low, $9.5 high.

What multiple does PSX deserve? From first principles: a business with ROIC ≈ cost of capital, no pricing power in its core, a narrow asset moat, and a secular headwind earns a below-market multiple. Cross-checks:
- Historical: VL median P/E is 20 on the sheet, but that median is distorted by trough-year EPS (a small denominator inflates the ratio); on normalized EPS PSX has historically changed hands around 8–11×.
- Peers (Petroleum Integrated): CVX 17.7×, BP 8.3× (S&O portfolios). PSX, being more refining-weighted and lower-quality than CVX but more integrated than a pure refiner, belongs between — call it 9–12×.
- Quality-justified: an average-ROIC cyclical with a growing, stable Midstream core justifies ~10–11× normalized earnings — a premium to a pure refiner (Valero/Marathon historically 6–8×) for the toll-road Midstream, a discount to CVX for the weaker overall returns.

Central estimate ≈ 10.5× × $9.00 ≈ $95/share.

The three universal cross-checks (Ch. 14), as arithmetic

  1. No-growth floor = normalized owner earnings ÷ r. Using OE ≈ $4,800M and r = 9%: $4,800 ÷ 0.09 = $53,333M ÷ 401M ≈ $133/share. Interpretation: if PSX never grows another dollar of owner earnings, the capitalized stream is ~$133 — below the $176 price. Even giving full credit to owner earnings, the price already assumes real growth. On the stricter owner-earnings reading (≈$8.7/sh, treating more of D&A as maintenance), the floor is ~$97 — far below price.
  2. Implied multiple = value ÷ normalized earnings. Our base IV $95 ÷ $9.00 normalized EPS ≈ 10.6× — defensible for this quality. The market's price $176 ÷ $9.00 = 19.6× normalized earnings — a multiple you would pay for a durable-growth franchise, not an average-ROIC cyclical refiner.
  3. Owner-earnings yield vs the long bond. At $176, owner-earnings yield = $4,800M ÷ ($176 × 401M) = $4,800 ÷ $70,576 = 6.8%. Against a 4.86% 30-yr Treasury, that is only a ~2-point premium for a volatile, no-pricing-power commodity business whose "coupon" can fall by half in a trough. On normalized net income ($3.5B) the earnings yield at price is just 5.0% — barely above the risk-free rate. The gravity of Ch. 12 (rates at 4.86% and possibly rising) pulls this valuation down, not up.

Discount rate as gravity (Ch. 12)

r = long rate + cyclicality premium = 4.86% + ~4.1% ≈ 9%. Given the "higher-for-longer, possibly higher" regime (Fed removed cut guidance, PCE 4.1%), if anything the honest r is 9–10%, which lowers IV. A DCF here would be false precision — the crack-spread inputs aren't trustworthy — so we lean on normalized-multiple and SOTP, and use the no-growth floor as the DCF-substitute sensitivity.

Triangulation

  • Method 1 (normalized multiple): $77–$104, central $95.
  • Method 2 (SOTP): ~$92.
  • Sanity vs an independent research service: VL's 18-month target midpoint is $199 and 2029–31 low is $150 — but those are price-momentum targets on optimistic estimates, not intrinsic-value estimates; we note them and set them aside, as the manual warns against anchoring to Mr. Market's targets.

Triangulated intrinsic value: low $78, base $95, high $115 per share. (We nudge the high to $115 to give full credit to a stronger mid-cycle and the Midstream re-rating, but even the high end sits well below the $176 price.)

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Put the live price beside the range:

  • Live price: $176.42 (equity_atlas, 2026-07-04).
  • Base IV $95. Discount/(premium) = ($95 − $176.42) ÷ $95 = −86% — i.e., the price is an 86% premium to base intrinsic value.
  • Against the high IV $115: still a ~53% premium ($176.42 ÷ $115 = 1.53×).
  • Against even the no-growth floor on full owner earnings ($133): the price is a ~33% premium.

There is no margin of safety here — the margin is negative. The manual's cornerstone (Ch. 13) requires buying far below the conservative end of the range; PSX trades far above even the generous end. Where does safety sit? For PSX it can only come from the purchase discount (a cyclical average-ROIC business has little "safety in the quality itself," per Ch. 13's reconciliation) — and today there is no discount, only a premium.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational? Euphoric on PSX. The stock is up 64% in a year (VL total return), the energy sector re-rated hard (+19% YTD), and the market is paying ~19.6× normalized earnings and ~17.4× a soft trailing year for a business whose refining core just lost money. The irony: even an independent research service, bullish enough to set a $199 target, concedes "long-term capital appreciation potential lies considerably below the an independent research service median" because the shares "are already trading well within our raised 3- to 5-year Target Price Range." Mr. Market has already paid for the recovery and the growth. That is precisely when the disciplined owner does nothing.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: GOOD (not wonderful) — a well-run integrated downstream operator with a genuine, narrow Midstream moat, but average through-cycle ROIC and no pricing power in its commodity core.

Verdict: a good business at a rich price — ADMIRE AND WAIT (avoid at $176). This is the textbook "fair company / wonderful price" inverted into "good company / demanding price." At ~$95 base IV against $176, the price embeds a recovery-plus-growth scenario with no cushion for the next trough — and cyclicals always have a next trough.

THE ONE QUESTION, answered. Through a full cycle PSX earns roughly $9/share (owner earnings ~$12/sh), worth ~$95 at a defensible ~10.5× — and no, that is not conspicuously more than $176; it is conspicuously less. The market is asking a franchise price for a cyclical.

Open questions the honest analyst still carries:
1. Does the Midstream NGL build-out durably re-rate PSX toward a higher blended multiple (a genuine bull case that could lift base IV)?
2. How deep and long is the next refining trough, and does the energy transition permanently compress mid-cycle crack spreads below the historical band we normalized on?
3. Will management resist buying back stock at 19× normalized earnings, or spend the return-of-capital budget destroying per-share value?

Universal lessons this case teaches:
1. Normalize before you capitalize. A cyclical's GAAP year tells you where you are in the cycle, not what it earns — capitalizing $6.44 or $18.25 both mislead. The −$9-to-+$18 EPS swing is the single most important fact in the file.
2. A cheap-looking P/E can be a trap in both directions. PSX's "17.4× live P/E" looks reasonable, but it sits on soft-year earnings; on peak earnings it would look absurdly cheap, on trough earnings absurdly dear. Only the normalized denominator is honest.
3. Price is everything, even for a good business. The same PSX at $95 would be a buy with a real margin of safety; at $176 it is a pass. Quality justifies a fair price, never any price (Ch. 13).

Reminder to the class: this is a teaching case in applying the method, not a recommendation to buy or sell PSX.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with PSX's own numbers)

  • Intrinsic value (IV). Discounted value of all future owner earnings. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. PSX triangulated: low $78 / base $95 / high $115.
  • Owner earnings. Cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. OE = Net income + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. PSX ≈ $3,500 + $3,000 − $1,700 − $0 = $4,800M ≈ $12.0/sh.
  • Normalized (mid-cycle) earnings. The through-cycle average, not a peak or trough. PSX normalized EPS ≈ $9.00 (vs GAAP $10.79, VL-adjusted $6.44, peak $18.25, trough −$9.06).
  • Maintenance vs growth capex. Maintenance = spend to stand still; growth = spend to expand. PSX 2026 budget splits explicitly: ~$1.1B sustaining + $1.3B growth.
  • ROIC. NOPAT ÷ invested capital. PSX ≈ $3,850M ÷ $49,000M ≈ 7.9% mid-cycle (vs ~9% cost of capital → value-neutral).
  • Cost of capital / discount rate (r). Long rate + risk premium. PSX: 4.86% (30-yr Treasury) + ~4.1% cyclicality premium = 9%.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value. OE ÷ r. PSX = $4,800M ÷ 0.09 = $53.3B ÷ 401M ≈ $133/sh — the floor, itself below the $176 price.
  • Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest. PSX normalized ≈ $5,000M ÷ $1,039M ≈ ~5×.
  • Net debt / owner earnings. (Total debt − cash) ÷ OE. PSX = $18,600M ÷ $4,800M ≈ 3.9×.
  • Current ratio. Current assets ÷ current liabilities. PSX = $17,271 ÷ $13,326 = 1.30.
