Salesforce, Inc. (CRM) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Analyst's write-up · July 14, 2026 · Live price $167.56

I work in the Graham–Buffett tradition, and I lean on its tools openly: Graham's margin of safety, his Mr. Market allegory, his refusal to pay up; Buffett's business eye — economic moats, owner earnings, management judged as partners. Those ideas are my foundation, and I credit them by name. But the analysis here, the arithmetic, and the conclusion are mine, and I own the result.

Salesforce is a case I have wanted to teach for a long time, because it forces a student to confront the single most consequential accounting judgment in modern equity analysis — whether stock-based compensation is a real cost — and because the answer changes the valuation by roughly a third. Almost every published number you will see about this company depends on which side of that question the author stands. Let us find out which side survives contact with the filings.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Salesforce sells software that companies rent rather than own. If you are a business with a sales force, a customer-service desk, a marketing department, or an online store, you need a system that records who your customers are, what they bought, what they complained about, and what you should sell them next. Salesforce hosts that system on its own computers and charges you an annual subscription per user — per "seat" — to log in. Founded in 1999, it pioneered the idea that business software should arrive through a browser rather than a CD-ROM, and it is now, in its own description, "a global leader in customer relationship management technology" (10-K filed 2026-03-02, Item 1, Business).

The revenue model is simple and worth stating precisely, because everything downstream depends on it. Of fiscal 2026's $41,525 million of total revenue, $39,388 million — about 95% — was "subscription and support," with the remaining $2,137 million from professional services (10-K, Item 8, consolidated statements of operations). The company invoices customers in advance, generally in annual installments, and collects before it delivers the service (10-K, Item 7, "Seasonal Nature of Unearned Revenue"). Hold on to that fact. It is the most economically important sentence in the whole filing, and I will come back to it when we look at the balance sheet.

A brief note on the calendar, because it trips up everyone who reads this company for the first time. Salesforce's fiscal year ends January 31, and it names the year for the calendar year in which it ends. So "fiscal 2026" ran from February 1, 2025 to January 31, 2026 (10-K, Note 1). The latest 10-K, filed March 2, 2026, covers that year. The latest 10-Q, filed May 28, 2026, covers the quarter ended April 30, 2026 — which Salesforce calls the first quarter of fiscal 2027. I will use those labels, and I will always give you the actual date alongside.

Management reports one operating segment, on the grounds that nearly everything runs on the same platform and the chief executive assesses the business on consolidated net income (10-K, Note 1, "Segments"). That is a genuine analytical handicap: we cannot see what Slack earns, or Tableau, or MuleSoft, or the newly acquired Informatica. We are handed one consolidated number and asked to trust it. The filing does disclose subscription revenue by offering (10-K, Item 7), which is the next best thing:

Marketing and Commerce grew 3%. The 23% grower is the bucket that contains Slack and the newly acquired Informatica, and the footnote tells us $388 million of that bucket's revenue came from Informatica, which Salesforce bought in November 2025 (10-K, Item 7, footnote 2). Strip that out and the bucket grew (8,882 − 388) ÷ 7,247 − 1 = 17.2%. The core franchise is growing at single digits. The renaming exercise puts an AI word on the front of businesses growing 8%.

The one question that decides this case. Salesforce generated $14,996 million of operating cash flow in fiscal 2026 (10-K, Item 8, consolidated statements of cash flows) and paid its employees $3,509 million in stock (10-K, Item 8, statements of operations, footnote 2). Depending on whether you treat that stock as a cost or as a costless non-cash add-back, this is either a business earning roughly $14.4 billion of free cash on $41.5 billion of revenue, or one earning roughly $10.9 billion. Is the stock Salesforce hands its employees a real cost to me as an owner — and if it is, is what remains enough to justify $167.56 a share? Everything else in this seminar is in service of that question.

The circle-of-competence gate. Chapter 4 of my method manual insists I ask, before anything else, whether I can genuinely forecast this business's economics a decade out. Let me be honest about where the line falls, because I think it falls in an unusual place here.

I can understand how Salesforce makes money. It is not complicated: recurring subscriptions, sold to large enterprises, billed annually in advance, with an attrition rate the company puts at approximately 8% (10-K, Item 7). I can read the contract book: total remaining performance obligation — all future revenue under signed contract not yet recognized — stood at approximately $72.4 billion at January 31, 2026, up 14% (10-K, Item 7). I can see roughly 24 months of revenue already under contract. That is a more forecastable business than most.

What I cannot forecast with confidence is what artificial intelligence does to a per-seat pricing model over ten years. Salesforce charges per human user. If AI agents make each human three times as productive, a customer may need fewer seats — and Salesforce's revenue falls even as its product improves. Salesforce's own answer is to sell the agents itself, under the Agentforce brand. An independent research service names the fear directly: AI "may reduce the number of customer seats at some companies by making each worker more productive. Still, this has not been seen at scale" (an independent research service industry report, Computer Software, 2026-05-01). The industry has a nickname for the fear — the "SaaS-pocalypse."

I am going to take an unusual position on the gate, and I want to justify it rather than assert it. The business itself sits inside my circle; the ten-year seat question sits outside anyone's. So I will proceed — but I will refuse to resolve the AI question in either direction, I will carry the uncertainty in conservative owner-earnings inputs and in a wide intrinsic-value range rather than pretending a discount rate can price it, and I will demand a wider margin of safety than a business with this cash flow would otherwise need. If at the end the arithmetic only works when I assume the AI question resolves favorably, then the honest answer is that the case fails the gate after all, and I will say so.

PRINCIPLE. A stock is a piece of a business, and you value only what you can understand. The boundary of the circle matters more than its size — and the disciplined move when a genuine unknowable sits inside an otherwise knowable business is to widen the range and the required discount, never to narrow them with a more confident-looking model.

THE MOAT — IS IT DURABLE?

This is the gate that can end the analysis before I spend an hour on the arithmetic, so I want to be skeptical rather than admiring. Chapter 5 of the manual is blunt: a moat you cannot name is a moat you do not have.

What I can name in Salesforce's favour. The source of advantage here is switching costs, and they are real. A large enterprise that has run its sales organization on Salesforce for a decade has built custom objects, custom workflows, integrations to a dozen other systems, and a workforce trained on the interface. Ripping it out means re-training thousands of people and re-plumbing the company's data while continuing to sell. The evidence for this is quantitative, not anecdotal, and it is the strongest number in the filing: attrition of approximately 8% (10-K, Item 7), disclosed again as approximately 8% at April 30, 2026 (10-Q filed 2026-05-28, Item 2). An 8% annual attrition rate implies an average customer relationship life of roughly 1 ÷ 0.08 = 12.5 years. Customers do not leave. That is a moat's fingerprint.

A second source is scale in distribution. Salesforce spent $14,345 million on sales and marketing in fiscal 2026 — 35% of revenue (10-K, Item 7). A start-up with a better mousetrap cannot fund a sales force capable of reaching the Fortune 500's procurement departments. And a third, weaker one is the ecosystem: consultants, trained administrators, and an app marketplace, which raise the cost of leaving a little further.

Now the skeptical half, which I find more persuasive than most write-ups do. Three things trouble me.

First — Salesforce's own words. The 10-K's competition section opens like this: "The market for our service offerings is highly competitive, rapidly evolving and fragmented, and subject to changing technology with low barriers to entry, shifting customer needs and frequent introductions of new products and services" (10-K, Item 1, "Competition"; emphasis mine). That is the company, under the securities laws, telling me its market has low barriers to entry. It then names as competitors "AI-native companies and emerging startups that leverage generative AI and large language models as the core foundation of their architecture, offering highly specialized, autonomous, or automated solutions that may bypass traditional business process workflows or displace established user interfaces" (same section). A moat protects against a rival building a better version of your product. It does not protect against a rival making your product's category unnecessary — and that is precisely the threat Salesforce itself describes.

Second — switching costs are an exit tax, not a growth engine. High switching costs explain why Sales and Service grew 8% instead of shrinking. They do not explain how Salesforce grows. Note also what the MD&A says about the source of that 8%: "volume-driven increases from new business... Pricing was not a significant driver of the increase in revenues for the period" (10-K, Item 7, Revenues). Read that again. A business with real pricing power raises prices. Salesforce, in a year of 3% general inflation and with customers locked in for 12.5 years on average, told its owners that price contributed essentially nothing. That is a moat that keeps customers in the building but cannot reach into their wallets. It is a defensive moat, not an offensive one.

Third — the moat has been bought, not built. We will see the numbers shortly, but the direction of travel is on the balance sheet: goodwill of $57,941 million at January 31, 2026, up from $51,283 million a year earlier and $48,620 million the year before that (10-K, Note 7, changes in the carrying amounts of goodwill). Much of what looks like Salesforce's competitive perimeter — Slack, Tableau, MuleSoft, and now Informatica — was purchased.

My verdict on the gate: the moat is real but NARROW, and it is defensive. It reliably protects the ~$39 billion of subscription revenue Salesforce already has from being taken by a direct competitor. It does not confer pricing power, and it offers no protection at all against the one threat that matters most — a change in the unit of consumption from seats to something else. I am not ending the analysis here, because a narrow moat around $39 billion of 12.5-year revenue is worth valuing. But I am going to carry this judgment into every section that follows, and it is why I will insist on a wide discount at the end rather than accepting a fair price.

PRINCIPLE. Durable earning power must be defended by something real, and the test of "real" is whether the customer would pay more rather than leave. Switching costs that hold a customer but cannot raise a price are a floor under revenue, not a franchise — and a moat that must be repurchased every few years with shareholders' money is being rented, not owned.

Three things leap out.

The first is that this balance sheet is mostly not a balance sheet of a business — it is a receipt. Goodwill of $57,941 million plus acquired intangibles of $6,815 million equals $64,756 million of purchase premium: money paid to previous owners of other companies over and above the fair value of the assets acquired. Set that against total equity of $59,142 million and you get tangible book value of 59,142 − 64,756 = −$5,614 million. Negative. Every dollar of Salesforce's accounting net worth, and $5.6 billion more, is the price it paid to buy other companies. An independent research service reaches the same figure independently and reports it in its footnote (C): "Includes intangibles: In 2025 [their label for FY2026], $64.756 bill., $69.71 per share" (an independent research service, 2026-05-01).

Now, the manual is emphatic that this is not, by itself, an indictment (ch. 7): a franchise carries economic goodwill far above its tangible capital, so book value understates a great business and overstates a poor one, and you must never anchor value to book. I am not anchoring to book. But there is a sharp distinction between economic goodwill — the excess earning power a franchise generates over its tangible capital, which never appears on the books — and accounting goodwill, which is a record of cash that left the building. Salesforce's $57.9 billion is the second kind. It tells me nothing about whether the business is good and everything about what was paid. We will test it in the next section by asking what it earns.

The second is the current ratio, and it looks alarming until you understand it. Current assets ÷ current liabilities = 28,222 ÷ 37,118 = 0.76. Graham would not have let a stock with a current ratio below 1.0 through the door. But look at what is inside current liabilities: unearned revenue of $24,317 million — 66% of the total. Unearned revenue is not a debt in any economic sense. It is cash customers have already paid Salesforce for service it has not yet delivered. It will never be settled in cash; it will be settled by turning on a server. Strip it out and current assets of $28,222 million cover the remaining current liabilities of 37,118 − 24,317 = $12,801 million by 2.2 times. Graham's ratio was built for a manufacturer whose current liabilities were bills owed to suppliers. Applied mechanically to a business paid in advance, it libels it.

The third is the best fact about Salesforce, and it hides in that same line. Customers fund this business. Unearned revenue of $24,317 million exceeds the entire tangible asset base. Salesforce collects a year's fees up front, then spends the year delivering. It has negative working capital by design — the definition of a capital-light franchise, and the reason the property and equipment line is a mere $3,120 million against $41.5 billion of revenue. Salesforce does not own the computers it runs on; it rents them, disclosing $16.7 billion of total commitments to infrastructure service providers and others, of which $3.7 billion falls due within twelve months (10-K, Item 7, "Contractual Obligations"). That is why capital expenditure is trivially small. Keep this in mind — it is the pivot of the entire case.

The earnings-quality scan

Chapter 7 of the manual gives me a list of red flags. Salesforce trips three of them, and I want to take each seriously and then say how much it actually matters.

Red flag 1: "one-time" restructuring charges that recur. The manual quotes the 1998 letter on this precise trick — a chunk of costs properly attributable to several years dumped into a single quarter. Look at the restructuring line: $988 million (FY2024), $461 million (FY2025), $586 million (FY2026) — three consecutive years, $2,035 million in total (10-K, Item 8, statements of operations). In the fiscal 2026 MD&A, management wrote: "We do not expect to incur significant additional charges in connection with our restructuring initiatives in the near term" (10-K, Item 7). The very next quarter, they took another $80 million (10-Q, condensed statements of operations, three months ended April 30, 2026), and the balance sheet still carries an expectation of "approximately $220 million to $250 million in future cash payments related to our restructuring initiatives" (10-K, Item 7, "Other Future Obligations"). Four straight years is not a restructuring. It is a cost of doing business — the annual price of rebalancing a sales force. I will not treat restructuring as non-recurring, and I will not exclude it from operating income. This matters because management's own adjusted figures do exclude it.

Red flag 2: mark-to-model gains. The manual is scathing about earnings produced by fair-value marks where no real market exists — "mark-to-model degenerates into what I would call mark-to-myth" (2002 letter, quoted in ch. 7). Now read what the MD&A actually says about that $1,017 million gain: "the gain on our strategic investment portfolio was primarily driven by unrealized gains on privately held equity investments of $1.5 billion partially offset by impairments on privately held investments of $496 million. Our mark-to-market unrealized gains in fiscal 2026 were driven largely by $1.2 billion in gains from one privately held equity investment" (10-K, Item 7, "Other Income and Expenses"). And the accounting policy note tells us how such things are valued: "Valuations of privately held securities are inherently complex and require judgment due to the lack of readily available market data... valued using significant unobservable inputs or data in an inactive market" (10-K, Note 1, "Strategic Investments").

So: $1.2 billion of Salesforce's $7,457 million of net income — 16% of it — came from marking up one private company, using unobservable inputs, in an inactive market. That is 15.4% of reported net income conjured by a model. It is not fraud and I am not alleging any; the accounting is entirely proper under GAAP. But it is exactly the class of earnings the manual tells me to discount, and it is why I excluded the gains line from owner earnings. I will still carry the portfolio as an asset — but at a haircut.

Red flag 3: the adjusted-earnings culture — and this is the big one. The 10-K itself is admirably GAAP-only; management does not push a non-GAAP operating margin inside the annual report, and I give them credit for that. But the adjusted figure dominates how this company is discussed, and I can reconstruct it exactly from the filed footnotes. The standard software add-backs are stock-based compensation, amortization of acquired intangibles, and restructuring:

GAAP income from operations: $8,331 million → margin 8,331 ÷ 41,525 = 20.1%
+ Stock-based compensation: $3,509 million (10-K, Item 8, statements of operations, footnote 2: 553 + 1,162 + 1,287 + 478 + 29)
+ Amortization of acquired intangibles: $1,687 million (footnote 1: 692 in cost of revenues + 995 in sales and marketing)
+ Restructuring: $586 million (statements of operations)
= "Non-GAAP" income from operations: $14,113 million → margin 14,113 ÷ 41,525 = 34.0%

The gap is 34.0% − 20.1% = 13.9 percentage points of margin, and $5,782 million of "profit." And of that bridge, stock-based compensation alone is 3,509 ÷ 41,525 = 8.45 points — 3,509 ÷ 5,782 = 61% of the entire gap.

You do not have to take my reconstruction on faith, because an independent research service independently publishes the adjusted figures and flags them. Their FY2026 column shows "Net Profit ($mill) 11,969" and "Earnings per sh 12.52" — against GAAP net income of $7,457 million and GAAP diluted EPS of $7.80. Their footnote (B) states plainly: "Diluted earnings. Non-GAAP starting in fiscal 2025" (an independent research service, 2026-05-01). Check it: 11,969 ÷ 956 diluted shares = $12.52. It ties exactly. The difference between an independent research service's earnings and Salesforce's earnings is $11,969 − $7,457 = $4,512 million of costs that GAAP charged and an independent research service put back.

This cascades. An independent research service's headline "P/E RATIO 14.5" is 186.27 ÷ 12.52 = 14.9 (they use a slightly different date). The GAAP P/E at their price was 186.27 ÷ 7.80 = 23.9. Their "Return on Shr. Equity 20.2%" is 11,969 ÷ 59,142 = 20.2%; the GAAP return on equity is 7,457 ÷ 59,142 = 12.6%. And the "ROE% 20" cell in my own equity_atlas.xlsx is that same non-GAAP number, imported. A stock that looks like a 14.5× P/E with a 20% return on equity is, on the accountant's numbers, a 21.5× P/E with a 12.6% return on equity. Same company, same day. The entire apparent cheapness of Salesforce is an artifact of an adjustment.

PRINCIPLE. The statements are evidence and cash is the truth-teller — but "cash" must mean cash after every cost, including the ones paid in paper. When a company's headline metrics and its audited statements tell two different stories, the analyst's job is not to pick the friendlier one; it is to compute the gap, name what is inside it, and decide each item on its merits.

Two observations, and they cut in opposite directions.

The good. Salesforce spent from 2017 to 2023 as a company that could not earn a profit — an operating margin of 3.3% on $31 billion of revenue in FY2023, and net income of $208 million. Then, under pressure from activist investors, it produced 20.1% in three years. That is a genuine transformation, and I do not want to be cynical about it.

The deceleration. Revenue growth has fallen off a cliff, and the compound rates make it plain:

5-year (FY2021→FY2026): (41,525 ÷ 21,252)^(1/5) − 1 = 14.3%
3-year (FY2023→FY2026): (41,525 ÷ 31,352)^(1/3) − 1 = 9.8%
2-year (FY2024→FY2026): (41,525 ÷ 37,895)^(1/2) − 1 = 9.2%
Latest year: 41,525 ÷ 37,895 − 1 = 9.6%

And the reported 9.6% flatters the underlying business twice over. Management discloses that foreign currency added about one percentage point (10-K, Item 7, Highlights), and Informatica contributed $399 million (10-K, Item 7, Revenues). So organic, constant-currency growth was approximately (41,525 − 399) ÷ 37,895 − 1 = 8.5%, less about one point of currency ≈ 7.5%. Salesforce is a mid-single-digit-to-7% organic grower wearing a growth company's reputation.

The contract book confirms the trend rather than contradicting it. Total remaining performance obligation grew 14% to $72.4 billion at January 31, 2026 (10-K, Item 7) — but by April 30, 2026 the growth rate had slipped to 11%, to $67.9 billion, with current RPO up 14% (10-Q, Item 2, Highlights), and even that includes a full quarter of acquired Informatica revenue and about two points of currency help (10-Q, Item 2). The forward book is decelerating too.

Was the margin expansion borrowed from the future?

This is one of the sharpest questions in the case, because cost cuts that gut research and development buy three good years and then a decade of decline. I expected to find evidence of that. I did not, and intellectual honesty requires me to say so.

Research and development held its ground. R&D was $4,906 million (14.1% of revenue) in FY2024, $5,493 million (14.5%) in FY2025, and $5,993 million (14.4%) in FY2026 (10-K, Item 7, Operating Expenses). In absolute dollars, R&D grew 22% over two years. Management states it "will likely remain consistent as a percentage of revenue over time" (10-K, Item 7). Nothing was gutted.

Headcount grew. Salesforce had 72,682 employees at January 31, 2024 (10-K filed 2024-03-06, Item 1), 76,453 at January 31, 2025 (10-K filed 2025-03-05, Item 1), and 83,334 at January 31, 2026 (10-K filed 2026-03-02, Item 1). That is up 15% over two years, in a period widely described as one of layoffs. Some of the FY2026 increase is acquired (Informatica came with its own workforce), but the direction is unambiguous.

So where did 5.7 margin points come from? Sales and marketing: 12,877 ÷ 34,857 = 36.9% of revenue in FY2024, falling to 14,345 ÷ 41,525 = 34.5% in FY2026 — 2.4 points. Cost of revenues fell from 24.5% to 22.3% — 2.2 points. That is operating leverage on a fixed-ish cost base plus better sales productivity, not vandalism.

My conclusion: the margin expansion is real and mostly durable. But note the ceiling. Revenue per employee was 34,857 ÷ 72,682 = $479,500 in FY2024 and 41,525 ÷ 83,334 = $498,300 in FY2026 — up just 3.9% in two years. Salesforce is not becoming structurally more efficient per person; it grew revenue slightly faster than it grew heads. That is a much smaller engine than the margin chart implies, and it is why I will not underwrite continued rapid margin expansion in the valuation.

Owner earnings — the number that decides the case

Chapter 7 defines it: Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital — the cash an owner could withdraw without weakening the business.

The formula's most treacherous word is "non-cash charges," because a mechanical reading says to add back stock-based compensation. The manual forecloses this in terms I will quote directly, because it is the crux of this entire seminar:

"Stock-based compensation is a non-cash charge, so a mechanical reading of 'add back non-cash charges' would restore it — and that is exactly wrong. It is the one non-cash charge you do not add back... Depreciation is added back because its real cost is captured elsewhere in the formula, as the maintenance capital expenditure that actually replaces the assets... Stock-based compensation has no such offset — its real cost is the dilution, the ownership handed to employees this period in place of cash, and that value has already left the owners' hands."
— How to Value a Business, ch. 7

Let me make that concrete, because a student should feel it rather than accept it. In fiscal 2026 Salesforce's employees performed $3,509 million of work. Salesforce did not pay them in cash. It paid them by handing over a slice of your company. Your claim on every future dollar of Salesforce's earnings is now smaller than it was. The tempting rebuttal — "but the company kept the cash!" — is a double-count: the cash the business retained is exactly the ownership you surrendered. You would never add back a salary. This is a salary paid in shares. Paying an employee in stock is cash compensation financed by issuing equity, and the issuance is the cost.

If you want the empirical proof rather than the argument, Salesforce provides it. In fiscal 2026 the company issued 17 million shares to employees and had to spend real cash buying stock back to stop the count from rising. At its own average repurchase price of $254.21 (10-K, Item 7, Share Repurchase Program), mopping up that dilution cost 17 × $254.21 = $4,322 million — more than the $3,509 million GAAP charged. The accounting expense, if anything, understates the cash cost, because GAAP measures grants at their fair value on the grant date while the company must repurchase at the market price today. The idea that this cost is imaginary does not survive five minutes with the cash flow statement.

So, my build:

Net cash provided by operating activities: $14,996 million (10-K, Item 8, statements of cash flows)
− Capital expenditures: $594 million (same statement)
= Free cash flow: $14,402 million
− Stock-based compensation, not added back: $3,509 million (same statement)
= Owner earnings, fiscal 2026 as reported: $10,893 million

Three judgments I owe you before I use it.

(a) Is $594 million really maintenance capex? It looks impossibly low — 1.4% of revenue, against D&A of $3,631 million. Ordinarily a business whose capex runs at one-sixth of depreciation is quietly liquidating itself. Here it is not, and the reason is structural: most of that $3,631 million is not depreciation of Salesforce's own plant. It is $1,687 million of acquired-intangible amortization (10-K, Item 8, footnote 1) plus amortization of right-of-use lease assets (10-K, Item 8, cash flow footnote 1). Salesforce does not buy data centres; it rents compute, and that rent is already inside operating expenses — hence the $16.7 billion of infrastructure and lease commitments disclosed in "Contractual Obligations" (10-K, Item 7). The maintenance capex is genuinely tiny because the maintenance capital is somebody else's. I accept $594 million, and note that it rose nowhere near revenue — capex was $736 million in FY2024 and $658 million in FY2025 (10-K, Item 8), so if anything, the trend is down. I am not adjusting it.

(b) Working capital. Already handled: a net drain of $781 million in FY2026, already inside operating cash flow, and no growth-driven inflation of the number. No adjustment.

(c) The acquisitions — and here I have to make a real judgment. Salesforce spent $9,268 million on business combinations in FY2026, $2,734 million in FY2025, and $82 million in FY2024 (10-K, Item 8, statements of cash flows) — a three-year average of (82 + 2,734 + 9,268) ÷ 3 = $4,028 million a year. Buffett's owner-earnings definition covers "capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment," not acquisitions; acquisitions are capital allocation, judged by the one-dollar test, not an owner-earnings deduction. So the orthodox treatment — and my anchor — excludes them. But the honest analyst notices the tension with the moat section: if Salesforce's market has "low barriers to entry" and AI-native startups "may bypass traditional business process workflows," then some of that $4 billion a year is not optional expansion. It is defence — buying the disruptors before they disrupt. I will not fold this into the anchor, because I cannot prove it. I will make it the low case of my valuation, where you can see exactly what it costs.

Normalizing, and the pro-forma the 10-Q forces on me

Now a step most write-ups of Salesforce published this summer will get wrong, and it is the single most consequential piece of arithmetic in this seminar. The 10-K is stale. Between the fiscal year-end and today, Salesforce completely rebuilt its capital structure, and the 10-Q filed May 28, 2026 documents it:

  • In March 2026 Salesforce issued $25.0 billion of senior unsecured notes with maturities from 2028 to 2066, netting $24.8 billion (10-Q, Note 8, "March 2026 Notes").
  • It used the proceeds to fund a $25.0 billion accelerated share repurchase (10-Q, Note 9).
  • It refinanced the Informatica facilities into a $6.0 billion five-year term loan (10-Q, Note 8).
  • Total carrying value of debt went from $14,439 million at January 31, 2026 to $39,280 million at April 30, 2026 (10-Q, Note 8, debt table). Total stockholders' equity collapsed from $59,142 million to $34,235 million (10-Q, condensed balance sheets).

Owner earnings are earnings after interest — they are the cash available to the owner once the lenders are paid. FY2026's $10,893 million was struck against almost no debt. Applying it to today's share count would be a straightforward fraud on the arithmetic. So I must charge the new interest.

The 10-Q's debt table gives every instrument's principal and contractual rate, so I can compute the run-rate exactly rather than guess (10-Q, Note 8):

March 2026 Notes ($25,000M): 3,500 @ 4.50% + 4,250 @ 4.65% + 3,750 @ 4.90% + 2,750 @ 5.20% + 4,500 @ 5.55% + 1,500 @ 6.40% + 3,750 @ 6.55% + 1,000 @ 6.70% = $1,340 million (weighted-average coupon 5.36%)
Legacy notes ($8,500M, issued 2018/2021): 1,500 @ 3.70% + 1,000 @ 1.50% + 1,500 @ 1.95% + 1,250 @ 2.70% + 2,000 @ 2.90% + 1,250 @ 3.05% = $230 million
2026 Term Loan ($6,000M @ 4.26%) = $256 million
Total run-rate annual interest = $1,825 million on $39,500 million of principal.

Cross-check, because a number this important deserves one. Fiscal 2026's actual interest burden was roughly $230 million (legacy notes) + about a quarter-year of the $6.0 billion Informatica facility ≈ $294 million — which sits right next to the "$276 million" of cash paid for interest disclosed in the 10-K's supplemental cash flow disclosure. ✓ And the 10-Q reports $317 million of interest expense for the quarter ended April 30, 2026 (10-Q, condensed statements of operations) — a quarter in which the March notes were outstanding for only about half the period. A full quarter at my run rate would be 1,825 ÷ 4 = $456 million; the legacy-plus-term-loan piece alone is $121 million, implying the new notes contributed $196 million against a full-quarter $335 million — i.e. about 1.75 months of a 3-month quarter, exactly consistent with a mid-March issue. ✓ The run-rate holds.

Incremental interest vs. FY2026: 1,825 − 294 = $1,531 million pre-tax
After tax at 22%: 1,531 × (1 − 0.22) = $1,194 million
Pro-forma owner earnings: 10,893 − 1,194 = $9,699 million → I will use $9,700 million.

This is my one owner-earnings figure. It is $9,700 million. It appears in every cross-check, every DCF, and the sidecar. Nothing else gets capitalized.

Now the denominator, which the ASR also rewrote:

Shares outstanding at January 31, 2026: 929 million (10-K, Item 8, balance sheet parenthetical)
− ASR initial delivery, March 2026: 103 million at an average $198.34 (10-Q, Note 9)
− Open-market repurchases in Q1 FY2027: 11 million at an average $192.00 (10-Q, Note 9)
+ Employee-plan issuance: ≈ 5 million
= ≈ 820 million outstanding at April 30, 2026

Cross-check: the reported weighted-average basic count for the quarter was 868 million (10-Q, Note 11). Starting at 929 and ending near 820 with the ASR block delivered around the mid-point of the quarter gives a weighted average of roughly 872. ✓ Close enough to trust the derivation.

But 820 is not the finished number. The ASR is only 80% settled: "The initial share delivery represented approximately 80 percent of the total shares expected to be repurchased," and the company separately discloses an unsettled ASR forward component of 36 million shares (10-Q, Notes 9 and 11). Those shares have already been paid for — the full $25.0 billion left in March — and they will be delivered at final settlement. So:

820 − 36 (unsettled ASR forward) + ≈ 6 (dilutive employee awards) = ≈ 790 million go-forward diluted shares.

Owner earnings per share = $9,700M ÷ 790M = $12.28

A note on internal consistency that a careful student should demand. I have expensed stock-based compensation and held the share count flat. Is that a double count? No — and the choice is deliberate. There are two coherent ways to handle employee equity: (a) add SBC back and forecast a rising share count, or (b) charge SBC as the expense it is and hold the count flat, because the ongoing buyback offsets the issuance. I have chosen (b), which is why my count stays at 790 million rather than drifting up. The two approaches converge; mixing them — adding SBC back and holding shares flat, which is what "non-GAAP EPS" does — is the error, and it is the error that produces an independent research service's $12.52.

Returns on capital — the moat's polygraph

This is where the moat either proves itself or is exposed as a story. Chapter 6: ROIC = NOPAT ÷ invested capital, where invested capital = equity + total debt − excess cash.

NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate) = 8,331 × (1 − 0.2167) = $6,526 million
Invested capital (spot, Jan 31, 2026) = 59,142 (equity) + 14,439 (total debt) − 9,565 (cash + marketable securities) = $64,016 million
ROIC (spot) = 6,526 ÷ 64,016 = 10.2%

And on average capital, which is the fairer measure since NOPAT is earned across the year:

Invested capital (Jan 31, 2025) = 61,173 + 8,433 − 14,032 = $55,574 million
Average = (64,016 + 55,574) ÷ 2 = $59,795 million
ROIC (average) = 6,526 ÷ 59,795 = 10.9%

I will call it 10–11%. Set that against my 10% discount rate and you have the whole case in one line: Salesforce earns approximately its cost of capital. Chapter 6 says the decisive comparison is ROIC against r, and that when ROIC merely equals r, growth is value-neutral — it neither creates nor destroys. A company can grow revenue at 10% forever and make its owners precisely nothing.

Return on equity tells the same story once you use the audited numerator: 7,457 ÷ 59,142 = 12.6% — not the 20% in an independent research service and in my atlas, which is the non-GAAP profit divided by GAAP equity, a ratio with a different company in the numerator and the denominator.

But now the most important calculation in this seminar, and the one that turns the whole case around. Chapter 6 contains a rule that most analysts skip: measure ROIC on tangible capital, not on the purchase price. "The managers of the units should be judged by the returns they achieve on the underlying assets; what we pay for a business does not affect the amount of capital its manager has to work with" (1987 letter, quoted in ch. 6). So let me strip out the acquisition premium — the buyer's price of ownership — and ask what capital Salesforce's managers actually have to work with:

Tangible invested capital = 64,016 − 57,941 (goodwill) − 6,815 (acquired intangibles) = −$740 million

Negative. The operating business of Salesforce employs less than zero net capital. Its customers fund it — that $24,317 million of unearned revenue — and it rents its computers. The return on tangible capital is not 10%. It is not 60%, the figure the manual cites for See's Candy, "the prototype of a dream business." It is mathematically undefined, because the denominator is negative. You could not put money into this business if you tried.

Sit with the contradiction, because it is the answer to the case:

  • The operating franchise is extraordinary — better, on this measure, than See's. It is capital-light in the purest sense: 77.7% gross margins, customers who pay a year in advance, an 8% attrition rate, and no plant to feed.
  • The consolidated enterprise earns 10.2% — barely its cost of capital.

The gap between those two facts is $64,756 million of goodwill and acquired intangibles. Nothing else. Salesforce did not build a mediocre business; it built a magnificent one and then spent $64.8 billion of shareholders' money buying other companies, and that is what dragged the return down to the cost of capital. The ROIC of 10.2% is not a verdict on the business. It is a verdict on the prices management paid.

That reframing changes what I am buying and what I must watch. I am not buying a 10%-return business. I am buying a spectacular business attached to a capital allocator who has historically converted its excess returns into goodwill at par. Which means the single variable that decides my return over the next decade is not Agentforce, and not AI. It is whether management stops.

PRINCIPLE. Durable earning power proven by returns on capital above the cost of capital is what you are really buying — but you must measure the return on the capital the operation employs, carrying the acquisition premium separately as the buyer's price of ownership. Consolidated ROIC blends two different questions: "how good is this business?" and "how well did management buy?" Separate them, or you will mistake a great business with a spending problem for a mediocre one — and misdiagnose the cure.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Can this business survive a bad year without being forced to sell assets, gut the franchise, or dilute owners at the worst moment? Twelve weeks ago I would have answered this in one word. Today the answer is more interesting, because Salesforce voluntarily traded a fortress for a leveraged position.

Leverage. On the post-ASR balance sheet (10-Q, Note 8 and condensed balance sheets, April 30, 2026):

Total debt: $39,280 million carrying / $39,500 million principal
Cash and equivalents $8,935M + marketable securities $2,902M = $11,837 million
Net debt = 39,280 − 11,837 = $27,443 million
Net debt ÷ owner earnings = 27,443 ÷ 9,700 = 2.8 years

Judge the burden by the stability of the earnings, not by an absolute rule. Salesforce's earnings are about as stable as corporate earnings get — 95% recurring subscription revenue, 8% attrition, $67.9 billion of contracted backlog. 2.8 years of owner earnings against a contract book of that quality is comfortable. A cyclical could not carry it. This company can.

Coverage.

Interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest = 8,331 ÷ 1,825 = 4.6×

This is the number that has moved most, and it deserves a hard look. A year ago the same calculation was 8,331 ÷ 294 ≈ 28×. Management traded 28× coverage for 4.6× in order to buy back stock. An independent research service, working from the pre-ASR balance sheet, reports "LT interest coverage greater than 25x" and a Financial Strength grade of A (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) — both of which are now stale, and I would not rely on either. 4.6× is adequate, not fortress. An ordinary bad year does not threaten solvency; but the cushion that made this company un-killable is gone, converted into shares repurchased at $198.

Liquidity. The current ratio at April 30, 2026 is 21,613 ÷ 27,502 = 0.79, and at January 31, 2026 it was 28,222 ÷ 37,118 = 0.76. As established, this is an artifact: $20,363 million of the current liabilities is unearned revenue, extinguished by delivering service, not by paying cash. Excluding it: 21,613 ÷ (27,502 − 20,363) = 3.0×. Salesforce also holds an undrawn $5.0 billion revolving credit facility maturing October 2029, with no borrowings outstanding (10-Q, Note 8), and was "in compliance with all debt covenants as of April 30, 2026" (10-Q, Note 8). Liquidity is fine.

The refinancing wall — and this is the genuinely good news. Contractual future principal payments (10-Q, Note 8):

Fiscal period Principal due ($M)
Remaining nine months of FY2027 0
FY2028 0
FY2029 6,000
FY2030 4,250
FY2031 0
Thereafter 29,250
Total 39,500

Nothing is due for nearly two years, and $29,250 million — 74% of the debt — matures beyond fiscal 2031, at fixed rates, stretching to 2066. Whatever I think of the decision to borrow $25 billion, the execution was excellent: management termed this out at fixed rates for forty years and left itself no near-term refinancing risk. There is no scenario in which a closed credit market forces this company's hand. That is the difference between leverage that is dangerous and leverage that is merely expensive, and it matters enormously for the manual's core rule that no outside force should ever be able to interrupt the compounding.

Hidden obligations. Operating and finance leases: $3.6 billion of noncancellable minimum payments, $0.9 billion within twelve months (10-K, Item 7, "Contractual Obligations"). Infrastructure and other commitments: $16.7 billion, with $3.7 billion due within twelve months — this is the real off-balance-sheet item, the rented compute that substitutes for capex, and it is the reason Salesforce's capital expenditure is only $594 million. There is no defined-benefit pension (an independent research service, 2026-05-01), removing an entire class of risk. And Salesforce states plainly: "We do not have any special purpose entities and we do not engage in off-balance sheet financing arrangements" (10-K, Item 7).

One more figure, for completeness rather than alarm: tangible book value at April 30, 2026 is 34,235 − 59,291 (goodwill) − 6,650 (intangibles) = −$31,706 million. Salesforce's accounting net worth is now deeply negative once you remove what it paid for other companies. For a business that needs no tangible capital, this is an accounting curiosity, not a solvency issue — the lenders are secured by $10 billion of annual owner earnings, not by furniture. But it is worth knowing that there is no asset backstop here at all. If the earnings go, nothing is left.

Verdict: ADEQUATE — no longer a fortress, and downgraded by choice rather than by circumstance. Net debt of 2.8 years of owner earnings and 4.6× coverage against a 95%-recurring revenue base with no maturity for two years is a position I can live with. But I want the student to notice what happened: a company with net cash chose to become a company with $27 billion of net debt in order to buy its own shares at $198.34, and the shares now trade at $167.56. The balance sheet did not deteriorate. It was spent.

PRINCIPLE. A great business financed recklessly can still be ruined, so verify staying power before you trust any stream of earnings to persist — and when you test it, test the current balance sheet. A 10-K four months old and a data provider's stale grade will both tell you this company has 25× interest coverage and an A rating. The 10-Q says 4.6×. The most dangerous number in analysis is a good one that has expired.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Chapter 9 asks me to watch how management deploys each dollar of free cash across the six uses, and to judge retention by the one-dollar test: a dollar kept should produce at least a dollar of value — retain only when incremental ROIC ≥ the discount rate, otherwise return it. Here the record is unusually legible, and unusually damning.

The share count over the decade

Intrinsic value is a per-share quantity, and the manual's Dexter Shoe lesson is about what happens when management uses stock as currency. Watch this (CRM_xbrl_facts.json; FY2024–FY2026 tie to the 10-K balance sheets):

707M (FY2017) → 730M → 770M → 893M (FY2020) → 919M → 989M (FY2022) → 981M → 971M → 962M → 929M (FY2026)

From 2017 to 2022, Salesforce increased its share count by 40% — 707 million to 989 million. It printed roughly 282 million shares. It did so to buy companies: the equity line ballooned from $8,230 million to $58,131 million over the same window, and total assets went from $17,585 million to $95,209 million. Salesforce bought its way from a $8.4 billion revenue company to a $26.5 billion revenue company substantially with its own paper, issued at prices set by a market that was, at the time, euphoric about software.

Since FY2022 it has been buying that paper back. And here is the arithmetic that I think teaches this case better than any other single figure.

Buybacks versus dilution: what does a retired share actually cost?

Fiscal 2026 alone (10-K, Item 7, Share Repurchase Program; Item 8, statements of stockholders' equity):

Shares repurchased: 50 million at an average price of $254.21 = $12,721 million
Shares issued to employees: 17 million
Net change in shares outstanding: 962 → 929 = 33 million retired
Gross cost per share bought: 12,721 ÷ 50 = $254.42
Cost per share ACTUALLY retired: 12,721 ÷ 33 = $385.48

Salesforce spent $385 for every share it permanently removed from your ownership — because 34% of everything it bought (17 of 50 million shares) went straight back out the door to employees. The stock trades today at $167.56.

Over three years (10-K, Item 8, statements of cash flows: $7,620M in FY2024, $7,829M in FY2025, $12,596M in FY2026):

Total cash spent on repurchases: $28,045 million
Shares outstanding: 981 (Jan 31, 2023) → 929 (Jan 31, 2026) = 52 million net retired
Cost per net share retired: 28,045 ÷ 52 = $539

$28 billion of shareholders' cash bought a 5.3% reduction in the share count, at an effective price of $539 per share, in a stock now quoted at $167.56. That is the buyback program stripped of its press release. Roughly half of the money was not returning capital to owners at all; it was paying the employees' compensation bill in cash while GAAP recorded it as a share repurchase.

The accelerated share repurchase — the one-dollar test in real time

Then, in March 2026, management did something remarkable (10-Q, Notes 8 and 9). It borrowed $25.0 billion at a weighted-average coupon of 5.36%, fixed, out to 2066 — and spent every dollar buying stock at an average of $198.34, receiving 103 million shares.

Judge it by the manual's rule: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value and destroy it above; buying back at any price is not a virtue. My base intrinsic value, which I will derive in the next section, is $216 a share, so at $198.34 the purchase was — on my own numbers — modestly accretive, and I should say so. But the mark today is unforgiving:

103 million shares × ($198.34 − $167.56) = $3,170 million of value transferred from continuing owners to selling ones, in four months.

And the low case of my valuation range comes in at $140, well below $198.34. So management bet $25 billion of borrowed money, at a fixed 5.36% for forty years, on the proposition that its shares were worth more than $198 — a proposition that requires its own optimistic case to be right. They did not have to be sure. They had to be sure enough to mortgage the balance sheet, and they were. They then authorized a further $50.0 billion in February 2026 (10-Q, Note 9), leaving $22.9 billion still available at April 30, 2026.

Here is what makes this so instructive: management is doing exactly the right thing in kind and getting the price wrong. Returning capital is correct — Salesforce's incremental ROIC of 10.2% barely clears the 10% hurdle, so the one-dollar test says retain nothing and return everything. Buying stock is the right vehicle. But you must buy it cheap, and $198.34 was not cheap enough to be safe. Had they waited four months, the same $25 billion would have retired 149 million shares instead of 129 million.

Acquisitions: disciplined, or the institutional imperative?

The goodwill ledger (10-K, Note 7):

$48,620M (Jan 31, 2024) → +$323M Spiff, +$284M Zoomin, +$1,812M Own, +$244M other → $51,283M (Jan 31, 2025) → +$704M Regrello, +$5,257M Informatica, +$697M other → $57,941M (Jan 31, 2026)

And it did not stop at year-end: in April 2026 Salesforce bought Qualified.com for $1.2 billion, recording $954 million of goodwill and $290 million of intangibles (10-Q, Note 6) — i.e. $1,244 million of the $1.2 billion purchase price was premium over net assets.

Informatica deserves its own arithmetic, because it is the largest capital-allocation decision of the year and we can actually test it (10-K, Note 7):

Total consideration: $9,636 million ($9,538M in cash)
Of which goodwill: $5,257 million; intangibles: $3,818 million
Premium over net tangible assets: 5,257 + 3,818 = $9,075 million — 94% of the price
Revenue contributed in the ~3 months to January 31, 2026: $399 million (10-K, Item 7) → annualizing to roughly $1.6 billion
Implied price paid: 9,636 ÷ 1,600 ≈ 6.0× revenue

Six times revenue, in cash, funded with debt, for a data-management business. For the deal to clear a 10% hurdle, Informatica must eventually produce about $964 million a year of owner earnings — roughly a 60% owner-earnings margin on its revenue. Salesforce's own consolidated owner-earnings margin is 9,700 ÷ 41,525 = 23.4%. I cannot see how the arithmetic works, and the segment disclosure is designed so that I will never be able to check. That is not an accident of reporting; it is a choice: one segment, consolidated net income, no visibility (10-K, Note 1).

The auditors, for their part, flagged the Informatica intangibles as a critical audit matter, noting the valuation "is complex and judgmental due to the use of subjective assumptions" and that they "involved a valuation specialist" (10-K, Item 8, Report of Independent Registered Public Accounting Firm). That is Ernst & Young telling you where the soft numbers are.

Candour

Mixed, and I want to be fair. In their favour: the 10-K is GAAP-only and does not push an adjusted margin at you; the disclosure of $12.7 billion of repurchases at an average of $254.21 is specific and unflattering and they printed it anyway; the "$1.2 billion in gains from one privately held equity investment" disclosure is genuinely more candid than required; and the segment revenue table lets a careful reader find the 8% core growth. Against: the "we do not expect to incur significant additional charges" on restructuring was contradicted within one quarter; the renaming of every product line to carry the "Agentforce" prefix — while stating "there were no changes in the allocation of revenue" (10-K, Item 7, footnote 1) — is a marketing exercise conducted inside a securities filing, and it makes 8% growth look like an AI franchise; and the one-segment structure conceals every acquisition's fate.

Verdict on management as partners: capable operators, poor buyers. They fixed the margin honestly, without gutting R&D. They term out debt intelligently. And they have converted an extraordinary business's excess returns into $64.8 billion of goodwill and $28 billion of buybacks at $539 per net share retired.

PRINCIPLE. Over time you ride with management's capital allocation, so judge it by the one-dollar test rather than the press release — and measure buybacks by dollars per share actually retired, not dollars spent. A repurchase that only mops up option dilution is not a return of capital; it is payroll routed through the equity account, and the company that reports "$12.7 billion returned to shareholders" while its share count falls 3.4% is telling you a true fact in a false unit.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

The discount rate, and why there is no beta here

Chapter 12 treats the discount rate as gravity. I anchor it to the long-term risk-free rate as one yardstick across all companies, but normalized and floored: not a depressed current yield, and never a real expected return below about 10%. I use r = 10%, held constant across every case I teach, so that intrinsic values stay comparable.

I add no beta and no company-specific risk premium. My atlas lists a beta of 1.20 for Salesforce (equity_atlas.xlsx). I am not using it, and I want to say why rather than just skip it. Beta measures how much this share price has wobbled relative to the market. That is a statement about the mood of Salesforce's shareholders, not about the durability of Salesforce's earnings. If the stock fell from $369 to $163.50 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, price range) while the contract book grew 14%, beta went up and the business became safer per dollar of price. Using beta would tell me to demand a higher return precisely when I am being offered a better one — which is exactly backwards. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Is this a wonderful, good, fair, or avoid-it business?

A wonderful business, owned by a fair capital allocator, and the second fact has swallowed most of the first.

The manual's 2007 taxonomy grades businesses on returns and reinvestment: the great one earns very high returns while needing almost no added capital; the good one earns attractive returns but only on the substantial capital it must keep putting up; the gruesome one grows while earning inadequate returns and demands continuous reinvestment. Salesforce is a genuinely strange specimen, because it is great and good simultaneously, at two different levels of the same corporate structure:

  • The operating franchise is great — greater than See's, on the arithmetic. See's earned roughly 60% pre-tax on the $8 million of capital it needed. Salesforce's operating business runs on negative $740 million of tangible invested capital, funded by customers who pay a year in advance and by rented computers. It is a savings account paying an infinite rate.
  • The enterprise is merely good, and barely — ROIC of 10.2%, against a 10% cost of capital. Growth here is value-neutral.

Everything between those two facts is $64,756 million of goodwill and acquired intangibles. The business did not become ordinary. Management bought its way to ordinary.

The one question, answered

I opened by asking: is the stock Salesforce hands its employees a real cost — and if it is, is what remains enough to justify $167.56?

It is a real cost, and the company's own cash flow statement proves it rather than merely implying it. Salesforce charged $3,509 million of stock compensation and then spent $4,322 million of actual cash (17 million shares at its own average repurchase price of $254.21) buying back stock just to stop the count from rising. The GAAP charge is not conservative; it is generous. Refusing the add-back moves the operating margin from the 34.0% you will read about to the 20.1% the auditors signed, and it moves earnings from an independent research service's $11,969 million to the audited $7,457 million.

And what remains is — just barely — enough to make the stock interesting, and not enough to make it compelling. Charging every cost, including the stock and the new interest bill, Salesforce still throws off $9,700 million, or $12.28 a share, of owner earnings. At $167.56 that is a 7.33% yield and 13.6× owner earnings, against a no-growth floor of $129.67 — meaning only 22.6% of the price is a bet on growth. My base case says $216; the price says $168; my required entry says $151. The honest answer is: close, but not yet.

That is a more interesting answer than either of the ones you will read elsewhere. The bulls are right that Salesforce is cheap — but only because they are counting $4.5 billion of costs as profit, and they would be right by accident. The bears are right that this is a decelerating 8% grower whose moat cannot price — but they are valuing it as though 8% deceleration justified pricing in 1.4% growth for a decade.

The two or three universal lessons this case teaches most vividly

1. "Non-cash" is not the same as "not a cost," and the distinction is worth a third of the company. Depreciation is added back because its true cost reappears as maintenance capex; stock compensation has no such offset, because its cost is the ownership you handed over. The gap between Salesforce's GAAP operating margin (20.1%) and its adjusted one (34.0%) is 13.9 points, and 61% of that bridge is stock. The discipline matters most exactly where a company most wants you to skip it — and the tell is that the same company that calls the expense imaginary spends $4.3 billion of real cash a year undoing it.

2. Measure returns on the capital the operation employs — then carry the purchase price separately. Salesforce's headline 10.2% ROIC would have made me pass in twenty minutes. Stripping the $64.8 billion of acquisition premium out of the denominator revealed negative tangible invested capital and a franchise better than See's. Same company, same day, opposite conclusions. The consolidated ratio blends "how good is this business?" with "how well did management buy?" A student who never separates them will spend a career mistaking great businesses with spending problems for mediocre ones — and will misdiagnose the cure, because the fix for a bad business is to sell it, while the fix for this one is simply for management to stop.

3. A buyback is a purchase, and purchases have prices. "$28 billion returned to shareholders" and "$539 per share actually retired, in a stock now at $167.56" are the same transaction described in two units. One is a press release; the other is what happened. Always divide the cash spent by the net reduction in shares, because the answer tells you how much of the "return of capital" was really payroll — at Salesforce, roughly half of it. And it disciplines the deeper point: repurchases create value only below intrinsic value. Management borrowed $25 billion at 5.36% fixed to 2066 to buy stock at $198.34 that trades at $167.56 four months later. They were not reckless and they were not stupid. They were merely certain, and certainty is not a margin of safety.

The post-purchase discipline

Chapter 16: once bought well, the default is to hold — monitor the business, not the quote. Only three things justify a sale, and it is worth naming them concretely for this case:

  1. A broken thesis. Here that means: attrition rising above ~8%, current RPO growth falling toward zero, or the first clear evidence that seat counts are shrinking at large customers. Not a falling share price — a Salesforce whose contract book still compounds at 11% has become less risky at $167.56 than it was at $369, not more.
  2. A price far above even a generous estimate of value. My generous estimate is $289. Above that, the quality no longer justifies the price.
  3. A decisively better opportunity, after tax and after the risk of being wrong twice.

Nothing on that list mentions volatility, beta, or the SaaS-pocalypse headline. The thing to watch is the contract book.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

Every technical term used above, defined in plain English, with the formula and Salesforce's own numbers.

Circle of competence — the set of businesses whose economics you can actually forecast a decade out. Salesforce: the subscription model is inside it; the 10-year impact of AI on per-seat pricing is outside anyone's.

Economic moat — a durable structural advantage protecting earnings from competition. Salesforce: switching costs, evidenced by ~8% attrition (10-K, Item 7) ⇒ average customer life ≈ 1 ÷ 0.08 = 12.5 years. Judged narrow — no pricing power.

Unearned (deferred) revenue — cash collected from customers before the service is delivered; a liability settled by working, not by paying. Salesforce: $24,317M at January 31, 2026 — 66% of all current liabilities, and the reason the current ratio understates liquidity.

Remaining performance obligation (RPO) — all future revenue under signed contract not yet recognized. Salesforce: $72.4B at January 31, 2026 (+14%); $67.9B at April 30, 2026 (+11%).

Attrition rate — the annual loss of contracted value. Salesforce: ~8%, excluding Slack self-service and current-year acquisitions.

Gross margin = gross profit ÷ revenue. Salesforce FY2026: 32,255 ÷ 41,525 = 77.7%.

Operating margin = income from operations ÷ revenue. Salesforce FY2026 (GAAP): 8,331 ÷ 41,525 = 20.1%.

Stock-based compensation (SBC) — employees paid in shares rather than cash. A non-cash charge that is never added back, because its real cost is the ownership transferred. Salesforce FY2026: $3,509M — 8.45% of revenue; cash actually spent offsetting the resulting dilution: 17M shares × $254.21 = $4,322M.

Non-GAAP ("adjusted") earnings — GAAP earnings with selected costs removed. Salesforce FY2026: GAAP operating income $8,331M (20.1% margin) vs. reconstructed non-GAAP $14,113M (34.0%); the 13.9-point gap is 61% stock compensation. An independent research service's "EPS" of $12.52 is this figure (their footnote B); GAAP diluted EPS is $7.80.

Free cash flow (FCF) = operating cash flow − capital expenditures. Salesforce FY2026: 14,996 − 594 = $14,402M. Note this still treats stock compensation as free — which is why it is not owner earnings.

Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital; the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Salesforce, my anchor: 14,996 − 594 (capex) − 3,509 (SBC, not added back) = $10,893M, less $1,194M of incremental after-tax interest on the post-ASR debt = $9,700M, or $12.28 per share on 790M shares.

Maintenance vs. growth capex — the split between spending needed to stand still and spending that expands; accounting does not separate them, so the analyst must. Salesforce: capex of $594M (1.4% of revenue) is essentially all maintenance, and is small only because Salesforce rents its computing — see the $16.7B of infrastructure commitments (10-K, Item 7).

Working capital = current assets − current liabilities. Salesforce FY2026 change: −2,160 − 2,811 + 819 + 1,014 − 567 + 2,924 = −$781M (a net drain, already inside operating cash flow).

NOPAT (net operating profit after tax) = operating income × (1 − tax rate). Salesforce FY2026: 8,331 × (1 − 0.2167) = $6,526M.

Invested capital = equity + total debt − excess cash. Salesforce at January 31, 2026: 59,142 + 14,439 − 9,565 = $64,016M (spot); average with the prior year = $59,795M.

Return on invested capital (ROIC) = NOPAT ÷ invested capital. Salesforce: 6,526 ÷ 64,016 = 10.2% (spot); 6,526 ÷ 59,795 = 10.9% (average).

Tangible invested capital — invested capital less acquisition goodwill and intangibles; the capital the manager actually commands. Salesforce: 64,016 − 57,941 − 6,815 = −$740M. Negative — the return on it is undefined, i.e. off the scale.

Economic vs. accounting goodwill — economic goodwill is the unrecorded excess earning power of a franchise; accounting goodwill is the recorded premium a buyer paid over net assets. Salesforce: $57,941M of the second kind — a receipt, not an asset.

Return on equity (ROE) = net income ÷ shareholders' equity. Salesforce FY2026 (GAAP): 7,457 ÷ 59,142 = 12.6%. On an independent research service's non-GAAP profit: 11,969 ÷ 59,142 = 20.2% — the "20%" in the atlas.

Net debt = total debt − cash and securities. Salesforce at April 30, 2026: 39,280 − 11,837 = $27,443M = 2.8 years of owner earnings.

Interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest expense. Salesforce: 8,331 ÷ 1,825 (run-rate) = 4.6× — down from roughly 28× before the March 2026 notes.

Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. Salesforce at April 30, 2026: 21,613 ÷ 27,502 = 0.79; excluding unearned revenue, 3.0×.

Accelerated share repurchase (ASR) — a contract to buy a large block immediately, with the final share count set later by the average price over the period. Salesforce: $25.0B in March 2026; 103M shares delivered at an average $198.34 (80% of the expected total), with a 36M-share unsettled forward still to come (10-Q, Notes 9 and 11).

Discount rate (r) — the return you require; it acts as gravity on value. Here: 10%, normalized and floored, anchored to the long-term risk-free rate, held constant across cases. No beta — Salesforce's 1.20 is rejected as a measure of risk, because price wobble is not the permanent loss of capital.

Cost of capital — the hurdle a dollar of retained earnings must clear. Salesforce: ROIC of 10.2% against r of 10% ⇒ growth is value-neutral.

The one-dollar test — a dollar retained should create at least a dollar of value; retain only when incremental ROIC ≥ r. Salesforce: fails narrowly ⇒ management is right to return capital, and wrong about the price at which it does.

Intrinsic value = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ — the discounted value of all future owner earnings. The definition of value, not always the best estimator of it.

Two-stage DCF / terminal value / the r − g spread — explicit growth for a period, then a perpetuity: TV = OE_n × (1 + g) ÷ (r − g). Salesforce base: 5% for 10 years, then 3% forever at r = 10% ⇒ $216.27. The r − g spread of 7% is what makes the answer swing 16.7% on a one-point change in r.

No-growth (earning-power) value = owner earnings ÷ r — the floor beneath a DCF, and the gauge of how much of a price is a bet on growth. Salesforce: 12.28 ÷ 0.10 = $122.78, plus $6.89 of haircut strategic investments = $129.67 ⇒ only 22.6% of the $167.56 price is a growth bet.

Owner-earnings yield = owner earnings ÷ price — the stock as a bond. Salesforce: 12.28 ÷ 167.56 = 7.33%, against a required 10%.

Reverse DCF — solving for the growth the current price implies rather than imposing your own. Salesforce: $167.56 implies 1.4% owner-earnings growth for 10 years, then 3% forever (at r = 10%).

P/E ratio = price ÷ earnings per share. Salesforce at $167.56: 21.5× GAAP; 13.6× owner earnings; an independent research service quotes 14.5× on non-GAAP earnings. No historical median exists — an independent research service shows NMF for FY2017–FY2023.

Margin of safety = (intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value. Salesforce: (216.27 − 167.56) ÷ 216.27 = +22.5% vs base; −19.6% vs the $140.14 low case. My required 30% ⇒ buy below $151.39.

Mr. Market — Graham's manic-depressive partner who quotes a price each day out of greed or fear; there to serve you, not to guide you. Today: depressed, for the nameable reason that AI may dissolve the per-seat model — a fear that "has not been seen at scale" (an independent research service industry report, 2026-05-01).

Risk — the permanent loss of capital, not volatility. Salesforce: the risks are the seat model dissolving, another $25B of goodwill, and overpaying — not the 1.20 beta.
