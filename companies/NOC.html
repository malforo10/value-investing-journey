Northrop Grumman (NOC) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition· Case dated 2026-07-04 · Live price $549.01

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Northrop Grumman is one of five American prime defense contractors. It builds things the U.S. government cannot buy anywhere else: the B-21 Raider stealth bomber (sole-source), the Sentinel ground-based ICBM that will replace the Minuteman III in the nuclear triad, restricted (classified) space and radar programs, missile-defense interceptors, and solid rocket motors. Four segments (2025 sales, from item7_mda_part2.txt):

Segment 2025 Sales Op. margin What it is
Aeronautics Systems $12,992M 6.3% B-21, B-2 sustainment, F-35 fuselage, Triton, Global Hawk
Mission Systems $12,506M 14.6% Radars, EW, C4ISR, microelectronics — the high-margin jewel
Defense Systems $8,002M 10.9% Sentinel, IBCS, munitions, hypersonics
Space Systems $10,771M 11.0% Restricted space, missile defense, launch, Cygnus

The U.S. government was 84% of 2025 sales (item1_business.txt), international 14%. Backlog at 12/31/25 was $95.7 billion — 2.3x annual revenue — with a book-to-bill of 1.1x.

Circle-of-competence gate. Can we forecast this business? Mostly yes, and that is unusual for a business this technical. The demand driver — U.S. and allied defense budgets — is visible years out through appropriations and multi-year programs, and the $95.7B backlog converts to revenue on a schedule the company itself discloses (35% within 12 months, 60% within 24). What is harder to forecast, and where the circle frays, is contract profitability: on fixed-price development work, cost overruns land as sudden charges (we'll see B-21 do exactly this). So the top line is inside the circle; the margin line is at its edge. Principle: the circle is not all-or-nothing. You can understand a company's revenues while honestly admitting its margins are only forecastable within a wide band — and you size that uncertainty into the margin of safety rather than pretending it away.

THE ONE QUESTION. For every case we force ourselves to answer one question in a sentence. For Northrop: Will the strategic-deterrence franchise (B-21 + Sentinel + restricted programs) generate durable, high-return owner earnings for a decade — or will fixed-price program execution keep clawing back the profits the moat should protect? Hold that question; the whole valuation turns on it.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Balance sheet — liquidity, leverage, goodwill (item8 financial position, 12/31/25)

  • Liquidity. Current assets $15,287M vs. current liabilities $13,882M → current ratio 1.10. That is thin by Graham's textile-era standard, but normal for a prime whose "inventory" is really unbilled receivables on government contracts ($6,544M) that will be collected. Cash $4,403M.
  • Leverage. Total debt $15,696M (LT $15,162M + current $534M) against equity $16,674M → debt is ~48% of capital. Net debt = 15,696 − 4,403 = $11,293M.
  • Goodwill and intangibles. Goodwill $17,437M — larger than the entire $16,674M of book equity. Tangible book value is negative. This is the ghost of the 2018 Orbital ATK deal and older acquisitions. Principle from Chapter 7: never anchor value to book. Book understates a franchise like this (the B-21 program is worth vastly more than any number on the balance sheet) and, here, book is dominated by acquisition goodwill anyway. We will value the earnings stream, not the equity account.

Income statement — margins, layer by layer, multi-year (item8 earnings; XBRL)

($M) 2023 2024 2025
Sales 39,290 41,033 41,954
Operating income 2,537 4,370 4,511
Operating margin 6.5% 10.6% 10.8%
Net earnings 2,056 4,174 4,182
Diluted EPS 13.53 28.34 29.08

Look at 2023: a 6.5% operating margin, a collapse from Northrop's normal double digits. That is the B-21: in Q4 2023 the company booked a $1.56 billion projected loss across the five low-rate initial production (LRIP) lots (item7_mda_part1.txt). Then in Q1 2025 it booked another $477 million on the same program. Strip the one-timers and the picture normalizes: the company's own MTM-adjusted EPS was $15.61 (2023), $26.08 (2024), $26.34 (2025). An independent research service's array of "nonrecurring" adjustments tells the same story — it excludes ($9.76) in 2023 and adds back $1.02 in 2025.

Principle (Chapter 7, "normalize before you capitalize"): a franchise's earning power is what it makes in a normal year, not its worst year or its best. Northrop's 2023 GAAP EPS of $13.53 understates the business; its 2025 GAAP EPS of $29.08 slightly overstates it (it contains a pension mark-to-market benefit and a divestiture gain). The truth is in the middle, around $26–28 of normalized EPS.

Cash flow — OCF vs. NI, capex, FCF (item8 cash flows)

  • OCF 2025 $4,757M vs. net earnings $4,182M — cash exceeds reported profit, a good sign. Note the big non-cash add-back of the $477M B-21 charge (it hit earnings but not cash yet; the remaining $1.3B loss accrual sits in Other current liabilities and will bleed out as cash later).
  • Capex $1,450M, roughly matching D&A of $1,472M. Management is spending more in 2026 to expand B-21 production capacity — that is growth capex, not maintenance.
  • Free cash flow $3,307M (company's own non-GAAP), up 26% on the year.

How the three articulate. Earnings feed retained earnings on the balance sheet and, adjusted for non-cash items and working capital, become OCF. OCF funds capex, dividends ($1,293M), and buybacks ($1,624M). The working-capital line is the joint to watch: unbilled receivables rose $754M in 2025 (cash tied up in contracts ahead of billing) — normal for a prime in a ramp year, but a drag on cash conversion. Quarterly OCF is wildly lumpy (Q1 2026 was negative $1,656M per XBRL) because government billing cycles bunch collections into year-end. Principle: judge cash conversion over a full year, never a quarter, for a business with a long operating cycle.

Earnings-quality scan

This is the heart of the Northrop case. Three things to interrogate:

  1. Cost-type vs. fixed-price accounting. 2025 sales split 50/50 cost-type ($20,944M) / fixed-price ($21,010M) (item1_business.txt). Cost-type contracts reimburse costs plus a fee — low risk, low margin. Fixed-price contracts (like B-21 LRIP) offer higher margins but put cost overruns on Northrop. The mix shift toward fixed-price development is precisely why earnings have become lumpier.
  2. EAC adjustments. Under cost-to-cost accounting, Northrop books profit as costs are incurred against an estimate at completion. When the estimate changes, a "net EAC adjustment" flows through immediately. In 2025 there were $1,696M favorable and $1,487M unfavorable EAC adjustments, netting to +$209M (item7_mda_part2.txt). This is the Critical Audit Matter, and rightly so: earnings are genuinely estimate-driven. Deloitte flags it; so do we.
  3. The B-21 loss accrual. Remaining loss reserve $1.3B at 12/31/25. Management "made no significant changes" in Q4 2025 — but the whole program is a standing reminder that a single fixed-price development contract can move EPS by a dollar or more in a quarter.

Principle: with a contractor, the accounting is honest but estimate-laden. The red flag is not fraud — the numbers are clean — it is that reported profit embeds forecasts of the future. Widen your margin of safety accordingly, and never capitalize a year that happens to be rich in favorable EAC catch-ups.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Earning-power trend (long XBRL)

Revenue compounded from ~$24.5B (2013) to $41.95B (2025), about 8.5% a year per an independent research service's 10-year rate — respectable for a defense prime. Normalized net profit runs ~$3.8–4.0B. The business is a steady, mid-single-digit-to-8% grower with occasional program-charge potholes.

Owner earnings — the real number

The manual's formula: Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital.

Step 1 — normalize reported earnings. Start with 2025 net earnings $4,182M. Strip the windfalls that won't recur: the MTM pension benefit (+$394M net of tax) and the training-services divestiture gain (~$180M after tax). Core recurring earnings ≈ $3,608M. Now add back the B-21 charge ($477M pretax, ~$377M after tax) as a normalization — not because charges won't recur (they might), but because we want a representative year: 4,182 − 394 − 180 + 377 ≈ $3,985M normalized net earnings (~$28.06/share, which matches an independent research service's own MTM-adjusted line almost exactly).

Step 2 — owner earnings. Add D&A ($1,472M); subtract maintenance capex. Total capex was $1,450M, but a chunk of that is B-21 capacity expansion — growth capex. I take maintenance capex ≈ $1,400M (roughly D&A, the honest default for a mature manufacturing base) and subtract a modest working-capital use of ~$100M:

3,985 + 1,472 − 1,400 − 100 ≈ $3,957M owner earnings ≈ $27.87 per share.

Principle (Chapter 7): the hardest term is maintenance capex. Northrop's capex ≈ its depreciation, so it is not quietly consuming more cash than it earns — a healthy sign. But the 2026 capex ramp for B-21 is growth spending, and I do NOT subtract it from owner earnings, because it is a bet on future volume, not the cost of standing still.

ROIC vs. cost of capital

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital. NOPAT = operating income $4,511M × (1 − 17.5% tax) ≈ $3,722M. Invested capital = debt $15,696M + equity $16,674M − excess cash $4,403M ≈ $27,967M. ROIC ≈ 13.3%.

That comfortably exceeds a ~9% cost of capital, so growth creates value — but note it is a far cry from See's Candy's 30%+. Northrop is capital-heavier than a branded-consumer franchise: it must sink real money into plant, tooling, and now B-21 capacity. And the ROIC is dragged down by ~$17.4B of acquisition goodwill in the denominator. On tangible invested capital the returns are higher, but the goodwill is real money that was spent, so 13.3% is the honest number. Principle (Chapter 6): a 13% ROIC business is worth owning and worth a premium to book — but it is a "good," not "wonderful," economic engine. It earns above its cost of capital; it does not print money the way a capital-light franchise does.

ROE and buybacks

An independent research service shows ROE ~24%. But watch the mechanism: equity is only $16.7B (held down by decades of buybacks and negative tangible book), so ROE is flattered by leverage and share shrinkage. Do not confuse a 24% ROE here with a 24% ROIC — the gap is debt and buyback accounting. Principle: ROE can be manufactured by leverage; ROIC cannot. Trust ROIC.

The moat — and its erosion risks

The moat is real and unusually durable:
- Sole-source / oligopoly. Only Northrop builds the B-21. Only Northrop and a tiny set of peers can build a strategic ICBM. Barriers to entry: security clearances, classified past performance, specialized facilities, decades of program history, and a customer (the DoW) that cannot switch suppliers mid-program without catastrophic cost.
- Switching costs approaching infinity on strategic programs — you do not re-compete a nuclear deterrent.
- Regulatory barrier — this is Buffett's "economic franchise" test partly satisfied: the product is needed, has no close substitute. But condition (3) — "not subject to price regulation" — fails. The government caps prices (fixed-price contracts, FAR/CAS cost rules, DCAA audits). That is the crucial asterisk on Northrop's moat: it has monopoly position without monopoly pricing power. The customer sets the terms.

10-year erosion risks: (1) Budget cycles — FY2026 appropriations were not enacted; a government shutdown ran Oct 1–Nov 12, 2025 (item7_mda_part1.txt); NOC operates under a continuing resolution. (2) Program cancellation / restructure — Sentinel already breached Nunn-McCurdy cost thresholds and was restructured. (3) Fixed-price cost overruns — the B-21 charges are the live example, and management calls lumpy results "the new normal." (4) Acquisition-reform executive orders pressuring contractor margins.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION so far: the franchise is durable, but its returns are capped by the customer and periodically taxed by fixed-price execution. This is a good business with a wide moat and a real, recurring headwind — not a flawless compounder.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Five questions from Chapter 8, each with Northrop's own numbers:

  • Leverage. Net debt $11,293M ÷ owner earnings $3,957M ≈ 2.9 years. Against EBIT ($4,511M), net debt is ~2.5x. A conservative business could clear its debt from roughly three years of its own cash. An independent research service rates Debt at 48% of capital; the equity atlas shows Debt/OCF 3.3x. Reasonable for a company with a $95.7B contracted backlog.
  • Coverage. EBIT $4,511M ÷ interest $665M ≈ 6.8x. (an independent research service cites 8.2x on a broader earnings base including pension.) Either way, a normal downturn does not threaten the interest bill. Comfortable.
  • Liquidity. Current ratio 1.10; $4.4B cash; an undrawn $3.0B revolver. Adequate, not lavish — but a prime's liquidity is really its government receivables, which are money-good.
  • Refinancing wall. $534M due within a year; the company terms out debt in the capital markets routinely (issued $1.0B of notes in May 2025). No cliff.
  • Hidden obligations — the pension. For a defense prime this is the off-balance-sheet item, and here it is a pleasant surprise: pension assets $31.1B vs. obligations $29.4B — the plan is overfunded (net pension asset of ~$3.2B sits on the balance sheet). Plan assets returned 11.3% in 2025 vs. a 7.5% assumption. This is a source of strength, not a hidden liability — a meaningful contrast with primes that carry pension deficits.

Verdict: FORTRESS. an independent research service's "A" Financial Strength grade and Safety rank 2 are earned. This business will survive a bad year and a shut credit market without ever becoming a forced seller. Principle (Chapter 8): the balance sheet is the precondition for trusting the earnings stream. Northrop passes decisively — which lets us focus our skepticism where it belongs: on margin durability, not on solvency.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

CEO Kathy Warden. Judge capital allocation across the six uses (Chapter 9):

  1. Reinvest — capex ~$1.45B, ramping for B-21 at ~13% incremental ROIC. Above cost of capital, so value-creating.
  2. Acquisitions — quiet lately; the last big one (Orbital ATK, 2018) is the goodwill on the books. Restraint here is welcome.
  3. Pay down debt — modest; debt is stable ~$15–16B.
  4. Dividends — declared $8.99/share in 2025, grown ~11.5%/year over a decade, ~32% payout. Well-covered, rising, rational.
  5. Buybacks — this is the big lever. Shares outstanding fell from 175.1M (2016) to 142.0M (2025) — a ~19% reduction, ~$1.5–3.7B/year (XBRL; item8 cash flows). At 2025's ~$500–640 range and normalized owner earnings of ~$28/share, NOC bought back at roughly 18–23x owner earnings.
  6. Share issuance — negligible dilution; stock comp is small ($119M).

The one-dollar test. Does each retained dollar create ≥ $1 of value? At 13.3% incremental ROIC vs. ~9% cost of capital, yes — retention is justified, and the dividend + buyback return of the rest is the correct residual. But apply the buyback price test: buybacks only create value below intrinsic value. At the Feb-2026 peak of ~$702, NOC's own stock was arguably near or above fair value, and buying there would have destroyed per-share value; at today's $549 the math is far more attractive. Management gets partial credit — the count is shrinking, but some was repurchased richly.

Candor. Strong. The B-21 charges are disclosed plainly, quarter by quarter, with the remaining accrual quantified. EAC adjustments are tabulated favorable-and-unfavorable. Sentinel's Nunn-McCurdy breach is discussed openly. Principle (Chapter 9): a management that reports its mistakes as clearly as its wins earns trust. Northrop's disclosure of program losses is exactly the candor Buffett prizes.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Anchor: normalized owner earnings ≈ $3,957M ≈ $27.87/share (derived above), on 142M shares. Discount rate r = 9% — the long bond (~4.5%) plus a premium for U.S.-government single-customer and fixed-price execution risk, dampened by a 0.60 beta and a fortress balance sheet.

Method 1 — Two-stage DCF (owner earnings)

Growth path: 6% for 10 years (below an independent research service's 7% earnings estimate and well below the 8.5% historical top line — conservative, reflecting margin-cap and budget risk), then 3% terminal. This is a business we can forecast a decade out because of backlog and program visibility, so a DCF is defensible here.

Base case (r = 9%, g₁ = 6%, g_term = 3%): IV ≈ $602/share. Implied multiple = 602 ÷ 27.87 ≈ 21.6× owner earnings.

Sensitivity (the point is the range, per Chapter 13):

r ↓ / g_term → 2% 3% 4%
8% $645 $728 $853
9% $547 $602 $678
10% $474 $512 $562

The estimate spans roughly $475 to $730 across individually reasonable assumptions.

Method 2 — Normalized-earnings multiple (triangulate, don't anchor to peers)

Normalized EPS ≈ $28.06 (equals VL's MTM-adjusted line). Where should the multiple come from?
- Historical median P/E: an independent research service's own median is 18× (and the trailing decade P/E has ranged ~14–23×). 18 × 28.06 = $505.
- Peer/industry sanity (naming names, not anchoring): the Aerospace/Defense group and primes — Lockheed (LMT), General Dynamics (GD), RTX, Boeing (BA) — trade in a high-teens-to-low-20s P/E on normalized earnings; Northrop's B-21/Sentinel franchise and overfunded pension justify the upper half of that band. 19–20× → $533–$561.
- Quality-justified from first principles: a 13% ROIC franchise growing 6% deserves ~18–20× owner earnings. 19 × 27.87 = $530.

Multiple methods cluster around $505–$560.

Reconcile & cross-check (the three tests)

  1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r = 3,957 ÷ 0.09 = $43,967M ÷ 142 = $310/share. So at today's $549, roughly $310 (56%) is paying for the business as-is, and ~$239 (44%) is a bet on growth. That is a moderate, not heroic, growth bet — appropriate for a visible-backlog defense prime.
  2. Implied multiple = base IV $602 ÷ OE $27.87 ≈ 21.6×. Is that defensible for 13% ROIC and 6%→3% growth? It's at the rich end — which is exactly why I do not use the raw DCF base as my central estimate. The multiple methods (18–20×) discipline it down.
  3. Owner-earnings yield = $27.87 ÷ $549 = 5.1%, and the coupon grows ~6%. Against a ~4.5% static long bond, that is a clearly superior claim — a real but not enormous edge.

Triangulated conclusion

The DCF pulls high ($602), the multiple methods pull to the middle ($505–$560), the no-growth floor sets the low anchor ($310). Weighting the disciplined multiple methods most heavily and using the DCF as the upside case:

IV low ≈ $475 · IV base ≈ $560 · IV high ≈ $650.

Sanity checks: An independent research service's 18-month target midpoint is ~$657 and its own median P/E implies ~$505 — my range straddles both. The live P/E of 17.2× on normalized earnings sits just below my base. Nothing here is heroic.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Live price: $549.01 (equity_atlas, 2026-07-04). Set it beside the IV range:

  • vs. base $560: price is at a ~2% discount — essentially fair value.
  • vs. low $475: price is ~16% above the conservative floor — no cushion there.
  • vs. high $650: ~16% below — upside exists if r is lower or growth holds.

Margin of safety against the base = (560 − 549)/560 ≈ 2%. Against the conservative low, it is negative. Principle (Chapter 13): a 2% margin is no margin. The manual is blunt — "if we calculate the value to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested." Real protection would want the low-to-mid $400s.

Mr. Market's mood. This is a vivid Mr. Market moment. In February 2026 the stock was $702.57 (P/E 24.8) — the sector had been re-rated on geopolitics, Ukraine, Middle East tensions, and talk of a $1.5 trillion defense budget. By late May, an independent research service's report caught it at $550: "down this year on fickle sentiment... valuations became indefensible... defense equities started to roll over." Today it sits at $549. Mr. Market swung from euphoria (24.8× in February) to sobriety (17.2× today) on the same business with the same $95.7B backlog. The business did not change by 22% in four months; his pocketbook did. That is Chapter 2 in the flesh — and it tells us the right time to own NOC was not February. Today it is merely fair.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Verdict: a GOOD business at a FAIR price — a HOLD / WATCH, not a table-pounding buy.

Northrop is a wide-moat, fortress-balance-sheet, candidly-managed defense franchise earning ~13% on capital with a decade of contracted backlog. That is genuinely good. But two things restrain the call: (1) its moat lacks pricing power — the government caps its returns and fixed-price development periodically claws profits back (B-21: $1.56B then $477M); and (2) at $549 the price offers essentially no margin of safety against a conservative valuation. The manual's discipline is clear: below the conservative range you buy in size; at or above it you admire the business and wait. We admire, and we wait — ideally for a Mr. Market tantrum into the $440s (the November-2025 lows were ~$426), where the franchise would come with a real cushion.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION: The strategic-deterrence franchise will generate durable owner earnings — but at a good, government-capped return, periodically dented by fixed-price execution. It is a compounder with a governor on the engine, not a runaway.

Open questions:
1. Will the B-21 accelerate to a higher production rate (upside to volume) — and can Northrop negotiate the NTE lots at margins that finally stop the bleeding?
2. Does Sentinel's restructured baseline hold, or does the ICBM program breach again?
3. Will acquisition-reform executive orders compress prime-contractor margins industry-wide?

Universal lessons:
1. Monopoly position ≠ pricing power. Northrop is sole-source on the B-21 yet cannot set its price — the customer does. Buffett's franchise test has three legs; missing the "no price regulation" leg caps the multiple you should pay.
2. Normalize before you capitalize. GAAP EPS swung from $13.53 (2023, charge-battered) to $29.08 (2025, pension-aided). Capitalizing either year would mislead by hundreds of dollars per share. The truth (~$28 normalized) is the only honest anchor.
3. Price, not quality alone, makes the investment. A wonderful business at 24.8× (February) was a worse decision than the same business at 17.2× (today) — and today is still only "fair." Let Mr. Market bring you the price; do not chase him up.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with NOC's numbers)

  • Intrinsic Value = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. Discounted value of all future owner earnings. NOC base ≈ $560/share.
  • Owner Earnings = Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. NOC: 3,985 + 1,472 − 1,400 − 100 ≈ $3,957M ($27.87/sh).
  • Normalized earnings — earning power in a typical year, stripping windfalls (pension MTM +$394M, divestiture gain +$180M) and adding back one-off charges (B-21 +$377M). NOC ≈ $3,985M, matching VL's MTM-adjusted $28.06/sh.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex — maintenance keeps unit volume/position; growth funds expansion. NOC capex $1,450M ≈ D&A; the 2026 B-21 build is growth, excluded from owner earnings.
  • ROIC = NOPAT ÷ invested capital = 3,722 ÷ 27,967 = 13.3%. Business quality; exceeds r, so growth creates value.
  • ROE = net earnings ÷ equity ≈ 24% — but flattered by leverage and buyback-shrunk equity; trust ROIC, not ROE.
  • Discount rate (r) — owner's opportunity cost ≈ long bond + risk premium. NOC: 9% (4.5% bond + ~4.5% for single-customer/fixed-price risk, low beta).
  • No-growth (earning-power) value = OE ÷ r = 3,957 ÷ 0.09 = $310/share. The floor; everything above is a growth bet.
  • Interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest = 4,511 ÷ 665 = 6.8×. Solvency cushion — comfortable.
  • Net debt / owner earnings = (15,696 − 4,403) ÷ 3,957 = 2.9 years to extinguish net debt. Staying power — strong.
  • EAC adjustment — cumulative catch-up when a contract's estimated cost-at-completion changes; NOC 2025: +$1,696M favorable, −$1,487M unfavorable, net +$209M. The reason earnings are estimate-driven.
  • Fixed-price vs. cost-type contract — fixed-price: higher margin, overrun risk on NOC (B-21); cost-type: cost + fee, low risk/margin. 2025 mix 50/50.
  • Backlog — remaining performance obligations = future contracted sales. NOC: $95.7B (2.3× revenue), book-to-bill 1.1×.
  • Margin of Safety = (IV − Price)/IV = (560 − 549)/560 ≈ 2% — no meaningful cushion; the reason the verdict is "wait."
  • Moat / economic franchise — durable advantage; NOC has position + switching costs + regulatory barriers, but fails the "no price regulation" leg — a governed franchise.
  • Mr. Market — the manic partner: $702.57 (Feb 2026, 24.8×) → $549 (Jul 2026, 17.2×) on an unchanged business. His pocketbook, not his wisdom.
