Leidos Holdings (LDOS) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition
Case date: July 4, 2026 · Live price used throughout: $108.84

A teaching case, not a recommendation. We are going to walk through one company slowly, showing every calculation as arithmetic and naming the principle behind each step. Our aim is not to tell you whether to buy Leidos. It is to hand you a repeatable way of thinking that outlives this company and this price.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Ben: Let us first say plainly what Leidos is, because you cannot value what you cannot describe.

Leidos Holdings is a holding company whose operating subsidiary, Leidos, Inc., sells scientific, engineering, systems-integration, and technical services — overwhelmingly to the U.S. federal government (10-K, Item 1 / Note 1). Its customers are the Department of War (formerly Defense), the Intelligence Community, Homeland Security, the FAA, the Department of Veterans Affairs, and a long tail of civil and foreign agencies. It employs about 47,000 people and books revenue on long-term contracts, recognized over time using the cost-to-cost method (10-K, Note 3; this was the auditor's Critical Audit Matter). FY2025 revenue was $17,174 million across four segments: National Security & Digital ($7,611M), Health & Civil ($5,069M), Commercial & International ($2,315M), and Defense Systems ($2,179M) (10-K MD&A, Segment Results).

The revenue mix by contract type tells you the economic character of the business (10-K MD&A, Contract Types):

  • Cost-reimbursement and fixed-price-incentive-fee: 44%
  • Firm-fixed-price: 43%
  • Time-and-materials / level-of-effort: 13%

Warren: So this is a government-services contractor — a business that sells hours, expertise, security clearances, and program management to a customer that is, in most of its markets, the only buyer. Roughly half its work is cost-reimbursable (the government pays cost plus a fee), which caps both the downside and the upside on margin. This is not a branded consumer franchise with pricing power. It is a scale services house with entrenched relationships.

The circle-of-competence gate

Ben: The manual's first filter (Chapter 4) is not price — it is predictability. Can we forecast this business within a sensible range?

We say yes, cautiously. The economics are legible: revenue is a function of the federal budget, the company's backlog, and its win rate on recompetes. There is no technology moonshot to handicap, no fashion cycle, no commodity price. That is genuinely inside a careful analyst's circle. But — and this is the boundary the manual insists we name — the level of federal spending and the political appetite for "efficiency" cuts are outside anyone's circle. We can forecast the machine; we cannot forecast the size of the government's checkbook. We proceed, but we hold that uncertainty in view the entire way.

PRINCIPLE (Circle of Competence). "The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." We can forecast Leidos's mechanics; we cannot forecast federal budget politics. Naming that boundary is what keeps us honest — and it will re-appear as the central risk in our valuation.

THE ONE QUESTION

Warren: Here is the single question this whole case turns on:

At roughly 10x earnings, is Leidos a steady, cash-generative franchise that Mr. Market has mispriced out of fear of government-budget cuts — or is it cheap for a good reason, because a monopsony customer can permanently reset its earning power in a single budget cycle?

Everything that follows is in service of answering that.

Warren: Coverage above 10× and net debt near 2× cash flow is a genuinely safe balance sheet. An independent research service rates Financial Strength A. We agree the company is in no danger of forced selling — which the manual (Chapter 3) reminds us is the thing that turns volatility into permanent loss.

The cash-flow statement (10-K Cash Flows, FY2025)

  • Net cash from operations: $1,750M
  • Capital expenditure (property, equipment, software): $(125)M
  • Free cash flow ≈ $1,750M − $125M = $1,625M
  • FCF per share ≈ $1,625M ÷ 126.4M ≈ $12.86

Ben — the articulation check. Do the statements tell a consistent story? Net income to Leidos was $1,448M; operating cash flow was $1,750M — higher than net income. That is the mark of a healthy, low-capital business: non-cash charges (D&A $290M) and working-capital discipline convert earnings to cash and then some. FCF of $1,625M actually exceeds reported net income of $1,448M. When cash flow chronically leads earnings, the accounts are usually clean.

PRINCIPLE (Articulation — the statements must agree). Earnings you cannot find in the cash-flow statement are suspect. Here cash flow exceeds earnings — a reassuring sign of earnings quality.

Earnings-quality scan

Warren: Let me run the manual's red-flag checklist (Chapter 7) against Leidos:

  • EBITDA dressed up as cash flow? Management leads with operating income, cash from operations, and diluted EPS — not EBITDA (MD&A). Good.
  • Serial "one-time" charges? "Acquisition, integration and restructuring costs" recur ($18M, $16M, $24M) but are small. The $596M goodwill impairment (2023) and $91M asset impairment were genuine and disclosed. No pattern of hiding operating costs in "restructuring."
  • Intangible amortization was $130M in FY2025, $147M in FY2024, $202M in FY2023 (Note 8). This is amortization of acquired customer relationships, technology, and backlog. We will treat it below as arguably a real, recurring cost — because it is the annual expensing of what the company paid to buy revenue, and Leidos buys revenue as a matter of strategy.
  • Auditor: Deloitte, unqualified opinion; served since fiscal 2000. The Critical Audit Matter (cost-to-cost revenue estimation) is inherent to the industry, not a warning sign.

PRINCIPLE (Earnings quality — treat acquisition amortization with suspicion, in both directions). Add-backs of purchased-intangible amortization flatter "adjusted" earnings. For a company whose growth engine is bolt-on M&A, that amortization is a recurring economic cost, not a free lunch. We keep it in view.

Backlog — the forward-revenue anchor

Ben: For a contractor, backlog is the closest thing to a crystal ball (10-K MD&A, Bookings & Backlog):

  • Total backlog: $49,032M (funded $9,685M; negotiated unfunded $39,347M) at Jan 2, 2026
  • Net bookings in FY2025: $17,500M
  • Book-to-bill = bookings ÷ revenue = $17,500M ÷ $17,174M = 1.02×

Total backlog of $49.0B is roughly 2.9× annual revenue. Funded backlog alone ($9.7B) covers more than half a year's revenue with money the government has already appropriated.

Warren: A book-to-bill above 1.0 means the company is replacing what it burns — the pipeline is not shrinking. But note management's honesty: bookings fell from $23.2B (2024) to $17.5B (2025), and an independent research service flags a trailing book-to-bill of only ~1.1 as "not great, but a positive." The backlog is a cushion, not a guarantee — the government "may cancel any contract at any time through a termination for convenience" (10-K). That single sentence is the monopsony risk in the company's own words.

PRINCIPLE (Backlog anchors the forecast, but a monopsony can revoke it). Backlog converts an opaque future into a visible one — but only to the extent the single customer keeps funding it. A ~2.9× revenue backlog is real evidence of durability; the termination-for-convenience clause is the reason we still demand a margin of safety.

OWNER EARNINGS — with the maintenance-vs-growth capex split

Ben: The manual (Chapter 7) is explicit: value runs on owner earnings, not GAAP net income.

Owner Earnings = Reported earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital

Let us build it for FY2025 (all figures from the 10-K cash-flow statement and Note 8):

  • Reported net income (Leidos): $1,448M
  • Add back D&A: +$290M (of which tangible depreciation ≈ $160M and intangible amortization = $130M)
  • Subtract maintenance capex: total capex was only $125M. This is a capital-light business — capex is less than 0.8% of revenue. Essentially all of it is maintenance; there is little growth capex. We take maintenance capex ≈ $160M (a hair above depreciation, to be conservative).
  • Working-capital change: receivables grew $46M, payables fell $308M — a modest cash drag in FY2025, already inside the $1,750M operating cash flow.

Two honest versions:

  1. Generous owner earnings (add back all of D&A): $1,448M + $290M − $160M ≈ $1,578M
  2. Conservative owner earnings (treat purchased-intangible amortization as a real recurring cost — do not add it back): $1,448M + $160M tangible dep − $160M maint capex ≈ $1,448M

And the market-tested cross-check: Free cash flow = $1,750M OCF − $125M capex = $1,625M.

Warren: So owner earnings land in a band of roughly $1,450M to $1,625M, or about $11.50–$12.90 per share on ~125.5M shares. We will carry a normalized owner-earnings figure of ~$1,600M (~$12.75/share) into the valuation — right between the conservative floor and the FCF reading. Notice the beautiful thing about a low-capex services model: FCF ($1,625M) actually exceeds net income ($1,448M), so we are not fighting a maintenance-capex sinkhole the way we would with a railroad or a mill.

PRINCIPLE (Owner earnings ≈ FCF when capex is low — but respect acquisition amortization). In a capital-light services firm, owner earnings closely track free cash flow. The one genuine haircut is purchased-intangible amortization: it is the recurring cost of a growth strategy built on buying revenue, and pretending it is free overstates worth.

ROIC — computed BOTH ways

Ben: Now the single best measure of business quality (Chapter 6): return on invested capital.

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital

NOPAT = Operating income × (1 − tax rate) = $2,109M × (1 − 0.234) = $1,615M (FY2025 effective rate 23.4%, per MD&A).

Invested capital, including goodwill:
= Total debt + Total equity − Cash
= $4,648M + $4,962M − $1,108M = $8,502M

ROIC (incl. goodwill) = $1,615M ÷ $8,502M = 19.0%

Invested capital, excluding goodwill:
= $8,502M − $6,342M goodwill = $2,160M

ROIC (excl. goodwill) = $1,615M ÷ $2,160M = 74.8%

Warren: This split is the whole story of Leidos's economics, and you must see both numbers to understand the business:

  • Excluding goodwill, the underlying operating business earns ~75% on the tangible/working capital it employs. That is spectacular — it tells you the core services operation is genuinely capital-light and high-return. You need very little hard capital to run a business of clearing-badge engineers and program managers.
  • Including goodwill, ROIC falls to ~19%. That is still comfortably above any reasonable cost of capital — but the collapse from 75% to 19% measures exactly how much the company paid for its acquired revenue. The goodwill is the price of growth-by-acquisition, and it dilutes the headline return by roughly four-fifths.

PRINCIPLE (ROIC with and without goodwill exposes what acquisitions cost). The underlying business is a high-return, capital-light machine (~75%). But the all-in return the owner actually earns on capital deployed — including the premiums paid for acquisitions — is ~19%. Both are true; the gap is the M&A bill. What matters for valuation is that even the all-in 19% clears the ~9-10% cost of capital, so growth has created value — so far.

The moat — and where it erodes

Ben: What protects that return? The manual's diagnostic (Chapter 5) is pricing power. Let us be precise about a moderate, asymmetric moat:

Sources of the moat (real):
- Security clearances and past-performance qualifications — you cannot bid classified work without cleared people and a track record; that is a genuine regulatory/credential barrier.
- Incumbency and switching costs — an agency that has run a program on Leidos for a decade faces real cost and risk to re-compete it away.
- Scale — $17B of revenue lets Leidos carry the bid-and-proposal, compliance, and overhead infrastructure that a small firm cannot.

Where the moat is weak (be specific):
- No pricing power. The customer is a monopsony that runs competitive recompetes and, on ~44% of the book, pays cost-plus-a-fixed-fee. Leidos cannot raise price without losing the bid. By the manual's own test — can it raise prices materially without losing volume? — the answer is no. So this is a moat of retention, not of pricing.
- Erosion vectors are concrete, not hypothetical: (1) DOGE-style efficiency drives — an independent research service notes the stock fell 39% in early 2025 on exactly these fears; (2) budget cuts and continuing-resolution paralysis delaying awards; (3) insourcing — the government bringing work back in-house; (4) protest/recompete losses — a lost recompete transfers years of backlog to a rival overnight.

PRINCIPLE (Moat diagnosis — retention is not pricing power). A business can have high switching costs and durable relationships (a real moat) yet no ability to raise price (a monopsony customer). Leidos keeps its customers but cannot dictate terms. That combination supports steady volume forecasting — and forbids the fat, expanding margins that justify a premium multiple. It is a good business, not a wonderful one.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Warren: The manual (Chapter 8) says: once a business throws off more cash than it needs, judge management by what it does with the dollar. Leidos generated $1,750M of operating cash in FY2025. Here are the five uses, with the actual figures (10-K Cash Flows & Statement of Equity):

  1. Reinvest in the business — capex $125M. Trivial, because the business is capital-light. High incremental returns, small absolute need.
  2. Acquire — $293M for Kudu Dynamics (FY2025); and, just after year-end, $2.4B for ENTRUST (March 27, 2026), a utility-infrastructure engineering firm, financed largely with new debt (10-Q, Note 3).
  3. Pay down debt — $1,019M of principal repayments in FY2025, including a $450M term-loan prepayment (MD&A).
  4. Dividends — $211M paid; $1.63/share, raised steadily from $1.44 (2022) → $1.46 → $1.54 → $1.63.
  5. Buybacks — $400M open-market plus a $500M accelerated repurchase; shares outstanding fell from 131.2M (Jan 2025) to 126.4M (Jan 2026) and to 125.8M by April 2026.

The one-dollar test

Ben: The manual's market test (Chapter 8): retain a dollar only if it creates at least a dollar of value — i.e., incremental ROIC ≥ r.

Leidos retained roughly $1,237M of FY2025 earnings (net income to Leidos $1,448M − dividends $211M). Over the last several years those retained dollars funded acquisitions and buybacks while EPS climbed from ~$5 to ~$11 and the all-in ROIC held at ~19% — well above a ~9-10% cost of capital. The company passes the one-dollar test on the record so far.

M&A discipline vs. empire-building

Warren: This is where I want you to be skeptical. The ENTRUST deal is $2.4 billion — a big check for a company that earns ~$1.6B of owner earnings a year. Management's own forecast, per an independent research service, is for "quite modest revenue gains following the acquisition," which "created questions about the assumed organic growth going forward." The stock fell 8% after that call. So: is bolt-on M&A here disciplined redeployment, or the institutional imperative — expanding for its own sake?

The evidence is mixed but not damning: the SES goodwill impairment in 2023 was an admission that at least one prior deal was overpaid (candor — good). The all-in ROIC of 19% says the acquisitions have, in aggregate, cleared the hurdle (good). But a $2.4B deal justified by "modest" growth is exactly the kind of transaction to watch — if the next three of these earn below the cost of capital, the compounding story breaks.

Buybacks on price

Ben: The manual is emphatic: the price paid is everything. At ~10× earnings and below our intrinsic-value estimate, repurchases are accretive — the company is retiring its own owner earnings at a ~10% yield. Management reduced the share count ~4% in a year at prices between roughly $107 and $205. Buying below value is value-creating; we approve of buybacks at this price and would grow wary only if they kept buying at $250+.

Candor

Warren: Management reports its impairments plainly, discloses the termination-for-convenience risk in its own filings, and does not lead with EBITDA. That is the candor the manual prizes.

PRINCIPLE (Capital allocation & the one-dollar test). Judge management on the dollar, not the narrative. Leidos has (so far) redeployed retained earnings at ~19% all-in ROIC, bought back stock below value, and paid down debt — value-creating on the record. The open question is M&A discipline: a $2.4B deal on "modest" growth is precisely the transaction the institutional imperative produces. Watch the incremental returns, not the press release.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Ben: The manual insists the output is a range, not a point (Chapter 9), and that for a stable, mature franchise the honest primary tool is a normalized-earnings multiple, cross-checked by a DCF and reconciled against peers and history. We will do all three, show where each multiple comes from, and then reconcile with an independent research service's aggressive $264 target.

Our normalized inputs: normalized EPS ≈ $12.00 (between reported diluted $11.14, VL-adjusted $11.99, and FCF/share $12.86); normalized owner earnings ≈ $1,600M; ~125.5M shares.

Tool 1 — Normalized earnings multiple (primary)

Where does the multiple come from? Three honest anchors:

  • (a) Leidos's own history. an independent research service's 10-year median P/E is 15×; current P/E is ~12-13×. The 15× reflects years (2019-2022) when defense budgets were rising and DOGE was not a word.
  • (b) Government-services peers. From the June 26 Selection & Opinion, SAIC — Leidos's closest pure government-IT comp — trades at ~13× (current P/E, Portfolio II, price $109.39). CACI ($507.56) and Booz Allen sit in a similar low-teens-to-mid-teens band. Leidos at ~10× live is at a discount to its nearest peer.
  • (c) A quality/growth-justified multiple. A ~7% grower earning all-in ROIC (~19%) comfortably above its ~9-10% cost of capital deserves a market-ish or slightly-below-market multiple. But the monopsony-and-no-pricing-power discount pulls it back down. Net, we think 12-13× normalized earnings is the fair central multiple, not the market's 18×.

Arithmetic:
- Low: 11× × $12.00 = $132
- Base: 13× × $12.00 = $156
- High: 15× × $12.00 = $180 (Leidos's own historical median)

Warren: The EV/EBIT lens lands in the same neighborhood — roughly $140–$173 at 10-12× EBIT, which is the right band for a steady defense-services name.

Tool 3 — Two-stage DCF on owner earnings (the discipline of gravity)

Ben: The manual's master equation: IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + terminal value. The discount rate is gravity (Chapter 11). With the 10-year Treasury at 4.47% and the 30-year at 4.97% (Selection & Opinion, 6/15/26), and a premium for the monopsony/budget risk, we set r = 10% as our base, and test 9% and 11%.

Starting owner earnings $1,600M, ~125.5M shares. Because a low-pricing-power business cannot compound like a branded franchise, we deliberately use modest growth — 5% base, not the 7-9% an independent research service assumes.

Sensitivity table — IV per share (owner earnings $1,600M, 10-year explicit stage):

g = 3% g = 5% g = 6%
r = 9% $209 $243 $262
r = 10% $181 $209 $225
r = 11% $159 $183 $197

Warren: Now — a teaching moment, and it is the most important arithmetic on this page. Look how high even the "conservative" DCF prints. At r = 10%, g = 5%, the model says $209/share — nearly double the price. Should we trust it?

No — and here is why. A discounted-cash-flow model rewards durable compounding. When you let a stream grow 5% for ten years and then forever, the terminal value swamps everything and the output balloons. But Leidos is a monopsony-customer services business with no pricing power. A single adverse budget cycle — a DOGE-style efficiency mandate, a lost recompete, a continuing-resolution freeze — can reset the base, not just dent one year's growth. The DCF has no way to price that "the whole staircase can drop a floor" risk except through the discount rate and the growth assumption, and even at r = 11%/g = 3% it still says $159.

PRINCIPLE (The DCF flatters businesses that cannot actually compound). A two-stage DCF assumes the base is permanent and only asks how fast it grows. For a business whose base can be revoked by one customer's budget decision, the DCF systematically overstates value. This is exactly why the manual makes the normalized multiple the primary tool for a mature, low-pricing-power franchise — and why we anchor our IV there, using the DCF only to confirm there is no downside surprise, not to set the ceiling.

Reconciliation — and where an independent research service's $264 comes from

Ben: Triangulating the three tools, and deliberately weighting the normalized multiple and EV/EBIT over the compounding-flattered DCF, we set:

Per share Basis
IV Low $120 10-11× normalized EPS / bearish-budget DCF
IV Base $150 ~12.5-13× normalized EPS; EV/EBIT ~10.5×; DCF sanity floor well above
IV High $185 15× historical median EPS; EV/EBIT ~12×; DCF at 9%/6%

Now reconcile with an independent research service's 18-month target of $264 (and 2029-31 range $210-$320). Where does that come from?

  • $264 ÷ $12.00 normalized EPS = 22×; $264 ÷ $12.80 (FY26 estimate) = 20.6× forward.
  • An independent research service is applying a ~20-22× multiple — roughly the market multiple (18×) plus a premium — and assuming 7-9% earnings growth with no budget disruption.

Warren: So the gap between our $150 base and an independent research service's $264 is entirely a multiple/growth judgment. An independent research service prices Leidos as a market-multiple grower. We price it as a cheap-but-budget-exposed services house deserving a below-market ~13× multiple. We think a 20-22× multiple on a monopsony-customer, cost-plus services business is optimistic — it treats the DOGE/budget risk as noise rather than a structural discount. We take an independent research service's target as an upper-bound scenario if the budget worries fully dissipate, not as a central case.

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth

Ben: One last check (Chapter 10). Growth adds value only when ROIC > r. Leidos's all-in ROIC (~19%) exceeds its cost of capital (~9-10%), so its growth — organic and the acquisitions that cleared the hurdle — has been value-creating. The warning: if future bolt-ons like ENTRUST earn below 10%, that growth starts consuming value even as revenue rises. Growth here is a servant of returns, not a virtue in itself.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Warren: Now we consult Mr. Market — and only now, after we have formed our own view (Chapter 2).

The an independent research service report of May 15, 2026 was struck at $148.81. The live price on July 4, 2026 is $108.84 — the stock has fallen ~27% in seven weeks. Mr. Market has had a mood swing.

Margin of safety, as arithmetic (Chapter 12: MoS = (IV − Price) ÷ IV):

Ben: So at $108.84 the stock trades below even our conservative low estimate, with a ~27% cushion to our base case. The "cushion price" — where the margin of safety against our $150 base disappears to a slim 10% — is about $135. Below that, we are being paid to bear the budget risk; above ~$150 we are not.

Is Mr. Market fearful or rational?

Warren: This is the crux of THE ONE QUESTION. An independent research service tells us why the price fell: fears of DOGE (Department of Government Efficiency) cuts knocked the shares 39% in early 2025; a recovery on strong bookings; then a disappointing Q1-2026 (sub-4% revenue growth, "modest" post-ENTRUST guidance) sent them back near the lows. The June macro note confirms the 10-year yield near 4.5% and a market trading at 18× — Leidos at ~10× is a genuine outlier.

Two readings, and honest people can differ:

  • Mr. Market is fearful (opportunity): The business is intact — $49B backlog, 1.0×+ book-to-bill, 19% ROIC, steady FCF, A financial strength. The market is extrapolating a budget scare into a permanent impairment that the numbers do not (yet) show. A cheap, steady cash compounder on sale.
  • Mr. Market is rational (value trap): A monopsony customer is signaling it wants to spend less. Bookings fell from $23B to $17.5B. Management itself guides to "modest" growth. Perhaps 10× is the correct multiple for a business whose single customer is turning frugal — cheap for a reason.

PRINCIPLE (Volatility is raw material — but "cheap" must be interrogated). A 27% drop on an intact business is opportunity, per Mr. Market's allegory. But the manual also warns of value traps: a low multiple on a business whose economics are genuinely deteriorating is not a bargain. The discipline is to decide which you are looking at — and to demand a margin of safety precisely because you cannot be certain. Here the ~27% cushion to base is real, but it is not the 40-50% we would want given monopsony risk.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Ben & Warren, together:

Verdict: A GOOD business, trading cheap, with a real monopsony overhang — not a wonderful one.

Leidos is not a wonderful business in the manual's strict sense: it lacks pricing power, its margins are thin (~12% EBIT), and its single customer can revoke its backlog. It is a good business: high underlying capital efficiency (~75% ex-goodwill, ~19% all-in ROIC), a durable retention moat (clearances, incumbency, scale), an A-rated balance sheet, strong FCF conversion, and disciplined-enough capital allocation. At ~10× earnings and below our $120 low estimate, it is priced as though the budget risk is closer to a certainty than a risk.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION

On the weight of the evidence — an intact $49B backlog, book-to-bill above 1.0, ROIC far above cost of capital, and cash flow that exceeds earnings — we judge that Mr. Market is being more fearful than the current numbers justify, and that at $108.84 the price embeds a harsher budget scenario than the filings show. But we hold that view with humility: the fear is not irrational, because the monopsony customer really is signaling frugality, and a services base can be reset. This is a good business trading cheap, with a genuine cloud — the kind of situation where a margin of safety, not a target price, is what protects you.

Open questions (the honest unknowns)

  1. Does the DOGE/efficiency drive cut Leidos's addressable spending, or merely reshuffle it? The whole valuation gap turns on this, and it is outside our circle.
  2. Was the $2.4B ENTRUST acquisition disciplined or empire-building? If its returns come in below ~10%, the compounding story cracks; if it clears the hurdle, the deal is fine. We won't know for two or three years.
  3. Can Leidos hold its recompete win rate as budgets tighten? Bookings fell from $23B to $17.5B — a one-year wobble, or a trend?

Universal lessons (portable to the next case)

  1. "Cheap" and "value" are not synonyms. A ~10× multiple is only a bargain if the earning power is durable. The work is not spotting a low multiple; it is deciding whether it is cheap-for-a-reason (value trap) or genuine value (Mr. Market's fear). We concluded the latter — provisionally, and with a margin of safety, never on faith.
  2. A monopsony customer is a structural risk that no DCF fully captures. When one buyer sets your price, funds your backlog, and can cancel for convenience, your "durable" cash flows are durable only at that buyer's pleasure. Discount for it explicitly — through a lower multiple — rather than trusting a growth-flattered model.
  3. Compute ROIC both ways. The gap between ex-goodwill (~75%) and all-in (~19%) returns is the clearest single measure of what a company's acquisitions actually cost — and whether its growth is creating or consuming value.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (in order of appearance, with LDOS's numbers)

Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can forecast within a sensible range; its boundary matters more than its size. LDOS: mechanics (backlog, win rate, budgets) are inside; the level of federal spending is outside — the pivotal unknown.

Monopsony. A market with a single dominant buyer. LDOS: the U.S. government buys the bulk of its output, can run competitive recompetes, pays cost-plus on ~44% of revenue, and can terminate contracts "for convenience" — the structural source of low pricing power.

Operating (EBIT) margin = Operating income ÷ Revenue. LDOS: $2,109M ÷ $17,174M = 12.3%.

Net margin = Net income ÷ Revenue. LDOS: $1,448M ÷ $17,174M = 8.4%.

Book value (why we ignore it). Accounting equity; understates great businesses, overstates poor ones. LDOS: equity $4,916M is smaller than goodwill $6,342M — book is mostly acquisition residue, useless as a value anchor.

Interest coverage = Operating income ÷ Interest expense. LDOS: $2,109M ÷ $203M = 10.4× (safe).

Debt / Operating cash flow = Total debt ÷ OCF. LDOS: $4,648M ÷ $1,750M = 2.66× (net debt basis: 2.02×). (an independent research service's 0.76× does not reconcile with the 10-K figures; VL's post-ENTRUST capital box shows total debt $6,334M.)

Free cash flow (FCF) = Operating cash flow − Capex. LDOS: $1,750M − $125M = $1,625M (~$12.86/share).

Backlog / book-to-bill. Contracted future revenue; book-to-bill = bookings ÷ revenue. LDOS: total backlog $49,032M (funded $9,685M), ~2.9× revenue; book-to-bill = $17,500M ÷ $17,174M = 1.02×.

Owner earnings = Reported earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. LDOS: $1,448M + $290M − ~$160M ≈ $1,578M (generous); ~$1,448M (conservative, treating intangible amortization as a real cost); normalized ~$1,600M.

Maintenance vs. growth capex. Capex to stand still vs. to expand. LDOS: total capex only $125M (<0.8% of revenue) — essentially all maintenance; a capital-light business.

NOPAT = Operating income × (1 − tax rate). LDOS: $2,109M × (1 − 0.234) = $1,615M.

Invested capital = Debt + Equity − Cash. LDOS: $4,648M + $4,962M − $1,108M = $8,502M (incl. goodwill); $2,160M (excl. goodwill).

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested capital. LDOS: $1,615M ÷ $8,502M = 19.0% (incl. goodwill); $1,615M ÷ $2,160M = 74.8% (excl. goodwill). The gap = the acquisition bill.

ROE = Net income ÷ Equity. LDOS: $1,448M ÷ $4,916M = 29.5%.

Moat / pricing power. A durable barrier protecting returns; its signature is the ability to raise price without losing volume. LDOS: moderate retention moat (clearances, incumbency, scale), no pricing power.

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). LDOS: retained ~$1,237M in FY2025; all-in ROIC ~19% > r ~9-10% → passes on the record.

Discount rate (r) — "gravity." Owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long rates + risk premium. LDOS: 10-yr Treasury 4.47%, 30-yr 4.97% → base r = 10% (tested 9-11%).

Intrinsic value (two-stage) = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. LDOS: DCF prints $159-$262 across the grid — flagged as overstated for a non-compounding, monopsony-exposed business; IV anchored instead on normalized multiple.

Normalized-earnings multiple (primary tool here). Fair P/E × normalized EPS. LDOS: ~13× × $12.00 = $156 base; anchors from history (VL median 15×), peers (SAIC ~13×), quality (7% grower, ROIC > r).

EV/EBIT. Enterprise value ÷ EBIT; equity = EV − net debt. LDOS: 10-12× EBIT → $140-$173/share.

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth. Growth adds value only when ROIC > r. LDOS: ~19% > ~10% → value-creating, conditional on future M&A clearing the hurdle.

Margin of safety = (Intrinsic value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic value. LDOS: at $108.84 — 27% to base ($150), 9% to low ($120), 41% to high ($185).

Mr. Market. The manic-depressive partner who quotes a price daily; serve, don't obey him. LDOS: quoted $148.81 (May 15) then $108.84 (July 4) — a 27% mood swing on DOGE/budget fear.

Risk (correctly defined). The probability of permanent loss of capital, not price volatility. LDOS: the real risk is a structural, monopsony-driven reset of earning power — not the 27% quote drop, which (if the business is intact) is opportunity.
