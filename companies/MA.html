Mastercard Inc. (MA) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Analyst's case study, 2026-07-13. Written in the Graham–Buffett tradition, using the method in "How to Value a Business — A Security-Analysis Manual." The voice and the conclusion are mine; the tools — margin of safety, Mr. Market, owner earnings, the moat, the one-dollar test — I credit to Graham and Buffett by name. This is a teaching case, not investment advice.

Opening the Case — The Business

Let me start where an owner would: what is this thing, and how does it actually make money?

Mastercard is not a bank and it does not lend you money. It runs the rails — a global electronic payments network that sits between the bank that issued your card (the issuer) and the bank that signed up the merchant (the acquirer), switching the transaction, setting the rules, and guaranteeing settlement. It "serves as a processor, franchisor, and advisor to about 25,000 financial institutions" (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, Business description). Crucially, the issuers and acquirers are its customers; you and the merchant are the users of a brand those customers license.

The money comes in two streams, and the latest quarter shows the split cleanly (10-Q, 2026-04-30, Item 2, Net Revenue table, Q1 2026):

  • Payment network — $4,948 million, up 12% — assessments charged on the dollar volume that crosses the network (domestic and cross-border) and on the number of transactions switched. This is the toll-booth: a tiny fraction of a percent on gross dollar volume, collected on tens of billions of transactions.
  • Value-added services and solutions — $3,450 million, up 22% — cybersecurity, fraud/identity, data analytics, consulting, and processing services sold on top of the rails. This is the faster-growing leg, and it is deepening the moat by making the network stickier.

Total Q1 2026 net revenue was $8,398 million, up 16% (10-Q, 2026-04-30, Item 2). For the full year 2025, revenue was $32,791 million (10-K, 2026-02-11, Item 8; MA_xbrl_facts.json, FY2025). Note one accounting feature you must respect: Mastercard reports revenue net of the rebates and incentives it pays customers — Q1 2026 payment-network revenue was struck after "$5,639 million of rebates and incentives provided to customers" (10-Q, 2026-04-30, Item 2). The reported top line is already a net number; do not double-count that give-back later.

The one question that will decide this case. Mastercard is, by almost any measure, a magnificent business — I will prove that with the returns on capital shortly. So the case does not turn on quality. It turns on this: is the network moat durable enough over the next decade — against interchange regulation, against account-to-account and real-time-payment rails that could route around the card networks, against the two governments that keep trying to build domestic substitutes — to justify paying roughly 31 times earnings today? Everything below builds toward answering that.

Circle of competence. Can I understand this well enough to forecast its economics ten years out? Mostly, yes. The revenue model is transparent (a toll on payment volume plus services), the demand driver is simple and secular (the decades-long migration of the world's spending from cash and checks onto electronic rails), and the two-player structure with Visa is stable and easy to reason about. What sits at the edge of my circle is the technology-and-regulation frontier: whether real-time account-to-account systems (Brazil's Pix, India's UPI, the EU's push under PSD2/instant payments) erode the card networks' share, and how far regulators will squeeze interchange. I can understand the business; I cannot forecast that frontier with precision — so I will carry that uncertainty exactly where the manual tells me to: in conservative inputs and a demanded margin of safety, not in a false-precision forecast. Principle: a stock is a fractional claim on a business, and you value only what you can understand — naming the edge of your competence matters more than the size of the circle (manual ch. 4).

The Moat — Is It Durable?

Before I spend an hour in the statements, I make the qualitative judgment that can end the analysis early: does Mastercard have a durable competitive advantage, and what could break it?

What protects the earnings. The moat here is one of the cleanest examples of a network effect in public markets, reinforced by scale and switching costs. More cardholders make the network more valuable to merchants; more accepting merchants make it more valuable to cardholders; more of both make it more valuable to the issuing and acquiring banks. That flywheel has produced a global duopoly with Visa in which the two networks are accepted almost everywhere and no new entrant can bootstrap either side of the two-sided market from scratch. Layer on top: enormous fixed-cost scale (the switching infrastructure is built; each incremental transaction is nearly free to process, which is why the operating margin is above 60%), high switching costs for the 25,000 issuing institutions wired into the rails, and a trusted brand that is one of the payment credentials a consumer will not leave home without. Buffett's franchise test — a product "needed or desired," with "no close substitute," not price-regulated (manual ch. 5) — is met on the first two; the third is where the pressure sits.

The diagnostic is pricing power, and Mastercard has it: revenue has compounded at 14.5% per year over the past decade (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, Annual Rates) on transaction and dollar volume that grows with the economy plus a secular mix shift, and it has repeatedly raised assessments and cross-sold higher-margin services without losing its network. A business that can do that has a moat.

Now be skeptical — what erodes it over ten years? Three threats, in order of how much they worry me:

  1. Disintermediation by new rails. Real-time account-to-account systems — Pix in Brazil, UPI in India, and instant-payment schemes the EU is mandating — let money move bank-to-bank without a card network in the middle. Where governments sponsor these and mandate low or zero cost, they genuinely take share from cards. This is the threat with teeth, and it is why Mastercard is buying its way into open-banking, real-time payments, and services — to be a toll-collector on the new rails too, not just the old.
  2. Interchange and network regulation. Regulators worldwide keep pressing on interchange and network fees (the EU under PSD2 forcing account access to third parties; U.S. merchant litigation running for two decades — see the Financial Strength section). This compresses the toll rather than removing the road.
  3. Government-built national substitutes. Some governments "mandate switching of domestic payments … by only domestic companies" (10-K, 2026-02-11, Item 1) — India, China, and others are explicit. This fences off domestic volume in specific large markets.

My judgment: the moat is wide but not impregnable — a genuine network-effect duopoly with pricing power, facing a slow, real, and government-assisted erosion of its most-regulated, most-substitutable volumes. That is a business whose rate of high-return growth I should discount toward the conservative end, not one whose earnings I should assume disappear. Principle: durable earning power must be defended by something real and namable; here it is the two-sided network effect, and the honest analyst argues the moat's durability rather than admiring it (manual ch. 5). I will now let the returns on capital confirm or expose it.

Reading the Statements Together

The extracted filing text I have gives me the footnote-level accounting policies and the litigation notes but not the full face statements, so I build the numbers from Mastercard's structured XBRL history (MA_xbrl_facts.json) and the an independent research service per-share array (2026-05-01), each cited as I go. The three statements tell one coherent story.

Income statement — a toll booth with widening margins. The march from revenue to profit, FY2025 (MA_xbrl_facts.json unless noted):

  • Revenue $32,791M.
  • Operating income $18,897M → operating margin = 18,897 ÷ 32,791 = 57.6% on a GAAP basis; an independent research service, which strikes it slightly differently, shows a 61.1% operating margin for 2025 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01). Either way, this is a business that keeps roughly sixty cents of every revenue dollar as operating profit.
  • Diluted EPS $16.52 GAAP (MA_xbrl_facts.json, FY2025). An independent research service's adjusted diluted EPS is $17.01, the difference being $0.49 of nonrecurring litigation-type losses it strips out (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, footnote A).
  • GAAP net income $14,968M, read directly off the 10-K consolidated statement of operations (10-K, 2026-02-11, Item 8, Consolidated Statement of Operations; ties out to diluted EPS $16.52 × 906M weighted diluted shares). I take it from the face statement rather than reverse-engineering it from an independent research service's per-share array. Net margin = 14,968 ÷ 32,791 = 45.6%.

The multi-year trend is relentlessly up and to the right: operating income $8,081M (2020) → $10,082M (2021) → $12,264M (2022) → $14,008M (2023) → $15,582M (2024) → $18,897M (2025) (MA_xbrl_facts.json). No cyclical sawtooth — a steady compounder.

Balance sheet — small, and that is the point. FY2025 (MA_xbrl_facts.json): total assets $54,157M, total liabilities $46,411M, stockholders' equity $7,737M. Note how little equity supports ~$15.0B of earnings — book value is a rounding error against the earning power, which is the fingerprint of a franchise. An independent research service reports book value per share of $8.65 for 2025, of which fully $16.91/share is intangibles and goodwill from acquisitions (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, footnote C) — meaning tangible book value is negative. That is not a red flag here; it is what happens when a company earns so much on so little capital that it can return essentially everything to owners and buy back stock until equity nearly vanishes. Never anchor value to book for a business like this: book understates a great franchise (manual ch. 7).

Two judgment-laden items to hold in mind. First, goodwill and indefinite-lived intangibles are tested for impairment annually with a qualitative screen (10-K, 2026-02-11, Item 8) — acquisition-heavy accounting to watch, but no impairments of note. Second, settlement assets and obligations: Mastercard "guarantees the settlement of many of the payment network transactions" and carries large offsetting settlement balances that inflate both sides of the balance sheet and the current accounts (10-K, 2026-02-11, Item 8). These largely net out and are short-dated (a few days), but they are why the raw current ratio looks thin — more on that under Financial Strength.

Cash flow — the truth-teller confirms the earnings. Operating cash flow FY2025 was $17,648M (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of cash flows; MA_xbrl_facts.json), above GAAP net income of $14,968M. When a company's operating cash exceeds its reported profit year after year, the earnings are real, not an accounting mirage. Capital spending is trivial: purchases of property and equipment were $489M (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of cash flows), about 1.5% of revenue. So free cash flow = 17,648 − 489 = $17,159M. The gap between operating cash ($17,648M) and net income + depreciation ($14,968M + $1,143M = $16,111M) — roughly $1.5B — is stock-based compensation added back ($597M) plus working-capital and deferred-tax timing; I deal with the stock-comp piece honestly in the next section.

Earnings-quality scan. Can I trust these numbers? Largely yes. Cash exceeds income (good). There is no EBITDA showmanship in the disclosure. The one thing to flag is the recurring "nonrecurring" litigation charge: An independent research service has stripped a nonrecurring loss from EPS in six of the last nine years — '17, '18, '19, '20, '23, '24, and '25 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, footnote A). When "one-time" charges appear almost every year, they are, in Buffett's sense, a recurring cost of doing business (manual ch. 7) — here, the perennial interchange litigation. I will treat that litigation drag as an ongoing cost, not a windfall to add back, which pushes me to anchor on GAAP net income (which bears it) rather than an independent research service's adjusted figure. Principle: the statements are evidence and cash is the truth-teller; when the three articulate and operating cash outruns reported profit, the earnings are trustworthy — but read "one-time" charges that recur as the ordinary cost they are (manual ch. 7).

Earnings Power & Returns on Capital

Is earning power growing? Unambiguously. Over the decade, revenue per share compounded 14.5% and earnings per share 18.0% per year (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, Annual Rates); operating income has risen every single year through the 2020 pandemic dip and out the other side (MA_xbrl_facts.json). This is stable-to-growing earning power, not a cyclical needing me to average across a cycle. So normalization here is not about a peak/trough — it is about (a) using GAAP rather than a flattered adjusted number, and (b) treating stock-based compensation as the real cost it is.

Estimating owner earnings. Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital (manual ch. 7). The subtle, decisive rule for Mastercard: stock-based compensation is NOT added back — it is a real cost paid in dilution, and the one non-cash charge you leave as a cost (manual ch. 7). I therefore anchor on GAAP net income, which already expenses stock comp, and I build up carefully:

  • GAAP net income (FY2025): $14,968M (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of operations)
    • Depreciation & amortization: +$1,143M (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of cash flows) — much of this is amortization of acquired intangibles, a genuinely non-cash charge
  • − Maintenance capex: −$489M (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of cash flows, purchases of property and equipment; MA is capital-light, so essentially all of its modest capex is maintenance)
  • − Incremental working capital: ≈ $0 (treated as neutral; settlement balances net out and non-settlement working capital is not a persistent cash drain)
  • = Owner earnings ≈ $15,622M

Note what I did not do: I did not add back the $597M of stock-based compensation (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of cash flows) that inflates operating cash flow — it is a real cost, left in the number. Had I instead built owner earnings up from free cash flow ($17,159M), I would have had to subtract that stock comp — 17,159 − 597 ≈ $16.6B — so my net-income-anchored $15,622M is the more conservative of the two constructions, which is exactly what I want entering a valuation.

Per share: $15,622M ÷ ~893M year-end shares ≈ $17.5 per share. This is my single normalized owner-earnings figure, and I use this same number — $17.5/share, ≈ $15.6B in aggregate — in all three universal cross-checks. (I strike per-share owner earnings on the ~893M year-end share count, while the GAAP EPS line above uses the ~906M weighted-average diluted count; $17.5 sits just above GAAP EPS $16.52, which is what you expect for a capital-light franchise where depreciation modestly exceeds maintenance capex.)

Now the moat's quantitative test — return on invested capital. This is where a real advantage separates from a story (manual ch. 6).

  • NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate) = $18,897M × (1 − 0.194) = $15,231M (operating income from MA_xbrl_facts.json; 19.4% tax rate from an independent research service, 2026-05-01)
  • Invested capital = debt + equity − cash = $19,000M + $7,737M − $10,566M = $16,171M (total debt and cash from an independent research service/MA_xbrl_facts.json; equity from MA_xbrl_facts.json)
  • ROIC = 15,231 ÷ 16,171 = ≈ 94%

Ninety-four percent. One honest caveat on the denominator: this is ROIC on year-end invested capital, and year-end capital is understated because the $11.7B of buybacks run through the year depleted the equity and cash that sat in the base. On average invested capital (a higher denominator) the figure is lower — but even doubling the base leaves ROIC many multiples of any plausible discount rate. And on tangible capital it is effectively infinite, because tangible equity is negative. So the spot-vs-average distinction changes the decimal, not the conclusion. This is the "great business" of the manual's savings-account metaphor — the account paying an extraordinarily high rate that rises over time, needing almost no new capital to grow (manual ch. 6). The moat is not a story; the returns prove it.

Return on equity is not a useful lens here — an independent research service marks it "NMF" (not meaningful) for recent years (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) because relentless buybacks have shrunk equity toward zero, so ROE explodes to meaningless triple digits (14,968 ÷ 7,737 = 193%). That number is an artifact of the capital structure, not a measure of quality; ROIC is the honest gauge, and it is spectacular.

Growth that creates value. With ROIC (~94%) enormously above the discount rate (~10%), every dollar Mastercard reinvests creates far more than a dollar of value (manual ch. 11). But here is the beautiful part: because it is capital-light, it barely needs to reinvest to grow — it returned ~97% of net income to owners in 2025 (see Management) and still grew earnings double digits. That is the capital-light, high-ROIC franchise the whole method prizes. Principle: persistently high ROIC far above the cost of capital is the moat's quantitative fingerprint; a business that grows without consuming capital is worth a high multiple of its earnings (manual ch. 6, 11).

Financial Strength & Solvency

Can Mastercard survive a bad year without becoming a forced seller? Read off the balance sheet, tested against the cash flow that services it.

A data-integrity flag first (rigor rule 5). The equity_atlas.xlsx cell for MA long-term debt reads "$20M," giving a Debt/OCF of 0.001 — that is a bad data cell, an XBRL-tag mismatch (the same "$20M, 2009" artifact appears in MA_xbrl_facts.json's LongTermDebt field). The true figure, which I use throughout, is an independent research service's: Total debt $19.00 billion, long-term debt $18.25 billion as of 12/31/25 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, Capital Structure). Never build a solvency verdict on an obviously broken cell.

Leverage. Net debt = total debt − cash = $19,000M − $10,566M = $8,434M (an independent research service; MA_xbrl_facts.json). Against owner earnings of $15,622M, net debt ÷ owner earnings = 8,434 ÷ 15,622 = 0.54 years. Mastercard could clear all its net debt with under seven months of owner earnings. For a business this stable, that is trivial leverage.

Coverage. Operating income ÷ interest = $18,897M ÷ $620M (long-term interest, an independent research service, 2026-05-01) = ≈ 30×. A normal year covers the interest bill thirty times over. It would take a near-total collapse of the payments economy to threaten the interest coverage.

Liquidity. The reported current ratio looks tight — current assets $23,558M ÷ current liabilities $22,762M = 1.03 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, Current Position 12/31/25) — but that is because short-dated settlement obligations sit in current liabilities and settlement assets in current assets; they are matched and clear within days (10-K, 2026-02-11, Item 8). Strip the settlement plumbing and the picture is a company sitting on $10.6 billion of cash (up 25% year over year, an independent research service, 2026-05-01) against a $749M current portion of debt. Liquidity is not a question here.

Refinancing wall / hidden obligations. Debt is well-laddered against $17.6B of annual operating cash; there is no maturity that must be rolled in a shut market. Operating leases are small ($187M annual rentals) and the pension is essentially fully funded ($499M assets vs. $452M obligations) (an independent research service, 2026-05-01). The one genuine off-balance-sheet exposure is litigation, not debt. The 10-Q lists the buckets separately, and it is worth keeping them separate rather than merging them into one band (10-Q, 2026-04-30, Item 1):
- U.S. MDL interchange cases — accrued liability $177M at 3/31/26 (down from $637M at year-end 2025 after first-quarter payments);
- U.K./Pan-European merchant damages — unresolved claims of over £0.3B (~$0.4B at 3/31/26);
- a distinct U.K. commercial-card collective claim — damages sought in excess of £1B (~$1.3B at 3/31/26);
- Portugal consumer collective action — ~€0.4B / ~$0.5B;
- Netherlands merchant collective action — in excess of €0.3B (~$0.3B).

These are real and recurring, but each is a manageable fraction of a single year's owner earnings — a chronic cost, not a solvency event.

Verdict: fortress. an independent research service's independent read agrees — Financial Strength A+, Safety rank 1 (its highest), Price Stability 90, Earnings Predictability 90 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01). Principle: solvency is the precondition that lets every other quality compound; here the balance sheet removes survival from the list of things I need to worry about, so the case rests entirely on price versus a durable moat (manual ch. 8).

Management as Partners

Judge management by where the free cash goes and how the share count moves (manual ch. 9).

The capital-allocation record. In FY2025 Mastercard generated $14,968M of net income and returned essentially all of it: $11,727M in buybacks and $2,756M in dividends (MA_xbrl_facts.json), a total of $14,483M — about 97% of net income. This is correct behavior for a capital-light franchise: with so few high-return internal projects requiring capital (it already earns ~94% on the little capital it uses), the one-dollar test says return the cash rather than retain it (manual ch. 9), and management does. Reinvestment and bolt-on acquisitions (into services, cyber, open banking) absorb only a modest slice, and those extend the moat.

The share count. Intrinsic value is a per-share quantity, so watch the count. Shares outstanding fell from 1,081M (2016) to 894M (2025) — a 17.3% reduction over the decade (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, per-share array), and an independent research service projects the buyback to continue toward ~810M by 2029-31. Steady, meaningful shrinkage that concentrates each remaining owner's claim.

The one caution — buyback price discipline. The manual is firm that buybacks create value only below intrinsic value and destroy it above (manual ch. 9). Mastercard buys back mechanically, in size, every year regardless of price — and at ~31× earnings, some of that repurchase is likely happening at or above a conservative intrinsic value. It is not value-destructive on the scale of a bad acquisition, but it is worth naming: I would prefer management flex the buyback down when the stock is dear. Set against that, there is no Dexter-Shoe sin here — acquisitions are small and paid mostly in cash, not in undervalued stock (manual ch. 9). Principle: over time you ride with management's capital allocation; Mastercard passes the one-dollar test and respects the share count, with the single qualifier that price-insensitive buybacks are a mild, not fatal, flaw.

What Is It Worth? — Intrinsic Value

Now the arithmetic — earned only because the business cleared every quality gate above.

Choosing the method. Mastercard is a stable, mature, extraordinarily profitable franchise with a forecastable secular tailwind. That is precisely the case where the manual says a normalized-earnings multiple is the steadier, more honest primary tool, cross-checked with a two-stage DCF (manual ch. 10, 14). I will not lean on book value (meaningless here) or a peer multiple as the anchor (that just borrows Mr. Market's opinion of Visa). I anchor on my normalized owner earnings of $17.5/share and reason the multiple up from first principles.

Deriving the multiple honestly. Three anchors:

  1. The company's own history. Mastercard's average annual P/E has run 26–48× over the past decade, clustering around 31–33× in normal years (2022: 31.8×, 2023: 31.3×, 2024: 32.7×, 2025: 31.4×), with a an independent research service median of ~32× (an independent research service, 2026-05-01). But paying its historical market multiple is not deriving value — it is accepting a decade of Mr. Market's enthusiasm, so I treat this as context, not an anchor.
  2. A quality-and-growth-justified multiple, reasoned from the discounting logic. A durable double-digit grower earning ~94% ROIC, capital-light so nearly all earnings are free, discounted at a normalized base r = 10%, mathematically supports a high multiple — but the r − g spread is treacherously thin for a fast grower, which is exactly the trap the manual warns against (manual ch. 10). I therefore cap growth conservatively below an independent research service's own 13% estimate — and haircut it further for the disintermediation risks the moat section named — and let the multiple fall out of the DCF rather than asserting it.
  3. Reconciliation: the DCF below, at conservative-to-moderate inputs and the base r = 10%, implies roughly 23–31× owner earnings — centered near 27× (26.6× at the base), which sits below the company's own historical ~31×. That the disciplined multiple lands under the market's habitual one gives me confidence the range is honest, not a rationalization of the quote.

The two-stage DCF and its sensitivity (rigor rule 4). Starting owner earnings $17.5/share; stage-one growth for 10 years, then a terminal growth g; discount rate r anchored to the normalized long-bond rate and carrying no beta premium — I explicitly reject an independent research service's beta of 1.10 as an input to r, per the manual's anti-beta doctrine (manual ch. 12). My own doctrine floors r near ~10%, so r = 10% is the base/central case (the bolded row below); r = 9% is carried only as an explicit upside sensitivity, not the anchor. Stage-one growth gets a modest haircut for the disintermediation risks — base ~9% (range 8–11%), terminal 3–4%. The grid shows how a single point swings the answer:

The spread — from ~$400 at the pessimistic corner to ~$800+ at the optimistic one — is the lesson, not a defect: a durable grower's value is acutely sensitive to r and g, so a single "target price" is an illusion (manual ch. 10, 13). I triangulate from the r = 10% base row, weighting conservative-to-moderate growth, and cross-check against the multiple lens:

  • Low ≈ $400 — r = 10%, conservative growth (9% stage-one / 3% terminal, $402, toward 8% growth); the pessimistic corner. ≈ 22.9× owner earnings.
  • Base ≈ $465 — the normalized-multiple anchor, ≈27× × $17.5 ≈ $465 (26.6× at the base), corroborated at r = 10% by moderate growth (9–11% stage-one / 3–4% terminal spans $443–$516) and by the r = 9% upside at conservative growth ($475 at 9%/3%). ≈ 26.6× owner earnings — below the company's own historical ~31×.
  • High ≈ $545 — the r = 9% upside sensitivity with 9–11% growth ($539 at 9%/4% toward $554 at 11%/3%); ≈ 31.1× owner earnings.

The three universal cross-checks — all on the same $17.5 owner-earnings figure (rigor rule 3):

  1. No-growth floor = owner earnings ÷ r = $17.5 ÷ 0.10 = $175/share. This is what Mastercard is worth if it never grows another dollar, capitalized at the base r = 10%. Against today's $537.70 price, it means roughly $363, or about 67% of the price, is a payment for growth (363 ÷ 537.70). That is the single most important number in the case: two-thirds of what you pay today is a bet that the moat delivers a decade-plus of double-digit compounding. For a fortress franchise that is a defensible bet — but it is a bet, and it is why quality alone cannot license any price.
  2. Implied multiple = base intrinsic value ÷ owner earnings = $465 ÷ $17.5 = ≈ 26.6×. Is ~27× defensible for a 94%-ROIC network duopoly growing ~9%? Yes — comfortably, and it sits below the ~31× the market habitually pays, which is where I want my disciplined multiple to land; if I had to shave growth to mid-single-digits, the defensible multiple falls toward the low-20s (the manual's discipline of interrogating the translation catches exactly this).
  3. Owner-earnings yield = owner earnings ÷ price = $17.5 ÷ $537.70 = 3.25%. Read as the first-year return on owning the whole business. Against a normalized long bond (~10% floor doctrine) — or even against today's live ~4.5% long Treasury — the static yield is thin. It clears the bar only because the coupon grows ~9% a year (manual ch. 10): a 3.25% starting yield compounding at 9% overtakes a 4.5% static bond coupon within about four years. The entire case rests on that growing coupon arriving — which loops straight back to the moat.

Sanity-check against an independent research service. My base ($465) sits well below an independent research service's 18-month target midpoint of $655 and far below its 2029-31 targets of $780–955 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) — as it should, because an independent research service capitalizes near the historical peak multiple and I have deliberately set the discount rate at the ~10% floor and capped growth. The manual is explicit that in a durable, high-ROIC franchise part of the safety lives in the quality itself, so a fair price plus a real moat can protect better than a wonderful price on a fair business — but quality justifies a fair price, never any price (manual ch. 5, 13). At $537.70 I am being asked to pay a full-to-rich price for a wonderful business, with the growing owner-earnings coupon as my only cushion and a ~15% premium — not a discount — on top of it.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational? Roughly rational, leaning rich. He is neither panicking nor euphoric about Mastercard specifically; he is pricing a great franchise at its habitual premium multiple. But remember what Mr. Market has offered before: this same business traded as low as $276.90 in 2022 and $416.50 in 2025 (an independent research service, 2026-05-01, price history) — 48% and 23% below today. A patient owner does not need Mastercard to become cheap on its fundamentals; he needs Mr. Market to have one of his periodic fear spells and offer the franchise back in the $400–$420 zone (roughly 15%+ below today, and into the low end of my range), at which point the quality and a real discount would both be working for him. Principle: insist on a cushion and use Mr. Market rather than obey him; when a wonderful business trades at or above fair value with no margin of safety, the disciplined move is to keep it on the list and wait for his next bad mood (manual ch. 2, 13).

Glossary — Terms & Formulas

  • Circle of competence — the set of businesses you can understand well enough to forecast; its boundary matters more than its size. MA: the payments toll model is inside; the new-rails/regulation frontier is at the edge.
  • Economic moat / network effect — a durable advantage protecting high returns; here a two-sided network where more users and more merchants each make the other side more valuable. Its signature is pricing power. MA: a global duopoly with Visa.
  • Pricing power — the ability to raise price without losing volume. MA: revenue compounded 14.5%/yr for a decade on rising assessments and services.
  • Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital — the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Stock-based compensation is the one non-cash charge you do NOT add back. MA: $14,968M + $1,143M − $489M − $0 ≈ $15,622M ≈ $17.5/share (on ~893M year-end shares).
  • Free cash flow (FCF) = operating cash flow − capital expenditures. MA: $17,648M − $489M = $17,159M (includes a $597M stock-comp add-back that owner earnings removes).
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex — capex to stand still vs. to expand; a judgment call. MA: capex is ~1.5% of revenue and treated as essentially all maintenance.
  • Stock-based compensation (SBC) — pay issued as shares; a real cost borne as dilution, already expensed in GAAP net income and therefore not added back to owner earnings. MA: $597M (10-K, 2026-02-11, consolidated statement of cash flows), left as a cost.
  • NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate). MA: $18,897M × (1 − 0.194) = $15,231M.
  • Invested capital = debt + equity − cash. MA: $19,000M + $7,737M − $10,566M = $16,171M.
  • Return on invested capital (ROIC) = NOPAT ÷ invested capital; the central quality gauge, compared to r. MA: 15,231 ÷ 16,171 = ~94%.
  • Return on equity (ROE) = net income ÷ equity; here distorted by buyback-shrunken equity. MA: 193% ("NMF" per an independent research service — an artifact, not a quality signal).
  • Interest coverage = operating income (EBIT) ÷ interest expense. MA: $18,897M ÷ $620M ≈ 30×.
  • Net debt = total debt − cash; net debt ÷ owner earnings = years to repay. MA: $8,434M ÷ $15,622M = 0.54 years.
  • Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. MA: $23,558M ÷ $22,762M = 1.03 (understated by matched settlement balances).
  • Discount rate (r) — the owner's opportunity cost, anchored to the normalized long-bond rate, floored near ~10%, with no beta premium; acts as "gravity" on value. MA: base r = 10% (with r = 9% carried as an upside sensitivity).
  • Intrinsic value = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ — the discounted value of all future owner earnings; the master equation, estimated as a range. MA: $400–$545, base $465.
  • Two-stage DCF / terminal value / r − g spread — explicit-period discounting plus a capitalized terminal value at growth g; acutely sensitive when r − g is small. MA: 10 years at 8–11% (base ~9%), terminal 3–4%.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value = owner earnings ÷ r — the floor beneath a DCF and the gauge of how much of a price is a bet on growth. MA: $17.5 ÷ 0.10 = $175; ~67% of today's price is growth.
  • Implied multiple = intrinsic value ÷ owner earnings. MA: $465 ÷ $17.5 = 26.6×.
  • Owner-earnings yield = owner earnings ÷ price, compared to the long bond; a growing coupon, not a fixed one. MA: $17.5 ÷ $537.70 = 3.25%, growing ~9%/yr.
  • P/E ratio = price ÷ earnings per share. MA: live ~31.6× on $17.01 EPS (equity_atlas.xlsx; an independent research service).
  • Margin of safety = (intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value. MA: (465 − 537.70) ÷ 465 = −15.6% — no cushion, a mid-teens premium.
  • One-dollar test — retain a dollar only if it produces ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r); otherwise return it. MA: returns ~97% of net income — correct for a capital-light franchise.
  • Economic vs. accounting goodwill — a franchise is worth far more than its tangible book, so book understates a great business. MA: tangible book is negative; irrelevant to value.
