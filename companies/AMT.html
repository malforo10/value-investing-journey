Valuing American Tower Corp. (AMT) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Co-taught by Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett
Method: "How to Value a Business — A Security-Analysis Manual"
Date: 2026-07-04 · Live price: $166.03 · This is a teaching case, not investment advice.

Welcome. Today we are going to do something the two of us disagree about just often enough to make it interesting: we are going to value a real-estate investment trust — American Tower Corp., a company that owns roughly 149,686 cell towers and 30 data centers around the world. Ben spent his life insisting you buy a dollar for fifty cents. I spent mine learning that a wonderful business at a fair price beats a fair business at a wonderful price. A tower REIT is a rare thing that tests both instincts at once: it has one of the finest real-asset moats we have ever studied, and it sits on a mountain of debt in a world of 5% interest rates. Hold both thoughts.

Before we touch a single number, one warning that governs everything that follows. If you value this company the way you value Coca-Cola — on earnings and the price-to-earnings ratio — you will get the wrong answer by a mile. The stock trades near 27 times reported earnings, which sounds expensive. It is not the right lens. We will spend a good part of today explaining why, and what the right lens is. That single lesson — value a REIT on AFFO, not EPS — is the one I want you to carry out the door.

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What American Tower actually does. Strip away the acronyms and this is a landlord. American Tower owns the vertical steel — the tower — and rents space on it to wireless carriers who bolt their antennas and radios onto it. As of 2025-12-31 the portfolio was 149,686 communications sites: 42,224 in the U.S. & Canada, 47,081 in Latin America, 32,524 in Europe, 27,857 in Africa & Asia-Pacific — plus 30 U.S. data-center facilities (the CoreSite business, acquired late 2021). Property operations were 97% of 2025 revenue (10-K, Item 1); the tower-leasing piece alone is about 82% of property revenue.

The economic engine — and why it is beautiful. Read this sentence from the 10-K slowly, because it is the whole moat in one line:

"our tenant leases … have initial non-cancellable terms of five to ten years with multiple renewal terms, with provisions that periodically increase the rent … typically annually, based on a fixed escalation percentage (averaging approximately 3% in the United States)…"

Three features do all the work:

  1. Escalators. Rent rises ~3% a year in the U.S. (an inflation index abroad) automatically, written into the contract. That is a built-in raise the landlord does not have to negotiate.
  2. Co-location. A tower built for one carrier can hold two or three. The steel, the land lease, the maintenance — those costs are already paid by tenant #1. Tenant #2's rent is almost pure profit; the incremental margin on the second and third tenant approaches 100%. This is the single most important economic fact about towers.
  3. Switching costs and irreplaceability. A carrier will not move its equipment off a tower — repositioning degrades network quality and is expensive — so leases renew. AMT's reported churn was ~2% of tenant billings in 2025 (10-K). And you cannot easily build a new competing tower next door: zoning, permitting, and community opposition make sites scarce. The result is a $54 billion backlog of non-cancellable tenant lease revenue already contracted (10-K, Item 1).

The CoreSite / data-center pivot. AMT is redirecting capital toward interconnection data centers — 30 U.S. facilities — betting that AI and cloud workloads make dense, network-rich real estate the next tower-like asset. An independent research service calls data centers "a primary growth driver … in the years ahead." Note it, but weigh it: it is still a small slice of a $10.6B revenue base, and it is a more competitive, more capital-hungry business than towers.

The international story. AMT has been pruning. It sold its India, Australia, and New Zealand businesses in 2024; Mexico fiber and Poland in 2023; South Africa fiber in 2025 (10-K, Item 1). Management is explicit: it is shifting capital "into developed markets, including the U.S. & Canada, Europe and the data centers markets." This is rational — emerging-market towers carried currency risk and one giant, troubled Indian tenant base — but it means the growth runway is narrower and more mature than the past decade's.

THE ONE QUESTION. For every business we ask a single question whose answer we must be able to defend. For AMT it is:

Will carriers keep paying escalating rent on irreplaceable towers for decades — and can American Tower carry ~$37 billion of debt through a 5% interest-rate world without ever being forced to sell an asset or cut the dividend at the bottom?

The first half is about the moat (easy to answer yes). The second half is about the balance sheet (the whole risk). Both must be yes.

CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE GATE. Can we forecast this business? The revenue is contractual, escalating, and diversified across tens of thousands of tenants and four continents; churn is 2%. That is about as forecastable a top line as exists in the public market — far inside the circle. The one part that strains the circle is the refinancing math: a REIT's fate depends on capital-market access we cannot control or fully predict. We keep it inside the circle, but we mark that dependency in red and demand a wider margin of safety for it. Gate: PASS.

PRINCIPLE. A moat you can see in the contract — non-cancellable leases with automatic escalators and near-100% incremental margins on co-location — is the kind of durable, forecastable economics a valuation can actually rest on. The circle of competence is not "do I like the story"; it is "can I forecast the cash." Here, largely, we can.

3. READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Now the crucial lesson. Let us look at what the three statements say, and then at why the first one lies to you if you're not careful.

What the income statement reports (FY2025, from the 10-K):

Item FY2025 ($M)
Total revenue 10,644.6
Operating income 4,845.8
Depreciation, amortization & accretion 2,041.6
Interest expense 1,359.4
Net income 2,628.5
Diluted EPS (GAAP) ~5.40

At $166.03 on $5.40 of GAAP EPS, the stock trades near 27–29× earnings. An independent research service's median historical P/E is shown as "NMF" — not meaningful — and it reports the stock on a P/FFO basis instead. That is your first clue that the market itself refuses to value this company on GAAP earnings.

WHY GAAP NET INCOME MISLEADS — THE HEART OF THE MATTER. Look at that depreciation line: $2,041.6 million, nearly as large as net income itself. GAAP forces American Tower to depreciate its towers on a schedule as if they were wasting assets — as if a steel tower on a leased, irreplaceable site is worth less each year, like a delivery truck. But a well-located tower does the opposite: it appreciates, because you keep adding tenants at ~100% incremental margin and raising rent 3% a year. The depreciation charge is non-cash and, economically, largely fictional. It crushes reported earnings while the real cash the business throws off keeps climbing. That is why GAAP EPS is the wrong denominator, and why a "27× P/E" is comparing a real price to a made-up number.

THE BRIDGE: NET INCOME → FFO → AFFO. The REIT industry solved this decades ago. We add the fictional real-estate depreciation back to get FFO (Funds From Operations), then subtract the real cash costs of keeping the assets running to get AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations). Here is American Tower's own reconciliation from the 10-K, which I want you to see in full because this is the earnings statement that matters:

Bridge (FY2025, $M) Amount
Net income 2,628.5
+ Real-estate depreciation, amortization & accretion 1,899.6
+ Losses on disposal / RE impairment 83.5
− Adjustments for unconsolidated affiliates & noncontrolling interests (450.7)
= Nareit FFO (common stockholders) 4,160.9
− Straight-line revenue (net of expense) (64.8)
+ Stock-based compensation 174.2
+ Deferred tax & other tax adjustments 150.0
+ Non-real-estate D&A 142.0
+ Amortization of financing costs 54.1
+ Other (expense)/income 576.2
− Maintenance capex (capital improvement $185.2 + corporate $10.4) (195.6)
+ Other NCI / affiliate adjustments 44.6
= AFFO (common stockholders) 5,041.6

Notice the two facts that make a tower REIT extraordinary. First, AFFO ($5,041.6M) is nearly double GAAP net income ($2,628.5M) — the depreciation add-back is that large. Second, maintenance capex is only $195.6M — under 2% of revenue. A tower, once built, barely needs money to keep standing. Compare that to an airline or a steel mill, where maintenance capex devours the cash flow. This is a capital-light franchise wearing a capital-intensive costume.

Per share: AFFO of $5,041.6M on ~466.3M shares = $10.81 of AFFO per share (this matches an independent research service's per-share FFO line of $10.81 for 2025). That is the number we value — not the $5.40 EPS.

Ratios as arithmetic:
- P/AFFO at $166.03 = 166.03 ÷ 10.81 = 15.4×. Suddenly the "expensive" stock looks reasonable — its own history runs 18–28× (an independent research service P/FFO), and it printed 18.8× in 2025.
- AFFO yield = 10.81 ÷ 166.03 = 6.51%. Hold that against the ~5% long bond — we will return to it.
- AFFO payout ratio = dividend $6.72 ÷ AFFO $10.81 = 62%. Comfortable coverage (an independent research service puts 2026's payout at 65% of its AFFO target). The dividend is not being paid out of thin air.

ARTICULATION CHECK. Do the statements tie together? Operating cash flow was $5,464M in 2025 (XBRL). AFFO of $5,041.6M sits just below OCF — sensible, since AFFO subtracts maintenance capex and some cash items OCF doesn't. Net income $2,628.5M is far below OCF because of the $2.0B non-cash depreciation. The three statements corroborate each other: the cash is real; the depreciation is the accounting fiction.

EARNINGS-QUALITY SCAN. The manual warns us: is EBITDA being passed off as free cash flow? Here AMT reports Adjusted EBITDA ($7,130M) and AFFO — and AFFO honestly subtracts maintenance capex, exactly as our owner-earnings definition demands. That is the right way to use these non-GAAP measures. One yellow flag to watch: "Other expense/income" of $576.2M was added back to reach AFFO; some of that is FX and mark-to-market noise, which is fair, but we keep an eye on how often "other" items recur. Straight-line revenue is properly removed — good discipline. Overall earnings quality is high and transparent.

PRINCIPLE. For a REIT, GAAP depreciation is an accounting fiction imposed on an appreciating asset. Add it back to get FFO; subtract real maintenance capex to get AFFO; and value the business on AFFO, which is simply owner earnings by another name. A "27× P/E" that becomes a "15× P/AFFO" is not financial engineering — it is finally measuring the right thing.

4. EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The trend (XBRL + an independent research service). Revenue per an independent research service: $5,785.7M (2016) → $9,356.9M (2021) → $10,644.6M (2025) — a steady climb, briefly dented by the India/asset sales (2024 revenue dipped to $10,127M as divested businesses left the books, then recovered). Operating cash flow marched almost straight up: $2,701.7M (2016) → $4,819.9M (2021) → $5,464.0M (2025) (XBRL). AFFO per share (an independent research service "FFO/sh"): $5.56 (2016) → $9.43 (2021) → $10.81 (2025), projected to $11.00 (2026e) and $11.40 (2027e). This is a business whose owner earnings compound like clockwork — the escalators and co-location doing exactly what the contracts promise.

Owner earnings = AFFO. The manual defines owner earnings as reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − Δworking capital. For a tower REIT, that computation is AFFO: we added back $2.0B of non-cash real-estate depreciation and subtracted only $195.6M of maintenance capex. Normalized owner earnings ≈ $5.0 billion, or $10.81 per share. The stream is unusually stable, so I do not normalize heavily — but I note 2024–25 carried some noise from asset sales, and I lean on the clean ~$10.81 AFFO/share as sustainable earning power.

The moat, argued not assumed. The manual's test for a moat is pricing power. AMT has it in a peculiarly durable form:
- Escalators = contractual pricing power. 3% annual raises, pre-agreed. No negotiation, no volume loss.
- Co-location = operating leverage no competitor can match. The economics of the second tenant (near-100% margin) mean AMT's returns rise as towers fill, and a new entrant starting from one tenant per tower simply cannot compete on cost.
- Switching costs + irreplaceable sites = a wall around the castle. 2% churn; carriers physically cannot leave cheaply; new sites are choked by zoning.

This is, genuinely, one of the best real-asset moats in the market. It qualifies as an "economic franchise" on all three of the manual's tests: the service is needed (wireless demand grows with data consumption), has no close substitute (you can't beam a network without antennas somewhere high), and is not price-regulated.

The moat's limits — argue the erosion too. (a) Tenant concentration: AT&T, Verizon, and T-Mobile together are ~49% of revenue (an independent research service). U.S. carrier consolidation — the Sprint/T-Mobile merger — permanently removed a tenant and drove a wave of churn as overlapping leases were cancelled; that is most of AMT's 2% churn today. (b) U.S. saturation: North American tower count is essentially flat (26,918 towers, "market saturation," an independent research service Q1 2026) — domestic growth now comes from adding tenants and escalators, not new towers. (c) The data-center bet is unproven and more competitive — Digital Realty and Equinix are formidable, and data centers are more capital-hungry than towers.

Returns on capital vs. cost of capital. Here we must be careful, because a REIT's balance sheet distorts the usual ratios.
- an independent research service reports Return on Shareholders' Equity of 81.5% for 2025. Do not take that at face value. It is flattered to the point of meaninglessness by two things: (1) the equity base is tiny (book value ~$3.65B against $63B of assets) because GAAP depreciation has written the asset base down, and (2) the enormous debt load. An 81% ROE on a sliver of depreciated book equity tells you almost nothing about business quality.
- The more honest read: AMT earns roughly $7.1B of Adjusted EBITDA on ~$63B of assets and generates ~$5B of AFFO. Return on the total capital employed (an independent research service's "Return on Total Cap'l") is a more sober ~8.4% for 2025. That is above but not dramatically above our ~8.5% cost of capital — which is the honest verdict: a wonderful business whose returns on invested capital, after you account for the price paid for CoreSite and the debt funding it all, are good but not the 25%-ROIC fireworks of a See's Candy. The moat is wide; the returns on the capital deployed are solid, not spectacular, and heavily levered.

PRINCIPLE. A contractual moat — escalators, co-location, switching costs — makes owner earnings unusually forecastable, which is precisely the input a valuation lives on. But do not let a REIT's 81% "ROE" fool you: leverage and depreciated book equity manufacture that number. Judge the economics on returns on total capital (~8%) against the cost of capital (~8.5%) — good, durable, but not spectacular, and earned with a great deal of borrowed money.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

This is the chapter that decides whether the wonderful business survives to keep being wonderful. For a leveraged REIT, it is the chapter.

The leverage, in the company's own numbers (from the 10-K and an independent research service, as of 3/31/26):
- Total debt ≈ $37,322M; long-term debt ≈ $31,203M; cash ≈ $1,608.8M.
- Net debt ≈ $35,713M.
- Adjusted EBITDA (FY2025) = $7,130M.

Net debt / EBITDA = 35,713 ÷ 7,130 = 5.0×. The REIT world targets the "5× area," and AMT sits right on it. This is high — a mediocre business at 5× leverage would frighten us — but it is carried against the most contractual, escalating, diversified cash stream in the equity market, which is exactly the case the manual makes for judging leverage "by the company's own stability." A utility-like stream can carry debt that would sink a cyclical. Still: 5× is the ceiling of prudent, not the middle.

(an independent research service's headline "Debt/OCF 7.1×" uses total debt over operating cash flow; net-debt/EBITDA at 5.0× is the cleaner solvency gauge and the one the credit-rating agencies use — but both say the same thing: this is a heavily levered balance sheet.)

Interest coverage.
- On operating income: EBIT $4,845.8M ÷ interest $1,359.4M = 3.6×.
- On Adjusted EBITDA: 7,130 ÷ 1,359.4 = 5.2×.

Coverage of 3.6× on EBIT is adequate — a normal year covers the interest bill several times — but it is not a fortress. A 300-basis-point jump in the average cost of debt (not just new debt) would take a real bite. That is the whole rate story.

THE MATURITY WALL — against a ~5% refinancing world. This is where I want every student leaning forward. Here is the actual debt-maturity schedule from the 10-K ($M):

Year Debt maturing
2026 3,387.8

Thereafter 13,099.9

American Tower must refinance roughly $4–7.5 billion every single year for the rest of the decade — and it is doing so into a market where the 30-year Treasury is ~5% and its own new notes price at 4.9%–5.35% (the 10-K shows 2025 issuance at exactly those coupons). Much of the maturing debt was struck in the zero-rate era at coupons like 1.3%, 1.375%, 2.4%, 2.95%. Every rollover swaps a ~2% coupon for a ~5% coupon. That is a slow, grinding headwind on AFFO that will play out over years, not a one-time shock. The good news: management has been extending maturities and its credit facilities now run to 2028–2030, so there is no single cliff. The risk: a REIT "depends on the continued kindness of lenders," in the manual's phrase, and if credit markets seize at the wrong moment, a company rolling $5B a year does not control its own fate. This is where the permanent-loss-of-capital risk sits — not in the towers, but in the refinancing calendar.

Liquidity. Cash ~$1.6B plus undrawn revolving credit facilities (multicurrency + 2021 credit facility, extended to 2028/2030) cover the near-term maturities. The current ratio is weak (current liabilities exceed current assets — normal for a REIT that funds long assets with rolling debt), which is by design but reinforces the dependence on market access.

Hidden obligations. Do not miss these. AMT leases the land under many towers, and the 10-K discloses $11,976.6M of operating-lease (ground-lease) obligations — a real, off-the-headline-balance-sheet claim on future cash. Add that to the $37.4B of debt and the total contractual burden is closer to $49B. There is no defined-benefit pension (a plus). Also note the Stonepeak mandatorily-convertible preferred equity — a form of leverage that sits above common.

ONE-LINE VERDICT: Adequate but leveraged. an independent research service rates Financial Strength B++ — investment-grade-ish, not a fortress. AMT will very likely survive; it is not going bust. But it carries no spare tire, and its earnings are exposed to the refinancing wall. We carry a wider margin-of-safety demand forward because of exactly this.

PRINCIPLE. A great business financed at 5× net-debt/EBITDA with a $5-billion-a-year refinancing wall in a 5% world is not fragile, but it is not free either. Leverage buys a little extra return in good times and threatens permanent loss in bad ones. When financial strength is merely "adequate," you do not pass — you widen the margin of safety and let the price pay you for the risk.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

The manual gives six uses of a dollar of free cash. Grade management across all six.

  1. Reinvest in the existing business. AMT spends ~$3.60/share of capital (an independent research service) building and upgrading towers and, increasingly, data centers. Where it funds high-tenancy towers, incremental ROIC is excellent (co-location). The data-center pivot is the open question — those returns are unproven.
  2. Acquire. The record is mixed. The 2021 CoreSite acquisition (~$10B+) is the big bet — bought at a full price at the top of the data-center cycle; the jury is out on whether it clears the cost of capital. Historically, tower M&A (Telxius, Viom) built the franchise.
  3. Pay down debt. Management has been repaying maturing notes and extending the ladder rather than levering up further — net leverage has drifted from ~6.5×+ toward ~5×. That is the right direction.
  4. Pay dividends. This is AMT's signature. It is a serial dividend raiser: dividends/share went from $2.17 (2016) to $6.72 (2025), a ~23.5% annualized 10-year growth rate (an independent research service) — and the 2026 payout is set higher again ($1.79/quarter). Crucially, test it on AFFO, not EPS: payout is 62% of 2025 AFFO (65% of the 2026e AFFO target). On GAAP EPS of $5.40 the $6.72 dividend looks unsustainable (>100% payout!) — which is exactly the trap of using the wrong metric. On AFFO it is well-covered. This is the FFO/AFFO lesson made concrete: the wrong lens would tell you the dividend is being paid out of borrowed money; the right lens shows a comfortable 62% payout.
  5. Repurchase shares. Minimal and opportunistic — $364.6M in 2025, essentially nil in 2021–2024 (XBRL). REITs must distribute 90% of taxable income, so buybacks are naturally a small tool here.
  6. Issue shares — watch dilution. This is the REIT-specific caution. Because REITs pay out most of their cash, they fund growth by issuing equity and debt. Share count crept from 427M (2016) to 466M (2025) — modest (~0.9%/year) dilution, and it funded real asset growth, so per-share AFFO still rose strongly. Acceptable, but the manual is right to make us watch it: intrinsic value is per-share, and a REIT that over-issues gives away the owners' claim.

The one-dollar test on the two big bets. On towers, retained/reinvested dollars clearly create more than a dollar of value — the co-location economics guarantee it. On CoreSite/data centers, the verdict is genuinely uncertain: bought at a rich multiple, funded partly with debt now refinancing at 5%, competing with Equinix and Digital Realty. Management is candid that the international pruning (India, Australia, South Africa fiber) was a rational reallocation away from sub-hurdle assets toward developed markets — that is exactly the discipline the one-dollar test demands, and I credit them for it.

Candor. The 10-K disclosure is thorough and legible: it reconciles every non-GAAP number to GAAP, breaks out discontinued operations honestly, and states the risk factors plainly. No EBITDA-as-cash-flow sleight of hand. This is a management that reports its arithmetic.

PRINCIPLE. Judge a REIT's management on the AFFO payout ratio, not the EPS payout — a 62% AFFO payout is prudent where a ">100% EPS payout" would look reckless, and only the right metric reveals which is true. And watch the share count: a REIT grows by issuing paper, so per-share discipline is the test of whether growth is enriching owners or diluting them. AMT largely passes.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Now the arithmetic. Our owner earnings are AFFO = $10.81 per share (FY2025; ~$11.00 forward for 2026e). The manual's master equation says value is the discounted stream of that owner earnings. We will estimate it three ways, cross-check three ways, and triangulate a range.

The discount rate is gravity — and for a REIT the gravity is heavy. Start from the ~5% long bond (30-year Treasury ≈ 4.9–5.0%, per an independent research service S&O). Add a premium for a leveraged, but highly contractual, cash flow. For AMT I set a base r = 8.5%, and I stress it hard from 7.5% to 9.5%, because for a bond-proxy REIT carrying $37B of debt, the discount rate is not one input among many — it is the swing factor. Rising rates hurt AMT twice: they lift r (crushing the present value of its cash) and they raise its refinancing cost (shrinking the cash itself). That double-hit is why we lean conservative.

Method (a) — P/AFFO, derived and reconciled

We anchor the multiple three ways:
- History: AMT's own P/FFO ran 18–28× over the last decade, 18.8× in 2025 (an independent research service). But those were mostly zero-rate years. In a 5% world the multiple deserves to compress.
- Peers: Tower REITs SBAC and CCI trade in a broadly similar 15–20× AFFO band today, also compressed by rates.
- Quality-justified: A wide moat with 3% built-in growth and 62% payout deserves a premium to the average REIT, but the leverage and the refinancing wall argue against stretching it.

I therefore assign a base multiple of 17× AFFO — below AMT's history (rate regime), above a no-moat REIT (quality). On $10.81: 17 × 10.81 = $183.8. Range: 16× = $173 (low), 18–19× = $195–205 (high). On forward 2026e AFFO of $11.00, 17× = $187.

Method (b) — Dividend-discount (Gordon) cross-check

AMT is a dividend machine, so the Gordon model is apt: Value = D₁ / (r − g), with D₀ = $6.72.

r ↓ / g → 3% 4% 5%
8.0% $138 $175 $235
8.5% $126 $155 $202
9.0% $115 $140 $176

At our base r = 8.5% and a defensible g = 4% (below the ~5% dividend-growth an independent research service projects, itself far below the historical 23% — dividend growth is normalizing as the business matures), the DDM says ≈ $155. This is the conservative estimator — it values only the cash actually distributed, ignoring the ~38% of AFFO retained and reinvested — so I treat ~$155 as the low-to-base anchor.

Method (c) — AFFO yield vs. the long bond

At $166.03, AMT yields 6.51% on AFFO, and that coupon grows ~3–4% a year via escalators. Against a static ~5% long-bond coupon, a growing 6.5% claim is clearly the superior instrument — the yield gap says the stock is not expensive relative to bonds. Flip it: for AMT's AFFO yield to merely match the 5% bond, the price would be $10.81 ÷ 0.05 = $216. So on a pure yield-vs-bond basis, fair value sits well above today's price — the growth is a free extra.

Two-stage AFFO DCF (the generous estimator)

Growing AFFO 5% for 5 years, 4% for the next 5, terminal g, discounted:

r ↓ / terminal g → 2.5% 3.0% 3.5%
7.5% $261 $280 $305
8.5% $216 $229 $243
9.5% $185 $193 $202

At base r = 8.5%, g = 3%: ≈ $229. This is the high estimator — it assumes the moat fully delivers a decade of ~4–5% AFFO growth, which the escalators support but the mature U.S. market and refinancing drag argue against taking at face value. I use it as the top of the range, discounted for optimism.

THREE UNIVERSAL CROSS-CHECKS (arithmetic)

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

The arithmetic. Live price is $166.03. Against our range:

  • vs. base $185: margin of safety = (185 − 166.03) ÷ 185 = +10.3%. Modest.
  • vs. low $150: the price is above our conservative floor by ~11% — no margin of safety at the low end.
  • vs. high $225: discount of ~26% — meaningful if the optimistic case holds.
  • vs. no-growth floor $127: the price is ~31% above the zero-growth value — so you are paying a reasonable, not reckless, premium for growth.

Where the safety sits — and where it doesn't. Part of AMT's safety lives in quality, exactly as the manual describes for a durable franchise: the escalators make today's AFFO the floor of tomorrow's, and the no-growth floor ($127) covers ~77% of the price. That is real protection. But the purchase-price discount is thin — ~10% to base, and negative to our conservative low. The manual is emphatic: a small margin of safety offers no protection. At $166 you are paying a fair price for a wonderful business — Buffett's own doctrine says that can be acceptable for a business this good — but you are not getting Graham's fifty-cent dollar, and the rate risk is precisely the kind of error the margin is supposed to cushion.

Mr. Market's mood on REITs. Why is a franchise this good available at only ~15× AFFO and a 4.36% yield? Because Mr. Market is in a foul, rate-obsessed mood about REITs. An independent research service ranks the REIT industry 87th of 94 for year-ahead performance; the whole sector is out of favor as a bond-proxy in a 5% world. That is the opportunity — Mr. Market is selling the sector for its rate sensitivity, not for any deterioration in AMT's towers. The business is intact; the quote is depressed by macro sentiment. That is exactly the gap the manual tells us to exploit — if the price falls far enough.

PRINCIPLE. A ~10% discount to a fair-value estimate that itself swings ±30% with interest rates is not a margin of safety — it is a coin flip dressed up as one. Mr. Market's rate-driven gloom on REITs is the source of opportunity, but the discipline is to demand that the gloom push the price to a real discount (toward the $140s–150s) before acting. At $166, you admire the business and wait for a wider margin — or you buy small, eyes open to the rate risk, and let a lower price bring you size.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Quality tier. American Tower is a high-quality, wide-moat business carried on an adequate-but-leveraged balance sheet. The moat — non-cancellable escalating leases, near-100%-margin co-location, irreplaceable sites, 2% churn, a $54B contracted backlog — is among the best real-asset franchises in the market. What keeps it from the very top tier is the leverage (5× net-debt/EBITDA, a $5B/year refinancing wall in a 5% world) and returns on total capital (~8%) that are solid rather than spectacular once you strip away the flattering 81% ROE.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION. Will carriers keep paying escalating rent on irreplaceable towers for decades, and can AMT carry ~$37B of debt through a 5% world without becoming a forced seller? On the first half — yes, with high confidence; the contracts and physics guarantee it. On the second half — probably yes, but this is where the real risk lives, and it depends on capital-market access AMT does not control. The honest answer is "yes, but the margin of safety must pay us for the refinancing risk."

Verdict: HOLD / accumulate on weakness — a wonderful business at a merely fair price. At $166.03 the stock is roughly fairly valued to modestly cheap against our $185 base, with a thin ~10% margin to base and none to our conservative low. That is not the fat, obvious discount the method demands for a decisive buy. It is a business worth owning, and the rate-driven sector gloom could hand you a better price. Discipline: I would want the low $150s or below — a 15–20% discount to base, which would restore a genuine Graham margin — before buying in size. Above ~$225 you would be paying for the optimistic case and should trim.

Open questions to monitor (the thesis-break watchlist):
- Long rates. The single most important variable. Track the 30-year Treasury and AMT's average cost of debt as the low-coupon notes reprice.
- CoreSite/data centers. Do the returns clear the cost of capital, or was it a top-of-cycle overpay?
- U.S. carrier behavior. Further consolidation or a churn spike from the big three (49% of revenue) would matter.
- AFFO-per-share growth holding in the 3–5% range as international pruning continues.

Universal lessons from this case:
1. Value a REIT on AFFO, not EPS. A 27× "P/E" became a 15× P/AFFO the moment we added back the fictional depreciation. The GAAP earnings of a REIT are an accounting artifact; the AFFO is the owner earnings. Get the denominator right before you argue about the multiple.
2. Leverage and rates are the REIT's real risk — not the towers. The permanent-loss danger sits in the refinancing calendar, not the income statement.
3. The discount rate is the master variable for a bond-proxy. When a business's value swings ±30% with long rates, "beta 0.85" wildly understates the real risk, and the margin of safety must be sized for the rate error specifically.
4. A wonderful business at a fair price is not a decisive buy. Quality earns a fair price; it does not repeal the margin of safety. Wait for Mr. Market's gloom to widen the discount.

11. GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

Ordered as they appeared. Each with its formula and AMT's numbers.

REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust). A company that owns income-producing real estate and, to avoid corporate tax, must distribute ≥90% of taxable income to shareholders. AMT: elected REIT status 2012; distributes via a serial-growth dividend.

Escalator. A contractual annual rent increase built into a lease. AMT: ~3% fixed in the U.S., inflation-indexed internationally — contractual pricing power requiring no negotiation.

Co-location. Multiple tenants on one tower. Because the tower's fixed costs are already covered by tenant #1, the incremental margin on tenants #2 and #3 approaches ~100%. AMT: the core driver of tower economics and its cost-advantage moat.

Churn. Tenant billings lost to non-renewal or cancellation ÷ prior-year billings. AMT: ~2% in 2025 (mostly U.S. carrier consolidation).

FFO (Funds From Operations). Nareit-defined REIT earnings measure: net income + real-estate depreciation/amortization/accretion − gains on property sales (± NCI/affiliate adjustments). Corrects for the fiction of depreciating appreciating real estate.

FFO = Net income + RE depreciation & amortization − RE sale gains + adjustments
AMT FY2025: $2,628.5M + $1,899.6M + $83.5M − $450.7M = $4,160.9M.

AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations). FFO adjusted for straight-line rent, stock comp, deferred taxes, non-RE D&A, financing amortization, and other items, less maintenance (recurring) capex. The REIT equivalent of owner earnings.

AFFO = FFO ± non-cash/normalizing items − maintenance capex
AMT FY2025: $5,041.6M, or $10.81 per share on ~466.3M shares. Maintenance capex was only $195.6M.

Owner earnings. Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − Δworking capital; the cash an owner can withdraw. For a REIT, owner earnings = AFFO. AMT: ~$10.81/share.

P/AFFO. Price ÷ AFFO per share — the correct valuation multiple for a REIT (the P/E is meaningless because GAAP EPS is depressed by non-cash depreciation).

P/AFFO = Price ÷ AFFO per share
AMT at $166.03: 166.03 ÷ 10.81 = 15.4× (history 18–28×; our base fair multiple 17×).

AFFO yield. AFFO per share ÷ price — the REIT's "coupon," to be compared to the long bond.

AFFO yield = AFFO per share ÷ Price
AMT: 10.81 ÷ 166.03 = 6.51%, growing ~3–4%/yr, vs. ~5% static long bond.

AFFO payout ratio. Dividend ÷ AFFO — the test of dividend sustainability (never use the EPS payout for a REIT).

Payout = Dividend per share ÷ AFFO per share
AMT: 6.72 ÷ 10.81 = 62% (65% on 2026e AFFO). On GAAP EPS it would falsely read >100%.

Adjusted EBITDA. Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, accretion (and stock comp), adjusted — a REIT's operating-cash-flow proxy and the denominator of the leverage ratio. AMT FY2025: $7,130.0M.

Net debt / EBITDA. The core REIT leverage gauge; ~5× is the prudent target.

Net debt / EBITDA = (Total debt − cash) ÷ Adjusted EBITDA
AMT: (37,322 − 1,608.8) ÷ 7,130 = 5.0×.

Interest coverage. How many times operating earnings cover the interest bill.

Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest expense
AMT: 4,845.8 ÷ 1,359.4 = 3.6× (5.2× on Adjusted EBITDA).

Maturity wall. The schedule of debt coming due that must be refinanced. AMT: ~$3.4B (2026), $4.7B (2027), $7.5B (2028), $3.8B (2029), $4.9B (2030) — ~$5B/year rolling from ~2% coupons to ~5% coupons.

NAV (Net Asset Value). The market value of a REIT's assets minus its liabilities — the private-market worth of the real estate, a secondary check on the AFFO-based value. AMT: book equity ~$3.65B understates NAV badly (GAAP-depreciated towers); true NAV of 149,686 sites + 30 data centers far exceeds book — a reason book value is a floor, not a fair-value measure, for this REIT.

Dividend-discount model (Gordon growth). Values a stock as its next dividend divided by (discount rate − perpetual growth).

Value = D₁ / (r − g), where D₁ = D₀ × (1 + g)
AMT base: D₀ = $6.72, r = 8.5%, g = 4% → 6.72 × 1.04 ÷ (0.085 − 0.04) = ≈ $155 (the conservative estimator).

No-growth (earning-power) floor. AFFO ÷ r — value if the business never grows again; the conservative floor and the gauge of how much of the price is a growth bet.

Floor = AFFO per share ÷ r
AMT: 10.81 ÷ 0.085 = $127.20 (77% of the $166 price is covered without any growth).

Discount rate (r). Owner's opportunity cost = long bond + risk premium; for a leveraged tower REIT, ~8.5% base (stressed 7.5–9.5%). The master variable here — the value swings ±30% across that band.

Margin of safety. (Intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value; the discount that protects against error.

MoS = (IV − Price) ÷ IV
AMT: (185 − 166.03) ÷ 185 = +10.3% to base; negative to the $150 conservative low.

Mr. Market. Graham's manic-depressive partner whose daily quote reflects mood, not value. AMT: currently gloomy on all REITs (industry ranked 87/94) for rate reasons — the source of the opportunity, if the price falls far enough.
