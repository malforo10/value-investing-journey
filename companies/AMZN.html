Valuing Amazon.com (AMZN) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Co-taught by Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett
Session date: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $242.67 (Jul 4, 2026, 11:45)

"Price is what you pay; value is what you get." We will spend this session refusing to confuse the two. Amazon is the hardest kind of case we teach — a magnificent business whose reported earnings have, for most of its life, badly understated its cash economics, and whose value hides inside three very different businesses stapled together. We will pull them apart.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Amazon is not one business. It is a retail-and-logistics empire, an advertising machine, and the world's largest cloud-computing utility, consolidated under one ticker. FY2025 total net sales were $716.9 billion (10-K, Statements of Operations). The honest way to see it is by the disaggregated revenue table (Note 10 area):

Revenue stream (FY2025) $B Share
Online stores (1P retail) 269.3 38%

AWS (cloud) 128.7 18%
Advertising services 68.6 10%
Subscription services (Prime, etc.) 49.6 7%
Physical stores (Whole Foods et al.) 22.6 3%
Other 5.9 1%

And the way management actually runs it — three reportable segments (Note 10):

Segment (FY2025) Net sales $B Operating income $B Op. margin
North America 426.3 29.6 6.9%
International 161.9 4.8 2.9%
AWS 128.7 45.6 35.4%
Consolidated 716.9 80.0 11.2%

Read that last column twice. AWS is 18% of sales but 57% of operating income. Advertising ($68.6B, growing 22% in 2025) sits inside the North America and International segments and is even higher-margin than AWS — it is the quiet second engine. The retail businesses are enormous, low-margin, capital-hungry flywheels; AWS and advertising are the profit.

Customers: hundreds of millions of retail consumers and third-party sellers; on the AWS side, "start-ups, enterprises, government agencies, and academic institutions" (10-K Item 1). Prime is the lock-in ring binding the retail flywheel — fast shipping, video, music — funded as a marketing tool "indefinitely" (MD&A).

The one deciding question: Will AWS's high-return cloud franchise keep compounding faster than the retail business consumes capital — and is management's colossal reinvestment (2025 capex ~$132B, guided higher in 2026) earning a return above its cost, or lighting money on fire in the AI arms race?

Circle-of-competence gate. (Manual Ch. 4 — "confine analysis to businesses whose long-term economics you can understand well enough to forecast within a sensible range.") Amazon fails the strictest form of this test and passes a looser one. The retail flywheel and AWS are understandable and their economics are directionally forecastable a decade out — people will buy more online and rent more compute. But bolted on are ventures we cannot forecast: Project Kuiper/Leo satellites (competing with Starlink), autonomous ride-hailing (Zoox), MGM/entertainment, custom silicon (Trainium/Gravitron), and a multi-billion Anthropic stake. Buffett's honest answer to those is "too hard." Verdict at the gate: the business is inside the circle at the level of AWS-plus-retail-plus-ads, and outside it at the level of the moonshots. We proceed — but we will not pay for the moonshots, and we will demand a margin of safety precisely because part of this company is unforecastable.

General principle: Knowing the boundary of your circle matters more than its size. When a great business straddles the boundary, value the part you understand and treat the rest as a free option — never as a paid-for certainty.

Here is the whole lesson in three numbers. Net income was $77.7B; operating cash flow was $139.5B (cash economics run far ahead of GAAP profit because D&A of $65.8B and SBC of $19.5B are non-cash charges); yet free cash flow collapsed from $38.2B to $11.2B because gross capex nearly doubled to $131.8B. Management (MD&A) tells you why: capex "primarily reflect[s] investments in technology infrastructure (the majority of which is to support AWS business growth)" and fulfillment capacity, "both of which we expect to increase in 2026." an independent research service pegs 2026 capex near $200 billion.

How the three statements articulate: the income statement's modest $77.7B profit understates the $139.5B of operating cash the business threw off (non-cash D&A + SBC); that cash, plus $25B of new debt, was almost entirely redeployed into the $132B of P&E now sitting on the balance sheet as the +$104B jump in net P&E. Cash in → assets on the balance sheet → future depreciation back through the income statement. The circle is Amazon's entire strategy: convert cheap capital into infrastructure faster than competitors can.

Earnings-quality scan

  • Stock-based compensation ($19.5B) is a real, recurring expense. It is added back in OCF but it is not free — it dilutes owners (diluted shares 10,492M → 10,827M over 2023–2025, ~1.6%/yr creep). We treat SBC as a genuine cost and do not credit it back to owner earnings.
  • Capex classification is the central judgment. GAAP lumps all $131.8B of P&E purchases together. The manual forces us to split maintenance capex (stand-still) from growth capex (expansion). With D&A at $65.8B and the business explicitly building new AI/AWS capacity and satellite/robotics capacity, the great majority of the excess over D&A is growth. We estimate maintenance capex ≈ D&A ≈ $65–70B, with the remaining ~$65B being discretionary growth investment.
  • The $15.2B "Other income" is non-operating and largely non-cash — excluded from earnings power.
  • Server useful lives are 5–6 years (Note 1). Amazon (like peers) has periodically extended these lives, which lowers depreciation and flatters AWS margin; watch this — it is a legitimate but earnings-elastic estimate.
  • Disclosure is clean. E&Y unqualified opinion; the only Critical Audit Matter is uncertain tax positions ($6.6B accrual) — routine for a firm this global. No EBITDA-as-cash-flow games; no serial "restructuring." Earnings-quality grade: high, provided you make the two adjustments above.

General principle: For a heavy reinvestor, net income is a rumor and free cash flow is a moving target. The truth is operating cash flow minus the capex genuinely needed to stand still. Everything above that line is a choice management is making about the future — judge the choice, don't mistake it for a shortfall.

The story the history tells: revenue compounded ~20%/yr for a decade (an independent research service: 20.0% past-10-yr). But profit is violently cyclical — a $(2.7)B loss in 2022 (over-building post-COVID) exploding to $77.7B in 2025. This is not a smooth annuity; it is a business whose reported earnings swing with its capex cycle. Earnings Predictability is only 45 (an independent research service) — low. That volatility is exactly why the manual insists on normalized earnings and a margin of safety.

Owner earnings (Manual Ch. 7: Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital)

Building it for FY2025:
- Start from core operating profit after tax (strip the $15.2B non-operating mark): NOPAT = $80.0B × (1 − 0.18) = $65.6B.
- Add non-cash D&A: +$65.8B.
- Subtract maintenance capex (our judgment ≈ D&A): −$66B.
- Working capital is a source of cash for Amazon (it collects from customers before paying suppliers — negative working-capital cycle), so ΔWC is roughly neutral-to-positive; we take $0 conservatively.
- Normalized owner earnings ≈ $65–66B, or ~$6.05 per diluted share.

Note what this does: it ignores the ~$65B of growth capex entirely, because growth capex is optional. Amazon's "true" stand-still earning power today is ~$65B. The reported FCF of $11.2B is low not because the business is weak but because management chose to spend $65B building the future. That is the profit-vs-cash gap the manual warns you to teach, run in reverse.

The moat, segment by segment

  • AWS — wide and deep. Sources: (1) switching costs — enterprises architect their entire stack on AWS APIs; migrating is painful and risky; (2) scale economies — the largest cloud buys compute, power, and now designs its own silicon (Trainium/Gravitron) cheaper than anyone; (3) a 20% revenue growth rate at a 35% operating margin is the signature of pricing power and a real franchise. Peers: Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud — a rational oligopoly, not a price war. Erosion risk over 10 years: AI could reshuffle the cloud hierarchy; if the next platform layer (foundation models) is where value accrues, AWS's infrastructure moat could commoditize beneath it. This is the single biggest long-term question mark.
  • Retail + marketplace + Prime — wide but low-return. Sources: network effects (more buyers → more sellers → more selection → more buyers), scale in fulfillment/logistics no one can replicate, and Prime lock-in (subscription switching cost). Peers: Walmart (scaling e-commerce and its own ad/marketplace business), plus Costco, Shopify-enabled merchants, Temu/Shein at the low end. Erosion risk: Walmart is a genuine, well-capitalized threat; regulatory/antitrust pressure on marketplace practices; thin margins leave little cushion.
  • Advertising — narrowing the gap to a franchise. $68.6B and growing 22%; it monetizes proprietary purchase-intent data (a structural advantage Google/Meta lack in the same form). High incremental margin. This is the most underappreciated moat asset.

ROIC vs cost of capital (Manual Ch. 6: ROIC = NOPAT / Invested capital; value is created only when ROIC > r)

  • Consolidated (crude): NOPAT $65.6B ÷ invested capital (equity $411B + financial debt $66B − cash-and-sec $123B ≈ $354B) ≈ ~18.5%. Comfortably above any plausible r (~9–11%). Growth creates value in aggregate.
  • Per segment — the honest view:
  • AWS: ~$45.6B operating income → ~$37.4B NOPAT on ~$183B of P&E-plus-working-capital invested → ROIC well into the mid-20s%. A compounding machine. (AWS P&E alone reached ~$183B by 2025 per Note 10.)
  • Retail: ~$34.4B combined operating income → ~$28B NOPAT on ~$150B+ of P&E → mid-to-high teens ROIC, still above cost of capital but thin and cyclical.
  • ROE: reported ROE 16.5–18.9% (atlas / an independent research service). Caveat: flattered in 2025 by the $28B unrealized-gains swing running through equity and the non-operating mark; the operating return on tangible capital is the number that matters, and it is strong.

General principle: A conglomerate's blended ROIC hides the truth. Amazon is a 25%+ ROIC cloud franchise carrying a mid-teens retail business on its back. Value the parts, because the parts deserve different multiples — and because the high-return part is what justifies the whole reinvestment thesis.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Founder Jeff Bezos is now Executive Chairman (owns 8.8% of the stock — deeply aligned); Andy Jassy, who built AWS, is CEO. Judge them by the manual's capital-allocation framework.

  • Reinvestment record — the core of the case. For twenty-plus years Amazon has retained essentially all earnings and plowed them back. The one-dollar test (retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of market value) has been passed spectacularly by history: a company with ~$19B of equity in 2016 became a ~$2.6T enterprise. AWS itself is the greatest single capital-allocation decision of the century — reinvesting retail cash flow into a cloud business that now earns 35% margins. This is textbook value-creating growth (ROIC ≫ r).
  • The open question is at the margin, today. The 2026 capex of ~$200B is being spent into an AI build-out the macro commentators themselves call "returns largely unproven" (an independent research service View). The one-dollar test is prospective, not just historical. Some of this spend (AWS AI capacity, advertising) is high-confidence; some (Kuiper/Leo satellites, Zoox robotaxis) is venture-grade. We give management the benefit of the doubt on their record, but we do not pre-pay for the moonshots.
  • Dividends / buybacks: none. No dividend ever; negligible net buybacks (in fact ~1.6%/yr dilution from SBC). This is correct while incremental ROIC exceeds r — the manual says distribute only when reinvestment earns less than shareholders could elsewhere. Amazon is not there yet.
  • Acquisitions: disciplined and rare — Zappos ('09), Whole Foods ('17, ~$13.7B), MGM. Not an empire-builder by acquisition; it builds. The largest recent "investment" is the convertible stake in Anthropic (+$2.7B in 2025) — a bet on AI it also monetizes through AWS.
  • Candor: Bezos's letters and 10-K disclosure are famously clear; the 2022 loss was reported plainly. Grade: high.

General principle: A manager who has passed the one-dollar test for two decades has earned trust — but trust is not a blank check. The record justifies believing the AWS and advertising reinvestment; it does not justify paying today for satellites and robotaxis whose returns are unknown.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

We hold the master equation IV = Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ as the definition of value, and choose the estimator Amazon deserves. Because this is a segment story with wildly different economics, we lead with a sum-of-the-parts (SOTP) and cross-check with a consolidated owner-earnings/FCF multiple and a two-stage DCF.

Method 1 — Sum-of-the-parts (the honest estimator)

We value AWS on EV/EBIT (its operating income is real and its multiple should track cloud peers), and Retail on EV/EBIT with an EV/sales cross-check (retail peers).

Where the multiples come from:
- AWS multiple (EV/EBIT of segment operating income): 20× (low) / 26× (base) / 30× (high). Basis: AWS grows ~20% at a 35% margin — economics that rhyme with Microsoft and Google's cloud franchises, which trade at premium multiples. A 26× base on operating income (≈ ~32× after-tax, i.e. a ~3% after-tax EBIT yield) is defensible for a mid-20s-ROIC franchise growing 15–20%; we deliberately keep it below the richest cloud comps to leave a cushion.
- Retail multiple (EV/EBIT of NA+Intl operating income): 16× / 20× / 24×. Cross-checked against EV/sales of 0.7×–1.3× on $588B of retail sales. Basis: Walmart and large-scale retail-plus-marketplace-plus-ads businesses (industry report peer set) support these ranges; the advertising income embedded here (high-margin) justifies the upper end.
- Net cash: $123.0B (cash + marketable securities) − $68.4B (financial debt incl. current portion) = +$54.6B, added to enterprise value. (Operating lease liabilities are treated as operating, matched by the leased assets in P&E — not double-counted as debt.)
- Shares: 10,827M diluted (FY2025).

Scenario AWS EV Retail EV + Net cash Equity value Per share

SOTP puts intrinsic value at ~$140–$208, base ~$178. Note that AWS alone is worth roughly $0.9–1.4 trillion — over half the company — which is why anyone valuing Amazon on a consolidated P/E is flying blind.

Method 2 — Consolidated normalized owner-earnings / two-stage DCF (cross-check)

(Manual Ch. 9: two-stage IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ)

Discount rate r. (Manual Ch. 11 — anchor to long rates plus a premium.) 10-year Treasury ~4.5–4.7%; Beta 1.20. We use r = 10% base (≈ 4.6% risk-free + ~5.4% equity/uncertainty premium, appropriate given low earnings predictability), with 9% (high value) and 11% (low value) sensitivity.

Starting owner earnings. Normalized ~$65B today (derived above). Because Amazon is still in heavy build mode, we start the explicit period near this level and let growth capture the payoff. Base OE₀ = $60B.

Growth. an independent research service projects ~17% EPS growth to 2029–31. We use g₁ = 14% (base) for 10 years, tapering to terminal g = 4% (base) — a franchise that can grow with GDP-plus given AWS/ads. This growth is value-creating because segment ROIC (18–25%+) exceeds r (10%) — the manual's decisive test (Ch. 10).

OE₀ r g₁ (10 yr) g_term IV ($/sh)
$50B 11% 12% 3% ~$119 (low)
$60B 10% 14% 4% ~$210 (base)
$70B 9% 15% 4% ~$323 (high)

DCF sensitivity is enormous — $119 to $323 — which is the point, not a defect. The literal DCF is hypersensitive; that is why we lead with SOTP and treat the DCF as a corroborating range.

Distinguishing value-creating from capital-consuming growth

This is Amazon's entire thesis. Each dollar of retained earnings reinvested into AWS at ~25% ROIC or advertising creates far more than a dollar of value. Each dollar poured into satellites/robotaxis at unknown ROIC may create less than a dollar. Our base case implicitly assumes the majority of reinvestment continues to hit the AWS/ads/retail-logistics targets. If AI capex proves to be a low-return arms race (the macro warning), the low case governs.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

(Manual Ch. 12: Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) / Intrinsic Value; buy only far below the conservative end.)

  • Live price: $242.67. Market cap ~$2.63 trillion.
  • Against base IV ~$200: price is ~21% above base value → negative margin of safety.
  • Against high IV ~$270: price sits ~10% below → only a slim cushion, and only if you accept the optimistic case.
  • Against low IV ~$150: price is ~62% above → no protection at all.

Margin-of-safety buy price. Demanding a ~25–30% discount to our base intrinsic value implies a buy zone of roughly $140–$155 — near our SOTP low case and well below today's tape. Even a lenient 15% discount to base implies ~$170.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational? Mr. Market is mildly euphoric on Amazon. The stock rebounded 20% off its March low on "enthusiasm for technology equities" (an independent research service), and the broad tape is up ~24% over 12 months on the AI capex boom that the an independent research service View itself flags as having "largely unproven" returns. The market is paying today, in full, for an AI/AWS payoff that is probable but not yet in the cash flows — and throwing in the moonshots for free-turned-priced.

The patient student's move: Wait. This is a wonderful business at a price that already discounts most of the good news. The manual's discipline is explicit — "If we calculate the value of a common stock to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested." Here it is not even higher; base value is below price. Do nothing, keep it on the watch list, and let volatility do its work. Amazon has offered 30%+ drawdowns three times in the last five years (2022 especially). A retreat toward $150–$170 — entirely plausible if the AI capex cycle disappoints or rates back up — would open a real margin of safety in a genuinely great business.

General principle: The quality of the business is not a license to overpay. A wonderful company bought at a full price is a mediocre investment; the same company bought at a margin of safety is a wonderful one. Price, not quality alone, makes the investment.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: WONDERFUL (AWS + advertising), GOOD (retail/logistics), stapled to a bag of unforecastable moonshots. Financial Strength A++, net cash, ~18% consolidated ROIC, 25%+ ROIC in the crown-jewel segment, honest accounts, and one of the great capital-allocation records in corporate history.

Investment verdict at $242.67: HOLD / WATCH — do not buy here. The price already embeds the AWS/advertising success and prices the moonshots as assets rather than options. There is no margin of safety against our base intrinsic value (~$200). This is a price verdict, not a quality verdict — the two must never be confused.

Answer to the one deciding question: AWS's high-return franchise almost certainly will keep compounding faster than retail consumes capital — the segment economics say so. But the market has already paid for that outcome, and the incremental $200B/yr of AI/venture capex carries genuine, unpriced risk. The reinvestment engine is real; the entry price leaves nothing for error.

Two or three open questions to keep watching:
1. Does AI commoditize or entrench AWS? If foundation-model providers capture the value, AWS's infrastructure moat erodes; if AWS becomes the default AI platform (Bedrock, Trainium), the moat widens. This governs half the valuation.
2. Will the $200B capex clear the one-dollar test? Track AWS operating-income growth against the capex — if AWS OI decelerates while capex accelerates, incremental ROIC is falling below r and the base case breaks.
3. How large is the moonshot cash burn (Kuiper/Leo, Zoox), and does management stay disciplined enough to kill losers?

Two or three universal lessons from this case:
1. Value the parts, not the blend. A conglomerate's consolidated P/E is a lie by averaging. AWS deserves a cloud multiple; retail deserves a retail multiple; sum them.
2. For a heavy reinvestor, low free cash flow can be a feature, not a flaw. Amazon's FCF fell to $11B because it chose to invest $65B in growth. The analyst's job is to separate stand-still owner earnings (~$65B) from discretionary growth spend, and to judge the spend by its ROIC — not to mistake the investment for a shortfall.
3. A great business is not a great investment at any price. The margin of safety is the cornerstone; a full price on a wonderful company offers no protection against being wrong about a future that is, by Amazon's own design, unusually uncertain.

This is a teaching case, not investment advice.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with Amazon's own numbers)

  • Intrinsic value (IV). The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. IV = Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ. Amazon: ~$150 / $200 / $270 per share (our estimate).
  • Owner earnings. Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital — the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Amazon FY2025: NOPAT ~$65.6B + D&A $65.8B − maintenance capex ~$66B − ΔWC ~$0 ≈ $65B (~$6.05/sh).
  • Free cash flow (FCF). Operating cash flow − capex. Amazon: OCF $139.5B − net capex $128.3B = $11.2B (2025), down from $38.2B (2024) — depressed by growth capex.
  • Maintenance vs growth capex. Maintenance = spend to preserve unit volume and competitive position; growth = spend to expand. GAAP does not split them. Amazon: of $131.8B gross capex, we judge ~$66B maintenance (≈ D&A) and ~$65B growth.
  • Stock-based compensation (SBC). Non-cash pay to employees; a real, recurring expense and a source of dilution. Amazon FY2025: $19.5B; diluted shares creeping ~1.6%/yr.
  • NOPAT. Net operating profit after tax = Operating income × (1 − tax rate). Amazon: $80.0B × (1 − 0.18) = $65.6B.
  • Invested capital. Capital funding operations = equity + financial debt − excess cash. Amazon: $411B + $66B − $123B ≈ $354B.
  • ROIC. NOPAT / Invested capital. Value is created only when ROIC > r. Amazon consolidated ≈ 18.5%; AWS mid-20s%; retail mid-to-high teens.
  • ROE. Net income / Shareholders' equity. Amazon FY2025: $77.7B / $411B ≈ 18.9% (an independent research service/atlas 16.5–18.9%); flattered by non-operating gains — read with caution.
  • EV/EBIT. Enterprise value ÷ operating income (EBIT); the multiple we apply per segment in the SOTP. Amazon SOTP base: AWS 26× × $45.6B; Retail 20× × $34.4B.
  • Enterprise value (EV). Market cap + debt − cash; the value of the operating business independent of financing. Amazon SOTP base EV ≈ $1.87T; + net cash $54.6B → equity ~$1.93T.
  • Sum-of-the-parts (SOTP). Valuing each segment on its own appropriate multiple, then adding net cash — the honest estimator for a multi-business firm. Amazon: AWS ($0.9–1.4T) + Retail ($0.55–0.83T) + net cash.
  • Two-stage DCF. IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ — explicit-period value plus discounted terminal value. Amazon base: OE₀ $60B, g₁ 14%×10yr, r 10%, g 4% → ~$210/sh.
  • Terminal value. The value of all cash flows after the explicit forecast, OEₙ₊₁/(r−g), discounted back. Amazon: the dominant component — hence the DCF's sensitivity.
  • Discount rate (r). Owner's opportunity cost = long-term rate + uncertainty premium. Amazon: base 10% (≈ 4.6% Treasury + 5.4% premium; Beta 1.20).
  • Terminal growth (g). Perpetual post-explicit growth rate; must be modest. Amazon: base 4%.
  • Sustainable growth. g = Reinvestment rate × ROIC. Amazon reinvests ~all earnings at ~18% blended ROIC → high internally-funded growth — the compounding thesis.
  • Value-creating vs value-consuming growth. Growth adds value only when ROIC > r; below r it destroys value. Amazon: AWS/ads reinvestment (ROIC ≫ r) creates value; unproven moonshots may not.
  • Margin of safety. (IV − Price) / IV; the discount of price to conservative value. Amazon: at $242.67 vs base IV ~$200, margin is −21% (negative) → no protection.
  • Moat / economic franchise. A durable competitive advantage (switching costs, scale, network, brand, data) whose signature is pricing power. Amazon: AWS switching costs + scale; retail network + Prime; advertising data.
  • Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can forecast; its boundary matters more than its size. Amazon: inside for AWS/retail/ads, outside for satellites/robotaxis.
  • Mr. Market. Graham's manic-depressive partner who quotes a price daily; serve you, not guide you. Amazon: currently mildly euphoric on the AI theme.
  • One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of market value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). Amazon: passed spectacularly for two decades; prospective AI capex is the open question.
  • Free cash flow vs owner earnings gap. For a heavy reinvestor these diverge sharply; the truth is OCF minus stand-still capex. Amazon: reported FCF $11.2B vs owner earnings ~$65B — the entire lesson of this case.
