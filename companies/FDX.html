FedEx Corporation (FDX) — A Value-Investing Case Study

A Graham & Buffett Seminar

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

FedEx moves physical things — packages, freight, documents — from one place to another, reliably and on a promised schedule, and it charges more for speed and certainty than for a slower, less certain delivery. That is the entire business in one sentence. It is not a technology company and it is not, fundamentally, a financial company; it is a hauling company with an enormous, custom-built physical network: nearly 700 aircraft, more than 175,000 vehicles, and hub-and-spoke sorting centers anchored by its Memphis "SuperHub" and a second national hub in Indianapolis. Reported for fiscal 2025 (year ended May 31, 2025), the company generated $87,926 million in revenue and $5,217 million in operating income (a 5.9% operating margin) on that network (10-K, Item 8, Consolidated Statements of Income).

How it makes money, segment by segment (FY2025, Item 7 MD&A):
- Federal Express segment — $75,304M revenue (86% of the total), $4,885M operating income, a 6.5% operating margin. This is the combined air-ground express and ground-delivery network (FedEx Ground and FedEx Express were fully merged into one operating company, "Federal Express," on June 1, 2024, under the "one FedEx" plan). It sells U.S. priority and deferred package delivery, U.S. ground delivery, and international priority/economy package and freight services to more than 220 countries.
- FedEx Freight segment — $8,892M revenue (10% of the total), $1,489M operating income, a 16.7% operating margin — more than two and a half times as profitable, per dollar of revenue, as the much larger Express/Ground business. This is FedEx's less-than-truckload (LTL) trucking business.
- Corporate, other, and eliminations — the balance, running an operating loss (FedEx Office, FedEx Logistics, FedEx Dataworks, and corporate overhead), a modest drag.

That gap in profitability between the two reportable segments is the single most important fact in this case, and it explains the biggest event on the horizon: in December 2024, FedEx's board decided to spin off FedEx Freight into a separate, publicly traded company, targeted for completion by June 1, 2026 (10-K, Note 1; 10-Q, Note 1). As of the most recent 10-Q (quarter ended February 28, 2026), the separation was well underway — FedEx Freight Holding Company had already privately placed $3.7 billion of its own senior notes, held in restricted cash pending the spin, and FedEx's bank credit agreements had been amended to release FedEx Freight's guarantees upon completion. We could not find, in this research set, any filing confirming the spin actually closed — no post-spin 10-K/10-Q, no separate FedEx Freight ticker in the equity atlas. The live price we value against (July 12, 2026) is later than the company's own June 1, 2026 target, so there is real uncertainty about exactly what business the current FDX quote represents. We treat this candidly as an open question throughout, rather than pretending precision we don't have.

The one question that will decide this case: does FedEx's network — an expensive, hard-to-replicate physical asset base that nonetheless earns only high-single-digit returns on the capital tied up in it — earn enough, and durably enough, to be worth today's price of $314.69 a share, once we've translated its accounting earnings into real, spendable owner earnings?

Circle of competence

Understanding what FedEx does requires no special expertise — everyone has received a package. Forecasting its economics a decade out is harder, for reasons specific to this business: (1) it is meaningfully cyclical (its own numbers show fiscal-2020 EPS of $9.50 against a fiscal-2022 peak of $20.61 — more than a two-fold swing in two years, driven by the pandemic's freight boom and bust); (2) it carries direct, largely unhedged exposure to jet fuel prices and global trade volumes, both hostage to geopolitics and tariff policy (the 10-K devotes pages to 2025's new U.S. tariff regime and retaliatory measures); (3) its pilots have been bargaining for a successor labor contract since 2021 without resolution; and (4) the business itself is about to change shape via the Freight spin-off, so the ten-year record we are about to lean on describes an entity larger than the one investors may soon actually own.

None of that puts FedEx outside the circle — a package-delivery network is a comprehensible business, and cyclicality and fuel exposure are known, bounded risks rather than unknowable ones. But it does mean the forecast must be modest and the margin of safety generous; this is a business to analyze with humility, not false precision.

Principle: A stock is a piece of a business, and you only value what you can understand — but "I can understand it" and "I can forecast it precisely" are different claims. Where the business is cyclical, contract-dependent, and in the middle of a corporate transformation, the right response is not to pass, but to demand a wider cushion for what you cannot know.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The balance sheet — what FedEx owns and owes

At May 31, 2025 (10-K, Item 8), FedEx's balance sheet reads like what it is: a capital-heavy transportation company.

  • Total assets: $87,627M. The largest single asset class is property and equipment — $87,622M at cost, $41,642M net of $45,980M of accumulated depreciation (aircraft $31,584M at cost, package-handling/ground-support equipment $18,878M, vehicles $10,949M, facilities $16,505M, IT $9,706M). Operating lease right-of-use assets add another $16,453M — FedEx leases a great deal of what it doesn't own outright (aircraft, terminals, vehicles).
  • Goodwill is modest for a company this size — $6,603M, about 7.5% of assets. This is not a business whose balance sheet is dominated by intangible "story" assets; the value, such as it is, sits in the physical network and the brand, not in acquired goodwill. That is a point in favor of trusting the accounts: there is little room here for goodwill write-down surprises to swing the picture.
  • Liabilities: current liabilities of $15,411M against current assets of $18,386M — a current ratio of $18,386M ÷ $15,411M = 1.19×. That is adequate, not generous; Graham's own preference ran toward 2× or better, though for an investment-grade industrial with steady cash generation, 1.19× is acceptable rather than alarming.
  • Long-term debt, less current portion: $19,151M, plus a current portion of $1,428M — total debt of $20,579M. Operating lease liabilities of $16,837M ($2,565M current + $14,272M long-term) sit alongside this — a lease footprint nearly as large as the funded debt, which is why we look at both when judging leverage below.
  • Common stockholders' investment: $28,074M, or $117.96 per share on the roughly 238M shares outstanding — this is FedEx's book value per share, which we will return to when we discuss price-to-book.
  • A judgment-laden item worth naming: self-insurance accruals of $5,891M (workers' compensation, vehicle accidents, cargo loss, disability), an actuarially estimated reserve the auditors flagged as a "critical audit matter" — a five-percentage-point swing in assumed claim severity would move the reserve (and the income statement) by about $295 million (10-K, Critical Accounting Estimates). This is a real, ongoing judgment call embedded in the numbers, not a red flag by itself, but a reminder that "net income" contains estimates, not just facts.
  • Pension plans are a source of strength here, not a hidden liability: U.S. pension assets of $26.6 billion exceed the $26.0 billion projected benefit obligation — a funded surplus of about $0.6 billion (10-K, Critical Audit Matters). Many older industrials carry pension deficits that eat into intrinsic value; FedEx does not.

The income statement — the march from revenue to net income

($ millions, FY ended May 31) 2023 2024 2025
Revenue 90,155 87,693 87,926
Operating income 4,912 5,559 5,217
Operating margin 5.4% 6.3% 5.9%
Net income 3,972 4,331 4,092
Diluted EPS (GAAP) $15.48 $17.21 $16.81

Revenue has been essentially flat for three years — this is not a growth story on the top line. Operating margin bounced between 5.4% and 6.3%, a narrow, single-digit band typical of a scale logistics business with heavy fixed costs and thin pricing power at the margin. Two specific, recurring items are worth stripping out to see the underlying trend clearly:

  • "Business optimization costs" (the DRIVE restructuring program) have run $309M (2023) → $582M (2024) → $756M (2025), and continued into 2026 ($162M in the first nine months, per the 10-Q, plus a further $443M of FedEx Freight separation costs). Management calls these one-time; the manual's own caution (Chapter 7) is that a charge that recurs for four straight years, in a program not yet finished, is functioning as a real, ongoing cost of doing business, not a true one-off. We treat it that way below.
  • Pension mark-to-market (MTM) adjustments added a non-cash $515M pretax ($390M after tax, $1.60/share) to net income in 2025 and $561M pretax ($426M after tax, $1.69/share) in 2024 — purely actuarial noise (discount-rate and asset-return assumptions), unrelated to operations, and it should be stripped from any read of "how the business is really doing," in whichever direction it runs.

Net income of $4,092M ÷ diluted shares of ~243.5M gives the reported $16.81. Strip the pension MTM noise and add back the genuinely episodic items (asset impairments of $16M/share-equivalent, an $0.37/share international regulatory/legal matter, $0.18/share of spin-off costs) — but leave the recurring DRIVE charges in — and a more conservative "normalized operating EPS" for 2025 comes to approximately $15.82 (versus an independent research service's own pro forma figure of $18.19, which adds the DRIVE charges back in full — a judgment call we respectfully differ with, and flag explicitly, per the manual's earnings-quality discipline).

Two things jump out. First, operating cash flow has been declining even as net income has been roughly stable — $8,848M (2023) to $7,036M (2025) — driven mainly by working-capital swings (a $4,700M cash drag from changes in receivables and payables in 2025 alone, per the MD&A liquidity table), which is a reminder that "net income + depreciation" (the simplified "cash flow per share" an independent research service tabulates) is not the same thing as actual cash generated — a good, concrete illustration of why the manual insists on reading the real cash flow statement rather than a shortcut. Second, and more importantly for valuation: free cash flow has consistently run well below net income — $2.98 billion of free cash flow in 2025 against $4.09 billion of net income. That gap is capital expenditure, and it is the crux of the owner-earnings estimate below.

Principle: The statements articulate together — net income flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet and starts the cash flow statement, but it is the cash flow statement's own arithmetic, not net income, that tells you what the business could actually pay out to its owners. Where a persistent, multi-year gap exists between "clean" net income and free cash flow, trust the cash.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Is earning power growing, stable, or fading?

The ten-year an independent research service record (and the XBRL history, back to fiscal 2016) shows a genuinely cyclical earnings stream, not a smooth compounder:

FY 2021 2022 2023 2024 2025
EPS (VL pro forma) $18.17 $20.61 $14.96 $17.80 $18.19

FY2020 (the pandemic year) produced just $9.50 of EPS; FY2022, riding a freight and e-commerce boom, peaked at $20.61; FY2023 fell back to $14.96 as that boom unwound and the Ground segment struggled with excess capacity. Averaging through this cycle, rather than capitalizing any single year, is exactly the discipline the manual insists on (Chapter 7) — a business this cyclical will mislead an analyst who capitalizes a peak or trough year as if it were "normal."

Estimating owner earnings

Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital. FedEx does not separately disclose a maintenance-versus-growth capex split, so — being conservative, per the manual's rule to use inputs that don't quietly spend the margin of safety — we treat the entirety of reported capital expenditure as maintenance capex (a cautious assumption, since some of it, such as Network 2.0 investment, arguably improves efficiency rather than simply replacing worn-out assets, but little of it corresponds to net new capacity given nearly flat package volumes over the period). Using operating cash flow (which already nets out working-capital swings) minus total capex as a clean, cash-verified proxy for owner earnings:

FY 2021 2022 2023 2024 2025
OCF ($M) 10,135 9,832 8,848 8,312 7,036
Capex ($M) 5,884 6,762 6,174 5,176 4,055
Free cash flow ($M) 4,251 3,070 2,674 3,136 2,981

Five-year average FCF = $16,112M ÷ 5 = $3,222M, or on roughly 240 million shares, about $13.40 per share — this is our working owner-earnings estimate. It sits meaningfully below both GAAP EPS (~$16.81) and an independent research service's pro forma EPS (~$18.19): $4,092 ÷ $3,222 = 1.27×, i.e., reported net income runs about 27% above the cash an owner could actually withdraw after funding the network's capital needs. That is the signature of a capital-hungry business, not a capital-light one (Chapter 6) — closer to Berkshire's old textile mills than to See's Candy, though far better, because the network moat lets FedEx earn a positive, if modest, return on all that reinvested capital, which the textile mills never did.

There is a genuine, good-faith case for a higher number: capital expenditure has fallen sharply and steadily — from $6,762M (2022) to $4,055M (2025) — as the DRIVE and Network 2.0 programs cut structural cost and capital intensity, and management guides 2026 capex to about $4.5 billion with aircraft spending declining further. If that trend holds, real owner earnings could drift toward $14–15/share over the next few years. We treat that as the optimistic end of a range, not the base case, since a restructuring program that has "not yet finished" for four years running earns some skepticism about exactly when the savings become durable.

The moat: real, but with edges under pressure

FedEx's moat is a scale and network-density advantage: nearly 700 aircraft, a hub-and-spoke system decades in the building, roughly 63,000 drop-off points, and connections to more than 220 countries. The 10-K states plainly that it would be "extremely difficult, costly, and time-consuming to replicate" this network — a defensible claim; no new entrant is building a competing global air-cargo network from scratch.

But the pricing-power test — can FedEx raise prices without losing volume? — comes back mixed. Federal Express implemented a 5.9% average list-price increase in January 2025, yet composite package yield rose only from $15.80 to $15.86 — essentially flat — because customers are shifting toward lower-yield deferred and economy services (international economy package volume was up 40% in 2025 while international priority volume fell 12%). List-price power exists; realized yield power is being diluted by mix shift, a distinction worth teaching in its own right. The 10-K's own competition section names the risk squarely: Amazon.com, historically a customer, is building out its own delivery network and has begun offering LTL freight services to third parties — "may be considered competitors in addition to customers." The industry report (an independent research service, Air Transport, May 2026) echoes this, calling out Amazon's push as a watch item for the whole sector. Government-subsidized foreign postal carriers add further, structurally lower-cost competition on international lanes. This is a moat, not a fortress — durable enough to keep FedEx a top-two global player, but not wide enough to guarantee expanding margins.

Return on invested capital — the central quality measure

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital.

  • NOPAT (FY2025) = Operating income $5,217M × (1 − 24.8% effective tax rate) = $3,923M.
  • Invested capital (debt + equity − cash) = ($20,579M debt + $28,074M equity − $5,502M cash) = $43,151M.
  • ROIC = $3,923M ÷ $43,151M = 9.1%.

If we count FedEx's $16,837M of operating lease liabilities as debt-like capital (reasonable, given how much of the network is leased rather than owned), invested capital rises to $59,988M and ROIC falls to 6.5%. an independent research service's own "Return on Total Capital" line shows 9.4% for fiscal 2025 — a close match to our unlevered-of-leases figure, and a useful independent check.

Compare either figure to a sensible discount rate of about 9% (developed below): FedEx earns at or modestly below its own cost of capital. This is the single most important quality finding in the case. It does not make FedEx a bad business — the network moat is real, and 9%-ish returns on a very large, tangible asset base is a perfectly respectable industrial outcome — but it firmly places FedEx outside the "wonderful compounder" category (a See's Candy earning 30%+ on capital) and inside the category where growth is roughly value-neutral rather than automatically value-creating (Chapter 11).

Return on equity, by contrast, looks better — $4,092M ÷ $28,074M = 14.6% (an independent research service shows 15.8%, using average rather than year-end equity) — a reminder that ROE, levered up by $20.6 billion of debt and $16.8 billion of lease liabilities, tells a rosier story than the unlevered ROIC. Both numbers are legitimate; the manual's advice to watch ROE for leverage flattery is exactly on point here.

One more data point worth carrying into the valuation section: the segment operating margins (Federal Express 6.5%, FedEx Freight 16.7%) imply the Freight segment likely earns a materially higher ROIC on its slice of invested capital than the Express/Ground trunk business does — which is precisely the economic logic behind the planned spin-off, discussed further below.

Principle: ROIC, not the growth rate or the size of the network, is the test of business quality. A network that is expensive and hard to copy is a real moat — but the moat is only valuable to the owner to the extent it produces returns above the cost of capital, and here it does so only modestly.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

  • Leverage. Total debt $20,579M − cash $5,502M = net debt of $15,077M. Against our five-year average owner earnings of about $3,222M, that is $15,077M ÷ $3,222M ≈ 4.7 years of owner earnings to clear the debt — a moderate, not alarming, burden for an investment-grade industrial, but not a fortress figure either. Against reported operating income ($5,217M), net debt is 2.9×.
  • Coverage. Operating income $5,217M ÷ interest expense $789M = 6.6×. A normal year covers the interest bill comfortably — this is a solvent, investment-grade capital structure, not a stretched one.
  • Liquidity. Current ratio 1.19× (above), plus $5.5 billion of cash and $3.5 billion of undrawn credit-facility capacity (10-K, Liquidity Outlook) — adequate to meet near-term obligations without forced asset sales.
  • Credit ratings and the refinancing wall. S&P: BBB (senior unsecured), Moody's: Baa2 — solidly investment grade but firmly in the lower-medium tier, not the top. FedEx actively manages its maturity ladder: in the first quarter of fiscal 2026 it issued $850 million of new notes (due 2032 and 2037) and used part of the proceeds to retire $500 million of notes maturing in August 2025 — a business managing its ladder in an open market, not one facing a looming, unhedged wall.
  • Hidden/off-balance-sheet items. Operating leases ($16,837M of liabilities) are the largest such item, but — thanks to modern lease accounting — they are already on the face of the balance sheet, not truly hidden. Self-insurance accruals ($5,891M) are a real, judgment-laden reserve, flagged by the auditors as a critical audit matter, but disclosed with sensitivity analysis. Pensions are overfunded, a rare positive in this category. The one true wildcard is the FedEx Freight spin-off itself: FedEx Freight Holding pre-funded $3.7 billion of its own debt ahead of the separation, which — once complete — will remove that debt (and the Freight segment's cash flows) from the consolidated FedEx balance sheet. This is a capital-structure change in progress, not a completed fact, as of the filings on hand.

Verdict: adequate. an independent research service's own Financial Strength grade of B++ (not the A-range reserved for the strongest balance sheets) agrees with this read: solvent, investment-grade, comfortably able to survive an ordinary bad year, but not a fortress. Combined with the ROIC finding above (returns near the cost of capital), the balance sheet earns a "pass, not a gold star" — which argues for demanding a real margin of safety rather than paying up on the assumption that quality alone will protect the investment.

Principle: Solvency is the precondition that lets every other quality compound. FedEx clears that bar — but "clears the bar" is different from "fortress," and the distinction matters directly for how much margin of safety we should insist on below.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

The clearest, most measurable fact about FedEx's capital allocation is the share count: 318 million shares outstanding in fiscal 2013, falling steadily to 238 million by fiscal 2025 and a projected 229 million by fiscal 2027 (an independent research service ten-year table). That is a real, sustained reduction — not simply offsetting option dilution — and it means every dollar of future owner earnings is being divided among fewer claims each year. In fiscal 2025 alone, FedEx repurchased $3.0 billion of stock (10.9 million shares at an average price of $274.34); through the first nine months of fiscal 2026 it repurchased shares at a notably lower average price (~$233), which is the right way to buy back stock — more shares per dollar when the price is cheaper, not a mechanical, price-agnostic program.

The dividend has been raised sharply and consistently — from $0.44/share (2010) to $5.52/share (2025), a 15.5%-per-year growth rate over the past five years — while the payout ratio has stayed moderate (around 29–31% of net profit), leaving room for continued growth without threatening the balance sheet.

The DRIVE / Network 2.0 restructuring is management's attempt to structurally cut costs and capital intensity — a legitimate, if slow, reinvestment in the existing business, though (per our earnings-quality discussion above) one that has run for four years without yet being "done," which tempers how much credit we give it today.

The boldest and, in our view, most encouraging capital-allocation decision is the planned spin-off of FedEx Freight — separating a 16.7%-margin trucking business from a 6.5%-margin express/ground trunk business rather than continuing to run them as one consolidated, harder-to-value conglomerate. This is the opposite of empire-building; it is a deliberate simplification, and it is exactly the kind of decision the manual credits management for making when it serves owners rather than management's own scope. The company's other recent capital commitment — a roughly $2.6 billion minority (37%) stake in a consortium acquiring InPost, a European parcel-locker network, alongside FedEx's existing business, pending in the second half of 2026 (10-Q, Note 1 on investments) — is a modest, bolt-on international expansion, not a scope-chasing mega-deal.

Applying the one-dollar test (retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ the discount rate): with company-wide ROIC running close to our assumed 9% discount rate, FedEx sits right at the boundary where reinvestment is only roughly value-neutral — which makes the buyback and dividend record (returning cash to owners rather than reflexively plowing it all back into a network earning near its cost of capital) a rational response, not a wasted opportunity.

Principle: Judge capital allocation by the one-dollar test and the share count, not the press release. FedEx's shrinking share count, disciplined (price-sensitive) buybacks, growing but well-covered dividend, and a rare instance of voluntary corporate simplification via spin-off are all evidence of a management team behaving like an owner's partner rather than an empire-builder — a real, if not spectacular, positive.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Choosing the method

FedEx is a mature, moderately cyclical, capital-intensive industrial with real (if imperfect) pricing power and ROIC running near its cost of capital. That profile argues for a normalized-earnings P/E multiple as the primary tool (Chapter 14's guidance: multiples are the steadier, more honest tool for mature, cyclical franchises where a precise DCF risks manufacturing false confidence from an acutely sensitive terminal-value assumption). We triangulate that primary estimate against the manual's three universal cross-checks — the no-growth floor, the implied multiple, and the owner-earnings yield versus the long bond — using our conservative, cash-verified owner-earnings figure of about $13.40/share (five-year average free cash flow).

The P/E anchor — three views, reconciled

(a) Historical median P/E. an independent research service's own decade-plus record gives FedEx a median P/E of 14.0×, with a range across the cycle from roughly 11.9× (troughs) to 20.6× (peaks). This is the company's own long-run normal, spanning a genuine boom-bust cycle (2020 pandemic trough through 2022 freight boom) — a solid, unbiased anchor.

(b) Peer/industry comparison. FedEx's closest public comparable is United Parcel Service (UPS), in the same an independent research service "Air Transport" industry. Per the equity atlas, UPS trades at a live P/E of about 18.2× against its own historical median of 17× (a ratio of 1.07× — roughly fair value versus its own history), while FedEx trades at 16.97× against a median of 14.0× (a ratio of 1.21× — moderately rich versus its own history, even though the absolute multiples are similar). The an independent research service industry report itself notes that FedEx's operating performance has recently been stronger than UPS's, which argues for some premium — but FedEx's bigger stretch above its own median tempers how much premium is warranted. A peer-anchored multiple in the 15–16× range is a reasonable midpoint.

(c) Quality-and-growth-justified multiple, from first principles. With ROIC (≈9%) sitting essentially at our assumed discount rate (≈9%), FedEx's growth is close to value-neutral (Chapter 11: value is created only when ROIC exceeds r). A business earning near its cost of capital, growing revenue at a an independent research service-projected 5%/year and EPS at 6.5%/year, does not deserve a rich multiple purely on growth grounds — a first-principles justified multiple here comes out lower than either the historical or peer anchor, in the 11–13× range.

Reconciling: these three anchors disagree, and the disagreement is informative. The historical (14×) and peer (15–16×) anchors reflect what the market has actually paid for FedEx and its closest comparable; the quality-justified anchor (11–13×) reflects what the raw ROIC-versus-cost-of-capital math actually supports. We weight the historical median (14×) as the central case, since it is the company's own long-run experience through a full cycle, but pull the low end of our range down toward the quality-justified figure (12×) to respect the ROIC finding, and let the high end run to 16× to give credit to the peer comparison and the spin-off's potential to unlock a better multiple on the higher-margin remaining pieces.

Building the range

Applying 12×/14×/16× to a normalized EPS band of roughly $15.82 (our conservative 2025 figure, stripping pension MTM noise but not adding back the recurring DRIVE charges) to $17 (giving some credit to the still-in-progress cost program and FY2026 estimates):

Value per share ≈$190 ≈$230 ≈$280

Triangulating with the universal cross-checks

Using our conservative owner-earnings figure of $13.40/share and a discount rate of r ≈ 9% (the 30-year Treasury yield of roughly 4.9–5.0% per an independent research service's July 2026 Selection & Opinion, plus a premium of about 4 points for FedEx's cyclicality, fuel exposure, and capital intensity):

  1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r = $13.40 ÷ 0.09 ≈ $149/share. This is the value of FedEx if it never grows another dollar of owner earnings — and it sits below even the low end of our P/E-anchored range. The gap between $149 and our $230 base case (about 35% of the base value) is a direct measure of how much of that estimate is a bet on growth materializing — a useful, humbling number given FedEx's essentially flat revenue over the past three years.
  2. Implied multiple at the current price. $314.69 ÷ $13.40 = 23.5× owner earnings — well above our reasoned 12–16× range, and above even an independent research service's own historical peak multiple (20.6×). Measured against reported normalized EPS (~$16.50), the current price implies 19.1× — still above our high case.
  3. Owner-earnings yield versus the long bond. $13.40 ÷ $314.69 = 4.3%, against a 30-year Treasury yield of about 4.9%. The stock's owner-earnings yield sits below the risk-free long bond — a clear warning sign per the manual (Chapter 12/14): at today's price, FedEx offers less current cash-on-cash return than a risk-free bond, and the entire investment case leans on growth or margin expansion that has not yet shown up reliably in the free cash flow.

All three cross-checks point the same direction, which is itself informative: they are not simply restating the same input three times by coincidence — they are testing the growth assumption, the multiple, and the yield-versus-alternatives separately, and all three say the current price assumes materially more than a conservative reading of FedEx's owner earnings supports.

A necessary caveat: the spin-off complicates the picture

We should be honest about a real limitation. If the FedEx Freight spin-off has already closed by the date of the live price we are using (July 12, 2026 — after the company's own June 1, 2026 target), the $314.69 quote may reflect a smaller "New FedEx" (Express + Ground only) being compared, unfairly, against consolidated historical EPS that still includes Freight's higher-margin contribution. We found no filing confirming closing, and no separate FedEx Freight entity anywhere in this research set, so we have treated the price as still applying to the consolidated business — the more conservative and better-evidenced assumption — but flag this explicitly as an open question that a student (or investor) must resolve before acting. A sum-of-the-parts read (Federal Express segment EBIT of $4,885M capitalized separately from FedEx Freight's $1,489M, each at a multiple appropriate to its own margin and moat) is directionally consistent with our whole-company range, but we do not present it as a precise, load-bearing number given how rough segment-level invested-capital assumptions necessarily are.

Principle: Value is, by definition, discounted owner earnings — but because a single DCF is acutely sensitive to its inputs, we triangulate a P/E-anchored estimate with the no-growth floor, the implied multiple, and the owner-earnings yield. When all three cross-checks agree that the price implies more optimism than the conservative case supports, that agreement — not any single number — is the signal worth trusting.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Current price: $314.69 (equity_atlas.xlsx, live as of July 12, 2026).
Intrinsic value range: $190 (low) – $230 (base) – $280 (high).

Margin of safety at the base case = (Intrinsic Value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value = ($230 − $314.69) ÷ $230 = −37%. Even at the high end of our range, ($280 − $314.69) ÷ $280 = −12%. There is no margin of safety at today's price on a conservative reading of owner earnings — the stock is trading above, not below, our reasoned fair-value range.

Where would a margin of safety appear? Using the base case ($230) and requiring a classic 25–30% discount to be "interested" in Graham's sense, a price in the $160–175 area would offer real protection; even a more modest 15% discount to the low end of our range ($190) points to interest emerging somewhere in the $160s. An independent research service's own 18-month target-price low of $290 (from the May 2026 report) is, notably, still above our entire range — a reminder that an independent research service's targets and a conservative owner-earnings-based estimate can disagree, and it is worth knowing why: An independent research service's own pro forma EPS ($18.19, adding back the DRIVE charges in full) is more generous than our normalized figure, and its target multiples embed some of the same optimism our cross-checks flag.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or roughly rational here? Neither extreme. FedEx's stock has actually fallen about 6% from the May 2026 an independent research service report price ($335.30) to today's $314.69, and the Air Transport industry as a whole was one of the better-performing groups in the market over the last six weeks (+14.0%, per the July 2026 Selection & Opinion) — so this is not a name in obvious distress, nor is it euphoric relative to its sector. It looks, instead, like a solidly-regarded, moderately-expensive industrial being priced for the DRIVE program and the spin-off to work out roughly as management hopes. That is a reasonable bet for someone who shares management's optimism — but it is exactly the kind of price at which the manual counsels patience, not purchase, for an investor demanding a cushion.

Per the manual's guidance on where the safety lives: FedEx is not the kind of high-ROIC franchise (Chapter 13) where quality itself supplies part of the margin of safety — its ROIC sits at, not comfortably above, the cost of capital — so here the entire margin of safety has to come from the purchase discount. At today's price, that discount does not exist.

Principle: Insist on a cushion; use Mr. Market, never obey him. A business whose quality is good-but-not-wonderful needs its margin of safety to come from price, and price alone — and right now, price is not offering one.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Returning to the one question we opened with: does FedEx's network — an expensive, hard-to-replicate asset base earning returns near its own cost of capital — earn enough, and durably enough, to be worth $314.69 a share once translated into real owner earnings? Our answer is no, not at today's price. The network moat is real and the business is solvent, sensibly managed, and shrinking its share count in owners' favor — but on a conservative, cash-verified reading of owner earnings, three independent cross-checks (the no-growth floor, the implied multiple, and the owner-earnings yield against the long bond) all agree the current price bakes in more optimism than the numbers, on their own, support.

This is a "fair-to-good," not a "wonderful," business. It has a real moat (scale, network density, brand) but not a wide one (ROIC near the cost of capital, pricing power partially offset by mix shift, a credible new competitor in Amazon); it is capital-intensive rather than capital-light; and it is mid-transformation (DRIVE, Network 2.0, the Freight spin-off), which makes today's numbers a moving target rather than a settled baseline.

Three things a student would need to understand better before getting comfortable:
1. Whether and how the FedEx Freight spin-off actually closed — the exact date, the terms of the distribution, and the resulting capital structure and share count of "New FedEx," none of which appear in the filings on hand.
2. Whether DRIVE's cost savings are durable — the program has produced "one-time" restructuring charges for four consecutive years; a student should track whether capital expenditure and business-optimization costs actually fall to a genuinely lower steady state, or whether "transformation" simply becomes a permanent line item.
3. How much of the moat is being eroded by Amazon's logistics build-out — a multi-year trend that the 10-K itself flags as worth watching, and that would directly affect the durability of the returns this valuation assumes.

Two or three lessons this case teaches most vividly:
- GAAP earnings and owner earnings can diverge for years at a time in a capital-intensive business, and the gap (here, roughly 25–30% of reported net income) is exactly the cash a passive owner would not actually get to keep. Always ask what the cash flow statement says before trusting the multiple the market is paying on net income.
- A real, durable-looking moat (a hard-to-replicate global network) is not automatically a wide moat in the return-on-capital sense — ROIC near the cost of capital is a specific, measurable finding, not a vibe, and it should discipline how much you're willing to pay regardless of how impressive the physical network looks.
- When three independent cross-checks — built from the same core number but stressing different assumptions — all point the same direction, that agreement is worth taking seriously, even when (as here) it conflicts with a well-regarded independent source's (an independent research service's) own price targets.

This is a teaching case, not personalized investment advice.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

  • Owner earnings — the cash a passive owner could withdraw from a business without weakening it: reported earnings + non-cash charges (D&A) − maintenance capex − incremental working capital. For FedEx: five-year average free cash flow of about $3,222M, or $13.40/share, versus reported net income of $4,092M ($16.81/share) in FY2025 — GAAP earnings overstate cash owner earnings here by about 27%.
  • Free cash flow (FCF) — operating cash flow minus capital expenditures. FedEx FY2025: $7,036M − $4,055M = $2,981M.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex — the (judgment-based) split between capital spending that merely replaces worn-out assets and spending that adds new capacity. FedEx doesn't disclose the split; we conservatively treated all $4,055M of FY2025 capex as maintenance, which understates owner earnings if some of it is truly growth-oriented.
  • Return on invested capital (ROIC) — NOPAT ÷ invested capital, the central measure of business quality. FedEx FY2025: 9.1% (or 6.5% counting operating leases as debt) — near, not above, our assumed 9% discount rate.
  • NOPAT (net operating profit after tax) — operating income × (1 − tax rate). FedEx FY2025: $5,217M × (1 − 24.8%) = $3,923M.
  • Invested capital — debt + equity − cash. FedEx FY2025: $20,579M + $28,074M − $5,502M = $43,151M.
  • Return on equity (ROE) — net income ÷ shareholders' equity. FedEx FY2025: $4,092M ÷ $28,074M = 14.6% (an independent research service: 15.8%, using average equity).
  • Interest coverage — operating income (EBIT) ÷ interest expense. FedEx FY2025: $5,217M ÷ $789M = 6.6×.
  • Net debt — total debt − cash. FedEx FY2025: $20,579M − $5,502M = $15,077M; against owner earnings of ~$3,222M, that's about 4.7 years to repay.
  • Current ratio — current assets ÷ current liabilities. FedEx FY2025: $18,386M ÷ $15,411M = 1.19×.
  • Discount rate (r) — the owner's opportunity cost of capital, anchored to long-term rates plus a risk premium; it acts as "gravity" pulling down the present value of future cash flows as rates rise. We used r ≈ 9% (30-year Treasury ~4.9–5.0% plus a premium of about 4 points for cyclicality and capital intensity).
  • Intrinsic value = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1+r)ᵗ — the master equation: value is the discounted sum of all future owner earnings. We estimated it here via a normalized P/E multiple (the more reliable tool for this business) rather than a literal multi-year discounting, and used the master equation's logic to build the cross-checks.
  • Margin of safety — (intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value. FedEx at the base case: ($230 − $314.69) ÷ $230 = −37% — no cushion; the price exceeds our estimate of value.
  • P/E ratio; justified/normalized P/E — price ÷ earnings per share. FedEx's live P/E is 16.97× versus a 10-year median of 14.0× (a 1.21× ratio — moderately expensive versus its own history); we anchored a normalized range of 12×–16× on a conservative EPS of $15.82–$17.50.
  • EV/EBIT; enterprise value — enterprise value (market cap + net debt) ÷ operating income. At $314.69 and ~238.6M shares, FedEx's EV is roughly $90 billion against $5,217M of operating income — about 17.3×, rich for a ~9% ROIC industrial.
  • Price-to-book (P/B) — price ÷ book value per share. FedEx: $314.69 ÷ $117.96 = 2.67×, against an ROE of ~15% — a premium to book consistent with, if a bit rich relative to, its return on equity.
  • Dividend yield; payout ratio — dividend per share ÷ price; dividends ÷ net income. FedEx: about 1.7% yield, with a payout ratio of roughly 29–31% of net profit — well covered, with room to keep growing.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value = owner earnings ÷ r — the floor beneath a DCF, showing how much of the price is a bet on growth. FedEx: $13.40 ÷ 0.09 ≈ $149/share — well below both the current price and our full valuation range, meaning a large share of today's price is riding on growth that hasn't yet shown up reliably.
  • Owner-earnings yield — owner earnings ÷ price, compared to the long bond. FedEx: $13.40 ÷ $314.69 = 4.3%, versus a 30-year Treasury yield of about 4.9% — the stock yields less than the risk-free bond on this conservative measure, a caution flag.
  • Economic moat — a durable competitive advantage protecting high returns; here, network scale and density, tempered by mix-shift pressure on realized pricing and a credible new competitor (Amazon).
  • Circle of competence — the set of businesses you can understand well enough to forecast; FedEx's business model is simple to understand, but precise forecasting is genuinely harder given cyclicality, fuel costs, labor negotiations, and the pending spin-off.
  • One-dollar test — retain a dollar of earnings only if it creates at least a dollar of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). With FedEx's ROIC near r, the company's shift toward returning cash via buybacks and dividends, rather than reflexively reinvesting all of it, is the economically sound choice.
  • EBITDA — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization; a metric we deliberately avoided as a stand-in for cash flow, per the manual's caution that it hides real capital consumption — FedEx's own capital intensity is exactly why this distinction matters here.
  • Book value — shareholders' equity per share; FedEx's is $117.96, not a ceiling on what the business is worth, but a useful cross-check point given the moat here is only moderate rather than commanding a huge premium to tangible capital.
  • Normalized / mid-cycle earnings — earnings averaged through a full boom-bust cycle rather than a single year, essential for a cyclical business like FedEx (FY2020 EPS of $9.50 versus FY2022's $20.61 shows why a single year would badly mislead a valuation).
