Honeywell International (HON) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition· Charlotte, NC · July 4, 2026

A teaching case. We value one real company end-to-end using the method in "How to Value a Business." Numbers are worked in the open so you can check every step. This is education, not investment advice — and, as you will see, HON is a company mid-transformation, so the very statements we read describe an entity that is about to cease to exist in its current form.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Honeywell is a diversified industrial with a 119-year-old brand, operating through four segments (FY2025 continuing-ops sales of $37.4 billion):

Segment 2025 Sales Segment profit Margin What it is
Aerospace Technologies $17,510M $4,284M 24.5% APUs, engines, avionics, defense/space — OEM + high-margin aftermarket
Building Automation $7,367M $1,953M 26.5% Building controls, fire, security, energy management
Industrial Automation $9,401M $1,743M 18.5% Process controls, sensing, warehouse automation
Energy & Sustainability (UOP) $3,134M $692M 22.1% Refining/petrochem process licensing, catalysts

(Segment profit and sales cited from 10-K Review of Business Segments; margins are profit ÷ sales.)

Two facts dominate this case and you must hold them from the first minute:

  1. Advanced Materials (Solstice, ticker SOLS) was spun off October 30, 2025 — every historical statement has been restated to exclude it.
  2. Honeywell announced (Feb 6, 2025) it will separate Aerospace into a standalone public company — the 10-Q dates completion June 29, 2026. In March 2026 the company raised $22 billion of new debt ($16.0B Aerospace senior notes + $6.0B term loan) to fund the split. The parent will become a pure-play automation company; Aerospace ($17.5B sales, $1.5B net income) leaves as "Honeywell Aerospace" (ticker HONA).

So we are valuing a company that, as of today (July 4, 2026), has essentially just split in two — or is within days of doing so. The historical financials describe a soon-to-change entity. The transferable principle (Manual Ch. 7): never capitalize numbers that no longer describe the business you would own. We will value the whole HON as it stands on the eve of separation, then cross-check with a sum-of-the-parts, because the market price of $229.86 is a claim on both pieces (an owner today receives HONA shares in the spin).

Circle-of-competence gate (Ch. 4). Can we forecast this? An industrial with a century-long brand, a $37.5B backlog, disciplined margins, and predictable aftermarket revenue is forecastable — an independent research service rates Earnings Predictability 95/100, near the top of its universe. That said, the mid-flight breakup adds a layer of "which entity, at what stand-alone cost structure?" uncertainty. We are inside the circle for the combined economics, but honest that stand-alone segment margins post-split are estimates. Gate: PASS, with a footnote.

THE ONE QUESTION (the thing that, if wrong, breaks the thesis): Will the two post-separation companies — a pure-play automation Honeywell and a stand-alone Aerospace — together sustain the ~24% segment margins and mid-single-digit organic growth that today's price assumes, or does the breakup destroy hidden scale economies and dis-synergies eat the value?

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The three statements must articulate — the income statement's earnings should show up as cash on the cash-flow statement and as growing equity (or returned capital) on the balance sheet. Let's read them as one.

Income statement (FY2025 continuing ops). Sales $37,442M; operating income $8,127M (XBRL, verified); reported net income attributable to Honeywell was messy — the four quarters summed to just $4,729M because Q4 2025 was negative $115M. Why? A pile of one-time items ran through 2025: a $724M goodwill impairment (held-for-sale classification of Productivity Solutions and Warehouse/Workflow), $270M held-for-sale asset impairment, a $148M asbestos-divestiture loss, offset by a $0.8B Resideo termination gain and a $239M debt-extinguishment loss in early 2026. This is exactly the kind of year the Manual warns against anchoring to (Ch. 7: "strip windfalls and one-time items in both directions").

An independent research service does the normalization for us: adjusted EPS of $10.67 for 2025 (VL explicitly excludes nonrecurring items — footnote A lists them: '25 excludes –$3.73). On ~640M average shares, that is roughly $6.8B of normalized net profit. We will anchor on the normalized figure, not the GAAP $4.7B.

Cash flow. Operating cash flow from continuing operations was $6.1B (10-K), $6.4B including discontinued (XBRL: $6,408M). Capital spending is light — an independent research service puts 2025 cap spending near $1.55/share × 640 ≈ $1.0B, only ~2.6% of sales. That capital-lightness is the signature of a franchise (Ch. 6). Note the Q1 2026 quirk: operating cash flow was –$650M, distorted by separation costs and working-capital timing — a reminder to average, not annualize a quarter.

Balance sheet (Q1 2026 10-Q). Total assets $73,988M; total debt is now large — long-term debt $29,010M plus $3,099M current maturities plus $4,630M commercial paper = ~$36.7B gross, against ~$12.0B cash. Equity is only $13,590M — and here is the earnings-quality flag: equity is thin because HON has bought back so much stock (treasury stock is –$43,904M!). Retained earnings are $51,029M. Do not anchor to book value (Ch. 7): HON's equity understates the business massively — a franchise earning ~45% ROE on a shrunken equity base is not a $14B company.

Earnings-quality scan. Green flags: clean aftermarket cash, high Earnings Predictability, dividends 16 raises in 15 years. Yellow flags: (1) the 2025 GAAP noise; (2) acquisition-heavy — $2.2B Sundyne (2025), CAES, Access Solutions, LNG, plus the pending $1.8B Johnson Matthey Catalysts — meaning goodwill and amortization of acquired intangibles inflate the balance sheet and depress GAAP earnings vs. cash; (3) the separation itself introduces $22B of new debt whose interest now hits the P&L (interest rose from $1,048M in 2024 to $1,344M in 2025, heading higher). The principle: acquisitive growth makes GAAP earnings understate cash earnings (amortization) but also plants the risk of impairment — both showed up in 2025.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Earning-power trend (long XBRL history). Revenue per share and EPS have compounded steadily. From the an independent research service decade array, adjusted EPS went $6.04 (2015) → $8.01 (2018) → $9.16 (2023) → $10.67 (2025), roughly 6% annualized — remarkably smooth for an industrial, which is why an independent research service grades Earnings Predictability 95. Operating income (XBRL): $8,022M (2022) → $7,563M (2023) → $7,667M (2024) → $8,127M (2025). Stable, mid-$8B earning power.

OWNER EARNINGS (Ch. 7). The cash an owner can actually withdraw:

Owner Earnings = Normalized net income + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital

  • Normalized net income (VL basis, ex one-timers): $6,830M
  • Add non-cash D&A: depreciation ~$1,388M + amortization of acquired intangibles; call total ~$1,500M (Q1 2026 ran D+A of $357M/qtr ≈ $1.43B annualized, plus growth). Amortization of acquisition intangibles is a genuine non-cash add-back.
  • Subtract maintenance capex ~$850M (of the ~$1.0B total capex, most is maintenance for an industrial; a small slice funds growth/new-product tooling). This is the hard, judgmental split the Manual insists you make yourself.
  • Subtract incremental working capital ~$150M (modest; HON manages working-capital turnover tightly).

Owner Earnings ≈ 6,830 + 1,500 − 850 − 150 = $7,330M, or ~$11.57 per share on 633.7M shares.

Notice owner earnings ($11.57) run above reported adjusted EPS ($10.67) — because acquisition-intangible amortization (non-cash) exceeds maintenance capex. That is the capital-light franchise signature: reported earnings understate the withdrawable cash.

NORMALIZE: we deliberately used VL's ex-items figure and averaged capex, not the GAAP $4.7B (depressed) nor a peak. A cyclical caution: Aerospace aftermarket is riding a post-COVID boom (an independent research service: "elongated boom period"); we should not assume 12% Aero growth persists — hence conservative 6% blended growth later.

ROIC (Ch. 6) — quality of the economics.

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital

  • Normalized operating income = $8,127M + $724M (gw imp) + $270M (HFS imp) + $148M (asbestos) − $800M (Resideo gain) = $8,469M
  • NOPAT at 21% tax = $6,691M
  • Invested capital = debt $34,580M + equity $13,904M − cash $12,487M = $35,997M
  • ROIC ≈ 6,691 ÷ 35,997 = 18.6%

Against a discount rate of ~9%, ROIC of 18.6% comfortably exceeds the cost of capital — this is a value-creating business (Ch. 11). And note invested capital is inflated by ~$18B of goodwill from decades of acquisitions; on tangible capital, ROIC would be far higher (30%+). The franchise is real.

ROE = 45.5% (equity atlas) — but read it with the leverage warning (Ch. 6). That eye-popping ROE is partly HON earning ~$6.8B on a treasury-stock-shrunken $14B equity base. It is a good sign (buybacks below-value concentrate ownership) but it also flatters — the denominator is small because of debt-funded repurchases, not because the business needs no capital.

The moat. HON's durable advantages, segment by segment:
- Aerospace: the deepest moat — certified, spec'd-in equipment with 20-40 year installed-base aftermarket annuities (razor/blade). Switching costs are enormous (recertification). Pricing power is high. Competitors (RTX, Safran, GE) are few.
- Building & Industrial Automation + UOP: installed-base + software (Honeywell Forge), high switching costs in process controls; UOP's process-technology licensing is a genuine IP moat in refining.
- Pricing power test (Ch. 5): in 2025, +4% price contributed to the +8% sales growth (10-K sales bridge) — HON raised prices materially without losing volume (volume was still +3%). That is a moat.

10-year erosion risks: (1) commoditization / new entrants in Industrial Automation (management flags this in risk factors); (2) the aerospace aftermarket boom normalizing; (3) the breakup destroying scale: two smaller companies carry duplicated corporate cost and lose cross-segment purchasing power; (4) goodwill from the acquisition spree impairing (as $724M just did). Goodwill note: the balance sheet carries ~$18B of goodwill; the 2025 impairment is a live reminder that acquisitive "growth" can reverse.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

An independent research service grades Financial Strength A+ and Safety 1 — its top marks. Let's verify with the Manual's five questions (Ch. 8).

  • Leverage. Gross debt ~$36.7B (Q1 2026), cash ~$12.0B → net debt ~$24.7B. Against owner earnings of ~$7.3B, that is net debt / OE ≈ 3.4× — a few years of cash flow clears it. Debt/OCF from the atlas is 4.5×. Elevated (the pre-separation $22B financing pushed it up) but manageable for a stable industrial. Caveat: much of that new debt travels to Aerospace at the split, so post-separation each entity's leverage will be lower than the combined snapshot suggests.
  • Coverage. EBIT $8,127M ÷ interest $1,344M (2025) = 6.0×. Comfortable. Even with rising interest post-financing, a normal year covers the bill many times over.
  • Liquidity. Current assets $30,387M vs. current liabilities $23,414M (12/31/25) → current ratio 1.30. Adequate — Graham would want more, but for a cash-generative franchise with $12B cash it is sound.
  • Refinancing wall. $4.6B commercial paper (3.71% weighted rate) + $3.1B current maturities. HON is investment-grade with capital-markets access; the March 2026 $22B raise proves it can tap markets at scale. The separation is the near-term financing event, now essentially complete.
  • Hidden obligations. Pension is a positive: assets $20.7B vs. obligation $15.4B — over-funded by ~$5.3B (an independent research service), a source of income, not a drain. Operating-lease rentals ~$263M/yr (small). Asbestos liabilities were divested in September 2025 — a genuine de-risking. Environmental (Superfund) remains but is reserved.

One-line verdict: FORTRESS — A+ balance sheet, over-funded pension, asbestos off the books; the only asterisk is the temporary leverage bulge from separation financing, which largely departs with Aerospace.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Capital allocation across the six uses (Ch. 9). In 2025 HON deployed ~$10.0B: capex, dividends, buybacks, and M&A. Scoring them:

  • Reinvest: modest capex (~$1B) at high incremental ROIC — good.
  • Acquire: aggressive — Sundyne $2.2B, CAES, Access Solutions, LNG, plus pending Johnson Matthey $1.8B. Here is the institutional-imperative risk (Ch. 9): a serial acquirer that just took a $724M goodwill impairment. Discipline is unproven-to-mixed. The Manual's caution about acquisitions being "frequently value-destroying" applies.
  • Pay down debt: deferred in favor of the separation raise.
  • Dividends: raised for the 16th time in 15 years; 2026 dividend ~$4.78/share, ~2.0% yield, payout ~45% of earnings — sustainable.
  • Repurchase shares: consistent. Share count fell from 760.8M (2016) → 649.8M (2024) → 633.7M (Q1 2026) — a ~17% reduction in a decade (XBRL, verified). Management states a commitment to reduce share count ≥1%/year. 2025 buybacks were $3.8B.

The one-dollar test (Ch. 9). Over the decade, retained earnings + buybacks translated into ~6% EPS growth and a stock that compounded well — each retained dollar plausibly created ≥$1 of value on the organic business. But the goodwill impairment shows M&A dollars did not all clear the bar. Verdict: passes on the core, mixed on M&A.

Buybacks on price vs. IV. HON bought back heavily in 2025 at prices ($180-$240) that, as we will show, sit near or below our IV base — so recent buybacks look value-neutral-to-accretive, not reckless. Contrast the Dexter lesson (Ch. 9): HON has largely used cash, not undervalued stock, for buybacks — the right currency.

Candor. Management reports the impairments and the messy Q4 plainly in the MD&A; the separation risk factors are unusually candid about dis-synergies and execution risk. Reasonable candor.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

We anchor on normalized owner earnings ≈ $7,330M (~$11.57/share) and normalized EPS $10.67 (2025) / $10.55 (2026E). For a stable, high-quality, predictable industrial mid-breakup, we lean on a normalized-earnings multiple as the steadiest estimator, cross-checked by a two-stage DCF and a sum-of-the-parts, then triangulated.

Method 1 — Normalized-earnings multiple

Where should the multiple come from? Three independent roads (Ch. 14 says show each and reconcile):

  1. Historical median P/E. an independent research service's trailing median P/E for HON is 21.0 (2026-04-03 report), and the decade shows P/Es of 17-23. HON has earned a ~20-21× multiple through the cycle.
  2. Peer/industry multiple. Quality aerospace/industrial peers (RTX, GE Aerospace, Eaton, Emerson) trade ~20-25× forward earnings in mid-2026; the broad VL median P/E is 18.2. HON sits mid-pack.
  3. Quality-justified multiple from first principles. A business with 18.6% ROIC, ~6% durable growth, and A+ strength, discounted at 9%, mathematically supports ~18-22× owner earnings (we confirm this with the DCF's implied multiple below).

Reconciled multiple: 18-21×. Applied to 2026E EPS of $10.55:
- 18× → $190
- 20× → $211
- 21× → $222

Method 2 — Two-stage DCF (Ch. 10)

Inputs, conservative by construction: starting OE/share $11.57; explicit growth 6%/yr for 10 years (below the recent boom, reflecting post-split maturity); terminal growth 2.5-3.0% (bounded below r and below nominal GDP); discount rate r = 9% (30-yr Treasury 4.86% + ~4pt premium for cyclicality and separation uncertainty).

r ↓ / g → g = 2.5% g = 3.0%
8.5% $258 $274
9.0% $238 $250
9.5% $220 $230

The DCF centers around $230-$250 — richer than the multiple, because the 6% growth assumption compounds. Being conservative, we weight the DCF's lower half.

Method 3 — Sum-of-the-parts (because the separation matters)

The market price already embeds both pieces (an owner receives HONA in the spin). A rough SOTP:
- Aerospace ($17.5B sales, ~$1.5B net income, ~24.5% margins, best moat) at a premium ~20-22× net income → ~$30-33B, or ~$47-52/share.
- Automation Honeywell (BA + IA + ESS, ~$19.9B sales, ~$4.4B segment profit, ~$3.5-4.0B net income) at ~18-20× → ~$70-78B, or ~$110-123/share.
- Combined equity ~$100-111B, or ~$158-175/share of operating value before the pension over-funding (+$5B) and net of ~$25B net debt already subtracted in the equity figures.

The SOTP lands lower than the DCF and near the low end of the multiple method — a healthy disagreement that says: don't over-pay for the breakup narrative. SOTP argues the pieces are worth perhaps $175-210 on conservative multiples; the market's $230 already prices in successful execution and multiple re-rating.

The three cross-checks (Ch. 14), as arithmetic

1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r = $7,330M ÷ 0.09 = $81.4B, or ~$128/share. At today's $229.86, ~$102 (44%) of the price is a bet on growth. That is a meaningful growth premium — appropriate for a franchise, but it means the thesis leans on growth materializing.

2. Implied multiple = value ÷ OE. Our base IV of ~$220 ÷ $11.57 OE = ~19× owner earnings. Is 19× defensible for an 18.6%-ROIC, 6%-growth, A+ franchise? Yes — it sits right at HON's own historical median. The DCF and the multiple agree, which is the reassurance we want (translating one into the other caught no runaway terminal value).

3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond. At $229.86, OE yield = $11.57 ÷ $229.86 = 5.03%, and that coupon grows ~6%. Against the 30-year Treasury at 4.86% (a static coupon), a 5.0% growing yield is a modestly superior claim — but only modestly. The gravity of a ~4.9% long bond (Ch. 12) is pulling all valuations down; at this price HON offers a thin cushion over bonds.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

  • Live price (equity_atlas.xlsx, 2026-07-04): $229.86
  • IV base: $220 → the price is a ~4-5% premium to base, arithmetic: (229.86 − 220) ÷ 220 = +4.5%.
  • IV low $195 → price is +18% above the conservative floor.
  • IV high $245 → price is 6% below the optimistic end.

Margin of Safety = (IV − Price) ÷ IV. At base: (220 − 229.86)/220 = −4.5% — a negative margin of safety.

There is no margin of safety at $229.86. Mr. Market, having watched the aerospace-spin catalyst approach, has bid the stock from below $200 in January to a record ~$230 (an independent research service's April note: "the quote is now 20% higher and approaching record levels. Accordingly, some of the luster is lost"). Mr. Market is in an optimistic, catalyst-chasing mood — pricing the successful breakup and continued aerospace boom before they are proven. The Manual is emphatic (Ch. 13): if value is only slightly above — or here, below — price, we are not interested in buying. Safety would sit around $175-185 (a 15-20% discount to base); that is where the disciplined buyer waits.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: GOOD, edging toward wonderful — a genuine franchise (18.6% ROIC, pricing power, A+ balance sheet, 95 predictability), held back from "wonderful" by serial-acquisition risk and the self-inflicted uncertainty of a mid-flight breakup.

The call: a wonderful-enough business at a full-to-rich price — ADMIRE, DO NOT BUY here. Not "avoid" (the business is too good and the quote is not absurd), but the price offers no cushion. Following the Manual exactly: below the conservative range you buy in size; at or above it you admire and wait. At $229.86 we wait.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION: Will the two post-split companies sustain ~24% margins and mid-single-digit growth? Probably yes for Aerospace (deep aftermarket moat), less certain for the automation parent, and the combined entity faces real dis-synergy risk. Because the answer is "probably, not certainly," and the price already assumes "certainly," the risk/reward is unattractive today.

Two or three open questions to keep watching:
1. Post-separation, what are the stand-alone margins and corporate costs of each entity — do dis-synergies show up?
2. Does the acquisition machine (Johnson Matthey pending) create or destroy value — more impairments, or accretion?
3. As the aerospace aftermarket boom matures, does organic growth fade toward GDP?

Universal lessons from this case:
- A catalyst is not a margin of safety. The market paid up 20% for the spin; you make your return by buying before the crowd sees the catalyst, at a discount to conservative value — not by chasing it to a record high.
- Normalize ruthlessly. HON's GAAP 2025 was a $4.7B mess of impairments and gains; its normal earning power was ~$6.8B. Capitalizing the wrong figure would have mis-valued the company by 40%.
- Read the balance sheet before the income statement, and never anchor to book. HON's $14B equity would fool you into thinking it small; $44B of treasury stock tells the real story of a franchise that has returned enormous capital.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with HON's numbers)

Intrinsic Value — discounted value of all future owner earnings, IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. HON: triangulated to $195/$220/$245 per share.

Owner Earnings = Net income + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. HON: 6,830 + 1,500 − 850 − 150 = $7,330M ≈ $11.57/sh. Runs above GAAP EPS because acquisition-intangible amortization exceeds maintenance capex.

Maintenance capex — the capex to hold competitive position/volume, vs. growth capex. HON: ~$850M of ~$1.0B total; the analyst's judgmental split.

No-growth (earning-power) value = OE ÷ r = 7,330 ÷ 0.09 = $81.4B ≈ $128/sh — the floor; ~44% of today's price sits above it as a growth bet.

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital = 6,691 ÷ 35,997 = 18.6%. Exceeds r (9%), so growth creates value. Understated by ~$18B goodwill; tangible ROIC far higher.

ROE = Net income ÷ Equity = 45.5% (equity atlas). Flattered by a treasury-stock-shrunken $14B equity base — read alongside leverage.

NOPAT — after-tax operating profit = normalized EBIT $8,469M × (1−0.21) = $6,691M.

Invested Capital = debt + equity − cash = 34,580 + 13,904 − 12,487 = $35,997M.

Discount rate (r) — opportunity cost, anchored to long rates. HON: 30-yr Treasury 4.86% + ~4pt premium = 9%.

g = Reinvestment Rate × ROIC — sustainable internal growth. HON reinvests a minority of earnings at 18.6% ROIC, funding ~6% growth used in the DCF.

Interest Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest = 8,127 ÷ 1,344 = 6.0×. Comfortable.

Net Debt / Owner Earnings = (Debt − Cash) ÷ OE = (36,700 − 12,000) ÷ 7,330 ≈ 3.4×. Elevated by separation financing that largely departs with Aerospace.

Margin of Safety = (IV − Price) ÷ IV. HON at base: (220 − 229.86)/220 = −4.5% — none at today's price. Buy zone ~$175-185.

Moat / pricing power — HON raised price +4% in 2025 while volume rose +3%: pricing power confirmed, especially in the aerospace aftermarket annuity.

Sum-of-the-parts — valuing Aerospace and Automation-Honeywell separately and summing; here ~$175-210/sh conservatively, a check on the consolidated DCF.

Mr. Market — the manic partner. Today: optimistic, having bid HON to a ~$230 record chasing the aerospace-spin catalyst. His pocketbook, not his wisdom, is useful (Ch. 2).
