OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Buffett: If you want to dig a copper mine in Chile, build a highway in Texas, or power an off-grid hospital, you are likely using Caterpillar. They are the world’s largest manufacturer of construction and mining equipment, diesel and natural gas engines, and industrial gas turbines (10-K, Item 1). They organize into three primary manufacturing segments: Construction Industries, Resource Industries (mining), and Energy & Transportation (engines/turbines), plus a Financial Products division that helps customers buy the iron.

The central question for this case is this: Is Caterpillar a permanently durable franchise whose economics can be forecast, and does today's price reflect the cyclical reality of heavy industry, or is it hallucinating a secular super-growth trend?

Let’s apply our Circle of Competence gate. Can we understand this business? Yes. They make physical machines that perform essential economic tasks. Their competitive position is visible, and the capital cycle of their end markets—mining, construction, and energy—is well documented. It sits squarely in our circle. But just because we understand the business does not mean we are blind to its cyclicality.

PRINCIPLE: A stock is a piece of a business, and you only value what you can understand. But understanding a business includes knowing whether its earnings are steady or violently cyclical.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Graham: To read Caterpillar’s statements correctly, you must recognize you are looking at two very different businesses stapled together: an industrial manufacturer ("Machinery, Power & Energy" or MP&E) and a bank ("Financial Products"). Consolidated figures blend them, which obscures the truth. Fortunately, management provides Supplemental Consolidating Data (10-K, Item 8), which strips them apart. We will evaluate the industrial engine (MP&E) on its own merits.

Let’s trace the income statement. In 2025, consolidated revenues were $67,589 million (10-K, Item 8, Statement 1). From that, they generated a consolidated net income of $8,884 million for common shareholders. But look at the cash. Consolidated net cash provided by operating activities was $11,739 million (10-K, Item 8, Statement 5).

Now, let's isolate the real industrial cash flow. The MP&E segment generated $12,278 million in operating cash flow (10-K, Item 8, Supplemental Cash Flow). Why is it higher than consolidated? Because the captive finance arm consumes cash to originate loans. MP&E’s capital expenditures (excluding equipment leased to others) were $2,758 million, plus $36 million in expenditures for leased equipment, giving total MP&E CapEx of $2,794 million (10-K, Supplemental Cash Flow).

Let's test the Earnings Quality. MP&E operating cash flow ($12,278 million) covers MP&E net income ($8,640 million) effortlessly. The profits are real, backed by cash, not paper accounting gains.

We look at book value, but we never anchor to it for a franchise. Caterpillar's consolidated shareholders' equity sits at $21,318 million at the end of 2025 (10-K, Item 8, Statement 3). Generating $8,884 million on a $20,406 million average equity base yields an astonishing 43.5% Return on Equity (ROE = $8,884 million ÷ $20,406 million). This tells us the accounting book value drastically understates the economic goodwill of the business.

PRINCIPLE: The statements are evidence, and cash is the truth-teller. Always decouple captive finance divisions from industrial operations to see the true cash generation of the core business.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Buffett: What protects those returns? Why can Caterpillar charge a premium for a bulldozer? The answer is their dealer network. Caterpillar sells through an independent global dealer network—41 in the U.S. and 109 abroad, serving 190 countries (10-K, Item 1). If a mining truck breaks down, it costs the operator thousands of dollars an hour. Caterpillar's dealers provide parts and service faster and more reliably than anyone else on earth. That is a wide, durable economic moat built on scale, network density, and brand trust.

Let's calculate the Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) for the MP&E segment to prove the moat mathematically. * MP&E Operating Profit = $10,884 million (10-K, Supplemental Results of Operations). * Consolidated effective tax rate = 24.0% ($2,768 million provision ÷ $11,541 million pre-tax profit). * NOPAT = $10,884 million × (1 - 0.24) = $8,272 million. * MP&E Total Assets = $60,061 million. * MP&E Operating Current Liabilities = $22,667 million current liabilities − $35 million short-term debt = $22,632 million. * Invested Capital = $60,061 million − $22,632 million = $37,429 million.

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital = $8,272 million ÷ $37,429 million = 22.1%.

Any capital-intensive manufacturer earning 22% on its capital over the cycle is a spectacular business. But it is cyclical. To calculate Owner Earnings, we must normalize. If we look at the XBRL history and an independent research service (2026-05-08), EPS swung from $6.09 in 2020 to $13.87 in 2022, to $21.90 in 2024, settling at $19.06 in 2025.

Let's find the 2025 MP&E free cash flow: * MP&E Operating Cash Flow = $12,278 million * Less MP&E CapEx = $2,794 million * MP&E Free Cash Flow = $9,484 million

With 472.3 million diluted shares outstanding (10-K, Statement 1), 2025 owner earnings per share = $9,484 million ÷ 472.3 million = $20.08.

However, we are clearly sitting near the peak of a cycle, driven by infrastructure spending and recent data center build-outs. We must normalize before we capitalize. Let's look at the 5-year average EPS (2021-2025): ($10.81 + $13.87 + $21.21 + $21.90 + $19.06) ÷ 5 = $17.37. To be safe, yet recognize structural growth, we will anchor on a single, normalized Owner Earnings figure of $17.50 per share. We will use this exact figure for every valuation step.

PRINCIPLE: Durable earning power, defended by a moat and proven by returns on capital above the cost of capital, is what you are really buying. For a cyclical, you must normalize through the cycle rather than capitalizing the peak.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Graham: A great business financed recklessly can still be ruined. Let’s interrogate the MP&E balance sheet (10-K, Supplemental Financial Position) to separate the industrial risk from the bank's leverage: * MP&E Cash: $9,333 million. * MP&E Debt: $35 million (due within one year) + $10,955 million (long-term) = $10,990 million total debt. * Net Debt: $10,990 million − $9,333 million = $1,657 million.

Let’s check the leverage and coverage ratios: * Leverage: Net Debt ÷ Normalized Owner Earnings = $1,657 million ÷ ($17.50 × 472.3 million shares = $8,265 million) = 0.20 years. It would take a mere two and a half months of normal free cash flow to pay off every cent of net debt. * Coverage: MP&E Operating Profit ÷ MP&E Interest Expense = $10,884 million ÷ $516 million = 21.1x.

Verdict: Fortress. The industrial business carries virtually no net debt and covers its interest bill 21 times over. It can survive any cyclical trough.

PRINCIPLE: Solvency is the precondition that lets every other quality compound. Verify staying power before you trust any stream of earnings to persist.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Buffett: We judge management by what they do with the cash. In 2025, MP&E generated $9,484 million in free cash flow. How did they allocate it? * They paid $2,749 million in dividends (10-K, Supplemental Cash Flow). * They spent $5,190 million repurchasing 14.1 million shares of common stock (10-K, Statement 4).

Total capital returned to owners: $2,749 million + $5,190 million = $7,939 million. They returned nearly 84% of their free cash flow directly to us. Look at the share count: it has dropped from 586.5 million in 2016 to 465.3 million in 2025 (XBRL/10-K)—a nearly 20% reduction.

The One-Dollar Test is passed with flying colors; every dollar retained over the last decade has produced far more than a dollar of market value. My only caution is that repurchasing stock at euphoric peak valuations destroys value. Management must have the discipline to halt buybacks when the price exceeds intrinsic value.

PRINCIPLE: Over time you ride with management's capital allocation, so judge it by the one-dollar test and their treatment of the share count, not the press release.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Graham: Intrinsic value is the discounted value of future owner earnings. We will triangulate using the methods this mature, capital-intensive business deserves: a justified multiple and a two-stage DCF, both anchored exclusively on our $17.50 normalized owner earnings.

Method 1: Normalized Earnings Multiple
Historically, Caterpillar's median P/E ratio is 17.0x (an independent research service, 2026-05-08). A wide-moat business generating 22% ROIC and steady cyclical growth can justify an 18x multiple. * Base Value = $17.50 × 18 = $315 per share * Low Value = $17.50 × 16 = $280 per share * High Value = $17.50 × 22 = $385 per share

Method 2: Two-Stage DCF
Let’s act as private owners. We require a 9% discount rate (r), given a 4.5% risk-free rate plus a standard equity risk premium. We will assume 5% growth (g) for 10 years, and a 3% terminal growth rate. * Year 1: $17.50 × 1.05 ÷ 1.09 = $16.86 * Year 2: $17.50 × (1.05^2) ÷ (1.09^2) = $16.24 * Sum of Years 1–10 PV = $143.29 * Terminal Value at Year 10 = (Year 10 CF × 1.03) ÷ (0.09 - 0.03) = ($28.51 × 1.03) ÷ 0.06 = $489.33 * PV of Terminal Value = $489.33 ÷ (1.09^10) = $489.33 ÷ 2.367 = $206.70 * Total DCF Base Value = $143.29 + $206.70 = $350 per share

DCF Sensitivity (Discount Rate vs. 10-Yr Growth):
| Growth g | r = 8% | r = 9% | r = 10% |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| 4% | $364 | $305 | $262 |
| 5% | $423 | $350 | $298 |
| 6% | $508 | $409 | $342 |

Triangulated Base Value: We will average our justified multiple ($315) and our base DCF ($350) to arrive at a central intrinsic value of $335 per share. The range is roughly $280 to $385.

The Universal Cross-Checks:
1. No-Growth Floor: $17.50 ÷ 0.09 = $194.44. Any price above this is a speculative bet on growth.
2. Implied Multiple: Today's live price is $952.41 (equity_atlas.xlsx). $952.41 ÷ $17.50 = 54.4x.
3. Owner-Earnings Yield: $17.50 ÷ $952.41 = 1.84%.

PRINCIPLE: Estimate value with whatever method the business makes most reliable, triangulate, and trust an honest range over a false point. The cross-checks will prevent you from lying to yourself.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Buffett: Here is where the lesson gets profound. An independent research service and the broader market are wildly euphoric about Caterpillar right now. The live quote is $952.41 (equity_atlas.xlsx).

Let's calculate the margin of safety against our base value of $335: * Premium = ($952.41 − $335) ÷ $335 = 1.843, or a 184% premium to intrinsic value. * Margin of Safety = ($335 − $952.41) ÷ $335 = -184%.

Why has Mr. Market lost his mind? Read the Selection & Opinion report (2026-07-10) and the an independent research service company tear sheet: "Massive spending on artificial intelligence (AI) played a significant role... The artificial intelligence growth story remains intact, and increasing orders from that category... including solar turbines used to build and maintain data centers."

Mr. Market has looked at Caterpillar—a company that digs dirt and builds highways—and priced it like a frictionless software stock because its backup generators are being sold to data centers. At $952, investors are paying 54 times normalized earning power for a cyclical industrial. The Owner-Earnings yield of 1.84% is less than half the yield of a risk-free 10-year Treasury bond. This is the definition of a bubble.

PRINCIPLE: Insist on a cushion; use Mr. Market, never obey him. When the market prices a cyclical company as a secular growth miracle, step away and let others hold the bag.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Graham: Let us return to our opening question. Caterpillar is a wonderful, enduring franchise with a spectacular moat and a fortress balance sheet. But the stock is a screaming AVOID at $952.41.

The margin of safety is violently negative. We would become interested only if the price fell below $280.

The Universal Lessons of this Case:
1. Quality justifies a fair price, never ANY price. A 22% ROIC and a wide moat are marvelous, but paying 54x earnings for them guarantees a permanent loss of capital when the cycle eventually turns.
2. Beware the halo effect of market fads. When investors attach an "AI" narrative to a heavy-equipment manufacturer, they extrapolate temporary windfalls into infinity. The arithmetic of the no-growth floor ($194) tells you exactly how much of the $952 price tag is pure speculation.

This is a teaching case, not personalized investment advice.

THE VALUATION CHECKLIST

  • Circle of Competence? PASS. The economics of heavy equipment are understandable.
  • Durable Moat? PASS. The global dealer network is virtually impossible to replicate.
  • High Returns on Capital? PASS. 22.1% ROIC over the cycle.
  • Trustworthy Financials/Management? PASS. Excellent cash generation and shareholder return.
  • Strong Balance Sheet? PASS. Industrial net debt is trivial; interest coverage is 21x.
  • Anchored Valuation? PASS. Triangulated around a normalized $17.50 owner earnings figure.
  • Margin of Safety? FAIL. At $952.41 against a $335 value, the stock trades at an extreme premium.

Post-Purchase Discipline: Since we are passing, there is no holding discipline to apply. We simply add it to our watchlist and wait for Mr. Market to swing from euphoria to depression.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

  • Owner earnings: The cash a passive owner could withdraw without harming the business. Formula: Operating cash flow − maintenance capital expenditures. (CAT 2025 MP&E: $12,278M − $2,794M = $9,484M).
  • Return on invested capital (ROIC): Measures how efficiently a company uses capital to generate profits. Formula: NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital. (CAT: $8,272M ÷ $37,429M = 22.1%).
  • NOPAT (net operating profit after tax): The cash earnings if the company had no debt. Formula: Operating income × (1 − tax rate). (CAT: $10,884M × (1 − 0.24) = $8,272M).
  • Invested capital: The cash actually put into the business by owners and lenders. Formula: Total assets − operating current liabilities. (CAT: $60,061M − $22,632M = $37,429M).
  • Return on equity (ROE): Net income generated per dollar of shareholders' equity. Formula: Net income ÷ average shareholders' equity. (CAT: $8,884M ÷ $20,406M = 43.5%).
  • Interest coverage: A measure of solvency. Formula: Operating income ÷ interest expense. (CAT: $10,884M ÷ $516M = 21.1x).
  • Net debt: Total debt minus cash. (CAT MP&E: $10,990M − $9,333M = $1,657M).
  • Cost of capital / Discount rate (r): The hurdle rate required by an investor, acting as "gravity" on valuation. Here, assumed at 9.0%.
  • Intrinsic value: The discounted value of all future owner earnings. Formula: Σ OE_t ÷ (1 + r)^t.
  • Margin of safety: The discount of price to value, protecting against errors. Formula: (Intrinsic value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic value. (CAT: ($335 − $952) ÷ $335 = -184%).
  • P/E ratio: Price-to-Earnings. Formula: Price ÷ EPS. (CAT Live: $952.41 ÷ $17.50 = 54.4x).
  • Two-stage DCF: A valuation model forecasting explicit cash flows for a set period (e.g., 10 years) plus a terminal value for all years after.
  • Terminal value: The value of the business beyond the explicit forecast period.
  • No-growth value: The baseline value if the business never grew again. Formula: Normalized owner earnings ÷ r. (CAT: $17.50 ÷ 0.09 = $194.44).
  • Owner-earnings yield: The actual cash return you get on the purchase price. Formula: Owner earnings ÷ Price. (CAT: $17.50 ÷ $952.41 = 1.84%).
  • Economic moat: A structural advantage protecting the business from competitors (e.g., CAT's global dealer network).
  • Normalized earnings: Earning power adjusted for the boom-and-bust cycle. (CAT normalized at $17.50 vs actual peak of $20.08).
  • One-dollar test: A check of capital allocation; every dollar of retained earnings should create at least one dollar of market value over time.
