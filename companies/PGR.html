THE PROGRESSIVE CORPORATION (NYSE: PGR)

A Value Investing Seminar — Graham & Buffett at the Lectern

July 1, 2026

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

GRAHAM: Class, today's exhibit is not a factory or a software house. It is a company whose product is a promise: The Progressive Corporation, of Mayfield Village, Ohio, the second-largest private-passenger auto insurer in the United States and the largest commercial auto insurer since 2015 (10-K item1, business parts 1 and 3). Progressive collects premiums from roughly 39.6 million policies in force (10-Q, MD&A) — cars, motorcycles, boats, RVs, small-business vehicles, and a modest book of homes — and pays claims when misfortune arrives. In 2025 it wrote $83.2 billion of net premiums, 87% of them Personal Lines and 13% Commercial Lines (Schedule III).

BUFFETT: And here is what makes an insurer a wonderful thing to own when it is run well — and a terrible thing otherwise. An insurer gets paid before it delivers. Premiums come in today; claims go out months or years later. In between, the company holds and invests money that belongs, in an economic sense, to its policyholders. We call that money float. At year-end 2025, Progressive's loss reserves of $43.3 billion plus unearned premiums of $25.2 billion, less the premiums it hadn't yet collected ($15.4B), deferred acquisition costs ($2.0B), and reinsurance recoverables ($4.1B), give float of roughly:

$43,310M + $25,219M − $15,362M − $2,044M − $4,083M ≈ $47 billion of float

If the underwriting merely breaks even, that $47 billion is an interest-free loan. If underwriting is profitable — as Progressive's reliably is — the company is paid to hold other people's money. The manual's Appendix A gives the equation: Cost of Float ≈ Underwriting Loss ÷ Average Float. Progressive's 2025 underwriting profit was about $9.1 billion, so its cost of float was approximately negative 19%. My friends, I have spent sixty years looking for that arrangement.

GRAHAM: Two segments, two channels. Personal Lines (auto, special lines, a small property book) sells through more than 40,000 independent agencies (43% of personal-vehicle premium) and, increasingly, direct over the internet and phone (57%, up from 54% two years ago) (item1 part1). Commercial Lines is mostly small-fleet commercial auto, plus insurance for Uber's subsidiaries in 14 states — 14% of that segment's premium, a customer concentration worth noting (item1 part1).

The one question that will decide this case: In 2025 Progressive earned a combined ratio around 89 — far better than its own stated through-cycle target of 96 — at the very top of an insurance pricing cycle that is now visibly softening. Do you value the peak, or do you value the cycle? Answer that honestly and the valuation nearly writes itself.

THE CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE GATE (manual ch. 4). Can we forecast this business a decade out? Auto insurance is among the more knowable businesses on the exchange: the product doesn't change (a legally required promise to pay), demand is non-discretionary, the accounting is old and well-policed, and the company's own history gives us seventy years of loss data. The genuinely hard parts are two: the pricing cycle (hard and soft markets, discernible "only in retrospect," as the 10-K itself admits — item1a part2) and the long-horizon question of autonomous vehicles, which the risk factors flag: technology that makes driving safer shrinks the premium pool (item1a part2). The first we can handle by normalizing — that is precisely what this seminar will teach. The second is real but slow; even optimists put widespread autonomy decades from materially shrinking the insured fleet, and Progressive insures the transition (people still crash semi-autonomous cars; someone must insure robotaxis, and Progressive already insures Uber). We judge this case inside the circle — with the honest caveat that our terminal-value assumptions must stay humble because of that second question.

PRINCIPLE: A stock is a piece of a business, and you only value what you can understand. An insurer is a knowable business if you understand that its true product is underwriting discipline and its hidden asset is float — and if you can say out loud which parts of the future you cannot see.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

GRAHAM: Open the 10-Q for the quarter ended March 31, 2026 — the freshest full set of statements — and let us read them as evidence.

The balance sheet (10-Q, Consolidated Balance Sheets)

What it owns: $94.1 billion of investments plus modest cash — against total assets of $122.2 billion. An insurer is its investment portfolio with an underwriting operation attached. Look at the quality of that portfolio (10-Q Note 2 and MD&A IV):

  • 47.2% U.S. government securities; weighted average credit quality AA−; duration 3.5 years (management's allowed range is 1.5–5.0).
  • Common equities just 4.2% of the portfolio ($3.9B) — and indexed to the Russell 1000, not stock-picked.
  • Pretax recurring book yield: 4.2%.

This is a deliberately boring portfolio, and that is a compliment. The risk-taking in this company happens in underwriting, where it has an edge, not in the bond market, where it has none. Note also what is absent: no goodwill of any consequence, no intangibles built by acquisition, no pension plan (an independent research service: "No Defined Pension Benefit Plan"). The judgment-laden account here is a different one — loss reserves, $44.4 billion gross — management's estimate of claims already incurred. We will test it below.

What it owes: unearned premiums of $27.9B (premiums collected for coverage not yet delivered), those loss reserves, and debt of just $8.4 billion against equity of $32.0 billion:

Debt-to-total-capital = $8,386M ÷ ($8,386M + $32,039M) = 20.7% (10-Q, MD&A II.A; policy ceiling 30%)

Book value per share: $32,039M ÷ 584.3M shares = $54.86.

BUFFETT: And remember the manual's warning: never anchor value to book — but for an insurer, book is at least meaningful, because the assets are marked-to-market securities, not rusting mills. Progressive's economic goodwill — its brand, its pricing data, its 39.6 million customer relationships — sits entirely outside that $54.86. That is why this company has commanded 3 to 5 times book for a decade (an independent research service array: price-to-book 213% in 2010 rising to 517% in 2024).

The income statement (10-Q p.1; Schedule III for FY2025)

The march from revenue to profit, Q1 2026: net premiums earned $20,968M + investment income $917M + fees/service $423M − securities losses $120M = revenues $22,188M. Then losses and loss-adjustment expenses $13,827M, acquisition costs $1,538M, other underwriting expenses $3,048M, interest $70M, tax $748M → net income $2,818M, diluted EPS $4.80.

An insurer's margin is read through the combined ratio — losses plus expenses, per dollar of premium earned. Below 100 means underwriting at a profit:

Q1 2026: loss ratio 65.9 + expense ratio 20.5 = combined ratio 86.4 (10-Q MD&A III.B) — an underwriting margin of 13.6 cents per premium dollar, against the company's own 4-cent (96 CR) calendar-year commitment.
FY2025, derived from Schedule III: ($53,959M losses + $6,096M DAC amortization + $12,553M operating expenses) ÷ $81,661M NPE ≈ 88.9.
The ten-year record (an independent research service array, underwriting margin line): 4.9%, 6.6%, 10.6%, 9.1%, 12.3%, 4.6%, 3.5%, 4.9%, 11.2%, ~12.9% — average ≈ 8%, i.e., a through-cycle combined ratio near 92, never above 96.5 in a decade.

Mark that: the worst year of the last ten (2022, the inflation shock in used-car and injury costs) still produced an underwriting profit. State Farm, GEICO, and Allstate all lost money underwriting auto in that stretch. This is the single most important exhibit in the case.

The cash flow statement (10-Q p.4; XBRL for history)

Operating cash flow in 2025: $17.5 billion against net income of $11.3 billion (XBRL). Cash exceeds profit year after year ($15.1B vs $8.5B in 2024; $10.6B vs $3.9B in 2023) — the signature of a float business: growing premiums arrive as cash before the accountant may call them earned. Capital expenditure is a rounding error — $63M of property additions in Q1 2026 against $4.4B of operating cash flow. This business converts nearly all of its earnings to cash and needs almost none of it for machinery. Its real "capex" is regulatory capital — surplus to support premium growth — and we will treat that properly in the owner-earnings section.

How the three statements articulate

Net income (+$2,818M) flows to retained earnings; the January payment of the previously declared $7,972M dividend (sitting on the year-end balance sheet as "dividends payable") drained short-term investments from $10.0B to $2.1B; the $574M after-tax mark-down of bonds bypassed the income statement into OCI, so comprehensive income was $2,244M. Now a puzzle to test you: equity rose in the very quarter an $8 billion dividend went out the door — $30,323M + $2,818M net income − $574M OCI − ~$0.5B buybacks ≈ $32,039M. How? Because the dividend was charged against equity in December 2025, when declared, not in January when paid; the quarter's cash outflow had no equity twin. A student who ties the three statements together like this cannot be fooled by either the income statement's serenity or the cash flow statement's violence.

The earnings-quality scan (manual ch. 7)

  1. Securities marks run through net income. Since 2018, GAAP forces unrealized equity gains/losses through the P&L. Strip them: 2022's reported EPS of $1.18 included −$2.90 of capital losses (an independent research service footnote B); the operating business earned about $4.08. Conversely 2020 included +$2.19 of gains. Lesson: for an insurer, always separate underwriting + recurring investment income from market noise.
  2. Reserves — the great judgment account — test conservative. Prior-year development was favorable $451M in Q1 2026 (2.2 points of combined ratio) and favorable $278M a year earlier (10-Q Note 6). Management has been over-reserving, then releasing — the honest direction of error. But note it cuts the other way for valuation: today's sparkling ratios are being flattered by yesterday's conservatism.
  3. No EBITDA anywhere. Progressive's disclosure is famously granular — monthly releases, segment combined ratios to the decimal, aggregate rate changes disclosed. The manual says opacity hides something; the corollary is that this level of candor usually means there is nothing to hide.
  4. One-timers exist and are visible: a $1.2B Florida statutory policyholder credit depressed Q1 2026 operating cash flow (10-Q liquidity discussion) — a genuine one-off, disclosed plainly.

PRINCIPLE: The statements are evidence and cash is the truth-teller. In an insurer, the truth-teller's two assistants are the combined ratio (is the product priced to a profit?) and reserve development (were past profits real?). Progressive passes both, with the caveat that current earnings sit far above their own through-cycle norm.

Owner earnings (manual ch. 7) — done the insurer's way

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital. For Progressive, physical capex is trivial (~$250M/yr vs $17.5B operating cash flow). The real subtraction is different: an insurer's "maintenance-plus-growth capex" is the statutory surplus it must retain to support premium growth. Progressive runs at about 2.9:1 premiums-to-surplus (10-K item1 part2: NPW $83.2B against statutory surplus $28.4B). So every $3 of new premium requires roughly $1 of retained surplus:

Grow NPW ~6–7% a year on an $83B base → ~$5–6B of new premium → ~$1.7–2.0B/yr of required surplus retention.

The second adjustment is normalization of the cycle — the heart of this case. Take a 2026-scale premium base (NPE ≈ $86.7B, per an independent research service's estimate and the Q1 run-rate) and vary only the underwriting margin:

Scenario UW margin Pretax (UW + $3.8B inv. inc. + $0.2B fees − $0.29B interest) Net (20.5% tax) EPS (584.3M sh)
At the 96-CR goal (floor) 4% $7.2B $5.7B $9.77
Ten-year average (CR ≈ 92) 8% $10.6B $8.5B $14.48
Modern normal (CR ≈ 91) 9% $11.5B $9.2B $15.66

So: reported 2025 EPS was $19.23–$19.25. Normalized earning power is roughly $14–16 per share, and the contractual floor the company itself promises is about $10. an independent research service's own 2026–27 estimates ($16.30, $16.20) sit at the top of our normalized band — a useful second opinion. Normalized owner earnings (deducting ~$1.8B of growth-surplus retention from the ~$8.5B mid-case) are about $6.5–7B distributable — and 2026 proves the concept: the company just mailed owners an $8.0 billion January dividend because 2025's earnings exceeded the capital the business needed.

The moat — and its evidence

BUFFETT: Auto insurance is a commodity — the policy is identical paper. In a commodity business the only durable moat is being the low-cost, best-priced producer, and that is exactly the moat GEICO taught me to prize. Progressive's version rests on three legs:

  1. Pricing and segmentation skill. Fifty years of specializing in risk selection, "billions of driving miles" of telematics data (Snapshot), and a model cadence competitors cannot match — personal auto model 9.0 already covering ~25% of premium within two quarters of rollout (item1 part1). Better segmentation is a compounding advantage: the insurer that prices risk most accurately wins the good risks and sends the bad ones to rivals — adverse selection runs in its favor.
  2. Direct-channel cost advantage. 57% of personal-vehicle premium now comes direct (item1 part1) — no agent commission — and scale amortizes the country's largest insurance ad budget ($1.5B in a single quarter, 10-Q MD&A) over 39.6M policies. Expense ratio ~20% against legacy captive-agent carriers several points higher: in a commodity, three points of cost is the whole game.
  3. Claims infrastructure at scale — claims handled by ~employees, a 4,700-shop network (item1 part2) — loss-adjustment cost and accuracy advantages.

The proof, as the manual demands, is in returns on capital. For an insurer, invested capital is essentially equity plus debt:

ROIC 2025 = NOPAT ÷ avg invested capital = ($11,308M + $280M interest × 0.795) ÷ avg($32.5B, $37.2B) ≈ $11.5B ÷ $34.9B ≈ 33%
Ten-year average ROE ≈ 25% against a cost of equity near 9%.

Returns nearly three times the cost of capital, persisting for a decade in a market with ~230 competitors (item1 part1) — that is what a real moat looks like in the numbers. Leverage is modest (debt 21% of capital), so ROE is not a leverage trick; and unlike a bank, the "leverage" of float costs less than nothing.

Now the skeptic's turn. What erodes this in ten years? (a) The cycle is turning now: Q1 2026 shows personal-auto rates cut slightly, average written premium per policy down 2%, policy-life-expectancy (retention) down 8%, ad spend up 20% to keep growth — the textbook look of a softening market (10-Q MD&A III.C–D). Margins will compress toward — perhaps below — the long-run norm; our valuation must assume it. (b) Autonomy and vehicle technology shrinking the premium pool over decades — flagged in the company's own risk factors. (c) Regulation — bans on rating factors (credit, occupation) could blunt the segmentation edge (item1a part1); insurance rates are state-approved, so the moat's pricing power is conditional, not absolute — the manual's franchise test ("not subject to price regulation") is only partially met. (d) The property book (4% of Personal Lines NPW) concentrates hurricane/hail exposure and has caused volatility, though reinsurance retention is capped at $200M per event ex-Florida (item1 part1). None of these kills the thesis this decade; all of them belong in the discount we demand.

PRINCIPLE: Durable earning power proven by returns on capital far above their cost is what you are buying. In a commodity business the moat must be cost and information, and its width is measured, not asserted: a decade of ~25% ROE with underwriting profit in every single year is measurement, not marketing.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

BUFFETT: Tricia Griffith has run this company since 2016 — the decade in the table above. Judge her the way the manual instructs, across the five uses of a dollar:

  1. Reinvest in the business — the first and best use: growth-surplus retention funding 14%/yr premium growth at ~25% incremental ROE. The one-dollar test (retain only if incremental ROIC ≥ r): 25% ≥ 9%, passed emphatically — each retained dollar has become roughly four dollars of market value over the decade (equity per share $13.29 → $54.86 while the stock went from ~$31 to ~$220).
  2. Acquisitions — essentially none. No serial goodwill, no empire-building. In an industry where the institutional imperative screams "diversify into life, into Europe, into anything," Progressive has stayed inside its competence for fifty years. ARX (property, 2015) is the exception, candidly a mixed one.
  3. Debt — restrained: 20.7% of capital against a self-imposed 30% ceiling; $1.5B raised in March 2026 at 4.6–5.15% — cheap, term, unforced (10-Q Note 4).
  4. Dividends — the crown jewel of candor. A token $0.10 quarterly, plus an annual variable dividend sized to the capital the business did not need: $4.50/share in early 2025, then $13.50/share — $7.9 billion — paid January 2026 (10-Q Note 10). Read what that mechanism is: management returns the residual rather than hoarding it or torching it on diworsification. It is the one-dollar test institutionalized as policy. (The XBRL history shows the same rhythm: big payouts in 2012, 2014, 2021 after fat years; near-nothing after lean ones.)
  5. Buybacks — priced, not programmatic. Policy: neutralize dilution, buy more only when "under-leveraged" — and Q1 2026 purchases (2.3M shares at avg $204.90) came at ~10.5× trailing earnings, below our value range. Modest in size, right in price. They do not buy back at any price — which the manual counts a virtue, not a failing.

And one more tell, rarer than any of these: they shrink when the price is wrong. Commercial Lines NPW fell 3.1% in 2025 (Schedule III) because rate adequacy came first; the 10-K states the priority in one sentence: "Our focus on achieving our underwriting profitability goal takes precedence over growth" (item1a part2). Gainshare — the all-employee bonus — is tied to growth and profitability jointly, aligning 70,000 people with that sentence.

GRAHAM: I find no promotional accounting, no adjusted-EBITDA theater, and disclosure granular to the point of monthly earnings releases. These managers speak to owners like partners.

PRINCIPLE: Over time you ride with management's capital allocation. The rarest skills are giving money back when the business doesn't need it and refusing growth that doesn't clear the hurdle — Progressive institutionalized both.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

BUFFETT: The master equation — IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ — defines what we're estimating: all the cash owners can take out, discounted. But this is an insurer at a cyclical peak; a single-point DCF on last year's earnings would be malpractice. We will triangulate with the estimators the business deserves: (1) an ROE-justified book multiple (the insurer's native tool), (2) a normalized-earnings multiple, and (3) a two-stage DCF on distributable owner earnings, with its sensitivity shown.

Setting the discount rate (manual ch. 11). Gravity first: the 10-year Treasury is 4.47%, the 30-year ~4.97% (Selection & Opinion, Selected Yields, 6/15/26). Progressive's cash flows are durable but cyclical and carry a slow secular question (autonomy). Long rate + ~4.5 points for uncertainty → r ≈ 9%, tested at 8.5–10%.

Method 1 — ROE-justified price-to-book

A financial business earning ROE above its cost of equity deserves a premium to book of roughly (ROE − g)/(r − g). Book value per share is $54.86 (10-Q).

Conservative: ROE 18%, g 4%, r 9.5% → P/B = (0.18−0.04)/(0.095−0.04) = 2.55× → $140
Base: ROE 22%, g 5%, r 9% → (0.22−0.05)/(0.09−0.05) = 4.25× → $233
The optimistic case computes to 6.8×; we cap our imagination at ~5× — the top of the stock's own historical range (an independent research service: 213%–517%) — → ~$260–275

Method 2 — Normalized earnings power × justified multiple

Normalized EPS from the earnings-power table: $14.50 mid-cycle (CR ≈ 92), $15.50–16.50 if the modern machine is structurally better than its own ten-year average (an independent research service's 2026–27 view). What multiple is deserved? The stock's median P/E is 16 (an independent research service); a 25%-ROE grower with a variable-dividend payout policy deserves at least the median on normalized (not peak) earnings; a skeptic pays 13×.

Low: $14.5 × 13 = $189 Base: $14.5 × 16 = $232 High: $16.3 × 16 = $261

Method 3 — Two-stage DCF on distributable owner earnings

Start from normalized net income ≈ $8.5B, deduct ~$1.8B growth-surplus retention → distributable OE₀ ≈ $6.1B (deliberately conservative: the retained $1.8B also compounds at ~25%, and we credit that only through the growth rate). Grow 7% for ten years (premium growth 6–7% plus margin normalization already taken), terminal g thereafter, discount at r. Per share (584.3M shares):

r \ terminal g 2.0% 2.5% 3.0% 3.5%
8.5% 239 252 267 285
9.0% 221 231 243 258
9.5% 205 213 223 235
10.0% 191 198 206 216

Look at that table the way the manual teaches: one point of discount rate moves the answer ~20%; half a point of terminal growth moves it ~5%. Every cell is defensible; that spread is the lesson. A single "target price" from this machine would be an illusion of precision.

The growth check (manual ch. 10). Progressive's growth is funded at incremental ROE ≈ 25% against r = 9% — growth here creates value, roughly $1.75 of value per dollar retained at the base P/B. This is the opposite of the capital-hungry growth trap; that is why the DCF's 7% growth assumption adds value rather than merely adding size.

The reverse-DCF closer. Ask what today's price already assumes. Market capitalization = $219.59 × 584.3M = $128.3B.

On normalized distributable OE of $6.1B at r = 9%: implied perpetual growth ≈ 4.1%.
On peak 2025 net income of $11.3B: implied perpetual growth ≈ 0.2% — zero.

Mr. Market is saying either "the peak never grows again" or "the normal business grows at 4% forever." Against a decade of 14% premium growth and 9% current policy growth, neither is a heroic demand — but remember, our normalization already gave back a third of peak earnings.

PRINCIPLE: Value is by definition discounted owner earnings, but you estimate it with the tools the business makes reliable — for an insurer, book-times-justified-ROE and normalized earnings — triangulate, sensitize, and trust an honest range ($180–$265, center ~$230) over a false point.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

GRAHAM: Now, and only now, the price. From equity_atlas.xlsx, June 30, 2026, 7:11 a.m.: $219.59. Trailing EPS $19.40 → live P/E 11.3 against a median of 16; price-to-book $219.59 ÷ $54.86 = 4.0×; market cap $128.3B.

Against our range:

vs. base $230: ($230 − $219.59) ÷ $230 = +4.5% discount — negligible.
vs. low $180: price stands 22% above the conservative estimate.
vs. high $265: 17% below.

The manual's rule: Margin of Safety = (IV − Price) ÷ IV, and it must be large. Four and a half percent against the central case is no cushion at all — it is spare change. For a meaningful 30% margin against the base, the buy price is $230 × 0.70 ≈ $161; even a 25% margin says ≈ $172. Note the market visited $191.80 within the past year (an independent research service, 52-week low) — these prices are not fantasies.

What mood is Mr. Market in? A year ago he paid $293 — 15× peak earnings, 5.7× book — euphoric about the hard market. He has since marked the stock down 24% (an independent research service, total return to 5/18/26) while earnings rose, because he now sees what we see: rate cuts, retention slipping, the cycle rolling over. So today he is neither manic nor depressed; he is, unusually, roughly rational — pricing peak earnings as a peak. The optical cheapness (11.3× trailing) is the classic cyclical trap in reverse-mirror: cyclicals look cheapest at the top. On normalized $14.50, the true multiple is 15× — about fair for the quality.

The patient student's assignment: this is a watchlist stock with a price. The business has earned its place; the price has not yet earned your capital. If the soft market gets ugly — or a hurricane season, or a panic — and Mr. Market offers $160–175, the margin of safety appears and you should act decisively, in size, exactly as the manual's Chapter 12 instructs. (Context: An independent research service's asset-allocation model sits at 65% stocks / 25% cash — its own counsel is patience with dry powder.)

PRINCIPLE: Insist on the cushion. The time to have your price ready is before the market offers it — Mr. Market serves the investor who has already done the valuation and merely awaits his quote.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

BUFFETT: Our verdict on the business: wonderful — a genuine franchise in a commodity product, built on cost and information advantages, proven by a decade of ~25% returns on equity, negative-cost float of $47 billion, underwriting profits in every year of the last ten, and management that allocates capital the way we would ourselves. Our verdict on the investment at $219.59: fairly priced — wait. A wonderful business at a fair price is Charlie's formulation for a hold; it is not a margin of safety for new capital.

The one question, answered. We asked: do you value the peak or the cycle? The cycle. Progressive's 2025 combined ratio near 89 is the crest of the hardest market in a generation; its own management promises only 96 through time, and its decade averages ~92. Normalize, and $19 of peak EPS becomes $14–15 of durable earning power — worth about $230, which is almost exactly what the market now asks. Whoever valued the peak at $293 last year has already paid the tuition for this lesson.

What a student must understand better before true comfort:
1. Where the soft market bottoms — whether Progressive's structural edge holds the trough combined ratio near 95–96 (as in 2016–17 and 2022–23) or competition pushes it above 100 for the first time in decades. Watch retention, rate filings, and ad-spend efficiency quarterly.
2. The autonomy timetable — the pace at which ADAS/autonomous technology shrinks claims frequency (good for margins early) and ultimately the premium pool (bad for volume late), and whether commercial/robotaxi insurance replaces it.
3. The property book and reinsurance costs — small (4% of Personal Lines NPW) but the segment with catastrophe teeth; a Florida mega-loss year would test the $200M/$75M retentions and the 96-goal discipline.

The three lessons this case teaches most vividly:
1. Normalize cyclical earnings or be ruled by them. A cyclical at peak earnings manufactures a low P/E; 11× peak = 15× normal. The combined-ratio history, not the trailing multiple, is the valuation input.
2. Float turns a mediocre-looking business into a compounding machine — but only underwriting discipline makes float free. The same $47 billion that enriches Progressive would bankrupt a careless underwriter; the asset and the risk are the same line item.
3. Capital allocation is observable — judge the record, not the rhetoric. A variable dividend sized to residual capital, buybacks only below value, growth refused when underpriced: you could have inferred Progressive's quality from its capital actions alone.

This is a teaching case, class — an exercise in method using real filings, not a recommendation to buy or sell any security. Your circle, your estimates, your margin.
