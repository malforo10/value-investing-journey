A Value-Investing Seminar: NVIDIA Corporation (NVDA)

In the Graham–Buffett tradition
Prepared 2026-07-04. Method: "How to Value a Business — A Security-Analysis Manual."

Welcome. Take your seats. Today we are going to do something that will make some of you uncomfortable, and that discomfort is the whole point of the exercise.

The company on the board is NVIDIA — ticker NVDA — the most valuable company in the world as we teach this, worth on the order of $4.7 trillion. It is a magnificent business. Its numbers are the most spectacular either of us has seen in a lifetime of reading annual reports. And by the end of the afternoon a good many of you may conclude that, precisely because it is so magnificent and so spectacularly priced, it belongs — for a disciplined value investor — in the pile marked "too hard."

I (Warren) want to say at the outset: I have spent sixty years cheerfully missing wonderful technology businesses because I could not tell you what they would earn in ten years. Missing a great investment costs you nothing but regret. Losing your capital costs you your capital. Those are not symmetric. Keep that asymmetry in front of you all afternoon.

And I (Ben) will remind you of the sentence that anchors everything: price is what you pay; value is what you get. We will spend most of today establishing what NVIDIA is worth, as a range, and only then glance at what Mr. Market is charging. Never the other way around.

Let us begin, as the manual instructs, by naming our sources.

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What NVIDIA actually is

Strip away the excitement and describe the business the way you would to a private buyer. NVIDIA designs the computing engines — graphics processing units, or GPUs — that have turned out to be the essential hardware for training and running artificial-intelligence models. It does not own a single chip factory. It is fabless: it designs, and it contracts the physical manufacturing to Taiwan Semiconductor (TSMC) and Samsung, with memory from SK Hynix, Micron, and Samsung, and assembly by firms like Foxconn (Hon Hai) and Fabrinet (10-K, item 1, "Manufacturing").

The company reports in two segments (10-K, item 1):
- Compute & Networking — the data-center accelerators, networking (NVLink, InfiniBand, Ethernet), and automotive/autonomous platforms. This is $193.5 billion of fiscal-2026 revenue, 90% of the total.
- Graphics — GeForce gaming GPUs and professional-visualization (RTX) cards. $22.5 billion, ~10%.

But the segment split undersells the real structure. By market platform, Data Center is roughly 90% of the whole company. NVIDIA today is, in its own words, "a data center scale AI infrastructure company." Gaming — the business that built the company — is now a rounding-error footnote by comparison.

The piece most people miss is that NVIDIA is not only a chip company. Since 2006 it has built CUDA, a software platform that runs on its GPUs, plus hundreds of libraries, frameworks, and tools. It reports over 7.5 million developers using CUDA (10-K, item 1). This software layer is the heart of the moat argument we will make later — hold that thought.

Customers. Here is the first genuine risk on the page, and it is not subtle. In fiscal 2026, one direct customer was 22% of total revenue and a second was 14% — 36% from two buyers (10-K, item 7, "Concentration of Revenue"). In the first quarter of fiscal 2027 it grew more concentrated still: three customers at 21%, 17%, and 16% — 54% of revenue from three accounts (10-Q, item 2). These are the hyperscale cloud companies — the Microsofts, Amazons, Alphabets, Metas of the world — the very companies that are also named as competitors in NVIDIA's own 10-K, because each is designing its own custom AI chips (10-K, item 1, "Competition"). Your largest customers are simultaneously your most credible future rivals. Write that on the inside of your eyelids.

The one question that decides the case

Every business reduces to one question that, once answered, settles the investment. For a stable consumer franchise it might be "will people still want this in twenty years?" and the answer is obvious. For NVIDIA the question is harder and it is this:

Is today's colossal earning power durable owner earnings — or is it the peak of a once-in-a-generation capital-spending boom — and can anyone honestly forecast it well enough to place a value on the business at all?

Notice that this is two questions welded together: a question about the level and durability of earnings, and a prior question about whether the future is forecastable. In most cases we answer the second question ("can I forecast this?") with a quiet yes and move on. For NVIDIA we cannot move on. So we must confront the circle-of-competence gate directly, before any arithmetic.

The circle-of-competence gate

The manual's Chapter 4 is blunt: discounting future owner earnings presupposes you can estimate them. "The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." And: "Too hard — I cannot forecast this" is a complete and respectable answer, and using it often is a competitive advantage.

Let us be brutally honest, the way we would be with our own money.

Can you tell me, with a straight face, what NVIDIA's data-center revenue will be in fiscal 2036? To answer, you would need to forecast: how large the total AI-infrastructure build-out becomes; how much of it is a durable productivity revolution versus a speculative over-build that digests painfully (the manual's peak-earnings trap); what share NVIDIA keeps as its own hyperscale customers pour billions into designing substitutes; whether AMD, Broadcom, and a dozen custom-silicon efforts erode pricing; how U.S.–China export policy evolves (NVIDIA was, at fiscal year-end, "effectively foreclosed from competing in China's data center market" — 10-K, item 1); and whether the CUDA software moat holds as open-source model ecosystems mature. Each of those is a genuine unknown. Multiply six genuine unknowns together and you do not have a forecast; you have a wish with decimal places.

Now — we will not pretend the moat is imaginary. It is real and, today, wide; we will measure it in Section 4. And an expert in semiconductors, someone who lives inside this industry, might legitimately place NVIDIA inside their circle with a defensible range. But for the generalist value investor — for most of the people in this room — the honest verdict on the gate is: NVIDIA sits at or just beyond the boundary. We can bound its value only within an embarrassingly wide range, and the manual warns that "a business just outside the circle … is where the expensive errors occur, because the analyst applies the machinery of valuation to forecasts that are merely guesses."

We will proceed anyway — because the purpose of this seminar is to teach you how the machinery works and where it breaks. But we proceed with our eyes open, and we will not let the elegance of a spreadsheet talk us out of the humility the gate demands.

PRINCIPLE. The first filter is not price; it is forecastability. Before you value a business you must decide whether you can forecast its owner earnings within a sensible range. For a hyper-growth business dependent on a brand-new, concentrated, policy-exposed end market, honesty may require you to answer "too hard" — and that answer costs you nothing but a missed opportunity, while its opposite can cost you your capital.

Now we read the three statements as one interlocking system — because, as the manual insists, the balance sheet, income statement, and cash-flow statement must articulate, each explaining the others. All figures from the 10-K (item 15) unless noted; $ in millions.

Read that table slowly. Revenue went from $27 billion to $216 billion in three years — an eightfold increase. Net income went from $4.4 billion to $120 billion. This is not a growth business; it is a detonation. Gross margin sits around 71–75% — the signature of a business selling something for which customers currently perceive no close substitute. (Operating margin is a barely-believable 60%.)

Two honest footnotes on the income statement:
- Gross margin dipped from 75.0% to 71.1% in FY2026, for two reasons management states plainly (10-K, item 7): the shift from selling Hopper systems to full Blackwell data-center solutions, and a $4.5 billion charge for excess H20 inventory and purchase commitments after the U.S. blocked H20 sales to China. That $4.5B charge is a one-time item that depressed FY2026 — a reminder that these numbers are not all sunshine.
- FY2026 "Other income" of $9.0 billion includes large non-cash unrealized gains on NVIDIA's equity investments (including a stake in Intel). That is not operating earning power; we will strip it out. (In Q1 FY2027 this got extreme — $13.4B of unrealized gains on public equities in a single quarter — 10-Q, item 2. Ignore it for valuation; it is mark-to-market noise.)

The balance sheet — a fortress with an unusual profile

As of January 25, 2026:
- Cash + marketable securities: $62.6 billion (10-K, item 7). Add the marketable equity securities and it is higher still.
- Total debt: $8.5 billion — trivially small. Net cash of roughly $54 billion.
- Inventories: $21.4 billion — more than double the prior year ($10.1B). A build this large is a genuine flag: it can mean confident scaling into demand, or it can mean you are exposed if demand digests. The auditor flagged inventory valuation as the sole critical audit matter (10-K, item 15, part 1).
- The item that dwarfs everything: $95.2 billion of inventory purchase and long-term supply/capacity obligations (10-K, item 15, part 1). This is NVIDIA's non-cancellable commitment to TSMC and others to buy future capacity. It is off the face of the balance sheet, in the notes — exactly where the manual (Chapter 8) tells you the real obligations hide. We return to it in Section 5.
- Goodwill jumped from $5.2B to $20.8B and non-marketable equity securities from $3.4B to $22.3B — the fingerprints of an aggressive acquisition-and-investment year (including a $13.0B outlay related to Groq and $17.5B into private companies — 10-K, item 15, part 1, cash-flow statement, and item 7).

Do not anchor to book value. Stockholders' equity is $157 billion. The market values the company at ~$4.7 trillion — thirty times book. The manual is emphatic (Chapter 7): "book value understates a great business." The gap here — roughly $4.5 trillion — is economic goodwill: the value of the CUDA franchise and the installed developer base, none of which appears on the balance sheet. Book value is nearly useless as a value anchor for this franchise. It matters here only as a solvency check, and on that score it is a fortress.

The cash-flow statement — the truth-teller

The manual's second conviction: statements are evidence; cash is the truth-teller. So we go to the cash-flow statement (10-K, item 15, part 1).

  • Operating cash flow FY2026: $102.7 billion (up from $64.1B).
  • Capital expenditures: $6.0 billion — astonishingly small against $216B of revenue. This is the beauty of the fabless model: TSMC carries the tens of billions of factory capex; NVIDIA carries the design. It is capital-light in the extreme.
  • Free cash flow (OCF − capex): ~$96.7 billion.

Now the articulation check — do the three statements agree? Net income $120.1B, but operating cash flow $102.7B is lower. Why? Two reconciling items tell the story:
1. Add back the non-cash equity gains of $8.9 billion — the statement removes them because they are not cash (confirming our decision to strip them from earning power).
2. Subtract a huge working-capital build: accounts receivable up $15.4B and inventories up $11.3B as the business scales. That is cash tied up in growth. It is why a hyper-growth business can report enormous profit while its cash flow lags — every dollar of new sales requires new receivables and inventory to sit under it.

The statements articulate cleanly. The earnings are real cash, not an accounting mirage — with the one caveat that ~$9B of reported net income is non-operating mark-to-market gain.

Earnings-quality scan (Chapter 7)

The manual asks: can you trust the numbers? Our scan:
- Stock-based compensation: $6.4 billion (10-K, cash-flow statement), added back as a non-cash charge. Here is the discipline the manual demands: SBC is a non-cash charge but a very real economic cost. NVIDIA pays a large slice of its 42,000 employees in stock. That stock comes out of your ownership unless it is bought back. Treating it as "free" is exactly the "stock options treated as free" red flag the manual lists (Chapter 7). We will subtract it, or hold it against the buybacks, when we compute owner earnings. Do not let anyone show you an "adjusted" NVIDIA number that adds SBC back and calls it earnings.
- Inventory and purchase-commitment reserves. The $4.5B H20 charge and $7.2B total FY2026 inventory provisions (10-K, item 7) show the reserving is active and material. Against $95.2B of purchase commitments, the honesty of these reserves is the single most important accounting judgment in the filing — which is why the auditor named it the critical audit matter. This is not a criticism; it is a location of risk.
- Customer concentration (Section 2) — an earnings-quality issue as much as a business risk, because 36–54% of revenue resting on two or three buyers means the "quality" of the revenue stream is only as good as those relationships and their in-housing plans.
- Disclosure quality: candid. Management states the China foreclosure, the margin pressures, and the concentration plainly. No EBITDA-as-cash-flow games. On the manual's red-flag checklist, NVIDIA is clean except for the SBC point.

PRINCIPLE. Read the three statements as one system and let cash arbitrate. Reported earnings can be inflated by non-cash mark-to-market gains and flattered by treating stock-based compensation as free; only owner earnings — cash the owner can actually withdraw after funding the business and after honestly counting the cost of shares handed to employees — belongs in a valuation. And never anchor a great franchise to its book value; the value lives in the economic goodwill the balance sheet cannot see.

4. EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The earning-power trend — and the peak question

We saw the explosion: net income from $4.4B (FY2023) to $120B (FY2026). An independent research service and NVIDIA's own guidance point higher still — an independent research service estimates EPS of $8.90 in FY2026's successor year (FY2027) rising toward $12.50, and revenue reaching $385 billion (an independent research service, 2026-06-19). Q1 FY2027 already printed $81.6 billion of revenue, up 85% year-over-year (10-Q).

Here is where a careless analyst destroys himself. He takes $120 billion of earnings — or the projected $200B+ — and capitalizes it into a perpetuity growing at 20%. The manual (Chapter 7) has a name for this: capitalizing a peak "is how careful arithmetic manufactures a large, precise, and wrong intrinsic value."

So we must ask the peak question with real skepticism. Is $120 billion of earning power a durable, mid-cycle number — or the crest of a wave?

The case that it is a peak (or near one):
- The demand is a capital-spending boom by a handful of buyers. An independent research service notes Big Tech is expected to commit over $1 trillion to data-center build-out; Microsoft alone budgeted $190 billion for AI capex in 2026 (Selection & Opinion, 07-10-2026). When customers are spending like this, a supplier's revenue can look like durable earning power right up until the build-out digests. NVIDIA has already had a demand air-pocket — FY2023's EPS of $0.17.
- The BIS warning (cited in Selection & Opinion): heavy reliance on debt and private credit to fund the AI build-out "could cause a credit crunch and a recession down the road if financial returns were to fall short." Much of NVIDIA's demand is being financed, not funded from operating profit.
- The customers are the competitors — every hyperscaler is designing custom silicon to reduce its NVIDIA bill.

The case that it is durable:
- The one-year product cadence (Hopper → Blackwell → Blackwell Ultra → Rubin) keeps raising the performance bar.
- The CUDA software lock-in (below) makes switching costly even when a rival chip is competitive on paper.
- AI adoption may be a genuine multi-decade platform shift, not a bubble.

The intellectually honest answer is: we do not know, and neither does anyone else. Which is why, when we value the business, we will not extrapolate hyper-growth forever, and we will present a range wide enough to hold both futures.

Owner earnings

Let us compute owner earnings the manual's way (Chapter 7): reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital.

Start from core net income, stripping the $8.9B of non-cash equity gains: $120.1B − $8.9B ≈ $111 billion of operating net income.

Now the adjustments:
- Add back depreciation & amortization ($2.8B) — non-cash.
- Subtract maintenance capex. NVIDIA's total capex is only $6.0B, and because it is fabless, most of that is growth (new buildings, R&D infrastructure), not maintenance. Maintenance capex is plausibly around the D&A level (~$3B). So D&A added back and maintenance capex subtracted roughly cancel.
- Incremental working capital. For a normalized owner-earnings figure we do not penalize maintenance-level operations for the growth-driven receivable and inventory build — that build funds expansion, not standing still.
- Stock-based compensation ($6.4B) — this is the one we must honor as a real cost. Subtract it, unless it is fully offset by buybacks at fair value.

Netting these, our central owner-earnings estimate is roughly $105–$115 billion; call it ~$110 billion. That is the cash an owner could withdraw this year without weakening the franchise — before deciding how much of the growth to fund. Note this is below the reported $120B, because we removed the equity gains and honored the SBC cost. That gap is the discipline.

The moat — genuinely wide today, and what could erode it

Why does NVIDIA earn a 60% operating margin? Because it has a moat, and today it is wide. The manual (Chapter 5) lists the sources; NVIDIA has an unusual number of them stacked together:

  • Switching costs / software lock-in (the big one). CUDA is sixteen years of accumulated software, libraries, and tooling, with 7.5 million developers whose skills, code, and models are written for NVIDIA. A rival can match the silicon and still lose, because the customer's entire software stack would have to be rewritten and re-validated. This is a classic high-switching-cost moat.
  • Network effects. More developers → more software and models optimized for NVIDIA → more valuable to the next customer → more developers. The manual's "network effect" source, operating at full strength.
  • Scale and full-stack integration. NVIDIA sells GPUs, CPUs, and networking as one co-designed data-center system. Scale funds $18.5 billion of R&D (FY2026) — more than most rivals' entire revenue — and a one-year product cadence competitors struggle to match.

By the manual's diagnostic test — pricing power — NVIDIA passes emphatically. A 71–75% gross margin on the most sought-after product in technology is pricing power made visible.

But the manual's caution (Chapter 5) is the load-bearing one: moats erode, and many dominant businesses prove to be "Roman candles." What could narrow this one over a decade?
- Customer in-sourcing. Amazon (Trainium/Inferentia), Alphabet (TPU), Microsoft, Meta — the largest customers are building custom ASICs specifically to reduce NVIDIA dependence. NVIDIA even introduced "NVLink Fusion" to let custom silicon plug into its platform — a tacit acknowledgment that some workloads will move to custom chips (10-K, item 1).
- AMD and merchant competition closing the hardware and software gap.
- Open-source AI ecosystems that, if they run well on cheaper hardware, weaken the CUDA lock-in — a risk NVIDIA names explicitly (10-K, item 7).
- China foreclosure, which the company warns "helped our competitors build larger developer and customer ecosystems to challenge us worldwide" (10-K, item 1) — a moat being actively drained by policy.
- Demand normalization — if the build-out digests, the moat can stay wide while the water in it recedes.

ROIC — astronomical, and therefore a magnet

Return on invested capital is the manual's single best measure of business quality (Chapter 6). NVIDIA's is off the charts. On ~$111B of core operating profit against an invested-capital base of well under $100B (equity of $157B, most of which is cash and investments, not operating capital), the return on the capital actually employed in operations is several hundred percent. An independent research service reports return on shareholders' equity of 74.4% in FY2026 and return on total capital of 71.1% (an independent research service, 2026-06-19); the equity-atlas ROE is 61.5%. However you cut it, ROIC vastly exceeds any plausible cost of capital.

Now hear the manual's warning, because it is the most important sentence in this section: "The dynamics of capitalism guarantee that competitors will repeatedly assault any business 'castle' that is earning high returns." A business earning several-hundred-percent returns on operating capital is not a stable equilibrium; it is a flare sent up to every engineer and every hyperscaler CFO on earth saying come and take this. Such returns rarely persist unchanged. Part of the value question is really: how fast, and how far, does this extraordinary ROIC regress toward the ordinary?

PRINCIPLE. ROIC far above the cost of capital is the signature of a genuine moat — and simultaneously the surest magnet for the competition that erodes it. Value the business on normalized owner earnings, not a possible peak; argue the moat's durability rather than assuming it; and remember that the very height of today's returns is evidence that they will be assaulted, so growth must be projected to decay, never extrapolated flat.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Chapter 8 asks the prior question: can the business survive long enough to keep earning? For NVIDIA the answer is an emphatic yes — this is a fortress — but with one obligation worth naming.

Running the manual's five questions (10-K, items 7 and 15; 10-Q):

  • Leverage. Total debt $8.5B against net cash of ~$54 billion and owner earnings of ~$110B. Net debt is negative. The company could retire all its debt from a few weeks of operating cash flow. An independent research service's Debt/OCF is 0.08 — essentially nil. Financial Strength rating: A+.
  • Coverage. Operating income of $130.4B against interest expense of $259 million — coverage of roughly 500 times. Interest is a rounding error.
  • Liquidity. Current assets $125.6B against current liabilities $32.2B — a current ratio of ~3.9. Graham would nod approvingly; this is the working-capital cushion he insisted on before he would even consider a stock.
  • Refinancing / maturities. Debt maturities are laddered gently — only $1.0B due within a year, $3.5B beyond ten years (10-K, item 7). There is no refinancing wall. A $25B commercial-paper program sits unused as a backstop.
  • Hidden obligations. Here is the one to name: $95.2 billion of inventory purchase and long-term supply/capacity commitments to TSMC and others (10-K, item 15, part 1), plus $3.5B of land/power/shell guarantees to early-stage partners (10-K, item 7). These are real obligations. If AI demand digested sharply, NVIDIA could be contractually bound to buy capacity it no longer needs — which is precisely how the FY2026 $4.5B H20 charge arose. The $95.2B is not a solvency threat against $62.6B of liquid securities plus $100B+ of annual operating cash flow — but it is the mechanism by which a demand shock would convert into a real charge. It belongs on your risk list even though it does not threaten survival.

Verdict: fortress. And the crucial teaching point: the balance sheet is not where NVIDIA's risk lives. A weak business with a strong balance sheet can survive to fight another day; NVIDIA is a strong business with an impregnable balance sheet. The risk here is entirely on the other two axes — durability of demand and the price you pay. Do not let a spotless balance sheet lull you into thinking the investment is safe; solvency safety and valuation safety are different things.

PRINCIPLE. Financial strength asks only whether the business can survive a bad year and a shut credit market without becoming a forced seller. NVIDIA passes overwhelmingly — net cash, ~500× interest coverage, no refinancing wall. But a fortress balance sheet protects the enterprise, not the shareholder who overpaid; the large TSMC purchase commitments are the one obligation that could turn a demand shock into a real charge, and valuation risk remains entirely untouched by the balance sheet's strength.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Chapter 9: once a business earns more cash than it needs, capital allocation determines how much of the earning power actually reaches you. NVIDIA is founder-led — Jensen Huang co-founded the company in 1993 and still runs it (10-K, item 1). Founder-led businesses often bring long horizons and genuine ownership thinking; they can also bring concentration of power. Let us judge on the record.

Share count and the SBC-versus-buyback contest

This is the crux of capital allocation for a company that pays heavily in stock. Follow the shares (10-K, statements of shareholders' equity; XBRL). Adjusting for the 4-for-1 (2021) and 10-for-1 (2024) splits, share count has been remarkably disciplined:
- FY2024: ~24.64 billion shares → FY2025: 24.48B → FY2026: 24.30 billion.

The count has actually fallen over the last two years, even as the company issued $6.4B of stock to employees. How? Enormous buybacks that more than offset dilution:
- FY2026 buybacks: $40.1 billion (282 million shares); FY2025: $33.7B; FY2024: $9.5B (XBRL; 10-K, item 7). In August 2025 the board added $60B to the authorization; in May 2026 another $80B (10-K item 7; 10-Q).

So dilution is being contained — the buybacks are winning the contest with SBC, and the share count is shrinking. Good. But now the manual's harder question (Chapter 9): buybacks create value only when done below intrinsic value, and destroy it above. NVIDIA is buying back tens of billions of dollars of stock at ~40× earnings and ~2% owner-earnings yield. If the stock is fairly priced or cheap, this is brilliant. If — as we will argue — the stock is priced for perfection, then NVIDIA is spending $40B a year buying its own shares at a rich price, which is value-neutral at best and value-destructive if the durability thesis disappoints. The buybacks are not automatically a virtue; the price paid is everything, and the price is high.

Acquisitions and the disciplined walk-away

The record shows both aggression and, once, discipline. The aggression: $13B related to Groq, $17.5B into private companies, goodwill up $15.6B in a single year (FY2026). Some of this is ecosystem-seeding — investing in the very AI companies that then buy NVIDIA chips, a circularity you should watch (it can flatter demand). The discipline: the abandoned ARM acquisition (2020–2022), a ~$40B deal NVIDIA walked away from in the face of regulatory opposition rather than fight to the death. Walking away from a deal you cannot get done cleanly is exactly the rational independence the manual praises — the opposite of the "institutional imperative" to expand for its own sake.

The one-dollar test

The manual's market test (Chapter 9): retain a dollar only if it produces at least a dollar of value — i.e., incremental ROIC ≥ r. NVIDIA reinvests at astronomically high incremental returns today, so retained dollars have unquestionably created many dollars of value over the past three years. It passes the one-dollar test emphatically on the historical record. The open question is forward-looking: as ROIC regresses and as more cash is poured into rich-priced buybacks and ecosystem investments, will the next retained dollar clear the bar? Probably, for now — but with a smaller margin than the rear-view mirror suggests.

Candor

Management's disclosure is candid: it states the China foreclosure bluntly, quantifies the margin hit, discloses the concentration, and does not dress up the H20 charge. On the manual's candor test, NVIDIA reports its troubles about as plainly as its triumphs.

PRINCIPLE. Share count is capital allocation. NVIDIA has, so far, won the contest between stock-based compensation and buybacks — the count is shrinking — and walked away from a deal it couldn't get done cleanly (ARM), both marks of rational management. But a buyback is only as good as its price: spending $40 billion a year repurchasing shares priced for perfection is value-creating only if the perfection is delivered. Judge management on the price of the buyback, not merely its size.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Now the arithmetic. And I (Warren) want to frame it with a warning the manual makes and that applies to NVIDIA with unusual force: the master equation, Intrinsic Value = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ, is the definition of value, but a literal DCF on a hyper-growth business is treacherously hypersensitive to the growth and terminal assumptions. You are about to watch a small change in one assumption swing the answer by a factor of four. That is not a bug in our method; it is the truth about this business, and the honesty to show it is more valuable than any single number.

Our starting owner earnings: ~$110 billion (Section 4). Net cash: ~$54 billion. Shares: 24.3 billion. Live price: $194.83, market cap ~$4.7 trillion.

First, the three cross-checks (Chapter 14) — run as arithmetic

Cross-check 1 — the no-growth floor (OE ÷ r). What is NVIDIA worth if it never grows another dollar? At r = 11%: $110B ÷ 0.11 = $1.0 trillion, or about $41 per share, plus ~$2 of net cash ≈ $43. At r = 10% it is ~$47; at r = 9%, ~$53.

Sit with that. The no-growth value of NVIDIA is roughly $43–$53 per share. The stock is $194.83. Therefore roughly $150 of the $195 price — about three-quarters — is a payment for future growth. The manual (Chapter 14): "The floor tells you exactly how much of the price rests on the input most likely to be optimistic." Here, three-quarters of what you pay is a bet on growth materializing. For a business whose growth is the single hardest thing on earth to forecast, that is the entire ballgame.

Cross-check 2 — the implied multiple. The price implies an operating value (market cap minus net cash) of ~$4.68 trillion on ~$110B of owner earnings — an implied ~43× owner earnings. Is 43× defensible? For a business growing owner earnings 20%+ with a wide moat, a rich multiple is arguable — but 43× means the market is confident the growth is durable and long. It is the sort of multiple that is right only if the optimists are right.

Cross-check 3 — the owner-earnings yield versus the long bond. At $194.83, NVIDIA's owner-earnings yield is $110B ÷ $4.74T = ~2.3%. The 30-year Treasury yields 4.86% and the 10-year 4.38% (Selection & Opinion, 06-29-2026). So you are buying a growing but deeply uncertain coupon of 2.3% against a certain government coupon of 4.4–4.9%. The manual (Chapter 12) calls interest rates gravity. For NVIDIA's 2.3% yield to beat a 4.4% risk-free bond, the coupon must grow fast for a long time — which, again, is the entire bet. And note the macro backdrop: the Fed is on hold with inflation re-accelerating and hawkish (Selection & Opinion), so the gravity is, if anything, strengthening.

All three cross-checks say the same thing in three languages: at $195, almost the entire value is a bet on durable hyper-growth.

Deriving a defensible multiple (three ways)

The manual is wary of peer multiples (they anchor you to Mr. Market's opinion of other businesses), so we derive the multiple three ways and reconcile.

(a) NVIDIA's own historical P/E range. an independent research service's record (2025-09-19 report) shows annual average P/Es of 33.3× (2017), 33.9× (2018), 40.5× (2019), 31.1× (2023), with a median of ~36×. NVIDIA has always commanded a high multiple. The live trailing P/E on reported FY2026 EPS of $4.90 is ~40×; on core EPS (ex equity gains) it is ~43×. So today's multiple is at the high end of, but not wildly outside, its own historical band — the market has consistently paid up for this franchise.

(b) Peer / industry multiple. In the Semiconductor industry, an independent research service's Model Portfolios show Broadcom (AVGO) at ~27× and AMD at a remarkable ~74× current P/E (Selection & Opinion, Portfolio I). NVIDIA at ~30× forward (equity-atlas Live P/E) or ~40× trailing sits between them. A premium to the group is justified by NVIDIA's wider moat and higher ROIC — but the whole group is elevated, and an independent research service itself cautions that "very strong price gains have limited the … appreciation potential" (industry report). Anchoring to peers here just anchors you to a market that may be collectively euphoric.

(c) Quality-and-growth-justified multiple from first principles. What multiple does the discounted-owner-earnings logic support? That is exactly what the two-stage DCF below computes — and the answer depends entirely on the growth you are willing to defend. A business that can grow owner earnings 15% for a decade then fade to 3% justifies roughly 25–30× in the central case; one that sustains 20%+ justifies 40×+; one that grows only 10% justifies ~18×. The "justified multiple" is not a fact about NVIDIA — it is a mirror of your growth assumption.

Reconciliation: all three methods agree that NVIDIA warrants a premium multiple; none of them independently justifies 43× except through an assumption of durable 20%+ growth. The multiple is defensible only if the optimistic growth case is.

The two-stage DCF — shown only to expose its sensitivity

Now the illustration the manual demands: run the model, and show how wildly the answer swings. Starting OE $110B, 10-year explicit growth, terminal growth g, discount rate r, plus net cash. Per-share results:

Conservative 12% 11% 3% ~$114

Optimistic 20% 11% 4% ~$220

Look at what just happened. Changing individually-reasonable assumptions moved the per-share value from $87 to $382 — a four-fold spread — and even holding growth fixed, a one-point change in the discount rate or terminal growth swings it by $30–$50 a share. In the manual's "Sample Co." example the spread was $28–$50, a factor of under two. NVIDIA's spread is a factor of four. The manual's warning is not abstract here; it is the dominant fact of the valuation.

In every scenario, note, the terminal value is 50–74% of the total — meaning most of the "value" sits beyond year ten, in the part of the future we admitted (Section 2) we cannot forecast. That is the manual's Chapter 10 guardrail flashing red: "the terminal value is usually the largest piece of the estimate and the easiest place to smuggle in optimism."

Value-creating vs capital-consuming growth

One more distinction (Chapter 11). Is NVIDIA's growth the good kind? Yes — decisively — because it grows at an ROIC far above r. Each reinvested dollar today produces far more than a dollar of value; this is a compounding machine, not the textile mill pouring capital into quicksand. That is genuinely in NVIDIA's favor and is why a premium multiple is warranted at all. The risk is not that the growth destroys value; it is that the quantity and duration of the growth are unknowable, and the price already assumes a great deal of it.

Triangulating a range

Weighing the cross-checks, the historical and peer multiples, and the DCF scenarios — and deliberately down-weighting the euphoric tail because the manual tells us to use conservative inputs — we triangulate a wide intrinsic-value range:

  • Low: ~$85–$95 (growth disappoints to ~10%; the peak-earnings case)
  • Base: ~$130–$150 (durable 12–15% growth for a decade, then fade)
  • High: ~$200–$230 (sustained 20% growth; the optimistic-but-defensible case)

Call it, in round numbers, a range of roughly $90 (low) to $220 (high), with a central estimate near $140. The euphoric $380 scenario we note but exclude from the actionable range — it requires assumptions no conservative analyst should underwrite.

Sanity-checks: An independent research service's 18-month target midpoint is $269 and its 2029–31 projection is $295–$440 — above our high case, because an independent research service is capitalizing continued hyper-growth as its base case. That divergence is instructive: it is the same business, valued optimistically. Our range is deliberately more conservative because we insist on discounting for the forecastability problem the circle-of-competence gate exposed.

PRINCIPLE. Value is discounted owner earnings — that is the definition. But when the growth and terminal inputs are this uncertain, the DCF is not a scale that weighs the business; it is an amplifier that turns your assumptions into a precise-looking number. The honest output is a wide range (here ~$90 to ~$220), the recognition that three-quarters of today's price is a bet on growth, and the candor to say that for a business like this, "I cannot value it precisely enough to act" is often the correct conclusion.

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Now — and only now — we consult Mr. Market. The manual is strict: value first, price last.

The price: $194.83 (equity-atlas, July 4, 2026).
Our intrinsic-value range: ~$90 (low) to ~$220 (high), central ~$140.

Do the arithmetic of the margin of safety (Chapter 13): Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) / Intrinsic Value.
- Against our central estimate of $140, the price of $195 is a ~39% premium — a negative margin of safety of about −39%.
- Against our high estimate of $220, the price offers a slim ~11% discount — but you are relying on the optimistic case being right.
- Against our low estimate of $90, you are paying more than double.

So the stock trades above our central intrinsic value and near the top of our entire range. There is no margin of safety at the central case. To buy at $195 you must believe the optimistic-to-euphoric end of the range is the true one — which is to say, you must be confident about the very forecast we established you cannot honestly make.

The manual's Chapter 13 doctrine — paying up for quality — cuts precisely here and it cuts both ways. It says a wonderful business deserves a fair price, not a huge discount. But it does not license paying any price: "quality justifies a fair price, not an assumption that quality is infinite." At $195, against a central value of $140, you are not paying a fair price for quality; you are paying a rich price that already embeds the quality and a decade of flawless execution and durable pricing power and a benign policy environment. The margin of safety exists to protect you against error and bad luck. At this price there is none — every dollar of protection has been spent on optimism.

Is Mr. Market euphoric on AI right now? The evidence in our own sources says: in this corner of the market, quite possibly. The Semiconductor industry is Timeliness 5-of-94 with an independent research service warning that price gains have "limited the … appreciation potential"; the AI boom is "in full swing"; the BIS is warning of a debt-financed build-out that could unwind; and the an independent research service Asset Allocation Model has actually raised cash-and-stock caution. Interestingly, the "Magnificent Seven" mega-caps were down ~2% year-to-date in mid-2026 even as the broader market rallied (Selection & Opinion) — a hint that some of the euphoria may already be cooling. Mr. Market is offering you NVIDIA at a price that assumes the best. Remember: he is there to serve you, not to instruct you. Today his pocketbook is not offering a bargain.

PRINCIPLE. A wonderful company can be a poor investment at the wrong price. At $195 against a central intrinsic value near $140, NVIDIA offers a negative margin of safety — the price already pays for the quality and for a decade of perfection on top. Quality justifies a fair price; it never justifies any price. When Mr. Market prices a business for perfection, the disciplined answer is to admire it and wait, not to buy.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

The verdict

Using the manual's four ratings:

NVIDIA is a genuinely wonderful business — but at $194.83 it is, for a disciplined value investor, an "avoid" on price and, for most investors, an "avoid" on circle of competence.

It is not a wonderful business trading at a fair price. It is a wonderful business trading above our central estimate of its intrinsic value, whose durability we cannot honestly forecast, and whose price already assumes the optimistic future. The business quality is A+; the investment at this price is not.

Answering the one question

Recall the question that decides the case (Section 2): Is today's earning power durable owner earnings or a peak, and can you value it precisely enough to act?

Our answer: We cannot tell whether it is a peak or a way-station, and we cannot value it precisely enough to act — and the price requires us to bet, heavily and without a cushion, that it is durable hyper-growth. That combination — irreducible uncertainty plus a price that leaves no margin for error — is the definition of a situation to pass on. Not because NVIDIA is a bad company. Because we cannot know, and the price does not pay us to be wrong.

Two or three honest open questions

  1. How fast does a several-hundred-percent ROIC regress? Capitalism guarantees the assault; the timing and slope of the erosion (from custom ASICs, AMD, in-sourcing) is the whole valuation and is genuinely unknowable.
  2. Is the AI build-out a durable platform shift or a financed over-build? The BIS thinks the financing is fragile; the hyperscalers are spending as if it is permanent. NVIDIA's FY2023 air-pocket proves the demand can pause.
  3. Does the CUDA software moat survive the maturation of open-source AI and the customers' own silicon — or does it prove a wide-but-draining moat, as China foreclosure already shows one can?

The universal lessons — the reason we taught this case

This seminar was never really about whether to buy NVIDIA. It was about three disciplines that will protect you across a lifetime of investing:

  1. The "too hard" pile is a tool, not a failure. The most respectable answer a value investor can give about a dazzling, un-forecastable business is "I cannot value this, so I will not." You will miss some great outcomes. You will also never blow up on a business you did not understand. The best investors use this answer often — it is a competitive advantage, not a confession.

  2. A wonderful business is not a wonderful investment at any price. Quality and price are two separate judgments. NVIDIA is the purest teaching example we have ever had of a company that aces every quality test and still fails the price test. "Price is what you pay; value is what you get" — hold them apart, always.

  3. Beware capitalizing a peak. The arithmetic of a DCF will cheerfully turn a boom-year earnings figure into a vast, precise, and wrong intrinsic value. When most of your value sits in a terminal number beyond a horizon you cannot see, and when three-quarters of the price is a bet on growth, the false precision is the risk. Show the sensitivity, widen the range, and let the width teach you humility.

This is a teaching case, not investment advice. We are showing you how to think, so that you can reach your own verdict — on NVIDIA and on the next dazzling business the market falls in love with.

11. GLOSSARY

Every term, ratio, and formula used above, with (1) definition, (2) formula, (3) NVIDIA's number. Figures are fiscal-2026 (ended Jan 25, 2026) unless noted; $ in millions except per-share.

Owner earnings. (1) The cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. (2) Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. (3) ~$110,000 (core NI $111,149 after removing $8,918 non-cash equity gains; D&A $2,843 ≈ maintenance capex; SBC $6,386 honored as a real cost) — below reported net income of $120,067.

Free cash flow (FCF). (1) Operating cash flow left after capital spending. (2) OCF − Capex. (3) $102,718 − $6,042 = $96,676.

Maintenance vs growth capex. (1) Maintenance capex preserves current competitive position/volume; growth capex funds expansion. (2) Judgment split of total capex. (3) Total capex only $6,042 (fabless model); maintenance ≈ D&A (~$2,843), the rest is growth.

ROIC (return on invested capital). (1) The central measure of business quality; value is created only when ROIC > r. (2) NOPAT ÷ Invested capital. (3) Several hundred percent on operating capital (most of equity is cash/investments, not operating capital); an independent research service return on total capital 71.1%.

NOPAT. (1) After-tax operating profit. (2) Operating income × (1 − tax rate). (3) $130,387 × (1 − 0.151) ≈ $110,700.

Invested capital. (1) Equity + debt funding operations, net of non-operating cash. (2) Operating assets − non-interest-bearing liabilities. (3) A small figure relative to earnings — NVIDIA carries ~$54B net cash and $22B of investments outside operations.

ROE (return on equity). (1) Profit per dollar of book equity. (2) Net income ÷ Shareholders' equity. (3) $120,067 ÷ $157,293 ≈ 76% (an independent research service 74.4%; equity-atlas 61.5% on a different basis).

Interest coverage. (1) How many times a normal year's earnings cover the interest bill. (2) EBIT ÷ Interest expense. (3) $130,387 ÷ $259 ≈ 503×.

Net debt (here, net cash). (1) Debt minus cash/near-cash. (2) Total debt − (cash + marketable securities). (3) $8,468 − $62,556 = −$54,088 (i.e., $54B net cash).

Current ratio. (1) Near-term liquidity. (2) Current assets ÷ Current liabilities. (3) $125,605 ÷ $32,163 = 3.9×.

Cost of capital / WACC. (1) The blended required return on the capital funding the business. (2) Weighted average of after-tax cost of debt and cost of equity. (3) Debt is trivial; effectively the cost of equity ≈ our discount rate ~10–12%.

Discount rate (r). (1) The owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long rates plus a premium for uncertainty. (2) Long bond + risk premium. (3) We used 10–12% (30-yr Treasury 4.86% + a wide premium for NVIDIA's forecast uncertainty).

Intrinsic value. (1) The discounted value of all future owner earnings; a per-share range, not a point. (2) IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. (3) Our triangulated range ~$90 (low) – $220 (high), central ~$140 per share.

Margin of safety. (1) The discount of price to conservative intrinsic value; the cornerstone. (2) (IV − Price) ÷ IV. (3) At central $140: ($140 − $194.83)/$140 = −39% (a negative margin — a premium, not a discount).

P/E ratio. (1) Price per dollar of earnings. (2) Price ÷ EPS. (3) $194.83 ÷ $4.90 = ~40× trailing; ~30× on an independent research service's forward $8.90 est.; historical median ~36× (an independent research service).

EV/EBIT. (1) Enterprise value per dollar of operating profit. (2) (Market cap − net cash) ÷ EBIT. (3) ~$4,681,000 ÷ $130,387 ≈ 36×.

No-growth (earning-power) value. (1) What the business is worth if it never grows again — the floor beneath a DCF. (2) OE ÷ r. (3) $110,000 ÷ 0.11 = $1.0T ≈ $41–$43/share (+ net cash); ~three-quarters of the $195 price is a bet on growth.

Owner-earnings yield. (1) Owner earnings as a return on the price paid; compare to the long bond. (2) OE ÷ Market cap. (3) $110,000 ÷ $4,735,000 = ~2.3%, versus the 30-yr Treasury at 4.86%.

Two-stage DCF / terminal value / r − g spread. (1) Explicit forecast for n years plus a terminal value capturing the rest. (2) IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r − g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. (3) Scenarios span $87–$382/share; terminal value is 50–74% of the total — the hallmark of a valuation that lives beyond the visible horizon. The r − g spread is the denominator of the terminal value and the source of the hypersensitivity.

Stock-based compensation (SBC). (1) Pay issued in shares — a non-cash charge but a real economic cost (dilution). (2) Add-back in the cash-flow statement. (3) $6,386; treated as a real cost in owner earnings, currently more than offset by $40,086 of buybacks.

Economic moat / franchise. (1) A durable advantage protecting high returns; its signature is pricing power. (2) Qualitative + pricing-power test. (3) CUDA software lock-in, 7.5M developers (network effect), full-stack scale; 71% gross margin = pricing power confirmed.

Economic goodwill. (1) The value of a franchise above its tangible capital. (2) Market value − tangible book. (3) ~$4.7T market value vs $157B book ≈ ~$4.5T of economic goodwill invisible on the balance sheet.

Circle of competence. (1) The set of businesses you can forecast well enough to value; its boundary matters more than its size. (2) Honest self-assessment. (3) NVIDIA sits at or beyond the boundary for the generalist — the decisive judgment of this case.

One-dollar test. (1) Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value. (2) Retain ⇔ incremental ROIC ≥ r. (3) Passes emphatically on the historical record; forward margin narrower as ROIC regresses and buybacks are done at rich prices.

Normalized / peak earnings. (1) Earning power in a normal year, not a boom or bust. (2) Average/adjust through the cycle. (3) The central uncertainty — is $120B (or the projected $200B+) durable, or a data-center-build-out peak? NVIDIA's FY2023 EPS of $0.17 proves it can trough.

Risk (as the manual defines it). (1) The probability of permanent loss of capital — not volatility. (2) — . (3) For NVIDIA, risk lives in durability and price, not the balance sheet; the 1.50 beta (volatility) is not the risk that matters.

Free cash flow yield. (1) FCF as a return on price. (2) FCF ÷ Market cap. (3) $96,676 ÷ $4,735,000 = ~2.0%.
