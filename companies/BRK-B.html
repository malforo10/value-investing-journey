Berkshire Hathaway (BRK-B) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition · Case date: July 11, 2026 · Live price: $493.71 per Class B share (Equity Atlas, prices as of July 11, 2026, 08:36). Figures in U.S. dollars; billions unless noted. This is a teaching case, not personalized investment advice.

A note on the occasion: the manual we teach from was distilled from Berkshire Hathaway's own shareholder letters. Today we value the classroom itself — and the letters' own method for valuing Berkshire, the two-column approach, will be our primary tool. The student gets to watch the method applied to the company that invented it, in the first year of its post-Buffett era.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Graham: Before a single number, let us say what we would own. A Class B share is 1/1,500th of an economic interest in a Delaware holding company with three engines bolted to one chassis:

  1. Insurance and its float. GEICO (third-largest U.S. auto insurer, ~11.6% share), Berkshire Hathaway Primary Group, and the reinsurance group (National Indemnity, General Re, TransRe) collect premiums before they pay claims. The money held in between — $176.9 billion of "float" at March 31, 2026 (10-Q MD&A; $176B at year-end 2025, up from $138B at end-2020, 10-K item 7 part 2) — is a liability that behaves like a loan, and because underwriting has run at a profit in each of 2023–2025 and in Q1 2026, the cost of that loan has been negative: Berkshire is paid to hold other people's money and invest it.
  2. Wholly owned operating businesses. BNSF (32,500 route-miles of Western freight rail), Berkshire Hathaway Energy (four regulated U.S. utilities serving ~5.4M customers, interstate gas pipelines, UK and Alberta grids), and a Manufacturing/Service/Retailing collection (Precision Castparts, Lubrizol, Marmon, Clayton Homes, NetJets, McLane, Pilot, See's, and dozens more — ~387,800 employees company-wide, 10-K item 1).
  3. A securities portfolio. $288.0B of listed equities at March 31, 2026 (Apple, American Express, Bank of America, Coca-Cola, Chevron, Occidental, a fast-growing Alphabet stake), $339.3B of Treasury bills, plus fixed maturities and equity-method stakes (10-Q, balance sheet and Notes 3–5).

Buffett: How does it make money? The insurers aim to break even or better on underwriting, which makes the float free (or better); the float and retained earnings are invested in securities and whole businesses; the businesses ship freight, deliver electricity, build houses, and sell candy; and nothing is paid out — no dividend since 1967. Every dollar earned is retained and redeployed. That makes capital allocation not a chapter of this case but its plot.

The one question that will decide the case: Can Berkshire's next generation — Greg Abel became CEO on January 1, 2026 (10-K item 1A) — redeploy roughly $380 billion of cash and bills plus $30 billion a year of retained operating earnings at returns that beat what you, the owner, could earn on those same dollars yourself; and does today's $493.71 pay you fairly, or fully, for that machine?

Circle of competence (manual ch. 4). Be careful here. Nobody — not the board, not we — can forecast the economics of every one of Berkshire's dozens of subsidiaries. But that is not the test. The test is whether the holding-company bargain is forecastable: a protected insurance franchise generating negative-cost float, hard-to-replicate rail and utility assets, a marketable securities portfolio you can price in the newspaper, and a stated capital-allocation doctrine. At that level, yes — this sits inside the circle, provided we use a valuation method that doesn't pretend to forecast the pieces we cannot. That is why we will not run one consolidated P/E or a heroic forty-year DCF; we will use the letters' own two-column method. Knowing which tool the boundary of your competence permits is the discipline.

PRINCIPLE. A stock is a piece of a business, and you value only what you can understand. When the business is a collection of businesses, honesty about the boundary of your competence dictates the valuation method, not just the verdict.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

We read the March 31, 2026 balance sheet (10-Q part 1), the FY2025 income statement and cash-flow statement (10-K item 8 part 1), and we make the three articulate.

The balance sheet — what it owns and owes

Total assets $1,252.3B; total equity $729.5B, of which $727.2B belongs to Berkshire shareholders (10-Q). The asset side is dominated by the investment reservoir:

Cash & equivalents (insurance & other) 51.5 10-Q balance sheet
Short-term U.S. Treasury bills 339.3 10-Q (includes $17.2B unsettled purchases)
Fixed-maturity securities 17.7 10-Q, Note 3
Equity securities, at fair value 288.0 10-Q, Note 4
Equity-method investments (KHC, OXY, Berkadia) 20.0 10-Q, Note 5
Investment assets 716.4

Against them: $17.2B payable for the unsettled T-bill purchases (an accounting gross-up — the same bills sit on both sides), $42.8B of insurance-and-other notes payable, and the great insurance liabilities: $151.9B of unpaid losses and loss-adjustment expenses (including $30.4B of retroactive-reinsurance reserves) and $32.8B of unearned premiums. On the operating side: $184.7B (year-end) of railroad/utility property, plant and equipment, financed with $86.1B of railroad/utility/energy notes payable that Berkshire does not guarantee (10-Q Note 16).

Three judgment-laden items deserve a student's pencil:

  • Goodwill of $83.2B and indefinite-lived intangibles of ~$18.9B (10-K Note 13). Book value is not an anchor here in either direction: See's-style franchises carry economic goodwill far above these figures, while Pilot's carrying value ($18.7B including $6.5B goodwill) had a fair-value cushion under 20% at the 2025 test (10-K item 7 part 5) — a flag, not a crisis.
  • Insurance reserves are estimates. The good news: development of prior years' reserves has been favorable — $1.85B in 2025, $2.3B in 2024, $3.5B in 2023 (10-K Note 16) — the signature of conservative reserving. The caution: reinsurance casualty estimates were strengthened in 2025, and a 5% error there is ≈ $1.8B pre-tax (10-K item 7 part 4).
  • A deferred tax liability of ~$48.4B sits against unrealized investment gains (10-K Note 20). The $203.4B of unrealized equity gains (10-Q Note 4) are real wealth, but roughly a fifth belongs to the Treasury if ever realized. Remember this in the valuation.

Do not compute a textbook current ratio for an insurer: claims reserves are not trade payables, and the assets are invested against them. The right liquidity questions — answered below — are redundancy, reserve honesty, and maturity structure.

The income statement — the march from revenue to profit

FY2025 (10-K item 8 part 1): revenues $371.4B (of which insurance premiums earned $88.9B), pre-tax earnings $82.5B, tax $15.2B (18.4% effective), net earnings attributable to Berkshire $66.97B — $31.04 per B share. But look inside: $39.1B of pre-tax investment gains ran through earnings (mostly mark-to-market on stocks still owned), and the equity-method line shows a $9.6B loss driven by $10.7B of pre-tax impairments of Kraft Heinz and Occidental. Compare 2023–25 net income: $96.2B → $89.0B → $67.0B. Did the business shrink 30%? No — the marks moved. Management says so itself: periodic investment gains and losses are "often meaningless… little analytical or predictive value" (10-K item 7). When a company tells you its own headline number is noise, believe it — in both directions.

The cash-flow statement — the truth-teller

2025: net income $67.3B (consolidated) reconciles to operating cash flow of $45.97B — subtract the $39.1B of non-cash investment gains, add back $13.5B of depreciation & amortization and the $10.7B non-cash impairments, and the bridge closes (10-K cash-flow statement). Capital expenditures were $20.9B (of which BNSF + BHE $14.4B), so conventional free cash flow = $45.97B − $20.93B = $25.0B. Note also: equity sales of $30.7B against purchases of $16.9B — a net seller of stocks by ~$13.8B in 2025 (after ~$134B net sales in 2024, mostly Apple) — buybacks of $0, dividends of $0, and $9.7B paid for OxyChem two days into 2026 (10-Q Note 2).

Articulation check (manual ch. 7): net income (less the zero dividend) flows to retained earnings — $696.2B → $763.2B, a $67.0B increase matching net income to Berkshire; the same net income tops the cash-flow statement; and the change in cash ties the two balance sheets. The statements agree. The profit-vs-cash gap is fully explained by non-cash marks (which inflate income) and heavy capex (which drains cash below D&A at the railroad and utility).

Earnings-quality scan (manual ch. 7). Favorable, with named exceptions. Berkshire does not wave EBITDA at you; it volunteers that its own GAAP headline is noise; reserve development has been favorable; disclosure is plain. The judgment-heavy zones: insurance reserves (especially long-tail casualty), retroactive-reinsurance deferred charges ($8.1B), goodwill (Pilot's thin cushion), and equity-method carrying values — Kraft Heinz was still carried $1.4B above its market value at 3/31/26 with no further impairment taken (10-Q Note 5). And one more subtlety: an independent research service's "Net Profit" ($44.46B for 2025, EPS $20.61) is Berkshire's operating-earnings construct, not GAAP. The Equity Atlas "live P/E" of ~14.8 uses trailing GAAP EPS ($33.59, inflated by investment gains); on operating EPS the same price is ~24×. Same company, same price — the multiple you compute depends on which earnings you trust. Always ask what is in the E.

PRINCIPLE. The statements are evidence and cash is the truth-teller — but for a company holding a securities portfolio, even cash flow needs sorting: mark-to-market noise must be stripped from earnings, and the remaining stream tested against the cash it actually delivers.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Normalizing owner earnings

Buffett: Owner earnings are reported earnings plus non-cash charges, minus the capex needed to hold position, minus incremental working capital (manual ch. 7; my 1986 letter). For this case we must first decide which earnings to normalize, because the two-column valuation below will count the investment portfolio at market — so the earnings column must exclude insurance investment income ($12.5B after-tax in 2025) or we would count the same dollars twice.

The operating engine excluding investment income earned, after tax: $26.2B (2023) → $30.9B (2024) → $30.4B (2025) (sum of underwriting, BNSF, BHE, MSR from the table above; three-year average $29.1B). The Q1 2026 run-rate annualizes to ~$29.6B ($1,717M underwriting + $1,377M BNSF + $1,114M BHE + $3,199M MSR = $7,407M × 4; 10-Q MD&A).

Now the two honest adjustments, in opposite directions:

  • Down: the underwriting cycle is at its top. 2025 underwriting of $7.26B after-tax rode a GEICO combined ratio of 84.7% (10-K item 7 part 1) — extraordinary. The industry report (an independent research service, 5/29/26) expects rate increases to fade to low-single digits as capacity returns, and Q1 2026 already shows GEICO's loss ratio up 4.9 points (bodily-injury severities +12–14%; combined ratio 87.3% vs 79.8%, 10-Q MD&A). A mid-cycle underwriting assumption is nearer $5B than $7B.
  • Up: the collection is still growing. BNSF's operating ratio improved from 68.0% to 65.5% in 2025; BHE is recovering as wildfire liabilities resolve; OxyChem ($9.5B, closed 1/2/26) adds a new earnings stream; Q1 2026 operating earnings ex-investment-income rose ~18% year-over-year.

On maintenance capex, be candid: consolidated capex of $20.9B against D&A of $13.5B blends maintenance and growth at a railroad and a regulated utility, and the accounts cannot split them — much BHE spending earns a regulated return on rate base, which is reinvestment at a decent return, not decay. Rather than pretend to a precise split, we capitalize the segment earnings as reported (they are struck after depreciation) and let the multiple carry the capital-intensity judgment. This is also why reported FCF of $25.0B understates distributable earning power while GAAP's $67.0B wildly overstates it.

Normalized operating owner earnings: $28B (low) / $30B (base) / $32B (high), after tax, excluding investment income. The low swaps peak underwriting for mid-cycle; the high credits the 2026 momentum and a full year of OxyChem.

Look-through earnings (manual ch. 7). The $288B stock portfolio's investees retain most of what they earn; Berkshire's income statement shows only their dividends. We do not add look-through earnings to the valuation — taking the portfolio at market value in Column 1 already capitalizes them — but the student should know the earning power is there, compounding inside Apple, American Express and Coca-Cola, invisible to the income statement. Berkshire is the textbook look-through case: mind it once, count it once.

The moat

  • Insurance: scale, an AA+/A++ claims-paying reputation backed by ~$333B of statutory surplus (10-K item 1 — no competitor is close), GEICO's low-cost direct model, and a culture that walks away from underpriced risk. The proof is in the float economics: $138B → $176.9B of float over five years at negative cost. This is a moat measured in decades.
  • BNSF: a western rail duopoly (with Union Pacific) on rights-of-way that cannot be reassembled at any price.
  • BHE: regulated monopolies — with the moat's mirror image, regulated returns and now demonstrated tail risk (wildfire).
  • MSR: a mixed bag of genuine franchises (Precision Castparts' certified aerospace parts, See's, NetJets) and ordinary businesses (retailing, Pilot's fuel margins — Pilot pre-tax profit fell 69% in 2025 to $190M, 10-K item 7 part 3).
  • The conglomerate itself: permanent capital and the ability to write an eight-figure-to-eleven-figure check in a panic, without asking anyone. That is a moat most analysts never price.

What could erode it in ten years? GEICO's telematics catch-up race against Progressive; loss-cost inflation outrunning rate filings; catastrophe concentration (Berkshire deliberately tolerates single events up to ~$15B pre-tax, 10-K item 1A); BNSF's coal runoff ($3.0B revenue, secularly declining); BHE's wildfire/climate liability regime; and — the one that matters most — capital allocation after Buffett, because the moat of the holding company was partly the man.

Returns on capital

Graham: Consolidated ROIC is not cleanly computable here — float and regulated rate base make an industrial "debt + equity" denominator misleading, and we will not manufacture false precision. Use the yardsticks the business model offers:

  • Reported ROE: $66.97B ÷ $649.4B beginning equity = 10.3% — but flattered by investment marks. On the operating basis an independent research service uses, ROE is ~6–7% — but that understates, because the denominator carries $203B of unrealized gains whose earnings (the investees') aren't in the numerator.
  • The cleanest single measure of the whole machine: growth in book value per share — $141.16 (2017) → $333.61 (2025), a CAGR of ~11.4% (an independent research service array; XBRL confirms equity $348B → $717B, ~9.5%/yr, with ~$78B of buybacks along the way). Over any long stretch, that per-share compounding is the return on the owner's capital.
  • Cost of float: negative in each of 2023–2025 (10-K item 7 part 2). Where else does the "debt" pay you?

Is ~10–11% above the discount rate we will justify (~9%)? Yes, modestly — and that "modestly" is the honest headline. Berkshire is no longer a 20%-compounder; it is a fortress that has compounded value at high-single to low-double digits, with returns capped by its own size and its $380B of T-bills yielding ~4%.

PRINCIPLE. Durable earning power is proven by returns on capital that persist above the cost of capital. Normalize before you capitalize — a cyclical peak (here, underwriting) must not be priced as a perpetuity — and when a company holds big minority stakes, count their earnings once: either look-through or at market value, never both, never neither.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Graham: Can it survive a dreadful year without becoming a forced seller? Run the five tests (manual ch. 8), each as arithmetic:

  • Leverage. Total debt at 3/31/26: $42.8B (insurance & other) + $86.1B (rail/utility/energy) = $128.9B. Against it, cash + T-bills (net of unsettled purchases) ≈ $380B. Net debt = $128.9B − $380B = net CASH of ~$251B. The conventional ratio (net debt ÷ owner earnings) is negative — the question answers itself. And Berkshire does not guarantee the $86B at BNSF/BHE (10-Q Note 16); those businesses carry their own debt on their own regulated and franchise cash flows.
  • Coverage. 2025 pre-tax earnings ex-investment-gains and ex-equity-method ($82.46B − $39.08B + $9.59B = $52.97B) plus interest expense $5.07B, gives EBIT ≈ $58.0B; $58.0B ÷ $5.07B ≈ 11.5× interest coverage (19× if you lazily include the gains). Expected interest payments: $4.9B in 2026, declining (10-K item 7 part 4).
  • Liquidity. $380B of bills and cash against >$30B of expected 2026 claim payments and ~$10B of 2026 purchase obligations (10-K item 7), with a self-imposed $30B minimum cash floor written into the buyback policy. The insurance subsidiaries could pay ~$33B of dividends in 2026 without even asking regulators (10-K Note 15).
  • Refinancing wall. None that matters: parent debt $19.9B (reduced $2.8B in Q1); April 2026 saw ¥272B (~$1.7B) of yen notes issued at a 2.4% weighted coupon out to 2056 — Berkshire borrows because markets pay it to, not because it must.
  • Hidden obligations. Here live the real questions: $151.9B of loss reserves whose honesty is the whole game (favorable development says they are honest); retroactive-reinsurance and asbestos/environmental tails (~$11.1B ultimate, 10-K Note 17); and PacifiCorp wildfire litigation — $2.9B accrued and mostly paid, but plaintiffs have sought sums up to ~$48–50B; the April 8, 2026 Oregon Court of Appeals reversal of the James Phase I verdict (10-Q Note 22) is a major de-risking, not a conclusion.

Verdict: fortress — the strongest balance sheet in corporate America (an independent research service Financial Strength A++, Safety 1). For an insurer we judge on capital and reserves, and both are redundant. The margin-of-safety section need demand no extra cushion for solvency; the cushions it must demand are for cycle and succession.

PRINCIPLE. Solvency is the precondition that lets compounding continue. For a financial business, judge it on reserve honesty, statutory capital, and liquidity redundancy — and remember that the balance sheet this strong is itself an earning asset: it is what lets the owner be greedy when others are fearful.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Buffett: Judge us — now Greg — by the six uses of a dollar (manual ch. 9), from these filings alone:

  • Reinvest: ~$15B/yr of capex heading into BNSF/BHE at regulated or franchise returns (2026 forecast ~$15B, 10-K item 7).
  • Acquire: disciplined and episodic — Alleghany (2022), Pilot completed (2024), OxyChem for $9.5B cash (January 2026), with Occidental retaining the legacy environmental liabilities — the structure tells you who negotiated better.
  • Debt: trivial at the parent; repaid $2.5B of maturities in Q1 2026.
  • Dividends: none since 1967. The one-dollar test is the justification: $141 → $334 of book value per B share in eight years says each retained dollar became roughly $1.30–1.50 of market value over the period. Historically: pass. Forward: this is precisely the Abel question.
  • Buybacks: the policy is the letters' doctrine codified — only below intrinsic value "conservatively determined," never if cash would fall below $30B. Watch the revealed preference: $78B repurchased 2018–2024 (peak years 2020–21 at ~$25–27B when the stock was cheap), zero in 2025, then a token $235M in March 2026 — 431,462 B shares at an average $486.92 (10-Q Part II Item 2), Abel's first repurchases, made under authority newly vested in "the Chief Executive Officer, after consultation with the Chairman" (item 5). Read that carefully, class: management that once bought $27B a year bought 0.02% of the company this quarter. Their own arithmetic evidently puts today's price near but not conspicuously below conservative intrinsic value — a data point straight from the people with the best information.
  • Share count: A-equivalents 1,438,223 at year-end 2025, 1,437,903 at 3/31/26 — flat to microscopically shrinking. No options stream, no stock-as-confetti. The per-share claim is respected.

Candor: the 10-K calls its own headline earnings analytically meaningless, discloses the Pilot goodwill cushion, quantifies reserve sensitivities, and admits the Kraft Heinz and Occidental write-downs ($8.3B after-tax) plainly. Berkshire's directors' voting control fell from 30.7% to ~15% (March 2026 proxy, per an independent research service) as Buffett's shares convert and go to philanthropy — the alignment era is ending; the doctrine must now carry itself.

PRINCIPLE. Over a holding period you ride with management's capital allocation. Judge it by the one-dollar test and by buybacks executed on price, not on schedule — and when a great allocator's own buyback pen goes quiet at the current quote, that silence is information.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Graham: Intrinsic value is the discounted stream of owner earnings — IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ — but that identity defines what we estimate; it does not command a forty-year spreadsheet. For a company that is half securities portfolio, half operating collection, a consolidated P/E misprices both halves and a monolithic DCF is false precision. The letters' own tool for this company (manual ch. 8, ch. 14) is the two-column method: investments at market, plus operating earnings capitalized separately.

Column 1 — investments per share

From the 3/31/26 balance sheet (all 10-Q):

$51.478B cash + $339.261B T-bills − $17.229B unsettled T-bill payable + $17.669B fixed maturities + $288.034B equities + $19.951B equity-method = $699.2B, less $42.835B insurance-and-other notes payable = $656.3B net.

$656.3B ÷ 2.1569B B-share equivalents (10-Q cover, 4/14/26) = $304 per B share.

Three honest caveats, taught rather than hidden: (a) this column stands behind $151.9B of claims reserves — it is float-funded, which is fine so long as underwriting stays disciplined (the float is the negative-cost loan financing part of the column); (b) ~$42.7B of deferred tax (21% × $203.4B unrealized gains) is embedded — about $20 per share — which our low case deducts; (c) equity-method stakes at carrying value roughly wash (KHC carried $1.4B above market, OXY $6.4B below), worth ~+$2 in the high case.

Column 2 — operating businesses per share

We capitalize the normalized $28B / $30B / $32B after-tax operating earnings (ex-investment income) from the earnings-power section. The multiple must be derived, not asserted (manual ch. 14). Three anchors:

  1. Quality-and-growth-justified. For a stream growing at g and discounted at r, the justified multiple ≈ (1+g)/(r−g). Anchor r to gravity: the 10-year Treasury at 4.38% and the 30-year at 4.86% (S&O Selected Yields, 6/29/26) plus an uncertainty premium → r ≈ 9% (8.5% only for the most durable case, 10% when stern). Berkshire's operating collection can plausibly grow 2–3% real-plus-inflation-share long-run (rail/utility volumes, insurance pricing, OxyChem-style bolt-ons funded off-column): g ≈ 1–3%. Then: r=10%, g=1% → 11.2×; r=9%, g=2% → 14.6×; r=8.5%, g=3% → 18.7× (we cap at 17× — no conglomerate deserves its most poetic case).
  2. The company's own history. an independent research service's decade array shows Berkshire trading at 106%–147% of book and average P/Es of ~19–24 on operating EPS — but those consolidated multiples mix the columns; they confirm only that our base multiple is not extravagant relative to how the market has actually priced these assets.
  3. Peers, with a caution. Pure-play peers price rails at ~18–20× (Union Pacific), regulated utilities ~17–19×, quality industrials ~15–20×, and P/C underwriting far lower and more cyclically. A weighted blend of Berkshire's mix lands in the mid-teens — consistent with anchor 1. (The manual would remind us peer multiples are Mr. Market's opinions of other businesses; we use them as a boundary check, not a source of value.)

Reconciliation: all three anchors point to a mid-teens base multiple; we adopt 11× / 14.5× / 17×, the low end acknowledging insurance-cycle normalization, conglomerate opacity, and Pilot-grade businesses in the mix; the high end requiring float to stay negative-cost and Abel to allocate like his teacher.

The two columns summed

Case Operating earnings × multiple Per share + Investments IV per B share
Low $28B × 11 = $308B $142.8 $284 (after ~$20 tax haircut) $427
Base $30B × 14.5 = $435B $201.7 $304 $506
High $32B × 17 = $544B $252.2 $306 (equity-method mark-up) $558

Call it $425–560, centered near $505. The width — roughly ±13% around base — is the honest confession of a range built on a cyclical underwriting normalization, a judgment multiple, and a succession unknown.

The three universal cross-checks (manual ch. 14)

  1. No-growth floor. $30B ÷ 0.09 = $333B → $154.6/share; plus $304 = $459. At r = 10%: $443. Today's $493.71 sits only ~7.6% above the 9% floor: strikingly little of this price is a bet on growth. (Compare that to what the S&P at ~23× earnings assumes, per the S&O Market Monitor.)
  2. Implied multiple. At the live price, the market pays ($493.71 − $304) × 2.1569B = $408.5B for the operating column = 13.6× normalized operating earnings. Defensible for franchises of this quality — and therefore not cheap; a bargain would read 9–10×.
  3. Owner-earnings yield. $30B ÷ $408.5B = 7.3% on the operating column, growing low-single digits, versus the 10-year Treasury's static 4.38% — a ~2.9-point spread. Adequate compensation; not the fat pitch of 2020, when Berkshire repurchased $24.7B of its own stock.

The checks agree with each other and with the columns — which validates the model, not the input; the input ($30B) was earned in the earnings-power section, and it, not the multiple, is where an error would live (most plausibly in underwriting normalization). A conventional DCF sensitivity is embedded in the multiple grid above: moving r by one point (9%→10%) alone drops the operating column ~$27/share — the reason a single target price would be an illusion.

One market cross-check: An independent research service's 18-month target midpoint is $559 and its 2029–31 range $720–$880 (10–16%/yr total return) — more optimistic than our base, roughly consistent with our high case compounding forward. And book value per share of $337 (3/31/26) puts our base IV at 1.50× book, near the top of the decade's actual trading range — a reminder that our "base" already credits substantial economic goodwill above book.

PRINCIPLE. Value is defined by discounted owner earnings but is best estimated by the method the business makes reliable. For a securities-heavy holding company: investments at market plus separately capitalized operating earnings, with every multiple derived — from r and g, from history, from peers — and the result triangulated until the roads agree.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

The arithmetic, against the live $493.71 (Equity Atlas, 7/11/26):

  • vs. base $506: (506 − 493.71) ÷ 506 = +2.4% — no margin worth the name.
  • vs. low $427: (427 − 493.71) ÷ 427 = −15.6% — the price exceeds the conservative estimate.
  • vs. high $558: +11.6%.

A meaningful margin for a franchise of this quality — where part of the safety lives in the quality itself (manual ch. 13) — is perhaps 15% off base: $506 × 0.85 ≈ $430, which almost exactly equals our conservative estimate. That coincidence is instructive: at ~$430, even the pessimistic case is fully paid for and the base case is bought at a discount. A Graham-stern 25% margin waits at ~$380. Do not launder a 2.4% discount into "safety."

Where does the safety sit here? Unusually, much of it is structural: $304 of the $494 price is cash, bills and marketable securities you can price daily, and the balance sheet forecloses the forced-sale catastrophe. What the price does not protect against is the pair of soft risks: underwriting normalization (already visible in GEICO's Q1 loss ratio) and mediocre redeployment of the cash mountain. Quality justifies a fair price; it never justifies any price.

And Mr. Market's mood? Split-minded, and usefully so. He is euphoric elsewhere — S&P up 20.9% in twelve months to record highs, median an independent research service P/E 18.2 (at the June 2 market top it was 17.7), an AI capex boom the BIS itself is warning about, and an independent research service holding a defensive 65/10/25 stock/bond/cash allocation (S&O 7/10/26). Yet toward Berkshire he is cool: −5.0% total return over the year against +29.3% for the market (VL 5/29/26) — the "Buffett premium" removed after the May 2025 succession announcement, Timeliness rank 5. The same market paying fancy prices for growth stories offers the fortress at ~2% off a careful base value — neither a gift nor an insult. The patient student notes the price at which management's own buyback pen restarted (~$487, in trivial size) and the prices at which it bought in size ($180–260 in 2020–21, then ~1.2–1.4× book), sets an alert near $430, and gets on with life. For choosing an entry, Mr. Market serves you only when he despairs; today he merely shrugs.

PRINCIPLE. Insist on a cushion sized to what can actually go wrong. Use Mr. Market's quotes — including the quote at which management itself resumes buying — and never his conclusions.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

A wonderful business at a full-to-fair price. It passes every quality gate the manual has: comprehensible at the right altitude, moated in its three engines, financed like a fortress, accounted for with candor, and governed by a written capital-allocation doctrine. It fails only the last gate — price. At $493.71 against a $427–558 range centered at $506, the margin of safety is ~2%, and we require far more before capital moves.

The one question, answered. Can the next generation redeploy $380B plus $30B a year at owner-beating returns — and does $494 pay you fairly for that machine? Our answer: the machine no longer requires genius — the float, the franchises and the balance sheet compound at high-single digits on their own — but the upside beyond ~9–11%/yr requires Abel to deploy the mountain well, and today's price pays for the machine in full while giving the deployment option away for roughly nothing… in both directions. You are not paying for heroics; neither are you being paid to bear the uncertainty. That is the textbook definition of a fair price, and the textbook response: admire, watch, wait.

Open questions a student must resolve before conviction:
1. GEICO's trajectory — is the Q1 2026 loss-ratio jump (73.9%, combined 87.3%) rate-fixable noise or the cycle turning with loss-cost inflation running 12–14% in bodily injury?
2. The Abel record — three years of observable decisions (OxyChem's returns, buyback behavior, the Alphabet build, whether a dividend ever enters the vocabulary) will tell you whether the one-dollar test still passes at the margin.
3. The casualty and wildfire tails — reinsurance casualty strengthening and PacifiCorp's remaining claims (Phase I reversed on appeal, April 2026, but ~$48B sought in aggregate) are the two places a fortress could spring a leak.

The universal lessons this case teaches best:
- Match the estimator to the business. One P/E on a company that is half portfolio, half operations misprices both halves; the two-column method exists because the letters' author refused to mislead himself about his own company.
- Headline GAAP can be honest and useless at once. $96B → $67B of "earnings" was marks, not business; the same discipline that strips phony losses must strip phony gains — and normalize a cyclical peak (underwriting) before capitalizing it.
- Watch what allocators do, not say. $27B of buybacks at 1.2× book; zero at 1.5×; $235M as a signal flare at $487. The margin of safety was priced for you by the people who know the company best.
- A fortress balance sheet is not a bonus feature; it is an earning asset — it buys the right to be aggressive precisely when everyone else must be liquid.

This is a teaching case, not personalized investment advice; we are professors here, not your fiduciaries.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

Float. Premiums an insurer holds between collecting them and paying claims — investable money it does not own. Berkshire: $176.9B at 3/31/26 (~$82 per B share), grown from $138B in 2020.

Cost of float = underwriting loss ÷ average float. Negative when underwriting is profitable — the insurer is paid to hold the money. Berkshire's cost was negative in 2023, 2024, 2025 and Q1 2026 (pre-tax underwriting profits every period).

Combined ratio = (losses + expenses) ÷ premiums earned; below 100% means underwriting profit. GEICO 2025: 72.3% loss ratio + 12.4% expense ratio = 84.7%; Q1 2026: 87.3%.

Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital: the cash an owner could withdraw without harming the business. Here estimated per segment, after tax, ex-investment income: $28–32B, base $30B.

Free cash flow (FCF) = operating cash flow − capex = $45.97B − $20.93B = $25.0B (2025). Understates Berkshire's earning power because capex includes growth spending at BNSF/BHE.

Maintenance vs. growth capex. Maintenance keeps current revenue intact; growth expands it. The accounts blend them ($20.9B capex vs $13.5B D&A); at a railroad/utility much "excess" earns a regulated return, so we capitalized post-depreciation segment earnings instead of guessing the split.

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ invested capital. Not cleanly computable for Berkshire (float and rate base distort the denominator) — an honest "we won't pretend."

ROE = net income ÷ shareholders' equity = $66.97B ÷ $649.4B = 10.3% (2025, flattered by marks); ~6–7% on operating earnings (understated by unrealized-gain-swollen equity).

Interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest expense ≈ $58.0B ÷ $5.07B ≈ 11.5× (2025, ex-investment gains).

Net debt = total debt − cash = $128.9B − ~$380B = net cash ≈ $251B (3/31/26).

Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities — deliberately not computed: an insurer's "payables" are claims reserves invested against; the right tests are reserve honesty and liquidity redundancy.

Discount rate (r) — the owner's opportunity cost: long-term Treasury (4.38–4.86% in July 2026) plus an uncertainty premium; we used 9% (8.5–10% band). It acts as gravity: r from 9%→10% cuts the operating column ~$27/share by itself.

Intrinsic value (master equation) IV = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1+r)ᵗ — value is discounted owner earnings; here estimated by the two-column method: $427 / $506 / $558 per B share.

Two-column method. Per-share investments at market ($304) + separately capitalized operating earnings ($143–252) — the letters' own procedure for Berkshire; prevents a single multiple from mispricing both halves and stops investment income being counted twice.

Justified P/E ≈ (1+g)/(r−g) for a stream growing at g forever: 14.6× at r=9%, g=2% — how our 14.5× base multiple was derived rather than asserted.

No-growth (earning-power) value = OE ÷ r = $30B ÷ 0.09 = $333B → $459/share including investments: the floor beneath the estimate; today's price sits only ~7.6% above it.

Implied multiple (at the live price) = market value of operating column ÷ OE = $408.5B ÷ $30B = 13.6× — the price translated back into the multiple's language.

Owner-earnings yield = OE ÷ price paid for the earnings = $30B ÷ $408.5B = 7.3%, vs the 10-year bond's 4.38% — Chapter 12's gravity applied at the moment of decision.

Price-to-book (P/B) = price ÷ book value per share = $493.71 ÷ $337.15 = 1.46×, the top of the decade's 1.06–1.47 actual range. Book is a floor-ish reference for a financial-heavy company, not the value.

Margin of safety = (IV − price) ÷ IV = (506 − 493.71) ÷ 506 = +2.4% at base — insufficient; a real cushion begins near $430.

Look-through earnings. Your proportional share of investees' earnings, retained as well as distributed. Not added here — taking the $288B portfolio at market already capitalizes it — but the concept explains why Berkshire's income statement understates its earning power.

Economic goodwill. Franchise value above tangible capital — why our base IV (1.50× book) exceeds accounting book value, and why book alone misprices See's-type businesses.

One-dollar test. Retain earnings only if each retained dollar creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). Berkshire 2017–2025: $141 → $334 book value per share with zero dividends — historically passed; Abel's version is the case's open question.

Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can genuinely forecast. Here it dictated the method (two-column at holding-company altitude) as much as the verdict.

Deferred tax on unrealized gains. ~21% × $203.4B = ~$42.7B (~$20/share) owed only if gains are realized — an interest-free government loan meanwhile; deducted in our low case.

Retroactive reinsurance. Contracts covering past events, taken for the float; reserves $30.4B with $8.1B of deferred charges amortizing against income — long-tail, judgment-heavy, and part of why insurance accounts require trust plus verification.

Statutory surplus. Regulatory capital of the insurance subsidiaries: ~$333B — the moat behind the promise that any claim, of any size, gets paid.
