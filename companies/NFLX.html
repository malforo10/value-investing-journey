Valuing Netflix, Inc. (NASDAQ: NFLX)

A Value-Investing Seminar — In the Graham–Buffett tradition · Seminar date: July 4, 2026 · Live price used throughout: $77.65 (split-adjusted, post 10-for-1 split of Nov 2025)

"Price is what you pay; value is what you get." We are going to spend this session insisting on the difference — nowhere more so than with a business whose accounting deliberately separates the cash it spends from the cost it reports. Netflix is the great teaching case for that separation.

A note on units before we begin. Netflix executed a 10-for-1 stock split in November 2025. Every per-share figure in this seminar is on the post-split basis (≈4,222 million shares). So when we say EPS of $2.53 and a price of $77.65, multiply both by ten to recover the "old" Netflix you may remember (~$25 EPS, ~$776 price). The business is identical; only the slicing changed.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What Netflix sells. A monthly subscription to a library of TV series, films, games, and — increasingly — live programming, streamed on demand to any internet-connected screen, in most countries on Earth. Revenue is overwhelmingly recurring membership fees. The 10-K (Item 1) states it plainly: "Our revenues are primarily derived from monthly membership fees for services related to streaming content." One operating segment. FY2025 revenue split: United States 40% / International 60% (an independent research service). Roughly 16,000 employees; the company stopped reporting the subscriber count after passing ~325 million members.

Two recent inflections you must hold in mind — they change the economics:
1. The ad-supported tier. A lower-priced plan carrying advertising, layered on top of subscription. An independent research service's analyst attributes the recent profit surge directly to "the successful implementation of its ad-supported service." It adds a second, higher-margin revenue stream (advertising) on the same content the company already pays for — pure incremental contribution.
2. The paid-sharing crackdown ("password-sharing"). Converting freeloading viewers into paying members or extra-member add-ons. This is why revenue reaccelerated in 2024–25 (+15% and +16%) after the flat, fearful 2022 (when the stock fell from $700 to $166 pre-split on a single quarter of subscriber loss).

The one deciding question for Netflix. Strip away the noise, and everything reduces to this:

Is Netflix a durable franchise — where brand, global scale, and a recommendation/data flywheel let it earn high returns on a content library that gets more valuable per dollar spent — or is it a hamster wheel, where it must spend ~$17 billion a year forever, merely to keep subscribers from leaving, with no lasting advantage?

If the first is true, the ~$17B of annual content spend is investment in a widening moat and the recent free-cash-flow turn is permanent. If the second is true, that spend is maintenance — the cost of standing still — and today's fat margins are borrowed from a future of escalating content-cost inflation. The entire valuation hinges on which story is right. We will not assume the answer; we will argue it.

Circle-of-competence gate. (Manual ch. 4 — "confine analysis to businesses whose long-term economics you can understand well enough to forecast within a sensible range.") Can we forecast Netflix?
- In favor: the revenue model is simple (price × members), recurring, and now global; the company is a decade past its cash-burn phase and its accounting, though intricate, is fully disclosed.
- Against: the hard-to-forecast term is content — hit-rate is unpredictable, competitors are deep-pocketed (Disney, Amazon, Apple, YouTube), and the terminal margin is genuinely unknown. This is a business we can understand approximately but whose 10-year owner earnings carry a wide band. Verdict: inside the circle, but near its edge — which is Buffett's warning zone. The correct response is not to refuse the analysis but to demand a larger margin of safety to compensate for the wider forecast band. Hold that thought for the end.

General principle: The first filter is predictability, not price. A business you understand only approximately is not disqualified — but it must be paid for at a wider discount, because the margin of safety is your only defense against a forecast that is honestly uncertain.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Netflix is the rare company where you cannot value it correctly until you understand one accounting mechanism. So we build it from the balance sheet up.

The content asset and content liability — the crux

(Definition — content asset: the capitalized cost of licensed and produced programming, carried on the balance sheet and expensed over time as "amortization of content assets" inside cost of revenues. Content liability: amounts owed for content the company has committed to but not yet paid.)

From the FY2025 balance sheet and Note "Content Assets, Net" (annual_report.txt):

Balance sheet item, 12/31/2025 $ millions
Content assets, net 32,778
— Licensed content, net 12,139
— Produced content (released, in production, in development) 20,640
Total assets 55,597
Current content liabilities 4,085
Non-current content liabilities 1,579
Long-term debt 13,464
Short-term debt 999
Stockholders' equity 26,615

Read that top line carefully. $32.8 billion — 59% of all assets — is content. This is not a factory; the "plant" of Netflix is its library. And the library is by far the largest single number on the balance sheet, larger than equity.

Now the footnote most analysts skip, from the content-obligations note: at 12/31/2025 Netflix had $24.0 billion of content obligations — of which only $5.66B (the current + non-current content liabilities above) is on the balance sheet, and $18.4 billion is off-balance-sheet because it "did not yet meet the criteria for recognition." That $18.4B is real, contracted, future cash you owe for content. A student who reads only the balance sheet understates Netflix's true content commitments by more than half.

General principle: Never anchor to book value, and always read the commitments footnote. For Netflix, book equity ($26.6B) is trivial against a $328B market value — because the asset that matters, the brand and the global subscriber flywheel, is an economic-goodwill franchise (Manual ch. 7) that appears nowhere on the balance sheet, while a large chunk of the real liabilities hides in the footnotes.

Operating margin is the analytically important line — it has expanded from ~13% (2019) to ~29.5% (2025), and an independent research service, which computes margin its own way, shows 66.6% "operating margin" because it treats content amortization differently; we use the GAAP operating margin of 29.5% and simply note the discrepancy.

The single largest expense inside cost of revenues is amortization of content assets: $16,422M in 2025 (2024: $15,302M; 2023: $14,197M), split $8,714M licensed + $7,709M produced. This is the accounting cost of the library being "used up." Operating margin has expanded because revenue is growing faster than content amortization — the crackdown and the ad tier added revenue against a content cost that is being spread over a growing, monetizing library. That is the mathematical signature of operating leverage, and it is the bull case in one sentence.

The cash flow statement — where the truth lives

Here is the number that separates Netflix from every ordinary company. On the cash-flow statement, content spending runs through operating activities, not investing. The FY2025 reconciliation (annual_report.txt):

Cash flow, FY2025 $ millions
Net income 10,981
+ Amortization of content assets (non-cash add-back) +16,422
− Additions to content assets (the real cash spend) −17,097
− Change in content liabilities −611
+ Depreciation of P&E and intangibles +333
+ Stock-based compensation +368
+ other working-capital & non-cash items ≈ +153
= Net cash from operating activities 10,149
− Purchases of property & equipment (true "capex") −688
= Free cash flow ≈ 9,461

Study the two content lines. Netflix adds back $16.4B of amortization (a non-cash charge) and then subtracts $17.1B of actual cash spent building next year's library. The cash content spend ($17.1B) now roughly equals the amortization ($16.4B) — a spread of only $0.7B. That near-equality is the whole FCF story: in the burn years the company spent far more cash on content than it amortized; now the two have converged, so operating cash flow ≈ net income + real capex is tiny, and FCF is large and positive.

Contrast the physical capex — only $688M on property & equipment. Netflix is physically asset-light (a technology and licensing company); its "capital expenditure" is almost entirely the ~$17B of content spend hidden inside operating cash flow. A student who computes FCF as "OCF minus $688M capex" and stops there has missed that the real capital intensity is $17B, embedded one line up.

The FCF history — from deep burn to gusher (XBRL, operating cash flow, which already nets content spend):

General principle: For a business that capitalizes its main expense, the income statement can look profitable for years while the company hemorrhages cash — or vice versa. The cash-flow statement is the referee. Netflix spent 2016–2019 reporting profits while burning ~$3B a year in cash; only when the two content lines converged did reported profit and cash finally agree. Always find the line where a company's core spending actually lives.

Earnings-quality scan

  • Content amortization assumptions (the estimate that matters most). The 10-K's critical accounting estimate: content is amortized on an accelerated basis over the shorter of the license window, estimated period of use, or ten years, with "over 90% of a licensed or produced content asset expected to be amortized within four years." This is management judgment. If they amortize too slowly, current profits are flattered and a future write-down looms; too fast, and profits are understated. Netflix states it has never taken an impairment of the content library. That is either evidence of a genuinely well-monetized library or a signal to watch closely — a value analyst files it as a key open question, not a settled fact.
  • Stock-based compensation — refreshingly clean. SBC was only $368M in 2025 — 0.8% of revenue. Netflix expenses options monthly at grant-date fair value and lets employees choose cash vs. options; there is no large hidden dilution machine here. This is a positive earnings-quality mark. (Compare a typical mega-cap tech company at 5–15% of revenue.) The share count is falling (buybacks), not ballooning.
  • Tax rate was a low 13.7% in 2025 (production tax incentives reduce content cost basis by ~$1.0B). Sustainable but worth normalizing upward in a conservative case.
  • No EBITDA games, no serial "one-time" charges, no pension assumptions. By the Manual's red-flag checklist (ch. 7), Netflix is clean. The one judgment-laden number is content amortization — and it is fully disclosed.

General principle: Trust cash over accruals, but respect that the biggest accrual here — content amortization — is honestly disclosed and internally consistent with the cash. Low SBC and a falling share count are quiet signs of a management that is not enriching itself at owners' expense.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Owner earnings — with the content-spend judgment as the hard part

(Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital — the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Manual ch. 7.)

The hard term, exactly as the Manual warns, is maintenance capex — and for Netflix it is not the $688M of servers and offices. It is the portion of content spend required to keep the existing subscriber base from leaving. Accounting does not split content spend into "maintenance" (replace expiring/aging titles to hold subscribers) versus "growth" (fund international expansion and new franchises). We must judge it.

Two honest ways to see owner earnings:

Approach A — take reported FCF as owner earnings. FY2025 FCF ≈ $9,461M ($2.24/share). This implicitly treats all $17.1B of content spend as maintenance — the conservative reading, because it assumes every content dollar merely holds position. Under this view owner earnings ≈ FCF and the FCF-turn is the owner-earnings story.

Approach B — recognize that some content spend is growth. Amortization ($16.4B) approximates the "maintenance" cost of running the current library; additions ($17.1B) exceed it by only $0.7B, and revenue is still growing double digits. If even a modest slice of the $17.1B is genuinely growth investment funding a widening moat (international, live sports, games), then normalized owner earnings sit somewhat above FCF, closer to net income of $10,981M — because a chunk of the content "capex" is building future earning power, not defending the present.

We will weight toward Approach A (owner earnings ≈ FCF ≈ $9.5B, growing) for the conservatism the Manual demands, while noting the true figure is likely a touch higher. The critical fact: owner earnings, GAAP net income, and FCF have finally converged for Netflix — $10.98B net income, $10.15B OCF, $9.46B FCF all within ~15% of one another. In the burn years they diverged violently (profit positive, cash deeply negative). Convergence is itself evidence the business has matured into a genuine cash machine.

General principle: When maintenance and growth capex are fused in one line, owner earnings become a judgment, not a calculation. Bound it: FCF is the conservative floor (all spend treated as maintenance); net income the optimistic ceiling (much spend treated as growth). Value on the floor, and let any upside be a bonus.

The moat — argued, not assumed

Buffett's franchise test (Manual ch. 5): a product (1) needed or desired, (2) with no close substitute in the customer's mind, (3) not price-regulated — and the diagnostic is pricing power. Score Netflix:

Moat source Netflix evidence Verdict
Brand "Netflix and chill" is a verb; the default video app on the TV. Real, strong
Global scale ~325M members, revenue in ~190 countries; can spread a single $17B content budget across the largest audience in the industry → lowest content cost per member-hour. This is the low-cost-position moat (Manual: GEICO/Costco analogue). Strongest single moat
Data / recommendation flywheel The most viewing data in the industry → better commissioning decisions and better retention. Improves with scale. Real, hard to replicate
Original IP Stranger Things, Squid Game, Wednesday — owned franchises the company can exploit forever without re-licensing. Growing, but hit-driven
Pricing power Repeated price increases (and the paid-sharing conversion) with rising, not falling, members and margins. Members did not flee. Confirmed — the decisive test passed

What erodes it in 10 years? Honesty requires naming the threats (Item 1A risk factors + industry report):
- Content is not a switching cost. A subscriber can cancel in one click; loyalty must be re-won every month with new hits. This is the "hamster wheel" fear made concrete.
- Deep-pocketed competition: Disney+, Max (WBD), Amazon Prime Video, Apple TV+, and — the biggest time-sink of all — YouTube. The 10-K explicitly names competition for both viewers and content as intensely competitive.
- Content-cost inflation from bidding wars for talent and sports rights.
- Consolidation: the Entertainment industry report describes Paramount Skydance acquiring Warner Bros. Discovery at ~$110B, creating a stronger rival with HBO, DC, Lord of the Rings, and Harry Potter.

The moat verdict: Netflix's moat is scale + brand + data, and the pricing-power test — the Manual's decisive diagnostic — is unambiguously passed: it has raised prices repeatedly while growing members and expanding margins. That is not a hamster wheel; a hamster wheel has no pricing power. But the moat is narrower and more contestable than a Coca-Cola or a See's, because the underlying product (a monthly video subscription) has genuinely close substitutes and near-zero switching cost. Call it a real but moderate-width moat that must be continuously re-earned with content — durable enough to justify a premium multiple, not so durable as to justify an unlimited one.

ROIC vs. cost of capital — the quality verdict

(ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital. Value is created only when ROIC > r. Manual ch. 6.)

  • NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax) = $13,327M × (1 − 0.137) = $11,501M
  • Invested capital = equity + total debt − cash = $26,615 + $14,463 − $9,034 = $32,044M
  • ROIC = 11,501 / 32,044 = 35.9% (≈28% if cash is left in the denominator)

Against any defensible cost of capital (r ≈ 8.5–10%, see below), ROIC of ~36% dwarfs r by roughly 3–4×. This is the signature of a capital-light franchise — once the library is built. Netflix earns extraordinary returns on the capital actually deployed in operations, because a single content budget monetizes across a 325-million-member base.

ROE = net income / equity = 10,981 / 26,615 = 41.3% (an independent research service shows 41.3% for 2025; the atlas's 31.5% is a forward/normalized estimate). Caveat, per the Manual: ROE is flattered by leverage ($14.5B of debt) and by the buybacks that have shrunk the equity base — so we lean on ROIC (36%), which already accounts for the debt, as the cleaner quality signal.

General principle: ROIC ≫ r is the mathematical definition of a wonderful business, because it means every dollar the company can reinvest turns into more than a dollar of value (ch. 10). Netflix's ~36% ROIC is the single strongest fact in this case — it converts the moat argument from qualitative to quantitative.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

(Judge capital allocation on the one-dollar test: retain a dollar only if it produces ≥ $1 of market value, i.e., incremental ROIC ≥ r. Manual ch. 8.)

Netflix's capital allocation has moved through three rational phases, visible in the XBRL:

  1. Reinvestment in content (2016–2021). The company retained everything and poured it into the library while ROIC on that spend exceeded its cost — the textbook right call for a franchise being built. Financing activities were strongly positive (raising debt: LT debt went $9.0B → $14.9B → $20.7B, 2017–2019) to fund the burn. Aggressive, but the one-dollar test was met: the library those dollars built now throws off $10B of cash.
  2. Debt discipline (2022–present). With FCF positive, Netflix stopped adding net debt and let the balance sheet de-lever relative to cash flow. Debt/OCF is now ~1.4× ($14.5B / $10.1B) — an independent research service rates Financial Strength A+. No forced-seller risk (Manual ch. 3's "never be forced to sell").
  3. Buybacks (2021, 2023–present). Repurchases scaled up sharply: $600M (2021) → $6,045M (2023) → $6,264M (2024) → $9,127M (2025). Share count is falling (4,278M → 4,222M).

The buyback judgment — price vs. IV is everything (Manual ch. 8). Buffett's rule: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value, destroy it above. Netflix bought back $9.1B of stock in 2025 at an average price that, split-adjusted, was in roughly the $75–$110 zone — the same zone as our intrinsic-value range below. So the buybacks were done at or modestly below our central IV estimate: value-neutral to mildly value-creating, not the reckless top-tick repurchasing that destroys owner value. A caution worth voicing: buying back stock at 30× earnings requires the growth to materialize; if it disappoints, these buybacks will look expensive in hindsight. Management is effectively expressing confidence in its own forecast — reasonable given the track record, but not free of risk.

Candor. Netflix's shareholder letters are famously direct — it reported the 2022 subscriber loss and the stock collapse without spin, and it discloses the content-amortization policy, the off-balance-sheet obligations, and the WBD walk-away in plain terms. On the WBD deal specifically: management signed to buy WBD for ~$82.7B, was outbid by Paramount Skydance at ~$111B, and voluntarily withdrew rather than overpay — collecting a $2.8B break-up fee and forcing a rival to overspend. That is precisely the discipline against the "institutional imperative" (Manual ch. 8) that Buffett prizes: the willingness to walk away from a deal that no longer makes financial sense is a strong positive signal about this management.

General principle: Judge management by what it does with a dollar of free cash, and judge buybacks only against price. Netflix passes the one-dollar test historically (content), de-levered when it should have, and — most tellingly — walked away from an $82B acquisition rather than overpay. Rational, candid, and disciplined against the institutional imperative.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Netflix is now a profitable, FCF-positive, high-ROIC grower. That makes two estimators defensible, and the Manual (ch. 9) says to choose the estimator the business deserves and reconcile them: (1) a normalized owner-earnings / FCF multiple, and (2) a two-stage DCF. We build both, show every input's origin, then triangulate.

Inputs, from first principles

Starting owner earnings: FY2025 FCF ≈ $9,461M (conservative, treats all content spend as maintenance). Net income of $10,981M is the optimistic ceiling.

Discount rate r. Manual ch. 11: r = long-term rates + a premium for cash-flow uncertainty. The 10-year Treasury and Fed funds are elevated (Fed funds 3.50–3.75%; inflation re-accelerating). For a franchise of moderate moat width with genuinely uncertain terminal margins and near-edge-of-competence forecastability, we take r = 9% as base, with a 8.5% (high) to 10% (low) band. Beta 1.30 argues for the higher end; the franchise quality and A+ balance sheet argue for the lower — 9% splits them.

Growth path. an independent research service projects EPS growth of ~21.5% near-term (EPS $2.53 → $3.15 in 2026 → $3.85 in 2027 → $5.25 by 2029–31) and revenue $45.2B → ~$79.8B by 2029–31 (~13.5% revenue CAGR). We must decelerate explicitly (the Manual insists growth cannot be extrapolated): Netflix cannot compound 20%+ forever as it approaches saturation and law-of-large-numbers bites. We model Stage 1 owner-earnings growth of 15% for 10 years (base) — below the near-term EPS estimate, respecting deceleration and letting margin expansion, not just subscriber adds, carry it — then a terminal g of 3.5% (roughly nominal GDP).

Is this growth value-creating? ROIC (36%) ≫ r (9%), so yes — each reinvested dollar of content spend, if the moat holds, creates well more than a dollar (Manual ch. 10). This is good growth, which is why a growth multiple is justified rather than a cigar-butt discount.

Two-stage DCF (Manual ch. 9)

Read that table as a humility lesson: a single percentage point on r moves value by ~$25/share; the terminal value is 70% of the base-case IV. This is why a precise "target price" for Netflix is an illusion, and why the decision must rest on a margin of safety, not on a point estimate.

Multiple cross-checks (name the multiples and their origin)

  • FCF multiple from first principles: a moderate-moat, ~36%-ROIC grower deserves a premium but not an infinite one. On FY2025 FCF/share of $2.24, a 35–45× FCF multiple → $78–$101. Netflix trades at ~34.7× FCF today.
  • Earnings multiple, historical: an independent research service shows an average annual P/E that has ranged wildly — high-40s to 70s in the growth years, ~29–33× in the fearful 2022–23 window, and an estimated 34.5× "average annual P/E" for 2029–31. The median P/E in the atlas (61×) reflects the euphoric years and is not a normal we would pay. A quality-justified 25–35× forward is more defensible.
  • 25–35× FY2025 EPS $2.53 → $63–$89
  • 25–35× FY2026E EPS $3.15 → $79–$110
  • an independent research service's own targets (independent third estimate): 18-month target $130 midpoint ($78–$182); 2029–31 projection $145–$215, built off EPS $5.25 × ~34.5× P/E ≈ $181.

Intrinsic-value range: ~$60 (conservative) — $95 (central) — $130+ (optimistic) per share, with a central estimate around $95.

Sanity check against the tape: live price $77.65; live P/E ~30.7×; FCF yield ~2.9%. The market is paying a premium multiple — consistent with a recognized-quality franchise, but not the 60× euphoria of the median. The live price sits in the lower half of our IV range, between the conservative floor ($60) and the central estimate ($95).

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

(Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) / Intrinsic Value. Manual ch. 12.)

Do the arithmetic against each end of the range at the live price of $77.65:

  • vs. central IV $95: (95 − 77.65) / 95 = +18% margin of safety. Positive, but thin.
  • vs. conservative IV $60: (60 − 77.65) / 60 = −29%. Against the conservative floor, the price is above value — no margin at all.
  • vs. optimistic IV $130: (130 − 77.65) / 130 = +40%. Ample — if you believe the bull case.

So the answer depends entirely on which IV you trust, which is exactly the point the sensitivity table made. The stock is cheap against the base/bull case and expensive against the conservative case. For a business near the edge of our circle of competence, the Manual is emphatic: value on the conservative case and demand a wide discount. On that standard, $77.65 does not offer the margin of safety Graham insists upon.

What margin-of-safety price would we require? Demanding a ~30% discount to our central $95 estimate implies a buy price around $66; demanding protection even against the conservative $60 floor implies waiting for the high-$40s to mid-$50s — the kind of price Mr. Market last offered during the 2022 panic (the stock's split-adjusted low that year was ~$16, and it traded in the $28–$50 band). An independent research service's own 18-month low target is $78 — essentially today's price — with the midpoint at $130.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational here? (Manual ch. 2.) Neither extreme. At ~31× earnings and a ~2.9% FCF yield, the market is pricing Netflix as the acknowledged winner of the streaming wars — rationally optimistic, not manic. It is a long way below the 60×+ euphoria of past cycles, and a long way above the 2022 despair. This is a fairly-to-fully-priced quote on a genuinely good business — the hardest situation for a value investor, because there is nothing obviously foolish to exploit.

The patient student's move: do nothing, and wait. Netflix is a volatile stock (Beta 1.30; it fell ~75% in 2022 on one bad quarter). A business this good, this cyclically-sentiment-driven, will be offered cheaply again — the last time was three years ago. Keep it on the watch list with a buy line in the mid-$50s to mid-$60s, and let Mr. Market's next mood swing hand you the margin of safety that today's price withholds.

General principle: A wonderful business at a full price is not a buy. The margin of safety, not the quality of the company, makes the investment — and for a volatile, sentiment-driven stock, patience is a position. "At $38 or above you do nothing and wait, however good the business" (Manual ch. 13).

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: GOOD, bordering on wonderful. ~36% ROIC, expanding margins, a real (if moderate-width) scale-and-brand moat that has passed the pricing-power test, a clean balance sheet (A+), disciplined and candid management, low SBC, and a decisive pivot from cash-burn to a $9.5B free-cash gusher. The one reservation keeping it from "wonderful" is that the product has close substitutes and near-zero switching cost — the moat must be re-earned every month with content.

Price verdict at $77.65: FAIRLY-TO-FULLY PRICED — a HOLD / WATCH, not a BUY. The live price sits in the lower half of a ~$60–$130 intrinsic-value range, offering roughly an 18% margin of safety against our central $95 estimate but no margin against the conservative $60 floor. For a business near the edge of the circle of competence, that is not the wide discount discipline requires.

Answering the one deciding question: Is Netflix a durable franchise or a hamster wheel? The evidence says franchise — but a franchise that runs on a treadmill. Pricing power is confirmed, ROIC is extraordinary, and FCF is now real and growing, which a hamster wheel could never produce. Yet the treadmill is real too: ~$17B of content spend every year is non-negotiable, and the moat is narrower than a consumer-staples brand. The correct synthesis: a genuine, cash-generative franchise whose durability is high but not permanent — worth a premium multiple, but not an unlimited one, and worth buying only on a pullback.

Two or three open questions to keep watching:
1. Content amortization / no impairment ever taken — is the library genuinely as well-monetized as the accelerated-amortization policy assumes, or is a future write-down being deferred? Watch the additions-vs-amortization spread.
2. Terminal margin — operating margin went 13% → 29.5% in six years. Where does it stop? The DCF is exquisitely sensitive to this, and the answer is genuinely unknown.
3. The ad tier and competition — will advertising keep compounding, and can Netflix hold pricing power as Paramount-WBD, YouTube, and Amazon press?

Universal lessons from this case:
1. Find the line where a company's real spending lives. Netflix hides $17B of "capex" inside operating cash flow as content additions. Standard "OCF minus capex" would miss it entirely. Read the cash-flow statement until profit and cash agree — or find out why they don't.
2. A premium multiple magnifies your errors. Seventy percent of Netflix's DCF value is terminal value; one point on the discount rate is ~$25/share. The higher the multiple, the wider the margin of safety you must demand, because the estimate is more fragile, not less.
3. Quality without price is only half the analysis. Netflix is a good business. At the wrong price it is still a bad investment. The margin of safety is the cornerstone — and for a stock that fell 75% three years ago, patience is not passivity; it is strategy.

This is a teaching case, not investment advice.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with Netflix's own numbers)

Intrinsic Value (IV). The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. Formula: IV = Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ. Netflix: central estimate ≈ $95/share (range $60–$130).

Two-stage DCF. Explicit forecast for n years plus a terminal value. Formula: IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. Netflix base: OE₀ $9,461M, g₁ 15%×10yr, g 3.5%, r 9% → $432,795M ÷ 4,222M sh = $102.51.

Owner Earnings. Cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Formula: Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital. Netflix: ≈ FCF $9,461M (conservative, all content spend treated as maintenance); ceiling ≈ net income $10,981M.

Free Cash Flow (FCF). Operating cash flow − capital expenditures. Netflix FY2025: $10,149M OCF − $688M PP&E capex = $9,461M. (Note: content spend of $17.1B is already inside OCF.)

Content asset. Capitalized cost of licensed + produced programming, amortized into cost of revenues. Netflix 12/31/25: $32,778M (licensed $12,139M + produced $20,640M) — 59% of total assets.

Content liability / content obligations. Amounts owed for committed content. Netflix: $5,664M on balance sheet + $18.4B off-balance-sheet = $24.0B total obligations.

Content amortization. The non-cash expensing of the content asset (accelerated, ≤10 yrs, >90% within 4 yrs). Netflix FY2025: $16,422M (licensed $8,714M + produced $7,709M).

Maintenance capex. Spending required just to hold competitive position and unit volume. Netflix: the judgment-laden portion of the ~$17B content spend needed to retain subscribers — not the $688M of physical capex.

ROIC — Return on Invested Capital. Quality of the business; compare to r. Formula: NOPAT / Invested Capital. Netflix: $11,501M / $32,044M = 35.9%.

NOPAT. After-tax operating profit. Formula: Operating income × (1 − tax rate). Netflix: $13,327M × (1 − 0.137) = $11,501M.

Invested Capital. Capital funding operations. Formula: Equity + debt − cash. Netflix: $26,615M + $14,463M − $9,034M = $32,044M.

ROE — Return on Equity. Net income / equity. Netflix: $10,981M / $26,615M = 41.3% (flattered by leverage & buybacks — prefer ROIC).

Discount rate (r). Owner's opportunity cost = long rates + uncertainty premium. Netflix: base 9% (band 8.5%–10%); Fed funds 3.50–3.75%, Beta 1.30.

Terminal value & terminal growth (g). Value of all cash beyond the explicit forecast, growing at perpetual g. Formula: [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. Netflix base: g = 3.5%; terminal value = ~70% of base IV.

r − g spread. The denominator of terminal value; tiny changes swing IV massively. Netflix: 9% − 3.5% = 5.5%; a 1-point change in either moves IV by ~$25/share.

Sustainable growth. Formula: g = Reinvestment rate × ROIC. Netflix: with 36% ROIC, even modest reinvestment funds double-digit growth — the mark of a capital-light franchise.

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth. Growth adds value only if ROIC > r. Netflix: 36% ≫ 9% → growth creates value.

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it produces ≥ $1 of market value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). Netflix: passed — retained content dollars built a library now yielding $10B of cash.

Moat / economic franchise. Durable advantage (low cost / brand / scale / network) whose signature is pricing power. Netflix: scale + brand + data; moderate width, pricing power confirmed.

Margin of Safety. Formula: (IV − Price) / IV. Netflix: vs. central $95 = +18%; vs. conservative $60 = −29% → insufficient at $77.65.

Mr. Market. The manic-depressive partner quoting a price daily; serve, don't be guided. Netflix: currently rational-to-optimistic (~31× P/E), neither the 2022 despair nor past 60× euphoria.

Risk. Probability of permanent loss of capital — not volatility. Netflix: real risks are content-cost inflation, competition, and overpaying — Beta 1.30 (volatility) is opportunity, not risk.

Stock-based compensation (SBC). Non-cash pay in equity; dilutes owners. Netflix: $368M = 0.8% of revenue — unusually low; share count falling.

Economic vs. accounting goodwill. A franchise is worth more than its tangible book. Netflix: $328B market value vs. $26.6B book equity — the difference is the unrecorded franchise; never anchor to book.
