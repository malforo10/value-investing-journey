United Parcel Service (UPS) — A Value Investing Case Study

A joint seminar in the manner of Graham and Buffett

Date of analysis: July 12, 2026

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

UPS moves 20.8 million packages a day, on average, across more than 200 countries, using an integrated air-and-ground network it has spent over a century building: airplanes, sort hubs (the giant Worldport facility in Louisville is the heart of it), package cars, and roughly 460,000 employees, about 80% of them union members under a national Teamsters contract. It earns money three ways, organized into two reporting segments plus a third bucket:

  • U.S. Domestic Package (67% of 2025 revenue, $59.5bn) — picking up and delivering letters and parcels within the United States, priced by speed (Next Day Air, Deferred, Ground).
  • International Package (21%, $18.6bn) — the same business outside the U.S., with meaningfully higher margins because of the value of cross-border logistics and customs brokerage.
  • Supply Chain Solutions ("SCS") (12%, $10.6bn) — freight forwarding, contract logistics, healthcare cold-chain, and digital businesses (Roadie, Happy Returns, UPS Capital).

In plain terms: UPS is a toll-road business for physical goods. It does not manufacture anything; it moves other people's goods for a fee, and its profitability is a function of how efficiently it can route a package through its network relative to what it charges for the privilege. That is a business a smart newcomer can understand in an afternoon — the products are simple, the economics (volume × price − cost to move each unit) are simple, and the disclosures are unusually granular (UPS reports revenue, volume, and price per package, by product, every quarter).

The one question that will decide this case: UPS's largest customer, Amazon, is both feeding UPS's network and building a competing one. Management is voluntarily shrinking its Amazon volume by more than 50% by mid-2026, waging a costly, multi-year "Network Reconfiguration" restructuring to replace that cheap, low-margin volume with higher-margin healthcare, SMB, and international freight. Will that trade — smaller but higher-quality volume — restore UPS's earning power, or will it simply leave UPS a smaller, still-commoditizing business in a world where its biggest customer is also becoming its most capable rival? Everything in the valuation below turns on how you answer that.

Circle of competence gate. This passes. A package delivery network is not a business that requires special technical expertise to underwrite — the input costs (labor, fuel, aircraft, real estate), the demand drivers (e-commerce growth, industrial production, trade volumes), and the competitive structure (a handful of national/global scale players plus regional and postal alternatives) are all things an ordinary, diligent investor can research and reason about. The harder judgment — whether management's multi-year "turnaround" succeeds — is a business-execution question, not a technical one outside anyone's competence. Verdict: inside the circle, though the near-term earnings path is genuinely uncertain, which is a different problem than incomprehensibility, and one the margin of safety is built to handle.

PRINCIPLE: A stock is a piece of a business, and you only value what you can understand. UPS's economics are plain enough to reason about; the uncertainty here is about execution, which a margin of safety — not a wider circle — is the correct tool to manage.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The balance sheet (10-K, Dec. 31, 2025; 10-Q, Mar. 31, 2026)

UPS held $5.9bn in cash against Total Current Liabilities of $15.6bn and Total Current Assets of $19.0bn — a current ratio of 19,045 ÷ 15,620 = 1.22×. That is thin by Graham's classic 2:1 standard, but not alarming for a company that collects a stream of predictable, weekly-billed cash and can draw on $3.0bn of undrawn, unused revolving credit facilities. Total debt was $24.1bn (10-K) and rose to $24.4bn by the March 2026 10-Q, mostly finance-lease growth from new aircraft. Shareowners' equity was only $16.3bn at year-end 2025 (and $15.8bn by Q1 2026) — a deliberately thin equity base, the product of 15 years of buybacks and, more importantly, an accumulated other comprehensive loss of $4.2bn, almost entirely unrecognized pension losses ($2.9bn of the $4.2bn AOCI deficit is pension/OPEB-related, note 12).

Judgment-laden items to watch:
- Goodwill ($5.8bn) and intangibles ($4.0bn) grew sharply in 2025 (goodwill +36% year over year) from the Frigo-Trans and Andlauer Healthcare Group (AHG) acquisitions — a bet on becoming the "number one complex healthcare logistics provider." The 10-K's own critical-audit-matter disclosure states that two reporting units, Global Freight Forwarding ($877mm goodwill) and Healthcare Logistics & Distribution ($738mm goodwill), "exhibited a limited excess of fair value above carrying value" in the July 2025 test — auditor language for "close to an impairment." Management even ran an interim impairment test on GFF in Q4 2025 because trade-policy volatility forced a downward revision to its long-term forecast. This is a live risk, not a footnote curiosity: a 100 bps increase in the discount rate or a 10% cut to projected cash flows would, per the 10-K's own sensitivity disclosure, make these units "reasonably possible" to impair.
- Pension and postretirement obligations: the U.S. pension plan is underfunded by $4.78bn and the U.S. postretirement medical plan by $1.75bn (funded status table, note 5), partially offset by a small international pension surplus ($264mm) — a net unfunded obligation of roughly $6.3bn. UPS is contributing ~$1.3–1.4bn per year to close this gap — real cash, every year, that never touches net income as an expense of the size it actually costs in cash.
- Self-insurance reserves ($3.1bn) for workers' compensation, auto liability, and general liability are actuarially estimated — a genuine judgment call the 10-K flags explicitly (a 5% deterioration in claim severity/frequency assumptions would move reserves by roughly $300mm).

Arithmetic: Operating margin 2025 = $7,867mm ÷ $88,661mm = 8.87%, down from 10.05% just two years earlier. Revenue actually declined in both 2024 and 2025 — an unusual thing to see from a company often labeled a "growth" logistics name — because management is deliberately walking away from low-margin Amazon and e-commerce volume (see MD&A: average daily package volume fell 7.0% in 2025) while raising rates on what remains (revenue per piece rose 6.6% in 2025). The net effect on the top line was a wash to negative, but the effect on profit was clearly negative in the near term, because the fixed cost of the network doesn't shrink as fast as the volume walking out the door.

One-time items to strip when reading 2025's $6.56 GAAP EPS: management's own non-GAAP reconciliation (MD&A, "Items Affecting Comparability") shows 2025 included $0.53/share of "Transformation Strategy Costs," $0.18/share of goodwill/asset impairment (including the MD-11 fleet retirement), a small net loss on divestiture, and a positive $0.13/share from reversing a tax valuation allowance — netting to a non-GAAP adjusted EPS of $7.16. Caution for the student: this "adjusted" $7.16 figure is what an independent research service itself prints as "Earnings per sh" in its per-share array (see footnote A on the report: "Pro forma diluted earnings") — not the GAAP $6.56 the company actually reported to shareholders. Always read the footnote. We will use GAAP net income as our starting point below and make our own, separate judgment about which "non-recurring" items really are one-time.

The earnings-quality red flag worth naming explicitly: UPS has now run three consecutive, overlapping "transformation" programs since 2020 — Transformation 2.0 ($835mm total cost, completed 2025), Fit to Serve ($463mm, completed 2025), and Network Reconfiguration & Efficiency Reimagined ($544mm incurred through 2025, expected to run through 2027) — each one excluded from non-GAAP earnings as "not ordinary." Taken together, that is over $1.8bn of cumulative "one-time" restructuring charges across roughly five years. A charge that recurs for five straight years, under three different program names, is not one-time — it is close to a normal, structural cost of running this business through a demand transition. We will not blindly add all of it back.

PRINCIPLE: The statements are evidence, and cash is the truth-teller. UPS's GAAP earnings in 2025 were flattered by one-time gains (sale-leaseback transactions, a tax valuation-allowance reversal) and burdened by charges that, taken together over several years, look recurring rather than one-time. Never anchor value to the headline EPS number without reading what management excluded and why.

Reading this honestly: 2021–2022 were a pandemic-driven windfall (e-commerce volumes surged; EPS nearly doubled from the pre-COVID run rate) — a peak that should be excluded from any normalization, exactly as the manual warns. 2023–2025 show earnings power fading, not growing, as volumes normalize downward and the company pays the price of restructuring. This is a business mid-cycle-trough, not mid-cycle-peak, and not a steady compounder at the moment.

Owner earnings

Starting from operating cash flow (which already nets out non-cash charges and working-capital swings) and subtracting capital expenditure:

  • 2023: $10,238mm − $5,158mm = $5,080mm (858.8mm shares → $5.92/share)
  • 2024: $10,122mm − $3,909mm = $6,213mm (852.8mm shares → $7.29/share)
  • 2025: $8,450mm − $3,685mm = $4,765mm (848.9mm shares → $5.61/share)
  • Three-year average: ≈ $6.27/share

Maintenance vs. growth capex — the hard judgment: UPS's capex has been falling ($5.16bn in 2023 to a guided $3.0bn in 2026), even as depreciation runs at $3.7–3.8bn a year. Management says roughly 80% of 2026's reduced capex is going to "network enhancement and technology initiatives" (largely automation to cut cost, not to add capacity) — in a shrinking-volume environment, this is closer to maintenance-plus-efficiency spend than growth spend. There is a real question whether capex now running below depreciation ($3.0bn guided vs. ~$3.7bn D&A) signals capital discipline or a slow undermaintenance of an aging aircraft and vehicle fleet; we treat this as a modest headwind to the "high" end of our range, not a certainty either way. Normalized owner earnings, blending the three-year average with a discount for the pension cash drag already embedded in OCF, land at roughly $6.00–6.50/share, which we will carry forward as our base case ($6.25), with $5.50/share as a stressed low case (turnaround stalls, margins stay compressed) and $7.25/share as a recovery high case (2023–2025's better years, e.g. 2024's $7.29, becomes the new normal as the network reconfiguration pays off).

The moat — what protects these earnings, and what threatens them

UPS's moat rests on scale and network density: an integrated air-and-ground system that took a century to build cannot be replicated overnight, and its cost per package falls as density rises. There is real pricing power — UPS pushed through a 5.9% average base-rate increase in both December 2024 and December 2025, and revenue per piece rose 6.6% in 2025 even as volume fell — evidence the network commands a price, not just a cost. There is also a genuine switching-cost/relationship moat with large B2B shippers who integrate UPS's shipping software into their own operations (the Digital Access Program).

But the moat is narrower than it looks, for one specific and important reason: Amazon is simultaneously UPS's largest customer (10.6% of 2025 revenue) and the most credible emerging competitor to UPS's core moat — a company building its own last-mile delivery network at a scale few others could contemplate. The an independent research service industry analyst puts this plainly: "Notably, Amazon.com is increasing pressure in the delivery sector." Management's own strategy — cutting Amazon volume by more than half by mid-2026 — is an implicit admission that this customer is not a stable, moat-reinforcing relationship but a shrinking, low-margin, potentially disappearing one. For small and medium shippers, switching costs are much lower (they can move to regional carriers, USPS, or, increasingly, Amazon's own network) — a real limit on how much pricing power UPS can exercise before losing volume. The moat, in short, is genuine but eroding at the edges: strong versus small/regional competitors, tested seriously by the one customer big enough to build an alternative.

ROIC — the central quality measure

  • NOPAT (2025) = Operating profit $7,867mm × (1 − effective tax rate 22.2%) = $6,120mm
  • Invested capital = Total debt $24,127mm + Shareowners' equity $16,255mm − Cash $5,887mm = $34,495mm
  • ROIC = $6,120mm ÷ $34,495mm = 17.7%

That comfortably clears any reasonable discount rate (we use r = 9% below) — a real, if not spectacular, sign of quality. But an independent research service's own "Return on Total Capital" line shows this fading, not improving: 41.4% (2016) → 33.1% (2020) → 21.9% (2023) → 16.7% (2025). Return on equity looks far more dramatic (36–75% across recent years) but is a mirage of a thin equity base, not evidence of an ultra-high-return business — book value per share is only $19.15, deflated by 15 years of buybacks and a $4.2bn accumulated pension-driven comprehensive loss. ROIC, not ROE, is the honest yardstick here, and it says: still a good business, clearly above its cost of capital, but with returns compressing as the network absorbs restructuring costs and loses scale from departing Amazon volume.

PRINCIPLE: Durable earning power, defended by a moat and proven by returns on capital above the cost of capital, is what you are really buying. UPS clears that bar today, but the trend in ROIC — down by more than half in a decade — is the signal to watch, and the fact that its largest customer is also its most dangerous emerging rival is the single sharpest risk to the moat's durability.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

  • Leverage: Net debt = Total debt $24,127mm − Cash $5,887mm = $18,240mm. Against our normalized owner earnings of roughly $5.3–6.2bn total (base case $6.25/share × 848.9mm shares ≈ $5.3bn), that is ≈ 3.0–3.9 years of owner earnings to clear net debt — a moderate burden, manageable given UPS's history of steady (if now-declining) cash generation, but not trivial, especially layered on top of the pension deficit below.
  • Coverage: Operating profit $7,867mm ÷ Interest expense $1,017mm = 7.7× — a solid cushion; an ordinary downturn would have to be severe to threaten interest payments.
  • Liquidity: Current ratio of 1.22× (above) is thin by Graham's classical standard but supported by $3.0bn of undrawn revolving credit and a highly predictable, short-cycle receivables collection pattern (customers pay roughly weekly).
  • Refinancing wall: Debt maturities are well laddered — only $500mm due in 2026, $1.0bn in 2027, $588mm in 2028 — trivial relative to $8.4bn of annual operating cash flow and $5.9bn of cash on hand. No near-term forced refinancing risk.
  • Hidden obligations: The $6.3bn net pension/OPEB deficit (above) is the single largest off-balance-sheet-style claim on future cash — UPS must contribute roughly $1.3–1.4bn a year for the foreseeable future regardless of how the business performs, a real and recurring subtraction from owner earnings that is easy to overlook if you stop at net income. Operating lease obligations add a further $4.5bn of committed payments (note 11), already on balance sheet.

Verdict: adequate, not fortress. an independent research service rates UPS's Financial Strength "A" (strong, but one full notch below the A+/A++ tier it earned a decade ago) — consistent with our read: interest coverage and debt maturities are comfortable, but a thin current ratio, a shrinking equity base, and a genuine multi-billion-dollar pension shortfall mean the balance sheet has less spare capacity than it once did. This matters directly for the margin of safety below: a balance sheet with this much recurring pension and restructuring cash drag deserves a wider discount to intrinsic value, not a narrower one.

PRINCIPLE: Solvency is the precondition that lets every other quality compound. UPS is not fragile, but it is carrying more recurring, off-income-statement cash obligations (pension funding, restructuring costs) than its GAAP earnings alone reveal — verify staying power with the cash flow statement, not the P&L.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Share count discipline: shares outstanding fell from 987mm (2010) to 849mm (2025) — a genuine, if modest, ~14% reduction over 15 years (roughly 1%/year), funded by consistent, if now much-reduced, buybacks: $2.25bn (2023) → $500mm (2024) → $1.0bn (2025). Buybacks were cut sharply exactly as the stock price fell into what our analysis suggests may be undervalued territory (low-$90s to low-$100s) — the opposite of the one-dollar test's ideal (buy more, not less, when the stock is demonstrably cheap). This is a defensible choice given the pension funding and restructuring cash calls competing for the same dollars, but it is a real opportunity cost worth naming: management is not using the buyback lever aggressively at what may be an attractive price.

Dividend discipline is now the central capital-allocation tension. The dividend grew from $1.88/share (2010) to $6.56/share (2025) — a strong, investor-friendly ~9% compound growth rate for over a decade. But the payout ratio (dividends ÷ net income) has ballooned from a sustainable 45–53% (2016–2020) to 92% in 2025 (an independent research service's "All Div'ds to Net Profit" line), because EPS fell far faster than the dividend was cut back. Dividend growth has now effectively stalled ($6.52 → $6.56 → an expected $6.56 again in 2026 — essentially flat for three years) — management's quiet way of defending the payout without formally admitting the old growth rate is no longer earned. This is the one-dollar test working in reverse: with 90%+ of earnings committed to the dividend, there is very little left to reinvest at UPS's still-respectable 17.7% ROIC, which is precisely why growth has stalled — not for lack of good opportunities, but for lack of retained capital.

Acquisitions look disciplined: the 2025 Frigo-Trans and AHG healthcare-logistics acquisitions ($2.0bn combined) were funded from operating cash, not stock — no dilution — and build directly on a stated, coherent strategy (become the leading complex-healthcare-logistics provider; healthcare already generates over $11bn of revenue). The 2024 divestiture of Coyote (low-margin truckload brokerage) for $1.0bn was a sensible pruning of a business that didn't fit. Neither looks like empire-building for its own sake.

PRINCIPLE: Over time you ride with management's capital allocation, so judge it by the one-dollar test, not the press release. UPS's management is candid about its restructuring costs and disciplined on M&A, but the stretched dividend payout ratio is quietly capping the compounding of a still-good business — a fact the dividend announcement itself will never say out loud.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Method chosen: UPS is a mature, capital-intensive, moderately-moated business in a cyclical trough — not a business whose decade-out cash flows are precise enough to trust in a single-point DCF, but stable and cash-generative enough that a normalized-earnings multiple, cross-checked against a short, transition-aware two-stage DCF, is the right combination of tools. We anchor on normalized owner earnings of $6.25/share (base case), built up above from the 2023–2025 average of GAAP-based free cash flow per share.

Anchor 1 — UPS's own historical multiple

An independent research service reports UPS's own 10-year median P/E as 17.0× (printed directly on the report: "Median: 17.0"). Its P/E has ranged from about 14.6× (2022, boom-year earnings) to 21.2× (2013) over the full cycle shown (2010–2025). Applying 17.0× to trailing GAAP EPS of $6.56 gives 17.0 × $6.56 = $111.5/share — almost exactly today's price of $112.47. This is an important, honest cross-check: on the simplest possible mechanical test (today's multiple vs. its own long-run median, on trailing GAAP earnings), UPS looks almost exactly fairly priced, not cheap — provided you are willing to treat a transition-year, restructuring-charge-laden $6.56 as a fair proxy for normal earning power. We are not fully willing to do that (see Owner Earnings section — the multi-year "transformation" charges look partly recurring, and 2025's GAAP number also includes offsetting one-time gains), which is why our own multiple is applied to owner earnings, not to this particular trailing GAAP figure.

Anchor 2 — the peer/industry multiple

UPS's only true direct peer is FedEx (FDX), in the same an independent research service "Air Transport" industry. FedEx's own 10-year median P/E is 14× (per equity_atlas.xlsx), versus UPS's median of 17× — UPS has historically traded at roughly a 20% premium to FedEx, reflecting its historically higher margins and more consistent capital discipline (a larger, steadier dividend). The an independent research service industry report itself notes FedEx is currently executing better than UPS ("It's peer, United Parcel Service, is not doing as well, but this is partly due to disruptions associated with management's repositioning of assets..."), which argues for narrowing, not widening, that historical premium until the turnaround proves out. We treat 14–17× as the peer-informed range, leaning toward the lower half until execution is demonstrated.

Anchor 3 — a quality-and-growth-justified multiple, derived from first principles

Using the manual's growth-value relationship, a justified P/E for a stable payer is approximately:

Justified P/E ≈ (1 − retention rate) ÷ (r − g), where g = retention rate × ROIC

With UPS's payout ratio near 90% (retention ≈ 10–15%), ROIC ≈ 17.7%, and r = 9%:
- g = 0.12 × 17.7% ≈ 2.1%
- Justified P/E = 0.88 ÷ (0.09 − 0.021) = 0.88 ÷ 0.069 ≈ 12.8×

This is materially below the historical median of 17× — and it shows precisely why: a business that pays out 90% of earnings cannot compound at its own high ROIC, no matter how good that ROIC is, because there is so little capital left to reinvest. The stretched dividend is not just a management-quality flag (above); it mechanically caps the multiple the business deserves. If UPS's payout normalizes back toward its historical ~68% (an independent research service's own 2029-31 estimate) as earnings recover, the reinvestment rate rises, g rises toward 4–5%, and the justified multiple recovers toward 15–17×.

Reconciling the three anchors

The three anchors disagree in an informative way: the historical-multiple anchor (17×, on trailing GAAP earnings) says "fair-to-slightly-rich here"; the peer anchor (14–17×) says "roughly in line with FedEx, maybe a touch generous given weaker recent execution"; the quality-derived anchor (12.8×, given today's stretched payout) says "meaningfully cheaper multiple is warranted until reinvestment normalizes." We weight the quality-derived anchor most heavily for the low end of our range (because it correctly penalizes the current payout-driven growth stall) and the historical/peer anchors for the base and high end (because they correctly capture what the multiple could become if the turnaround succeeds and payout normalizes).

Triangulated multiple range: 12.8× (low) → 15.0× (base) → 17.0× (high), applied to owner earnings of $5.50 / $6.25 / $7.25 per share respectively:

  • Low: 12.8 × $5.50 = $70/share
  • Base: 15.0 × $6.25 = $94/share
  • High: 17.0 × $7.25 = $123/share

This recovery-scenario DCF lands almost exactly at our multiple-based high case ($123) — good agreement, and it tells us plainly: our "high" case is really "the turnaround as management describes it, succeeding."

The three universal cross-checks (base case, owner earnings $6.25/share, r = 9%)

  1. No-growth floor = $6.25 ÷ 0.09 = $69.4/share. At our base IV of $94, roughly $25 of the $94 (26%) is a bet on growth/recovery materializing — a modest, not heroic, growth assumption. At today's price of $112.47, more than a third of the price ($43 of $112) depends on growth arriving as forecast.
  2. Implied multiple = Base IV $94 ÷ OE $6.25 = 15.0× — squarely inside our peer and quality-derived range; nothing about the arithmetic implies an ungrounded multiple.
  3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond = $6.25 ÷ $112.47 = 5.56%, versus the 30-year Treasury yield of 4.86–4.9% (Selection & Opinion, July 2026). A modestly positive spread (~65–70 bps) — UPS's claim is priced only slightly better than a riskless bond despite carrying real business and execution risk, which is not a generous cushion.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Triangulated intrinsic value range: $70 (low) — $94 (base) — $123 (high) per share.
Live price (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026): $112.47.

  • Against the base case: Margin of safety = ($94 − $112.47) ÷ $94 = −19.7% — the price is roughly 20% above our base-case value. There is no margin of safety at the base case; there is a premium.
  • Against the high case ($123, essentially "the turnaround succeeds as management projects"): margin of safety = ($123 − $112.47) ÷ $123 = +8.6% — a thin cushion, only available if you believe the optimistic scenario.
  • Against the low case ($70): the price is 61% above — the amount of downside if the turnaround stalls and margins stay compressed.

A revealing, double-edged signal: UPS's dividend yield today (5.8–6.5%, depending on the exact price used) is far above its own 15-year historical range (2.1%–4.7%, an independent research service). A yield this far outside history usually means one of two things: either the market has mispriced a good business out of fear (a bargain), or the market correctly senses the dividend itself is at risk (a warning). Given the payout ratio has climbed to 92% of earnings — precisely the kind of stretched coverage that historically has preceded dividend freezes or cuts at other industrial companies — we read this as more warning than bargain. Mr. Market is not obviously euphoric nor obviously despondent about UPS specifically; he has bid the shares up materially in the last six weeks (Air Transport was one of the seven best-performing an independent research service industries over the trailing six weeks, +14.0%, per Selection & Opinion, July 10, 2026) — a move that looks like it is pricing in the second-half 2026 recovery management has promised, ahead of any confirming evidence in the numbers (Q1 2026 operating profit was still down 24% year over year, per the 10-Q).

The broader market backdrop (an independent research service's Asset Allocation Model: 65% stocks / 10% bonds / 25% cash, a modestly risk-on stance, and major indices near all-time highs) is not a "panic" environment in which wonderful businesses go on sale — which makes it less likely, not more, that UPS's current price reflects unwarranted pessimism. This is a fair-to-rich price on a good-but-not-wonderful business, in a market that is not currently handing out bargains broadly.

PRINCIPLE: Insist on a cushion; use Mr. Market, never obey him. A double-digit dividend yield premium to history is a signal worth investigating, not automatically trusting — here, the stretched payout ratio argues it is closer to a warning than a gift.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Answering the one question we opened with: Is the smaller, higher-margin UPS that emerges from the Amazon-volume reduction and network reconfiguration worth more than the larger, lower-margin UPS of a few years ago? The evidence so far says: not yet, and not obviously. Operating margin has fallen for three straight years, ROIC has been cut by more than half over a decade, and the "turnaround" that management promises for 2027 is a forecast, not a fact — Q1 2026 results moved in the wrong direction. The moat is real (scale, network density, demonstrated pricing power) but is being tested by the one customer large enough to threaten it from the inside.

This is a good business, not a wonderful one, currently priced as if the turnaround already happened. At $112, the market is paying close to the historical-average multiple on a transition-year earnings number and close to our full recovery-scenario intrinsic value — leaving little room for anything to go wrong, in a business where a great deal (union contract renewals through 2028–2030, an Amazon relationship still being unwound, a $6.3bn pension shortfall, goodwill sitting close to impairment in two reporting units) could still go wrong.

Three open questions a student should resolve before getting comfortable:
1. Will the 2027 earnings recovery (to a targeted $8.00/share) actually materialize, or will "Transformation Strategy Costs" simply acquire a fourth program name and continue indefinitely?
2. How much further will Amazon's own delivery network erode UPS's addressable volume beyond the announced 50% cut, and is the higher-margin healthcare/SMB replacement volume large enough to fully backfill it?
3. Is the dividend, at a 92% payout ratio, sustainable at its current level and growth rate, or does a disappointing 2026/2027 force a rebasing that would itself re-rate the stock?

Universal lessons this case teaches most vividly:
- A business can have a real moat and still not be a table-pounding buy at every price — quality justifies a fair price, never any price, and UPS at ~17× trailing, thinly-normalized earnings, with a stretched dividend, is a fair price, not a bargain.
- A yield far outside a stock's own historical range is a question, not an answer — it forces you to ask whether the market is wrong (bargain) or right (warning), and the payout ratio is exactly the kind of arithmetic that resolves the question.
- "Non-recurring" charges that recur for five straight years under three different program names are not non-recurring — earnings-quality discipline means reading what management excludes, not just what it reports.

This is a teaching case, not personalized investment advice.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

  • Owner earnings = Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital; the cash an owner could withdraw without weakening the business. UPS 2025: built here from Operating Cash Flow ($8,450mm) − total capex ($3,685mm) = $4,765mm (~$5.61/share), averaged with 2023–2024 to a normalized ~$6.25/share.
  • Free cash flow (FCF) = Operating cash flow − capital expenditures. UPS 2025: $8,450mm − $3,685mm = $4,765mm.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex — the split between capital spending needed just to sustain today's business versus spending that expands it; a judgment call. UPS's capex is falling toward (and below) its depreciation run-rate, which we treat as mostly maintenance/efficiency spend in today's shrinking-volume environment.
  • Return on invested capital (ROIC) = NOPAT ÷ Invested capital. UPS 2025: $6,120mm ÷ $34,495mm = 17.7%.
  • NOPAT = Operating income × (1 − tax rate). UPS 2025: $7,867mm × (1 − 0.222) = $6,120mm.
  • Invested capital = Debt + Equity − Cash. UPS 2025: $24,127mm + $16,255mm − $5,887mm = $34,495mm.
  • Return on equity (ROE) = Net income ÷ Shareholders' equity. UPS's reported ROE (36–75% in recent years) is inflated by a thin, buyback- and pension-loss-shrunk equity base ($16.3bn) — ROIC is the more honest yardstick here.
  • Interest coverage = Operating income (EBIT) ÷ Interest expense. UPS 2025: $7,867mm ÷ $1,017mm = 7.7×.
  • Net debt = Total debt − Cash; Net debt ÷ owner earnings = years to repay. UPS: ($24,127mm − $5,887mm) ÷ ~$5.3bn total owner earnings ≈ 3.4 years.
  • Current ratio = Current assets ÷ Current liabilities. UPS 2025: $19,045mm ÷ $15,620mm = 1.22×.
  • Discount rate (r) — the owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long-term rates plus a premium for uncertainty; acts as "gravity" on value. We use 9% here, reflecting the ~4.9% 30-year Treasury yield plus a premium for UPS's cyclicality and execution risk.
  • Intrinsic value = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1+r)ᵗ — the master equation: the discounted sum of all future owner earnings.
  • Margin of safety = (Intrinsic value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic value. UPS at the base case: ($94 − $112.47) ÷ $94 = −19.7% (a premium, not a discount).
  • P/E ratio = Price ÷ EPS; justified/normalized P/E is derived from the business's own quality and growth, not asserted. We derived a justified P/E of ≈12.8× for UPS from its ROIC, discount rate, and (thin) retention rate.
  • Price-to-book (P/B) — not the primary lens here (UPS is an operating, not a balance-sheet-driven, business), but worth noting UPS's book value per share ($19.15) is deflated by pension losses and buybacks and should not anchor value for a franchise like this.
  • Dividend yield = Dividend per share ÷ Price; Payout ratio = Dividends ÷ Net income. UPS: yield ≈ 5.8–6.5% (far above its own 2.1–4.7% historical range); payout ratio ≈ 92% (up from ~45–53% a decade ago).
  • Two-stage DCF, terminal value, and the r−g spread: an explicit-period forecast (here, 5 years of recovering owner earnings) plus a terminal value (OEₙ₊₁ ÷ (r−g)) capturing everything after. Our recovery-scenario DCF: ≈$117/share.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value = Owner earnings ÷ r — the floor beneath a DCF. UPS base case: $6.25 ÷ 0.09 = $69.4/share — the value if UPS never grows earnings again.
  • Owner-earnings yield = Owner earnings ÷ Price, compared to the long bond. UPS: $6.25 ÷ $112.47 = 5.56%, versus a ~4.9% 30-year Treasury — a thin, ~65–70 bps spread.
  • Economic moat — a durable competitive advantage (here: network scale and density, demonstrated pricing power) that protects returns from competition; UPS's is real but tested by its largest customer's (Amazon's) own delivery build-out.
  • Circle of competence — the set of businesses an investor can understand well enough to forecast; UPS's simple, well-disclosed economics sit inside it.
  • One-dollar test — retain a dollar of earnings only if it creates at least a dollar of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r); UPS's 92% payout ratio means very little capital is being tested against this standard at all right now — nearly everything is distributed.
  • EBITDA — earnings before interest, tax, depreciation & amortization; not used as a valuation anchor here, consistent with the manual's skepticism of EBITDA as a stand-in for cash flow.
  • Normalized / mid-cycle earnings — the earnings power of a business averaged through its cycle, excluding unusual peaks (here, 2021–2022's pandemic e-commerce boom) and troughs (2025's restructuring-heavy trough), used as the input to valuation rather than any single year's reported number.
