The Procter & Gamble Company (NYSE: PG)

A Value-Investing Seminar — in the Graham–Buffett tradition

July 4, 2026

A teaching case. We value one real business end to end, working P&G's own numbers against the method in How to Value a Business — A Security-Analysis Manual. Nothing here is investment advice; it is a demonstration of a way of thinking.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Let me tell you what Procter & Gamble actually is, because the whole valuation hangs on understanding the machine before we price it.

P&G sells branded, daily-use consumer packaged goods in about 180 countries. It is organized into five reportable segments (10-K, Note 2), and I want you to notice how boring and how enormous each one is (FY2025 net sales, item8 part2):

Segment FY25 Net Sales Marquee brands Stated global share
Fabric & Home Care $29,617M (36%) Tide, Ariel, Downy, Gain, Dawn, Cascade, Febreze >35% fabric, >30% home
Baby, Feminine & Family Care $20,248M (24%) Pampers, Always, Tampax, Bounty, Charmin >30% baby & feminine
Beauty $14,964M (18%) Pantene, Head & Shoulders, Olay, Old Spice, SK-II ~20% hair
Health Care $11,998M (14%) Crest, Oral-B, Vicks, Metamucil, Pepto-Bismol ~30% oral
Grooming $6,662M (8%) Gillette, Venus, Braun >45% grooming, >60% blades

Total FY2025 net sales: $84,284M. Net earnings attributable to P&G: $15,974M. Diluted EPS: $6.51; management's "Core EPS" (stripping the Argentina liquidation charge and the prior-year Gillette impairment): $6.83 (item7 part1).

This is a portfolio of number-one and number-two brands in categories people buy whether the economy is booming or in recession. Gillette holds more than 60% of the global blades-and-razors market. Tide, Ariel, Downy give P&G over 35% of the fabric-care markets it competes in. That is not a company; that is a set of tollbooths on the daily habits of humanity.

The circle-of-competence gate (Manual ch. 4). Can we forecast this business within a sensible range? Yes — and that is the whole point. An independent research service rates P&G's Earnings Predictability at 100 (the maximum) and Price Stability at 100. Look at the per-share earnings march: $3.53 (2010) → $4.52 (2019) → $5.66 (2021) → $6.83 (2025, core). No lost decade, no existential technology threat, no commodity cliff. A diaper is a diaper; a razor cartridge is a razor cartridge. This sits squarely inside the circle. The rare business where you may actually trust a ten-year forecast is the one worth forecasting.

THE ONE QUESTION. For a wonderful, mature, high-return franchise, the analysis almost never turns on quality — it turns on price. So our one question is:

Is this wonderful staples business available today at a fair price — or are we being asked to pay a rich staples multiple for a mid-single-digit grower?

Hold that question. Everything below is built to answer it.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Numbers first, story second. Let us read the three statements as one articulated system (10-K, item8 part1).

The balance sheet (as of June 30, 2025)

The first thing to see is that goodwill + intangibles = $63,560M — more than the entire $52,284M of book equity. Most of that traces to the 2005 Gillette acquisition; the Gillette brand alone carries $12.8 billion as an indefinite-lived intangible (item8 part1, Critical Audit Matter). Note also treasury stock of $(138,702)M — decades of buybacks sit as a giant contra-equity line.

Principle — do not anchor to book value (Manual ch. 7). For a franchise, book value understates the business: P&G's economic goodwill — the real, durable value of Tide and Pampers and Gillette — vastly exceeds any number on the balance sheet, and the accounting goodwill that is there is a historical-cost artifact, not a measure of worth. We will value P&G on the cash it throws off, never on its net assets. Book value per share is roughly $22 (VL); the stock trades at $151. Anyone "anchoring to book" here would have missed the greatest consumer franchise of the last century.

One oddity Graham loved to flag: at June 30, 2025 current liabilities exceeded current assets by $10.7 billion (item7 part3). A current ratio of 25,392 ÷ 36,058 = 0.70 would fail Graham's classic solvency screen. But this is deliberate: P&G runs a negative-working-capital model — it collects from customers and turns inventory faster than it pays its suppliers, financing itself on the float of its trade payables ($15,227M). We will return to this under Financial Strength; it is a feature, not a distress signal.

The income statement — margin by margin

Reading FY2025 top to bottom (item8 part1), and computing each layer:

  • Net sales: $84,284M
  • Cost of products sold: $41,164M → Gross profit $43,120M, gross margin 43,120 ÷ 84,284 = 51.2%
  • SG&A: $22,669M → Operating income $20,451M, operating margin 20,451 ÷ 84,284 = 24.3% (an independent research service, which strips some corporate items, shows ~27.6%)
  • Net earnings attributable to P&G $15,974M → net margin 15,974 ÷ 84,284 = 19.0%

A 51% gross margin and a ~24% GAAP operating margin on $84 billion of soap and razors is the fingerprint of pricing power. The multi-year trend is remarkably flat-to-rising: An independent research service's operating margin runs 26.2% (2016) to 27.6% (2025); net margin 16.0% → 19.9%. Stability of margin is itself moat evidence — competition has not been able to compete these returns away.

Strip the noise. FY2024 operating income was depressed by a $1,341M Gillette impairment; FY2025 net earnings absorbed $801M after-tax from the substantial liquidation of the Argentina operation (a non-cash accumulated-FX charge). That is why management's Core EPS ($6.83) exceeds diluted EPS ($6.51). Neither of these is a recurring operating cost, but note the caution below — P&G takes some restructuring every single year.

The cash-flow statement — where the truth is

How the three articulate. Net income of $15,974M converted into $17,817M of operating cash — OCF exceeds net income, the sign of high earnings quality (D&A and SBC are non-cash add-backs that more than cover the modest working-capital drag). Capex is only $3,773M on $84,284M of sales — 4.5% of sales — because a laundry-detergent plant does not need to be rebuilt every few years. That is the capital-light franchise the Manual prizes (ch. 6): most of the earnings are free. Management's own "adjusted free cash flow" was $14.6 billion at 87% free-cash-flow productivity (FCF ÷ net earnings; item7 part3).

Earnings-quality scan (Manual ch. 7).
- Organic vs. reported growth. FY2025 reported net sales rose just 0.3% ($245M), but organic sales grew 2% (item7 part1) — reported was dragged by FX and the Argentina exit. Nine months into FY2026, organic sales again +2%, reported +4% (FX turned favorable). This is a low-single-digit organic grower dressed up or down by currency. We will forecast off organic reality, not the reported headline.
- Restructuring recurrence — the red flag to respect. P&G discloses that it takes "an ongoing annual level of restructuring... generally $250 to $500 million" every year (item8 part2, Note 3). On top of that sits a new June-2025 "Focused Portfolio, Supply Chain and Productivity Plan": $1.5–$2.0 billion pre-tax over two years, up to 7,000 non-manufacturing jobs cut (item7 part1). Principle: when "one-time" charges recur annually, treat the baseline as a real cost of doing business. We normalize by leaving the ~$250–500M ongoing level inside earnings and only adding back the genuinely episodic pieces (Argentina, Gillette).

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Now to the engine. First the long record, then owner earnings, then the moat that makes the record durable.

EPS roughly doubled over fifteen years (3.53 → 6.83), a ~4.5% compound rate — precisely an independent research service's "10-year 4.5% / est'd 4.5%" earnings-growth reading. Dividends compounded a touch faster (~5.5–6%). This is a mid-single-digit compounder, not a rocket. Own that fact; it is the crux of the price question.

Owner earnings (Manual ch. 7)

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital

Building it for FY2025, per-share (÷ ~2,454M diluted shares implied by $15,974M ÷ $6.51):

  • Core net earnings (normalized): ≈ $16,760M
    • D&A: $2,847M
    • Share-based comp: $476M
  • − Maintenance capex: ≈ $2,900M (P&G's total capex is $3,773M; with D&A at $2,847M and a chunk of spend going to U.S. capacity expansion, maintenance is ~$2.9B and growth capex ~$0.9B)
  • − Incremental working capital: ≈ $300M (a small drag; P&G's WC is deeply negative and barely moves)

Owner earnings ≈ $16,880M, or about $6.88 per share — essentially equal to Core EPS. This equality is itself the teaching point: for a capital-light franchise whose capex barely exceeds depreciation and whose working capital is negative and stable, reported (core) earnings are close to the cash an owner can withdraw. Contrast the old textile mill of the Manual, where maintenance capex chronically devoured the reported profit. Here the accounting tells close to the truth. Two P&G-specific hooks: (1) capex intensity of 4.5% of sales is remarkably low; (2) the negative working capital means growth is partly self-funding — expanding sales releases supplier float rather than absorbing cash.

ROIC — how good are the economics? (Manual ch. 6)

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital

  • NOPAT: operating income $20,451M × (1 − 20.3% tax) = $16,291M
  • Invested capital (debt $34,852M + equity $52,284M − cash $9,556M) = $77,580M
  • ROIC = 16,291 ÷ 77,580 = 21.0%. an independent research service's "Return on Total Capital" reads ~22% — a clean cross-check.

Twenty-one percent on capital, against a discount rate we will set near 8.5–9%, is the signature of a real moat. But now strip the goodwill and acquired intangibles — the $63.6B that Gillette and prior deals loaded onto the books. Invested capital falls to ~$14,020M, and the return on tangible operating capital explodes past 100%.

Principle (Manual ch. 7, economic vs. accounting goodwill): reported ROIC of ~21% is weighed down by the purchase price P&G once paid for its brands. The underlying business — making and selling Tide out of factories and working capital — earns cash-on-tangible returns that are almost off the chart. That gap is exactly why book value is meaningless here and why the franchise deserves a premium multiple. ROE confirms the picture: $15,974M ÷ $52,284M = 30.6% (VL shows ~32%), though note this is flattered by leverage and by the buyback-shrunken equity base — watch it as a levered figure, not a clean one.

The moat — and its ten-year erosion risks (Manual ch. 5)

The moat is a brand-plus-scale-plus-distribution fortress. The diagnostic is pricing power, and P&G shows it in black and white: in the March-2026 quarter, net sales rose on "higher pricing of 1%" and volume +2% (item7, 10-Q) — the ability to raise price without shedding volume. An independent research service's industry review states it plainly: "pricing initiatives have enabled some to pass along higher operating expenses to customers," and demand is "fairly inelastic — customers are likely to replace an empty container of laundry detergent." Buffett's three franchise conditions (Manual ch. 5) all hold: the products are needed, are thought to have no close substitute (brand loyalty on Tide, Pampers, Gillette), and are not price-regulated. Add scale advantages — Walmart alone is 16% of sales, and only a top-three supplier can command that shelf space — and R&D of $2.1B/year that keeps the products genuinely superior.

But durability must be argued, not assumed (Manual ch. 5). The ten-year erosion risks:
1. Private label & retailer power. When wallets tighten, consumers trade down to store brands; and Walmart/Amazon at 16%+ concentration have their own leverage. P&G's answer is "irresistible superiority" — but this is the permanent war.
2. Demographics & geography. Half of sales are international (item7); Greater China, historically a growth engine, has wavered.
3. FX and geopolitics. The Argentina liquidation and an independent research service's note on the Strait of Hormuz / input-cost spikes show how much a globe-spanning staple can be buffeted by currency and commodities.
4. Slow-growth ceiling. The moat protects the returns; it does not create growth. This is a 2–4% organic grower. The risk to the investor is not that the moat breaks — it is paying a growth multiple for a no-growth-plus-inflation reality.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Can it survive a bad year with a shut credit market? Five questions (Manual ch. 8), answered with P&G's own numbers.

  • Leverage. Total debt $34,852M (10-K contractual table) less cash $9,556M = net debt ~$25,296M. Against owner earnings of ~$16,880M, that is net debt ÷ OE ≈ 1.5× — P&G could clear all its net debt from about a year and a half of owner earnings. An independent research service puts long-term debt at 30% of total capital. For a business this stable, that is conservative.
  • Coverage. Operating income $20,451M ÷ interest expense $907M = 22.5× interest coverage (an independent research service's total-interest figure gives ~24×). A routine recession does not dent this; you would need a catastrophe. Fortress-level.
  • Liquidity. The current ratio of 0.70 looks alarming by Graham's old rule, but it is the deliberate negative-working-capital model — P&G finances itself on $15.2B of trade payables. The proof it works: 87% free-cash-flow productivity and an unbroken dividend. This is one place the classic Graham screen must yield to understanding the business.
  • Refinancing wall. The maturity ladder is well spread (item7 part3): of $34,852M total debt, $9,553M is due in under a year, $6,801M in 1–3 years, $6,150M in 3–5 years, $12,348M beyond five. Total interest payments of $5,605M spread over the whole schedule (~$878M/yr). Credit ratings Aa3 (Moody's) / AA− (S&P), stable (item7 part3). P&G controls its own fate; it is not dependent on the kindness of lenders.
  • Hidden obligations. Pension is modest and largely outside the U.S.: the balance sheet carries a $3,026M pension obligation (item8 part3), minimum required funding is only $617M total (item7 part3), and an independent research service notes plan assets ($8.175B) actually exceed the obligation ($7.653B). No time bomb here. No material off-balance-sheet financing (item7 part3).

One-line verdict: FORTRESS. an independent research service's Financial Strength A++ and Safety rank 1 are earned. This balance sheet will never make P&G a forced seller.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Judge management on the six uses of a dollar (Manual ch. 9), the one-dollar test, and candor.

Capital allocation, the six uses:
1. Reinvest in the business — modest capex (4.5% of sales) at very high incremental returns; correct for a capital-light franchise.
2. Acquisitions — deliberately quiet lately ($11M in FY2025, $765M in FY2023). After the Gillette experience (an eventual $1.3B impairment) and the recent exit of the Glad JV (~$500M proceeds, ~$250–300M gain in FY2026), management is pruning the portfolio, not empire-building. Encouraging.
3. Pay down debt — kept roughly flat; leverage is conservative and stable.
4. Dividends — the crown jewel. In FY2025 P&G paid $9,872M in dividends. The board just raised the quarterly payout 3% (to $1.089), the 70th consecutive annual increase — a genuine Dividend King. Dividends per share: 1.80 (2010) → 4.08 (2025) → 4.32E (2026).
5. Buybacks — judge on price. P&G repurchased $6,500M of stock in FY2025 and has spent $5–11B/year for a decade. Share count fell from 2,838M (2010) to 2,342M (2025) — a ~17% reduction, roughly 1.2%/yr, that quietly lifts per-share everything. The Manual's warning (ch. 9): buybacks create value only below intrinsic value. With the stock near our estimate of fair value (below), recent buybacks are roughly value-neutral — fine, not brilliant. If the stock ran to 28–30× earnings, continued buying would start destroying per-share value.
6. Share issuance — minimal; option grants are small (SBC $476M) and more than offset by repurchases. Share count is respected.

The one-dollar test (Manual ch. 9). Over the decade P&G retained roughly 40% of earnings (payout ~60%) and grew EPS ~4.5% while ROE ran ~30% and ROIC ~21%. Retained dollars earning 20%+ against a ~9% cost of capital clearly create more than a dollar of value each — P&G passes the one-dollar test. The honest caveat: with only 2–4% organic reinvestment runway, the marginal retained dollar increasingly finds its best home in dividends and buybacks rather than in the business — which is exactly the high payout we observe.

Candor. Management reports Core vs. GAAP transparently, disclosed the Gillette impairment and the Argentina liquidation plainly, and flagged the ongoing (not "one-time") nature of restructuring. Note also a CEO transition between the March and June an independent research service reports (Jon Moeller → Shailesh Jejurikar per the June sheet) — a thing to watch, though P&G's promote-from-within bench is deep. In April 2026 the board rightly advised holders to reject a $100/share mini-tender offer as a "30% discount to market value" — a small but telling defense of shareholders.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

We anchor on normalized owner earnings ≈ Core EPS ≈ $6.88/share (FY2025), rising to ~$7.00 in FY2026. Because P&G is a stable, mature, high-ROIC franchise, the Manual points us to the normalized-earnings multiple as primary, a dividend-discount cross-check, and a two-stage DCF — the methods a slow, predictable compounder deserves. We build a range, not a point.

The discount rate — gravity (Manual ch. 12)

The 10-year Treasury sits at 4.38%, the 30-year at 4.86% (Selection & Opinion, 2026-07-10), in a hardening rate regime — PCE inflation 4.1%, the Fed on hold at 3.50–3.75% with "half of FOMC members still expecting at least one hike this year." For a business with Beta 0.65, Earnings Predictability 100, and A++ strength, a modest equity risk premium is appropriate. We anchor r = 8.5% as base, and show 8.0% and 9.0% as sensitivities. Rates are gravity: were the 10-year to fall back toward 3%, every value below would lift; were inflation to force it higher, they would fall.

Method 1 — Normalized-earnings multiple (primary)

Where does the multiple come from? Three independent roads (Manual ch. 14 discipline — we name our multiples and reconcile):

  1. P&G's own history (years cited). an independent research service's average annual P/E: 16–17× in 2010–2013, rising to 23.8× (2021), 25.7× (2022), 24.5× (2023), 23.5× (2024), 24.6× (2025). Median P/E = 23.0. The stock has re-rated over the decade from a mid-teens multiple to a low-20s multiple as rates fell and staples became bond proxies.
  2. Peers / industry. The Household & Personal Care group — Colgate (CL), Kimberly-Clark (KMB), Clorox (CLX), Church & Dwight (CHD), Unilever (UL), Kenvue — trades at broadly similar low-20s multiples; P&G, the highest-quality and largest, has historically earned a slight premium. (The Manual is wary of peer multiples as a primary anchor — they import Mr. Market's opinion of other businesses — so we use them only as a sanity rail.)
  3. Quality-justified from first principles. A franchise earning 21% ROIC, growing owner earnings ~4.5%, discounted at 8.5%, with ~60% of earnings paid out and the rest reinvested above the cost of capital, supports a mid-20s multiple. A Gordon-style payout model (60% payout, g 4.5–5%, r 8.5%) yields ~16–18× on the paid-out portion alone; crediting the value-creating retained portion lifts the justified multiple toward the low-to-mid 20s.

Reconciling: all three roads land at roughly 21–24× normalized earnings. Applying that band to FY2026 core EPS of ~$7.00:

  • 21× → $147 · 22× → $154 · 23× (VL median) → $161 · 24× → $168

We take the multiple method's fair range as ~$147–$168, midpoint ~$157.

Method 2 — Dividend-discount cross-check (Manual, Gordon growth)

P&G is a Dividend King — the DDM fits. Inputs, justified against the record:
- D₁ (next-year dividend) ≈ $4.42 (current ~$4.23 annualized × ~1.045)
- g = 4.5% — matched to an independent research service's 4.5% projected dividend-growth rate and the 5.5% ten-year record (we use the conservative forward figure)
- r = 8.5%

Value = D₁ ÷ (r − g) = 4.42 ÷ (0.085 − 0.045) = $110

Sensitivity: at r 8.0% / g 5.0% → $148; at r 9.0% / g 4.0% → $88. The DDM base clusters near $110. Why it reads low: the DDM counts only the ~60% paid out and ignores the value created by the ~40% retained at 20%+ ROIC. For a company that compounds real value inside the business, the DDM is a deliberately conservative floor on the income, not the full worth. It tells us the dividend alone, discounted, is worth ~$110 — useful as a lower bound.

Method 3 — Two-stage DCF (Manual ch. 10)

Owner earnings $6.88/share; Stage 1 growth 4–6% for 10 years (the moat's horizon), terminal g 2.5–3.0% (below the ~4–5% nominal economy cap):

r g₁ 4% / gₜ 2.5% g₁ 5% / gₜ 3.0% g₁ 6% / gₜ 3.0%
8.0% $144 $166 $180
8.5% $132 $151 $163
9.0% $121 $138 $149

DCF base case (r 8.5%, g₁ 5% / gₜ 3%) = $151.

The three mandatory cross-checks (Manual ch. 14)

1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r. $6.88 ÷ 0.085 = $81 (range $76 at 9% to $86 at 8%). So of a ~$150 price, about $81 is the business standing still and ~$69 is the payment for growth — a bit under half the price rests on P&G actually compounding at the mid-single digits an independent research service projects. That is a reasonable, not heroic, bet for a franchise with 100/100 earnings predictability.

2. Implied multiple = value ÷ OE. Our DCF base of $151 ÷ $6.88 = 21.9× owner earnings. Is that defensible for a 21%-ROIC franchise growing ~5% then ~3%? Yes — it sits below an independent research service's 23 median and squarely in the historical band. The machine has not run away; DCF and multiple are telling the same story in two languages. ✓

3. Owner-earnings / dividend yield vs. the long bond. At $151, owner-earnings yield = $6.88 ÷ $151 = 4.55%; the dividend yield is 2.79%. Against a 10-year Treasury at 4.38%, the owner-earnings yield is barely above the bond — but P&G's "coupon" grows ~4.5% a year while the bond's is static. So the claim is superior to the bond, but only modestly so at this price; the cushion is thin. If the stock were at 25–28× (owner-earnings yield below 4%), it would lean entirely on growth arriving — a warning. ✓ (with caution)

Our low ($128) exceeds the no-growth floor ($81) and the rejected $100 mini-tender — consistent with a franchise that is clearly worth more than its liquidation-of-growth value. The triangulation coheres.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Now, and only now, we consult Mr. Market. Today he quotes $151.41 (equity_atlas, 2026-07-04).

  • Price vs. IV. $151.41 sits essentially at our base case of $150 — a premium of about 1% to base, a discount of ~11% to our high ($170), and a premium of ~18% to our low ($128).
  • Margin of safety = (IV − Price) ÷ IV. Against base: (150 − 151.41) ÷ 150 = −0.9% — no margin of safety. Against the high case: (170 − 151.41) ÷ 170 = +11% — a thin one. Against the low case, the price is above value.
  • Yield check. Today's dividend yield of 2.79% sits at the low end of P&G's own historical band (an independent research service shows 3.0–3.4% in 2010–2019, compressing to 2.4–2.5% in 2020–2025). A yield near the bottom of history is Mr. Market telling you the stock is fully priced by its own standards — not euphoric, but not cheap.
  • Mr. Market's mood on staples. Note the whipsaw the Manual would have you exploit: the stock was $163.51 at an independent research service's March review, fell ~14% to $140.28 by the June review, and has since bounced to $151.41. Staples are out of favor for year-ahead action (industry Timeliness 75/94; P&G's own Timeliness lowered to 4) even as the group just had a strong six-week run. This is the volatility the Manual calls raw material — a further pullback toward the $128–$135 zone would open a real margin of safety on a business whose value has not changed.

Where the safety sits (Manual ch. 13). For a mediocre business, all safety must come from the purchase discount. For P&G, part of the safety lives in the quality itself — a conservatively projected, dividend-growing, 100/100-predictable earnings stream makes today's estimate close to the floor of tomorrow's. But quality is not a license to pay any price. At $151, you are paying for the quality in full and getting essentially no discount.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: WONDERFUL — a wide-moat, 21%-ROIC, cash-on-tangible-capital-over-100%, A++ balance-sheet, Dividend-King franchise, as close to a perpetual toll on daily human habit as public markets offer.

The investment call at $151.41: FAIR PRICE, NOT A BARGAIN — ADMIRE AND WAIT. This is a wonderful business trading at roughly fair value, with no margin of safety against our base case. The Manual's rule is explicit (ch. 13–14): below the conservative end of the range you buy in size; at or above it, however good the business, you do nothing and wait. At $151 we are essentially at base value. GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with P&G's numbers)

Intrinsic value. The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. For P&G we triangulate to $128 / $150 / $170 per share.

Owner earnings. Reported (normalized) earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. P&G FY2025: 16,760 + 2,847 + 476 − 2,900 − 300 ≈ $16,880M ≈ $6.88/share.

Maintenance capex. Capital spending to preserve competitive position and unit volume, vs. growth capex. P&G total capex $3,773M; we split ~$2,900M maintenance / ~$0.9B growth (capex is only 4.5% of sales).

ROIC. NOPAT ÷ invested capital. P&G: $16,291M ÷ $77,580M = 21.0%; ex-goodwill/intangibles, >100%. Compare to r ~8.5% — value-creating.

ROE. Net income ÷ equity. P&G: 15,974 ÷ 52,284 = 30.6% (levered; buyback-shrunk equity — read with care).

Discount rate (r). Opportunity cost, anchored to long rates plus a premium. P&G base r = 8.5% (10-yr Treasury 4.38% + premium; Beta 0.65).

No-growth (earning-power) value. OE ÷ r = worth if it never grows again. P&G: 6.88 ÷ 0.085 = $81 — the floor; ~half of a $150 price is payment for growth.

Two-stage DCF. Explicit forecast + terminal value. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. P&G base (OE $6.88, g₁ 5%/10yr, gₜ 3%, r 8.5%) = $151.

Dividend-discount (Gordon). Value = D₁/(r−g). P&G: 4.42 ÷ (0.085−0.045) = $110 — income floor; ignores value of retained earnings.

Sustainable growth. g = reinvestment rate × ROIC. P&G: ~40% × 21% ≈ 8.4% ceiling; actual ~4.5% (limited category runway), the rest returned to owners.

Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest. P&G: 20,451 ÷ 907 = 22.5×. Fortress.

Net debt / owner earnings. (Total debt − cash) ÷ OE. P&G: (34,852 − 9,556) ÷ 16,880 = ~1.5×. A year and a half of cash clears the net debt.

Margin of safety. (IV − Price) ÷ IV. P&G at $151 vs base $150 = −0.9% (none); vs high $170 = +11% (thin).

Economic vs. accounting goodwill. Economic goodwill = franchise value above tangible capital (P&G: enormous — cash-on-tangible >100%). Accounting goodwill = $41,650M balance-sheet figure from acquisitions. Book value understates the franchise; never anchor to it.

Moat / economic franchise. Durable advantage protecting high returns; signature is pricing power. P&G: 45–60%+ category shares; +1% pricing with +2% volume; needed, no-close-substitute, unregulated products.

Core EPS (P&G non-GAAP). Diluted EPS excluding non-recurring items. FY2025 $6.83 vs GAAP $6.51 (adds back $801M Argentina charge, nets prior-year Gillette). We use core as the earning-power anchor.

Organic sales growth (P&G non-GAAP). Net sales excluding acquisitions/divestitures and FX. FY2025 +2%, FY2026 nine-month +2% — the real growth rate beneath a currency-noisy headline.

Adjusted free cash flow productivity (P&G non-GAAP). Adjusted FCF ÷ net earnings. FY2025 87% — near-complete conversion of profit to cash, the hallmark of the capital-light franchise.
