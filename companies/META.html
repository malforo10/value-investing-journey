Meta Platforms, Inc. (META) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Co-taught by Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett
Date: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $582.90

A teaching case. Nothing here is investment advice. We are using a real company to practice a method — the one laid out in How to Value a Business: A Security-Analysis Manual — not to issue a recommendation.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

WARREN: Let me tell you what you are buying when you buy a share of Meta, because it is genuinely two businesses stapled together, and the whole art of this case is refusing to let them blur.

Business One — the Family of Apps (FoA). Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger. In December 2025 these reached 3.58 billion daily active people — call it two of every five human beings alive — up 7% from 3.35 billion a year earlier (10-K, item7 MD&A). Meta shows them advertisements. That is essentially the entire revenue engine: of $200.97 billion in 2025 total revenue, the Family of Apps produced $198.76 billion and Reality Labs a rounding-error $2.21 billion (10-K, Note 15, Segment Information). The way the money is made is elegant and, once you see it, almost boring in its durability: more people, more time, more ad impressions, sold at higher prices. In 2025, average revenue per person (ARPP) was $57.03, up 15%; in Q1 2026, ad impressions rose 19% and the average price per ad rose 12% (10-Q MD&A). Volume and price, together. That is the fingerprint of pricing power.

Business Two — Reality Labs (RL). The metaverse, augmented- and virtual-reality hardware, neural interfaces, and now the AI-infrastructure ambition management calls "Superintelligence." Financially, RL is not a business; it is a hole. In 2025 it earned $2.21 billion of revenue and posted a $19.19 billion operating loss (Note 15). Management tells you plainly it "expect[s] our 2026 RL operating losses to remain similar to 2025" and that these are bets on "products that may only be fully realized in the next decade" (item7 MD&A part1).

BEN: So the arithmetic of the whole company is this: a magnificent advertising franchise earning $102.47 billion of segment operating income, minus a $19.19 billion annual voluntary loss, nets to $83.28 billion of consolidated operating income (Note 15; Consolidated Statements of Income). The franchise is carrying the experiment on its back. The principle — Manual Chapter 8's special case and Chapter 7's normalization — is that when a company contains a wonderful business and a capital sink under one roof, you do not value the blur. You value each part on its own economics and then combine them. A single consolidated P/E prices both wrong.

The circle-of-competence gate (Manual Ch. 4). WARREN: Honesty first. I can forecast the Family of Apps within a sensible range — a business selling attention to 3.5 billion people, growing ad price and volume, is inside my circle after twenty years of watching it. What I cannot forecast is whether $19 billion a year poured into headsets and "superintelligence," plus $115-135 billion of capital expenditure guided for 2026 alone (item7 MD&A part2), earns its cost of capital. That is a venture-capital forecast wearing a large-cap suit. Chapter 4 says "too hard" is a respectable answer — so we will not pretend to value the AI moonshot as if it were a bond. We value what we can and treat what we cannot as an option with a wide error bar.

THE ONE QUESTION:

Is a wonderful advertising franchise being handed to us at a fair price — or is that franchise being used to fund an AI-and-metaverse build-out so vast that it consumes the very earnings we are paying for?

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

BEN: Pull the three statements side by side; they only tell the truth when read together.

The Balance Sheet (Consolidated Balance Sheets, 12/31/2025)

  • Cash + marketable securities: $35.87B + $45.72B = $81.59 billion. Add $27.52 billion of non-marketable equity investments (up from $6.07B — this is the AI-partner and infrastructure investing).
  • Long-term debt: $58.74 billion (senior unsecured notes, maturing 2027-2064), up sharply from $28.83B — Meta issued ~$30B of notes in November 2025 to help fund the build-out.
  • Property & equipment, net: $176.40 billion, up from $121.35B — the data-center army.
  • Operating lease liabilities: $2.21B current + $22.94B non-current = $25.15 billion present value; finance leases add only $1.18B on the balance sheet.
  • Stockholders' equity: $217.24 billion; retained earnings $121.18B.

WARREN: Note two things a careless reader misses. First, net cash is thinner than Meta's reputation suggests: cash-and-securities $81.59B minus debt $59.93B (notes + finance leases) is only $21.7 billion of net cash, about $8.56 per share. The fortress balance sheet is being spent down and levered up to feed capex. Second — and this is the hidden obligation of Manual Chapter 8 — the leases on the balance sheet ($26B) are dwarfed by leases not yet commenced: approximately $103.77 billion, plus $131.05 billion of non-cancelable contractual commitments (cloud capacity, servers, data centers), with $30.63B due in 2026 alone (Note 11). That is roughly $235 billion of future spending contracted but invisible on the face of the balance sheet. The tide is a long way from going out on these commitments.

BEN: Look at the paradox in the last two rows. Operating income rose 20% in 2025, yet net income fell from $62.36B to $60.46B and diluted EPS slipped from $23.86 to $23.49. Why? A tax event. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, enacted July 2025, forced a $15.93 billion charge in Q3 2025 (mostly a $14.03B valuation allowance against deferred tax assets), pushing the effective tax rate to 29.6% ($25.47B ÷ $85.93B pretax) versus just 11.8% in 2024 (item7 MD&A part2; and see the Q3 2025 net income of only $2.71B in the XBRL facts — a one-quarter tax air-pocket). Management guides the 2026 rate back to 13-16% (MD&A part2).

This is Chapter 7 in the flesh: reported 2025 earnings are understated by a one-time tax item. Normalize it. At a 15% tax rate on $85.93B of pretax income, normalized net income is about $73.0 billion (≈ $28.87/share), and net income excluding the OBBBA charge is $76.4 billion. We will value the normalized figure, not the headline.

WARREN: There it is in one table. The Family of Apps runs a 51.6% operating margin — a level of profitability almost no business on earth sustains — and grew its profit 18% in 2025. Reality Labs has lost $53 billion over three years with revenue going essentially nowhere. The core is a masterpiece; the experiment is a running tab.

The Cash Flow Statement (Consolidated Statements of Cash Flows)

  • Operating cash flow: $115.80 billion (2025), up from $91.33B. Note OCF exceeds net income by $55B — because we add back $18.62B depreciation, $20.43B share-based compensation, and $18.74B of deferred taxes (the non-cash side of the OBBBA charge).
  • Capital expenditure: purchases of P&E $69.69B + finance-lease principal $2.52B = $72.22 billion — up from $39.2B in 2024. This is the AI build-out.
  • Free cash flow (Meta's own definition, MD&A part3): $115.80B − $72.22B = $43.59 billion — and notice FCF actually fell from $52.10B in 2024, even as OCF rose, because capex outran it.

How the three articulate: BEN — the income statement's rising operating profit ($83B) is real; the cash flow statement shows $116B of operating cash generated but $72B consumed by capex, so only $44B reaches the owner as free cash; and the balance sheet records where that capex went — P&E up $55B — and how the gap was funded — $30B of new debt. Read alone, each statement flatters or frightens. Read together, the story is coherent: a gusher of operating cash, most of it being reinvested in infrastructure faster than it can be proven to earn a return.

Earnings-quality scan (Chapter 7)

  • SBC is a real cost, and a big one: $20.43 billion in 2025, larger than the RL loss, with $54.81 billion of unrecognized SBC still to come over ~3 years (Note, item8 part5). We will not add it back in owner earnings; a dollar of stock handed to an employee is a dollar taken from us.
  • Capex capitalization: In January 2025 Meta extended the useful lives of most servers to 5.5 years, cutting 2025 depreciation by $2.92 billion and lifting net income by $1.00/diluted share (Note 1). Legitimate, disclosed — but a reminder that reported D&A understates the true consumption of a fast-obsolescing asset base. It argues for treating maintenance capex conservatively.
  • Segment disclosure: Exemplary. Meta hands you FoA and RL income separately (Note 15). We should thank them and use it.
  • Don't anchor to book: BEN — equity is $217B against a market cap of $1.48 trillion. The economic goodwill of the Family franchise is nowhere on the balance sheet (Chapter 7). Book value is almost irrelevant here except as a solvency check.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

WARREN: Now the heart of it. What can this business actually earn for an owner, and will it last?

The earning-power record (XBRL, verified)

Revenue compounded from $27.6B (2016) to $201.0B (2025) — about 25% a year for a decade. Operating income went from $12.4B to $83.3B. This is one of the great earning-power records in the history of commerce. An independent research service's decade array (2025 report) shows the same: revenues per share from $9.56 (2016) to $79.43 (2025), earnings per share from $3.49 to $29.69 (VL's adjusted figure).

Owner earnings — and the judgment that decides this case

The Manual's formula (Ch. 7): Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital, keeping SBC as a cost.

For Meta the whole game is the maintenance-vs-growth capex split. Capex in 2025 was $72.2 billion against depreciation of $18.6 billion. Is Meta really consuming $72B a year to stand still? Emphatically no. The Family of Apps ran perfectly well on a fraction of this; the incremental $50B+ is growth capex — new data centers and GPUs betting on AI and future ad-load.

  • Maintenance capex — the spend to keep 3.5 billion people served and the ad system humming — is best proxied by depreciation, ~$18.6 billion (arguably even less, given the useful-life extension).
  • The rest is discretionary growth/venture capex, and by Chapter 7's logic it does not reduce owner earnings — it is a choice, funded from owner earnings, whose value depends entirely on its return.

So whole-company owner earnings, normalized:

Normalized NI ($73.0B at a 15% tax rate) + D&A ($18.6B) − Maintenance capex (~$18.6B) − ΔWC (~0) ≈ $73.0 billion, or ≈ $28.87 per share.

(Working capital is roughly neutral: Meta collects from advertisers fast and its receivables growth is modest against its scale.)

BEN: But Chapter 8 tells us to go further and value the core separately, because the RL drag is buried inside that $73B. The Family of Apps alone produced $102.47 billion of operating income; taxed at a normalized 15%, that is ≈ $87.1 billion of core NOPAT, or ≈ $34.43 per share of pure franchise earning power — before the $19B RL loss and before growth capex. That is the number the franchise is worth a multiple of.

ROIC and ROE — the quality verdict (Chapter 6)

  • Consolidated ROIC = NOPAT ($83.28B × 0.85 = $70.8B) ÷ invested capital (equity $217B + debt $60B − cash & securities $82B = $196B) = ≈ 36%.
  • Core FoA earns far higher — its $87B of NOPAT sits on a much smaller operating-capital base than the consolidated figure (which is bloated by the AI build-out and the cash pile), so franchise ROIC is comfortably north of 40%.
  • ROE = normalized $73B ÷ $217B equity = ≈ 34% (an independent research service shows 35.2% for 2025).

Against a discount rate of ~9%, these returns are enormous. This is the "capital-light franchise" of Chapter 6 — at the core. The complication is that management is now choosing to make it capital-hungry by bolting on an AI build-out. Chapter 11's test looms: does that reinvestment earn above r?

The moat (Chapter 5)

The Family of Apps has a genuine, multi-layered moat:
1. Network effects — your friends and family are on it; a rival must recreate 3.5 billion relationships, not one app.
2. Data and ad-targeting scale — two decades of behavioral data make Meta's ad targeting hard to match; the 12% rise in price per ad (Q1 2026) is the pricing-power signature Chapter 5 demands.
3. Scale economics — R&D and infrastructure spread across 3.5 billion users; no sub-scale rival can match cost per user.

An independent research service grades Financial Strength A++ and the Internet-Industry report (May 2026) names Meta a top pick alongside Alphabet.

The 10-year erosion risks (Chapter 5's "argue durability, don't assume it")

  • Regulation / antitrust: FTC consent-order modification fight; EU GDPR (a €1.2B fine already); California and New Mexico "addictive-design" suits; a $725M privacy settlement (Note 11 and item3). A forced change to the ad-targeting model is the single biggest moat threat.
  • Competition for attention: TikTok and the next thing — the young are fickle.
  • AI disruption of search/social: the same AI Meta is spending on could commoditize the feed (the Internet-Industry report flags exactly this fear).
  • Ad-market cyclicality: ~98% of revenue is advertising — a recession hits it directly (recall 2022, when net income fell to $23.2B).
  • Platform dependence: Apple and Google own the phones; ATT's 2021 privacy change already cost Meta billions.

WARREN: The moat around the core is wide and, I judge, durable for a decade. The risk is not that Facebook disappears; it is regulation reshaping the ad model, and management's own appetite to spend the franchise's earnings on an uncertain future.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY (Chapter 8)

Run the numbers even though the answer is obvious:

  • Leverage: Net debt = cash & securities $81.59B − total debt $59.93B = $21.7B of NET CASH. Net Debt / Owner Earnings is negative. Even on gross debt, $59.93B ÷ $73B owner earnings = 0.82 years to clear it from one year's cash. (an independent research service: LT Debt / Op Cash Flow = 0.51×.)
  • Coverage: Interest expense is trivial — cash paid for interest, net, was $696 million (2025 cash flow statement) against $83.28B of operating income. Interest coverage ≈ 120×. The interest bill is a rounding error.
  • Liquidity: Current assets $108.72B vs. current liabilities $41.84B — current ratio 2.6×. Graham's precondition is met with room to spare.
  • Maturities: Debt is well-laddered, 2027 through 2064; no refinancing cliff.
  • Hidden obligations: The real leverage, per Chapter 8, is off the balance sheet — $103.77B of leases not yet commenced + $131.05B of contractual commitments. These are not debt, but they are contractually promised cash. A business generating $116B of operating cash can carry them; a sharp ad recession would make them uncomfortable.

One-line verdict: A fortress — but a fortress that is deliberately taking on obligations and debt to fund a build-out. Solvency is not remotely in question; the question is return on the spend, not survival.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS (Chapter 9)

BEN: The six uses of a dollar, judged on Meta's record.

  1. Reinvest in the existing business: For the Family of Apps, incremental ROIC has been spectacular (>40%) — textbook value-creating reinvestment. This is the good kind.
  2. The AI/RL build-out — the biggest capital-allocation question in this case: $19B/year of RL losses (guided flat for 2026) plus $115-135B of 2026 capex. Apply the one-dollar test (Ch. 9): will each retained dollar create at least a dollar of value? For the ad-serving portion of AI capex — better targeting, more engagement — plausibly yes; the 12% price-per-ad gains hint the machine already pays. For the Superintelligence/RL portion, it is an unproven bet. Unsure, and honest people should say so.
  3. Pay down debt: N/A — Meta is adding debt cheaply to fund capex; defensible at these rates given the returns on the core.
  4. Dividends: Initiated March 2024; $2.10/share in 2025 ($5.32B paid). A small, rational return of a sliver of excess cash.
  5. Buybacks — judged on price vs. IV (Ch. 9): $26.25B repurchased in 2025 (40M shares), $30.13B in 2024, $130B+ over five years (XBRL). At an average well below our base intrinsic value, these have been value-creating — Chapter 9's condition (buy below IV) has largely been met. $25.03B remains authorized.
  6. Share count / SBC: Here is the candor test. Over the decade, shares went from ~2,892M (2016) to 2,530M (2025) — down ~12% net, despite $20B+/year of SBC. Buybacks have more than absorbed dilution. But note: the net-share-settlement of RSUs cost $18.40 billion of cash in 2025 (financing activities) — SBC is not free; Meta pays cash to keep the count down. Keep it in owner earnings, which we did.

Governance candor (the dual-class point): Mark Zuckerberg controls ~99.8% of Class B super-voting shares (10 votes each), giving him majority voting control of a $1.5 trillion company while owning a minority of the economics (an independent research service; item1). BEN — you are a passenger, not a partner, in governance. The AI spend is his call, and there is no board vote you can win to stop it. That is a real, permanent feature of this investment. It cuts both ways: founder control let Meta make the (correct, in hindsight) 2022 "Year of Efficiency" pivot fast — but it also means the RL/AI bet continues as long as one man believes in it.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

WARREN: Meta is the textbook case for the Manual's two-column method (Ch. 8) plus a DCF cross-check — a wonderful operating business, a large cash-and-securities pile, and a distinct capital sink. We value the parts and sum them, then sanity-check with a whole-company DCF.

Anchor: normalized owner earnings

  • Core (Family of Apps) NOPAT: ≈ $87.1B, or $34.43/share (FoA operating income $102.47B × 0.85).
  • Whole-company owner earnings (normalized): ≈ $73.0B, or $28.87/share.

Method A — Two-column (core value + net cash − RL drag)

Where the multiple comes from — and the reconciliation (Ch. 10, Ch. 14):

  1. Historical median P/E: an independent research service reports a median P/E of 22 and a trailing P/E of 22.6 (2026 report). Over 2016-2025 META's average annual P/E ranged from ~17.5 (2023) to ~33.5 (2016), settling around 21-23 in recent years.
  2. Peer multiple: Alphabet (GOOGL), the closest comparable ad-franchise, and the broader mega-cap Internet group trade in the low-to-mid 20s on forward earnings. Meta at a live P/E of 21.2 sits at a slight discount to that group. (BEN's caution, Ch. 14: peer multiples anchor us to Mr. Market's opinion of other firms — use as a check, never as the anchor.)
  3. Quality-justified multiple from first principles: A 40%+ ROIC franchise growing ad revenue double-digits, with pricing power, deserves a premium multiple. Chapter 14's arithmetic: a business growing owner earnings ~10-12% then fading to 3%, at r = 9%, supports something in the low-to-mid 20s× on a clean earnings stream.

Reconciliation: All three roads converge on ~17-24× core NOPAT as the defensible band — the low end applies a haircut for regulatory and AI-spend uncertainty; the high end credits the franchise's quality.

Now combine (per share):

Core FoA NOPAT × multiple $34.43 × 17 = $585 $34.43 × 21 = $723 $34.43 × 24 = $826

RL drag: capitalize five years of after-tax RL losses (~$16.3B/yr) discounted at 9% ≈ $63B ≈ $25/share — treating the metaverse/AI moonshot as a five-year cash burn before it either turns or is curtailed. In the high case we credit half of it back, betting the "option" has some positive expected value. Note the drag is deliberately bounded: capitalizing $19B/year losses into perpetuity would be its own error (Ch. 7 — don't capitalize a peak, or here a trough, forever).

Method B — Two-stage DCF (whole-company owner earnings), as a cross-check

Starting owner earnings $28.87/share, explicit 10-year growth then terminal g, r = 9% (Ch. 10, 12):

BEN: The DCF runs hot — $850 to $1,300 — precisely because it capitalizes double-digit growth over a full decade. That is the machine faithfully reporting what today's momentum implies. But Chapter 10's guardrail says interrogate the implied multiple: the DCF's central $986 is 34× owner earnings, which bakes in a decade of flawless double-digit compounding and assumes the AI capex quietly earns its keep. We do not have the circle-of-competence confidence to underwrite that. So we let the DCF define the optimistic ceiling and anchor our conclusion on the more conservative two-column method.

The three cross-checks (Ch. 14), as arithmetic

  1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r. Whole-company: $28.87 ÷ 0.09 = $321/share. Core FoA NOPAT: $34.43 ÷ 0.09 = $383/share. At the live price of $582.90, roughly $260 of the price — about 45% — is a payment for growth. Everything below ~$320-380 is what the business is worth if it never grows another dollar. That is a meaningful floor well under today's price, but not at it.
  2. Implied multiple = value ÷ OE. At our base $706, that is 24.5× whole-company owner earnings (or ~20× core NOPAT) — defensible for this franchise. The live price of $582.90 is 20.2× whole OE and 16.9× core NOPAT — cheaper than our base value, which is the point.
  3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond. At $582.90, whole-company OE yield = $28.87 ÷ $582.90 = 4.95%; core NOPAT yield = 5.9% — and that coupon is growing double-digits. Against a ~4-4.5% long Treasury, a ~5-6% and growing yield is a superior claim. Chapter 12's gravity is favorable but not screaming.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET (Ch. 2, 13)

  • Live price: $582.90 (equity_atlas, July 4, 2026).
  • Base intrinsic value: ~$700. Margin of safety = (700 − 582.90) ÷ 700 = ≈ 17%.
  • Against the low estimate ($560): the price is slightly above it — meaning at today's quote you have essentially no cushion against the pessimistic case.
  • Against the high ($860): a 32% discount — but that case leans on the AI bet paying off.

BEN: So where does the safety sit? A modest ~17% discount to base, and no discount to the conservative floor of the range. This is Chapter 13's uncomfortable middle: a wonderful business at a fair-ish price, not a wonderful business at a wonderful price. The Manual is explicit — "if we calculate the value of a common stock to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested in buying." A 17% margin against a business whose biggest risk (AI capex earning its return) is one we admit we cannot forecast is thin.

WARREN — the Mr. Market teaching contrast: Look at Meta's own price history in the an independent research service array. In 2022, amid metaverse-spending despair and an ad-recession, the stock traded as low as $88 — Mr. Market in a black mood, offering a 40%+-ROIC franchise for roughly 10× earnings. By 2025-2026, amid AI euphoria, it reached $796 and now sits at $583. The business did not swing that violently; his mood did. The lesson (Ch. 2): the 2022 despair was the gift; today's price, after a 6-fold move off the low, is Mr. Market in a fair-to-optimistic frame — useful to consult, not to be swept along by. Chapter 3: the falling quote in 2022, on a strengthening business, was less risk, not more.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: WONDERFUL core / SPECULATIVE appendage. The Family of Apps is one of the finest businesses in the world — 51.6% operating margins, 40%+ ROIC, a wide network-effect moat, pricing power demonstrated in real time. Reality Labs and the uncontracted AI moonshot are a venture bet we cannot underwrite.

Verdict: a WONDERFUL business at a FAIR price — admire, and wait for a wider margin. At $582.90 against a ~$700 base value, the ~17% discount is real but thin, and it evaporates against the conservative end of our range. Chapter 13's discipline: below the conservative range you buy in size; near or above it you wait. We are near it. This is a watch-list "hold your fire," not a "back up the truck."

THE ONE QUESTION, answered: Yes — a wonderful advertising franchise is being offered at a fair price. But the franchise's earnings are being routed, at management's sole discretion, into a $115-135B/year AI build-out whose return we cannot forecast. You are paying a fair price for the ad business and being handed the AI bet for free-to-slightly-negative — which is fine if the bet is neutral-to-good, and painful if it is Chapter 11's "bad growth" (reinvestment below r). The price does not yet compensate you enough for that uncertainty.

2-3 open questions:
1. Does the AI/infrastructure capex earn above the ~9% cost of capital, or is it Chapter 11's quicksand — the old Berkshire textile mill in a data-center costume?
2. Will regulation (FTC, EU, addictive-design suits) force a change to the ad-targeting model that narrows the moat?
3. How long will $19B/year of Reality Labs losses continue before the market — or Zuckerberg — calls time?

2-3 universal lessons:
- Separate the wonderful core from the speculative capital sink (Ch. 7, 8). Never let a masterpiece and a moonshot blur into a single multiple. Value each on its own economics; the discipline of Note 15's segment table is the whole case.
- Growth that creates vs. consumes capital (Ch. 11). A 40% ROIC franchise reinvesting at 40% is a compounding machine; the same franchise pouring $100B into a project earning less than r is destroying value while looking busy. The maintenance-vs-growth-capex split is not accounting trivia — it is the valuation.
- Normalize before you capitalize (Ch. 7). Meta's 2025 headline EPS fell on a one-time tax charge while the business grew 20%. Capitalize the normalized figure, not the accident of a tax year — in either direction.

Teaching-case disclaimer: This is an educational exercise in applying a method, not advice to buy, sell, or hold Meta Platforms. The figures are drawn from public filings as of the dates cited and will change.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with META's numbers)

Intrinsic Value — the discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. For META, triangulated to ~$560 / $700 / $860 per share.

Owner Earnings — Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC, keeping SBC as a cost. META normalized: $73.0B NI + $18.6B D&A − ~$18.6B maint capex − ~0 = ≈ $73B ($28.87/sh).

Maintenance vs. Growth Capex — spend to stand still vs. spend to expand. META's total capex $72.2B; maintenance ≈ depreciation $18.6B; the remaining $50B+ is growth/venture capex — the crux of the case.

ROIC — NOPAT ÷ invested capital; the quality measure. Create value only when ROIC > r. META: ~36% consolidated, 40%+ core, vs. r ≈ 9%.

ROE — net income ÷ equity. META normalized: $73B ÷ $217B = ~34% (VL: 35.2%).

Moat / Economic Franchise — durable advantage whose signature is pricing power. META: network effects, ad-targeting data scale; price per ad +12% (Q1 2026) is the proof.

Two-Column Method (Ch. 8) — value a company holding a large securities pile as operating value + investments. Here adapted: core FoA value + net cash − capitalized RL drag.

No-Growth (Earning-Power) Value — OE ÷ r; the floor. META whole: $28.87 ÷ 0.09 = $321/sh; core: $383/sh. Below the $583 price — most of the price is a growth payment.

Margin of Safety — (IV − Price) ÷ IV. META at $582.90 vs. $700 base = ≈ 17% (and ~0% vs. the $560 low). Thin.

Discount Rate (r) — opportunity cost anchored to long rates plus uncertainty premium. Used 9%; sensitivity run at 8-10% (Ch. 12's gravity).

Terminal Growth (g) — perpetual growth after the explicit period; must be < r and ≤ economy's growth. Used 3-4%.

Owner-Earnings Yield — OE ÷ price, vs. the long bond. META whole 4.95%, core 5.9%, and growing — superior to a ~4% static Treasury coupon.

Share-Based Compensation (SBC) — stock paid to employees; a real cost, not added back. META: $20.4B in 2025; $54.8B unrecognized; $18.4B cash spent on RSU net-settlement to hold the share count.

Net Cash — cash & marketable securities − total debt. META: $81.59B − $59.93B = $21.7B ($8.56/sh); negative Net Debt / Owner Earnings.

Interest Coverage — EBIT ÷ interest. META: $83.28B ÷ ~$0.70B = ~120×. Solvency is a non-issue.

Dual-Class Control — Class B shares carry 10 votes; Zuckerberg holds ~99.8% of them, controlling the company while owning a minority of the economics. You are a governance passenger on the AI-capital-allocation decision.

One-Dollar Test (Ch. 9) — retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). META: passed on core reinvestment and sub-IV buybacks; unproven on the AI/RL build-out — the central open question.
