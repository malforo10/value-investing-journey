A Value-Investing Seminar: Microsoft Corporation (MSFT)

In the Graham–Buffett tradition
Seminar date: July 4, 2026
Method: "How to Value a Business — A Security-Analysis Manual"

Good morning. Today we sit down with one of the great franchises of the age — Microsoft — and we are going to do to it exactly what we would do to a corner hardware store: figure out what the business earns, whether it will keep earning it, and what a rational owner should pay. The company's size and glamour change nothing about the method. If anything, the glamour is the danger. A wonderful business bought at a foolish price is still a foolish investment. So let us keep our heads and work the pipeline in order.

We will teach you to think, not to take dictation. Where we show a number, we show where it came from and the arithmetic that moves it. Where we reach a judgment, we state the principle behind it so you can apply it to the next company yourselves.

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

What Microsoft is. Strip away the mystique and Microsoft is three businesses stapled to the largest software installed base on Earth. The 10-K (Item 1; MD&A segment results) and an independent research service report it in three segments. Here are the fiscal-2025 figures straight from the 10-K MD&A:

Segment FY25 Revenue FY25 Operating income What's inside
Productivity & Business Processes $120,810M $69,773M Microsoft 365 (Office), LinkedIn, Dynamics
Intelligent Cloud $106,265M $44,589M Azure, server products, enterprise services
More Personal Computing $54,649M $14,166M Windows, Gaming (Xbox/Activision), Search
Total $281,724M $128,528M

How it makes money. Three engines, in order of quality:

  1. Subscriptions — Microsoft 365, LinkedIn Premium, Dynamics 365, Xbox Game Pass. Recognized ratably; sticky; invoiced in advance (which builds "float," more on that below).
  2. Cloud consumption — Azure, billed by usage. This is the growth engine: Azure grew 34% in FY25 and ~37–39% into 2026, and Microsoft Cloud revenue reached $168.9 billion.
  3. Licenses — Windows OEM, on-premises server products, perpetual Office. The mature, cash-rich, slow-growth base.

Customers. Overwhelmingly enterprises and governments — the stickiest, highest-switching-cost customers in software — plus roughly 89 million consumer Microsoft 365 subscribers and the vast Windows/Office installed base.

The one question that decides the case. For a genuinely wonderful business, the question is almost never "is it a good company?" — it plainly is. The question is: Is this wonderful business available at a fair enough price, and is the enormous new AI/data-center capital spending going to earn a return above its cost — creating value — or is it capital being poured into the ground? Hold that question in your mind through everything that follows. It has two halves — price and the quality of the reinvestment — and both must clear.

The circle-of-competence gate (Manual ch. 4). Before we value a business we must ask whether we can forecast it a decade out. Enterprise software and cloud infrastructure are, unusually, more forecastable than most technology — because the moat (below) makes the revenue recurring and the customers captive. We can say with fair confidence that in ten years the world's enterprises will still run on Windows servers, Office documents, and one of two or three hyperscale clouds, and that Microsoft will be one of them. What we cannot forecast with confidence is the pace and profitability of the AI build-out — how much of the $190 billion-a-year capex converts to durable, high-return revenue. So Microsoft sits inside the circle for its core franchise and at the fuzzy edge for its AI bet. An honest analyst forecasts the core tightly and the AI upside conservatively, and never pretends the edge is the center.

PRINCIPLE. "Too hard" is a complete answer, but so is "understood in part." When a business is knowable at its core and speculative at its frontier, you value the core and treat the frontier as optionality — you do not let the exciting, unknowable part set the price.

3. READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The three statements are one story told three ways. Read them together or you will be fooled by any one of them. All figures from the FY2025 10-K (Item 8) unless noted; nine-month figures from the Q3-FY26 10-Q.

The balance sheet (June 30, 2025)

  • Cash, equivalents, and short-term investments: $94.6 billion (plus $15.4 billion of equity and other investments). This is a war chest.
  • Long-term (bond) debt: $40.2 billion, current portion $3.0 billion — trivially small against the earning power. Note: An independent research service's May-2026 sheet lists "Total Debt $123 billion," which folds in ~$80 billion of finance-lease liabilities for data centers plus commercial paper. The actual bond debt at March 31, 2026 was only $40.3 billion (10-Q, Note 10). Keep the two separate — the leases are real obligations, but they are not a refinancing time bomb.
  • Goodwill $119.5 billion and intangibles $22.6 billion — the accumulated residue of LinkedIn, GitHub, Nuance, and Activision Blizzard. This is accounting goodwill; the economic goodwill of the franchise is far larger and appears nowhere on the balance sheet.
  • Unearned (deferred) revenue: $67.3 billion — money customers have paid in advance for services not yet delivered. On the balance sheet it is a liability, but economically it is negative-cost float: cash Microsoft holds and invests before it does the work. A growing deferred-revenue balance is a feature of a strong subscription franchise, not a debt to fear.
  • Property & equipment, net: $205 billion at June 2025 → $283 billion at March 2026. That $78 billion jump in nine months is the AI story written on the balance sheet. Hold that thought.
  • Total stockholders' equity: $343 billion (June 2025), $414 billion (March 2026).

The income statement (FY2025 vs FY2024)

Revenue $281,724M $245,122M $211,915M
Gross margin $193,893M (68.8%) $171,008M (69.8%) $146,052M (68.9%)
Operating income $128,528M (45.6%) $109,433M (44.6%) $88,523M (41.8%)

Look at each layer. A 68.8% gross margin tells you the marginal cost of another Office seat or Azure hour is very low — the signature of software economics. A 45.6% operating margin after spending 12% of revenue on R&D is extraordinary; few businesses on Earth keep 45 cents of operating profit per revenue dollar. The 36% net margin survives taxes and interest. And the trend is up across every layer over three years while revenue grew 33% — margins expanding as the business scales. From the XBRL, the ten-year arc is even more striking: revenue roughly tripled from ~$92 billion (2015) to $282 billion (2025), and EPS rose from $2.65 to $13.64.

The FY25 gross-margin dip of about one point is worth naming: MD&A attributes it to "scaling our AI infrastructure" — the new data-center depreciation beginning to press on Azure's margin. That is the first visible cost of the AI build-out flowing through the income statement.

Articulation — do the three tie out? Net income of $101.8 billion becomes $136.2 billion of operating cash because the non-cash charges (D&A $34.2 billion, SBC $12.0 billion) are added back and working capital moves. The company then spent $64.6 billion on property and equipment (the balance sheet's $78 billion P&E jump), and returned $42.5 billion to owners via buybacks and dividends. The statements tie. Good.

But notice the crude free cash flow fell slightly in FY25 despite net income rising $14 billion — because capex jumped $20 billion. And in the nine months to March 2026, capex was $80.1 billion, up 69% from $47.5 billion. Reported earnings are rising; the cash an owner can actually pocket is being consumed by the build-out. This is the whole valuation problem in one line, and it is exactly why we insist on owner earnings rather than net income.

Earnings-quality scan (Manual ch. 7)

  • Stock-based compensation ($12.0 billion) is a real expense. It is added back in the cash-flow statement because it is non-cash, but it transfers ownership from you to employees just as surely as cash would. We will not treat it as free. Note MSFT also spent ~$5.9 billion buying back shares partly to offset dilution — some of the "buyback" is really paying cash for the SBC. Honest analysts count SBC as a cost.
  • Deferred revenue — a positive signal here, as discussed. No games.
  • Capitalized software — modest and disclosed; Microsoft expenses most R&D and capitalizes little. Clean.
  • Acquisition accounting — $119 billion of goodwill means past acquisitions were expensive; watch for future impairments, but none material to date.
  • EBITDA misuse — Microsoft does not lean on EBITDA, and neither will we. As the Manual says, EBITDA "deletes the very real cost of maintaining the asset base" — and with $64+ billion of capex a year, that cost is enormous.
  • One caution on FY26 net income: the nine-month FY26 net income ($97.98 billion) includes a ~$5.9 billion pre-tax gain (about $4.5 billion after-tax) from the OpenAI recapitalization — a one-time, non-cash equity-method dilution gain (10-Q, Note 3). That is not operating earning power. We will strip it out when we normalize.

Never anchor to book value. Microsoft's book equity is $343 billion; the market values the enterprise near $3 trillion. Book value badly understates this franchise, because the brand, the installed base, and the switching costs — the real assets — are not on the balance sheet. As the Manual insists, book understates a great business and overstates a poor one.

PRINCIPLE. The income statement tells you how good a year looked; the cash-flow statement tells you what an owner could have taken home. When capex is surging, the two diverge sharply — and the cash flow, adjusted for what is maintenance versus growth, is the one to trust.

4. EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The trend in earning power

From the XBRL and filings, net income compounded from $12.2 billion (2016) to $101.8 billion (2025) — a roughly 24% annual clip, with margins rising, not falling, as it scaled. This is the fingerprint of a business that grows without competing away its returns. Very few franchises in history have done this at this size.

Owner earnings — and the maintenance-vs-growth capex judgment

The Manual's definition:

Owner Earnings = Reported earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital

The whole game for Microsoft is splitting the $64.6 billion FY25 capex ($80.1 billion nine-month FY26 run-rate) into maintenance (what's needed to keep today's business running) and growth (new Azure/AI capacity built to win future revenue). Accounting does not split them; we must, and we must be honest that this is judgment, not fact.

Our estimate of the split. Historically, Microsoft's capex ran near depreciation and around $25–30 billion when the business was already enormous (FY22–23). Depreciation and amortization is now ~$34 billion. A reasonable read is that maintenance capex is on the order of $30–40 billion — roughly the level of D&A plus modest real growth of the existing cloud — and that the remaining $30–70 billion is growth capex, built ahead of demand for AI. Management itself frames the spend as "capacity-constrained" expansion, i.e. building for revenue not yet earned.

Normalized owner earnings, FY2025 (our central estimate):

Line Amount Note
Reported net income $101,832M FY25 10-K; no one-time distortions this year
+ Depreciation, amortization & other +$34,153M non-cash
− Stock-based compensation (real cost) −$11,974M we subtract SBC as a true economic cost
− Maintenance capex (estimate) −$35,000M our judgment; ~D&A-level
− Incremental working capital ≈ $0 deferred revenue growth roughly offsets receivables growth
Normalized owner earnings ≈ $88,000M the cash a rational owner could withdraw while holding market position

That is roughly $88 billion, or about $11.85 per share on ~7.43 billion diluted shares. Note this is below reported EPS of $13.64 — because we charged SBC and because we assume Microsoft must keep spending ~$35 billion to stand still. That conservatism is deliberate. We treat the extra ~$30–45 billion of growth capex as a separate value question, not as a subtraction from normal earning power — because if it earns above the cost of capital it adds value, and if it earns below it destroys value. We come back to that in §7.

A subtle point worth pausing on: the surging growth capex depresses reported free cash flow now but should build Azure revenue later. Whether that trade is good depends entirely on the return the new data centers earn — which is why "growth" is never automatically good (Manual ch. 11).

The moat — genuinely wide and durable

Microsoft's moat is a stack of reinforcing advantages, among the widest in business:

  • Switching costs. An enterprise running Windows, Active Directory, Office/Microsoft 365, and Azure cannot leave without re-training every employee, rewriting integrations, and migrating data. The cost and risk of switching are enormous. This is the classic high-switching-cost moat.
  • Network effects. Teams and LinkedIn get more valuable as more people use them; Office file formats are the lingua franca of business documents.
  • Scale. Azure is one of three hyperscale clouds; the capital and expertise to build a fourth are prohibitive. Scale lowers unit cost and funds the very AI capex we are debating.
  • Ecosystem lock-in. The bundle — identity, productivity, cloud, security, and now Copilot AI woven through all of it — is stickier than any single product. Selling one more Copilot seat to an existing Microsoft 365 customer costs almost nothing.

The diagnostic test is pricing power: Microsoft raised Microsoft 365 Consumer prices in 2025 and grew revenue-per-user while keeping subscribers. It can raise prices materially without losing volume. That is a moat by the Manual's own test.

What could erode it in ten years? Three real threats — argue durability, don't assume it:
1. Cloud competition from AWS and Google Cloud — a genuine three-way rivalry that could compress Azure pricing.
2. AI disruption — the "SaaS-pocalypse" thesis (an independent research service industry report): if AI agents let smaller firms replicate software cheaply, or reduce the number of paid "seats" per company, per-seat pricing power could weaken. This is a real, if unproven, risk.
3. Regulation and antitrust — Microsoft's bundling has drawn scrutiny before and will again; the OpenAI relationship attracts lawsuits (e.g., the Musk suit noted by an independent research service).

Our judgment: the core moat is wide and likely to endure a decade, but the AI transition is the first thing in twenty years that could plausibly change the shape of the moat. That argues for a robust but not infinite forecast horizon.

ROIC vs cost of capital — the mark of a real moat

ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital

Rough FY25: operating income $128.5 billion, at an 18% effective tax rate → NOPAT ≈ $105 billion. Invested capital (equity $343B + bond debt $40B − cash & ST investments $95B ≈ $288 billion of operating capital; or, excluding the huge cash pile and using a tighter operating base, lower still). On ~$288 billion, ROIC ≈ 36%. Even loading in all goodwill and the new data-center capital, ROIC comfortably exceeds any plausible cost of capital (we will use ~9%). Returns on capital multiples of the cost of capital are the arithmetic signature of a wide moat — and the reason growth here can create enormous value, provided the new capital earns at similar rates.

A caution on ROE: An independent research service shows ROE around 29–32% and projects it toward 21–27% as equity swells. High ROE partly reflects leverage and buybacks; ROIC is the cleaner quality gauge, and it is stellar.

PRINCIPLE. A moat is not a slogan; it is the reason ROIC stays far above the cost of capital for years. When it does — as here — quality itself becomes part of your protection. But the analyst's job is to keep asking what could break it, and for Microsoft that question now has a real answer: the AI transition.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

The Manual's prior chapters asked whether Microsoft earns well; this one asks whether it can survive. The answer is: about as safely as any company that has ever existed. An independent research service rates Financial Strength A++, its top grade, and Safety 1. Let us verify with the five questions.

  1. Leverage. Bond debt $40.3 billion (March 2026) against $94.6 billion of cash and short-term investments — Microsoft is net cash, not net debt. Even including the ~$80 billion of finance-lease liabilities for data centers, net obligations are a fraction of one year's operating cash flow. Debt/OCF is 0.32 (Atlas). A conservative business could clear its bond debt from a few months of cash flow.
  2. Coverage. Operating income $128.5 billion against interest expense of ~$2.4 billion → interest coverage ≈ 54×. That is not a cushion; it is an ocean.
  3. Liquidity. Current assets $191 billion vs current liabilities $141 billion → current ratio ≈ 1.35, and the current liabilities are dominated by deferred revenue (services owed, not cash owed) and payables. Ample.
  4. Refinancing wall. None. The 10-Q debt-maturity table shows modest maturities ($9.25 billion in FY27, $2 billion in FY29, $34.9 billion thereafter). Microsoft never depends on the kindness of lenders.
  5. Hidden obligations. The real "leverage" here is the $178.7 billion of operating and finance leases and $110 billion of purchase commitments (mostly data centers) in the FY25 10-K contractual-obligations table, plus $32 billion of construction commitments. These are the AI build-out's forward commitments. They are large and real — but they are investments in capacity, backed by a fortress cash flow, not desperate borrowings. Pension obligations are minimal (no defined-benefit plan).

Verdict: a fortress (AAA-rated), unambiguously. And here is the lesson to carry: for Microsoft, the balance sheet is not the risk. The valuation is. You will not lose money owning Microsoft because it goes bankrupt; you will lose money only if you overpay or if the moat quietly erodes. Point your worry at the price, not the solvency.

PRINCIPLE. Financial strength is the precondition that lets you trust an earnings stream to persist and guarantees you are never a forced seller. Microsoft clears it with room to spare — so the analysis now turns entirely on price and on the quality of the reinvestment.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Once a business earns more than it needs, management's capital allocation determines how much of that earning power reaches you. Judge management across the six uses of a dollar (Manual ch. 9).

The record (FY2025, from the cash-flow statement and Note 15):
- Dividends: $24.7 billion declared, per-share $3.32, growing ~10–11% a year for two decades. A grower, not a high payer (yield under 1%). Appropriate — a business earning 36% ROIC should retain most of its cash.
- Buybacks: ~$13.0 billion open-market repurchases (31 million shares) plus ~$5.9 billion to offset SBC dilution. A new $60 billion authorization ($44 billion remaining at March 2026). Buybacks are value-creating only below intrinsic value; at 30×+ earnings in 2025 they were, at best, neutral — a fair criticism. At today's lower price they make more sense.
- Reinvestment in the business: the $64.6 billion (rising to $110 billion+) AI/cloud capex — the central bet, examined in §7.
- Disciplined M&A: LinkedIn (2016, $26B), GitHub (2018), Nuance (2022), and Activision Blizzard (2023, ~$69 billion). LinkedIn and GitHub look value-creating; Activision is still proving itself (Gaming operating income is up, Xbox content +16%). The debt raised for Activision has largely been repaid — bond debt is back near $40 billion.
- The OpenAI investment: total funding commitments of $13 billion ($11.8 billion funded by March 2026), now an ~27% equity-method stake after the October 2025 recapitalization. This produced a one-time $4.5 billion accounting gain but also periodic equity-method losses; economically it is a strategic option on the frontier of AI, not a cash cow — and we treat it as such.
- Share count: roughly flat-to-slightly-down over the decade (from ~8.0 billion in 2015 to ~7.43 billion in 2025) — net buybacks have modestly outrun SBC dilution. That is respectable stewardship of the per-share claim.

The one-dollar test (Manual ch. 9). Has each dollar Microsoft retained over the past decade produced at least a dollar of market value? Emphatically yes — retained earnings compounded into a business worth roughly ten times its 2015 market value. The forward question is narrower and sharper: will the dollars retained for AI capex clear the same test? That is genuinely open, and it is the crux of the case.

Management candor under Satya Nadella and CFO Amy Hood is generally high — clear segment reporting, honest disclosure of the AI margin pressure, and a plain statement that Azure is "capacity-constrained." We give them the benefit of a strong track record while insisting the AI-return question be answered by results, not rhetoric.

PRINCIPLE. The one-dollar test is retrospective and prospective. A management that has passed it for a decade has earned trust — but trust is not a substitute for watching whether the next wave of retained capital earns its keep.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Now the arithmetic. Recall the master equation is the definition of value:

Intrinsic Value = Σ OEₜ / (1 + r)ᵗ

We do not pretend to compute it to the dollar. We build a range, anchored on normalized owner earnings ≈ $88 billion (~$11.85/share), cross-check it three independent ways, and then demand a margin of safety. We will primarily reason with a normalized-earnings multiple, deriving the justified multiple three ways, and use a two-stage DCF as a sanity check.

Step A — The discount rate (Manual ch. 12)

With the 30-year Treasury at 4.86% and the 10-year at 4.38% (S&O Selected Yields), and inflation reaccelerating (PCE 4.1%, Fed on hold), the risk-free anchor is ~4.4–4.9%. For a franchise this durable we add a modest equity premium. We use r = 9% as our central discount rate, and we will show sensitivity to 8% and 10%. (Higher rates than the 2010s zero-rate era are a real headwind to all high-multiple stocks — the Manual's "gravity.")

Step B — Derive the justified multiple three ways

(a) Microsoft's own historical P/E over a cycle. an independent research service's data grid shows the average annual P/E climbing from single digits (9.6–14 in 2011–2014, the pre-cloud era) to the high-20s and low-30s in the cloud era (27.4 in 2020, 32.2 in 2022, ~31–33 estimated for 2026–27). An independent research service's stated median P/E is 27. The honest reading: the multiple re-rated permanently higher when Microsoft became a cloud compounder — but 27–32× reflects the zero-rate cloud-boom mood, and at 9% discount rates that is rich. A through-cycle normalized multiple in the low-to-mid 20s is a more defensible anchor.

(b) Peer/industry multiple. The Computer Software industry (an independent research service, 2026-05-01) trades at premium multiples: Oracle, Adobe, Salesforce, Intuit — high-quality software peers typically at ~20–35× earnings depending on growth. Alphabet (a cloud/AI peer) trades near 32× (S&O Portfolio III). Microsoft's superior moat, scale, and balance sheet justify a premium to the average software company but not to the most speculative. Call it ~22–26× normalized owner earnings as a peer-justified band. (We use peers only as a reasonableness check — the Manual rightly warns that pricing off comparables anchors you to Mr. Market's opinion of other businesses.)

(c) Quality-and-growth from first principles. A durable double-digit grower at ~36% ROIC warrants a premium multiple, because so little reinvestment is needed to sustain growth that most earnings are free. Tie it to the two-stage logic: owner earnings ~$88B growing ~10% for a decade then ~4% terminal, discounted at 9%, implies a justified multiple in the mid-20s on normalized owner earnings (we confirm with the DCF below). This corroborates a ~22–26× justified range.

Reconciling the three: all three roads land in roughly the same place — a justified ~22–26× normalized owner earnings, with the history hinting higher (the cloud re-rating) and the discount-rate reality pulling lower. We center on ~24×.

Step C — Translate to a value range

Normalized owner earnings ~$11.85/share:

Multiple on normalized OE Value/share
20× (conservative / low) ~$237
24× (central) ~$284
28× (optimistic / high) ~$332

But this understates the case in one important way: it charges full maintenance capex and ignores the value the growth capex is building. If the AI/Azure growth capex earns above the cost of capital, the true owner-earnings power in five years is materially higher than today's normalized figure. So we lift the range to account for value-creating growth — but conservatively, because that return is unproven.

Step D — Two-stage DCF as a sanity check (Manual ch. 10)

Microsoft's forecastability justifies a fuller model. Start with owner earnings ~$88B, grow 10% for 10 years (below the 12% EPS growth an independent research service and the Atlas project — deliberately conservative, and reasonable given the moat), then 4% terminal, discount at 9%.

  • Sum of 10 explicit years' discounted owner earnings ≈ $650B
  • Terminal value = OE₁₁ / (r − g) = (~$251B) / (0.09 − 0.04) ≈ $5.0 trillion, discounted back ≈ $2.12 trillion
  • Total intrinsic value ≈ $2.77 trillion → ~$373/share

Sensitivity (the point is the spread, not the point estimate):

r g = 3% g = 4% g = 5%
8% ~$390 ~$450 ~$540
9% ~$330 ~$373 ~$430
10% ~$285 ~$315 ~$350

The DCF, run conservatively, produces roughly $285–$450/share across reasonable assumptions — centered near $373. Notice how a single point of terminal growth or discount rate swings the answer by $100+. That spread is the reason we never quote a single "target." The DCF corroborates and slightly lifts the multiple-based range, because it explicitly credits ten years of value-creating growth.

Step E — The three cross-checks (arithmetic, Manual ch. 14)

1. No-growth floor — how much of the price is a growth bet.
Value Microsoft as if it never grows another dollar: OE ÷ r = $88B ÷ 0.09 = $978 billion, or about $132/share. At the live price of $390, roughly $132 is the no-growth business and ~$258 (about two-thirds of the price) is a bet that Microsoft keeps growing. That is a large growth component — appropriate for a genuine compounder, but it tells you plainly that you are paying substantially for the future, not the present. If growth disappoints, the floor is far below.

2. Implied multiple — is the model's output defensible?
Our central IV (~$284 on multiples, ~$373 on the DCF; call the blended center ~$320–$330) divided by normalized OE (~$11.85) implies ~27–28× normalized owner earnings. Is that defensible for a 36%-ROIC franchise growing ~10%? At the top of reasonable, yes — but it leaves little margin, and it is why we lean on the conservative end for decisions.

3. Owner-earnings yield vs the long bond.
At $390, normalized owner-earnings yield = $11.85 ÷ $390 = 3.0%, and that coupon should grow ~10% a year. Against a 4.86% long bond (static coupon), the starting yield is below the bond. This is Chapter 12's gravity biting: the entire case rests on the ~10% growth of that coupon arriving as forecast. A growing 3.0% claim beats a static 4.9% one only if the growth is real and durable. This check is the sternest — it says Microsoft at $390 is priced for continued strong growth, with little cushion if rates stay high or growth slows.

Step F — The value-creating vs capital-consuming growth verdict

This is the heart of the AI question. Microsoft is spending ~$110B+ (FY26) of growth capex. If that capital earns above 9% (and Azure historically earns far more), the growth is value-creating — every reinvested dollar becomes worth more than a dollar, and our estimates understate the business. If AI demand disappoints and those data centers earn below the cost of capital, the growth is capital-consuming — the capex becomes a drag, and reported earnings overstate true owner earnings. We cannot yet know which. an independent research service and the BIS both flag the risk that "financial returns fall short." Our conservative posture: forecast the core at ~10% and treat the AI upside as optionality rather than baking it into the base — and demand a price low enough that we win even if the AI bet only earns its cost of capital.

At $390, Microsoft trades above our base estimate and offers a margin of safety only against the optimistic end of the range. In plain terms: even after a ~30% fall from its 2025 highs, the stock is not yet cheap against a disciplined, conservative valuation. There is no wide gap between price and conservative value — and the Manual is unambiguous: "If we calculate the value of a common stock to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested in buying."

Where safety sits for a business like this. Here is the subtle and important teaching. For a durable, high-ROIC franchise, part of the margin of safety lives in the quality itself: a conservatively projected, growing owner-earnings stream makes today's estimate the floor of tomorrow's. So a fair price on Microsoft can protect you better than a cheap price on a mediocre business — because time widens the moat while you hold. But — and this is where discipline bites — quality justifies a fair price, never any price. At $390 against a $320 base, we are being asked to pay a modest premium to fair value, not a discount. The quality argument reconciles you to paying up to fair value; it does not license paying above it.

Is Microsoft priced for perfection? Partly. The no-growth floor (~$132) and the below-bond starting yield (3.0%) both say the price embeds continued strong growth and successful AI returns. Mr. Market has recently swung from euphoria (near $555, ~33× earnings, late 2025) to worry (high $360s–$390s on capex fear). That swing is the pocketbook, not the wisdom — and it is doing our negative analysis for us: the fear has removed the worst of the froth, but not yet delivered a bargain. If the AI-spending fear deepens and the stock fell toward the $250–$290 zone, the margin of safety would open up and the quality-plus-price case would become compelling.

PRINCIPLE. In a wonderful business, be willing to pay a fair price and let quality do part of the work of safety — but hold the line at fair value. A ~30% fall from the top is not the same as a bargain; measure the price against your conservative value, not against its own recent high.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Verdict: a genuinely WONDERFUL business — currently priced at fair-to-slightly-rich, not cheap.

Microsoft is a textbook wide-moat, high-ROIC franchise: fortress balance sheet, 36% ROIC, expanding margins, sticky enterprise customers, honest accounts, and capable, candid management with a decade-long record of passing the one-dollar test. On business quality it is close to the top of the market. This is exactly the kind of company Chapter 5 tells you to hunt for.

The one question, answered. Is this wonderful business available at a fair enough price, and is the AI capex value-creating? On price: at $390 it is fair-to-slightly-rich, not the wide-margin bargain we require — it sits above our $320 base and offers safety only against the optimistic end. On the AI capex: genuinely undetermined — the returns on ~$110B+ of annual growth capex are the swing factor, and they will be proven by Azure results over the next few years, not asserted today. So the honest verdict is: admire it, keep it on the on-deck list, and wait for either a lower price or clearer evidence the AI capex is earning its keep. For an existing owner who bought lower, the quality argues for holding (Chapter 16), not selling into fear.

Two or three open questions to keep watching:
1. Do the AI/data-center dollars earn above the cost of capital? Watch Azure margins, the gap between capex and depreciation, and whether AI revenue (Copilot, Azure AI) scales into the $190B/year spend. This is the whole ballgame for value-creating vs capital-consuming growth.
2. Does the cloud stay a three-way oligopoly, or does pricing power erode under AWS/Google competition and the "SaaS-pocalypse" AI-disruption thesis?
3. Does the multiple hold at higher interest rates? With the 30-year near 4.9% and inflation reaccelerating, the cloud-era re-rating that took the P/E to 30× is under pressure — gravity is real.

Two or three universal lessons from this case:
1. A wonderful business at a fair price beats a fair business at a wonderful price — but "fair price" still has an upper bound. Quality reconciles you to paying up to fair value, never above it. A 30% drop from the peak is not automatically a buy.
2. Quality is part of the margin of safety. In a durable, growing franchise, part of your protection lives in the earnings stream itself, which makes today's value the floor of tomorrow's. That is why you can pay a fair price for greatness — but it is a reason to pay fair value, not to abandon the discount discipline entirely.
3. Distinguish value-creating from capital-consuming growth. A tidal wave of capex is good only if the capital earns above its cost. Never let a big, exciting growth number substitute for the return-on-that-capital question — that is the difference between See's Candy and the textile mills.

This is a teaching case, not investment advice. We have shown you the method; the judgment — and the responsibility — is yours.

11. GLOSSARY

Each term: (1) definition, (2) formula, (3) Microsoft's number.

Owner earnings — (1) the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. (2) Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔworkingÂ capital. (3) FY25 ≈ $101.8B net income + $34.2B D&A − $12.0B SBC − ~$35B maintenance capex ≈ $88B (~$11.85/share).

Free cash flow (FCF) — (1) operating cash left after all capex. (2) Operating cash flow − total capex. (3) FY25 = $136.2B − $64.6B = $71.6B (crude; treats all capex, growth included, as a subtraction).

Maintenance vs growth capex — (1) maintenance = spending to hold today's position; growth = spending to expand. (2) Judgment split of total capex. (3) FY25 capex $64.6B → we estimate ~$35B maintenance, ~$30B growth; FY26 running ~$110B+, with the growth portion ballooning for AI.

ROIC — (1) return on invested capital, the central quality gauge. (2) NOPAT ÷ invested capital. (3) NOPAT ≈ $105B (op. income $128.5B × (1−0.18)); invested operating capital ≈ $288B → ≈ 36%.

NOPAT — (1) after-tax operating profit. (2) Operating income × (1 − tax rate). (3) $128.5B × 0.82 ≈ $105B.

Invested capital — (1) the capital funding operations. (2) Equity + debt − excess cash. (3) $343B equity + $40B bond debt − $95B cash/ST inv ≈ $288B.

ROE — (1) return on shareholders' equity (reflects leverage). (2) Net income ÷ average equity. (3) FY25 ≈ $101.8B ÷ ~$306B avg ≈ ~33%; an independent research service/Atlas project ~21–27% forward as equity swells.

Interest coverage — (1) how many times earnings cover interest. (2) EBIT ÷ interest expense. (3) $128.5B ÷ ~$2.4B ≈ 54×.

Net debt — (1) debt net of cash. (2) Total debt − cash & short-term investments. (3) $40.3B bond debt − $94.6B cash/ST inv ≈ −$54B (net cash).

Current ratio — (1) short-term liquidity. (2) Current assets ÷ current liabilities. (3) $191B ÷ $141B ≈ 1.35.

Cost of capital / WACC — (1) blended required return on the firm's capital. (2) Weighted debt & equity costs. (3) ≈ 8–9% (equity-heavy, low debt cost; we use r = 9%).

Discount rate (r) — (1) owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long rates + uncertainty premium. (2) Long bond + premium. (3) 30-yr Treasury 4.86% + premium → r = 9% central.

Intrinsic value — (1) discounted value of all future owner earnings; an estimate and a range. (2) Σ OEₜ / (1+r)ᵗ. (3) Low $250 / Base $320 / High $420 per share.

Margin of safety — (1) discount of price to conservative value; the cushion against error. (2) (IV − price) ÷ IV. (3) At $390 vs $320 base → −22% (no margin); vs $420 high → +7%.

P/E ratio — (1) price per dollar of earnings. (2) Price ÷ EPS. (3) Live 23.3× ($390 ÷ $13.64 latest EPS); median (an independent research service) 27×; on normalized owner earnings $11.85 → ~33×.

EV/EBIT — (1) enterprise value per dollar of operating profit. (2) (Market cap + net debt) ÷ EBIT. (3) ≈ ($2.9T − $54B net cash) ÷ $128.5B ≈ ~22×.

Dividend yield / payout — (1) dividend per dollar of price / of earnings. (2) DPS ÷ price; dividends ÷ net income. (3) Yield ~0.96%; payout ~24% — a grower, not a payer.

No-growth (earning-power) value — (1) value if the business never grows again; the floor. (2) OE ÷ r. (3) $88B ÷ 0.09 = $978B ≈ $132/share — about a third of the live price; the rest is a growth bet.

Owner-earnings yield — (1) owner earnings per dollar of price; compare to the long bond. (2) OE ÷ price. (3) $11.85 ÷ $390 = 3.0%, growing ~10%/yr — below the 4.86% long bond at the start.

Two-stage DCF / terminal value / r−g spread — (1) explicit forecast + capitalized tail; terminal value is the tail, r−g its sensitivity. (2) Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. (3) OE $88B growing 10% for 10 yrs then 4%, r = 9% → ≈ $2.77T ≈ $373/share; terminal value ≈ $2.1T of it — the tail dominates, so g and r drive the answer.

Stock-based compensation (SBC) — (1) a real, non-cash cost that transfers ownership to employees. (2) Expensed at grant-date fair value. (3) FY25 $12.0B — we subtract it in owner earnings; ~$5.9B of "buybacks" merely offset its dilution.

Deferred (unearned) revenue — (1) cash received before delivery; economically negative-cost float. (2) Balance-sheet liability. (3) $67.3B at June 2025 — a strength, not a debt.

Economic moat — (1) a durable barrier protecting high returns; its signature is pricing power. (2) Qualitative + ROIC persistence. (3) Wide: switching costs, network effects, Azure scale, ecosystem lock-in; demonstrated 2025 price increases without seat loss.

Economic goodwill — (1) the value of a franchise above its tangible capital. (2) Not on the balance sheet. (3) Enormous for MSFT — market ~$3T vs $343B book equity; accounting goodwill ($119.5B) is a separate, smaller figure.

Circle of competence — (1) the set of businesses you can forecast; its boundary matters more than its size. (2) Honest self-assessment. (3) MSFT core = inside; AI-capex returns = fuzzy edge.

One-dollar test — (1) retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value. (2) Retain ⇔ incremental ROIC ≥ r. (3) Passed for a decade; the forward AI-capex test is the open question.

Value-creating vs capital-consuming growth — (1) growth adds value only when the reinvested capital earns above the cost of capital. (2) Value-creating ⇔ ROIC > r. (3) MSFT core reinvestment: strongly value-creating (36% >> 9%); AI capex: undetermined — the pivotal issue.

Triangulation — (1) testing a valuation with cross-checks that fail differently. (2) No-growth floor, implied multiple, owner-earnings yield vs bonds. (3) Floor $132; implied ~27–28× normalized OE; yield 3.0% vs 4.86% bond — together they say "priced for growth, little cushion."
