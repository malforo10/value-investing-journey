OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Graham: We are buying a piece of The Home Depot, not a price chart. It is the largest home-improvement retailer, selling home-improvement merchandise, building materials, garden and décor goods, installation and rental services through 2,359 stores at fiscal year-end, websites, and a fulfillment network (2026 10-K, Item 1). Its Primary retail segment supplies do-it-yourself homeowners, customers who hire an installer, and professional contractors. SRS—acquired in 2024—and GMS—acquired in 2025—add over 1,250 specialty-distribution locations serving roofers, landscapers, pool contractors, and interior-construction Pros (2026 10-K, Item 1).

Buffett: In plain English, HD earns a retail gross margin on a huge basket of merchandise, then earns further value from convenient locations, availability, delivery, knowledgeable associates, trade credit, and the contractor relationship. In the latest quarter, the legacy Primary segment produced $37.763 billion of sales and $4.965 billion of operating income; the newer SRS/GMS “Other” operations added $4.002 billion of sales but only $16 million of operating income (2026 10-Q, Note 2). That contrast is important: the central question is not whether Home Depot is familiar, but whether its trusted retail/Pro ecosystem can sustain high returns while new specialty distribution and housing-sensitive demand do not permanently dilute those returns.

Circle-of-competence gate

We judge this understandable. Demand is tied to housing turnover, repair, project activity, prices, and contractor spending; all are cyclical, but the retail model, economics, competitors, and capital needs are intelligible. We can forecast a conservative range over a decade, not quarterly housing starts. The limiting judgment is the enduring return on the SRS/GMS capital and the length of the housing downturn—not technological obsolescence. It therefore passes, with a cyclical-normalization warning (Manual, ch. 4; 2026 10-K, Items 1 and 7).

PRINCIPLE: A share is fractional ownership. Stay within a circle where you can explain how cash is earned and what could change it; the circle’s edge matters more than its size.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

1. The balance sheet: what must be paid before owners are paid

At May 3, 2026, HD had $107.904 billion of assets, including $1.601 billion cash, $27.280 billion inventory, $22.479 billion goodwill and $10.244 billion net intangibles (2026 10-Q, balance sheet and Note 4). It had $94.030 billion of liabilities and $13.874 billion of accounting equity (2026 10-Q, balance sheet). Goodwill plus intangibles are $22.479 billion + $10.244 billion = $32.723 billion, larger than reported equity; they principally arose from acquisitions, including SRS and GMS (2026 10-Q, Note 4; 2026 10-K, Note 13). They are judgment-laden: they are not cash and need impairment testing, but neither does their presence itself prove that the underlying customer relationships are worthless.

Liquidity is adequate but not Graham-conservative:

  • Current ratio = current assets $37.172 billion ÷ current liabilities $35.580 billion = 1.04x (2026 10-Q, balance sheet).
  • Working capital = $37.172 billion − $35.580 billion = $1.592 billion (2026 10-Q, balance sheet).
  • Cash coverage of debt due within a year = cash $1.601 billion ÷ (short-term debt $3.503 billion + current long-term-debt installments $5.178 billion) = 18.4% (2026 10-Q, balance sheet).

The modest current ratio is normal for a fast-turning retailer with supplier financing, but it means the enterprise depends on continuing operating cash flow and capital-market access rather than a hoard of cash. Supplier-finance obligations were $336 million at quarter-end, recorded in accounts payable; they deserve attention as a liability embedded in a working-capital account (2026 10-Q, Note 1).

2. The income statement: margins reveal the economic weather

For fiscal 2025, sales were $164.683 billion, cost of sales $109.818 billion, gross profit $54.865 billion, operating income $20.890 billion, interest expense $2.412 billion, and net income $14.156 billion (2026 10-K, consolidated statements of earnings). Work the margins rather than admire the sales total:

  • Gross margin = gross profit $54.865 billion ÷ sales $164.683 billion = 33.3% (2026 10-K, statements of earnings).
  • Operating margin = operating income $20.890 billion ÷ sales $164.683 billion = 12.7% (2026 10-K, statements of earnings).
  • Net margin = net income $14.156 billion ÷ sales $164.683 billion = 8.6% (2026 10-K, statements of earnings).

The historical record is strong but not a straight line: revenue rose from $132.110 billion in fiscal 2020 to $164.683 billion in fiscal 2025, while net income moved from $12.866 billion to $14.156 billion, after a pandemic-led peak of $17.105 billion in fiscal 2022 (HD XBRL facts JSON, revenue and net-income annual series; 2026 10-K, statements of earnings). Fiscal-2025 operating margin was $20.890 billion ÷ $164.683 billion = 12.7%, down from fiscal-2024’s $21.526 billion ÷ $159.514 billion = 13.5% (2026 10-K, statements of earnings). Acquired SRS/GMS’s lower margin, higher payroll, and amortization contributed (2026 10-K, Item 7).

The latest quarter confirms that the recovery has not yet arrived: net sales grew $41.765 billion − $39.856 billion = $1.909 billion, or $1.909 billion ÷ $39.856 billion = 4.8%, but operating income fell $4.981 billion − $5.133 billion = -$0.152 billion, or −$0.152 billion ÷ $5.133 billion = −3.0%; operating margin was $4.981 billion ÷ $41.765 billion = 11.9% versus $5.133 billion ÷ $39.856 billion = 12.9% (2026 10-Q, statements of earnings and MD&A). Comparable sales grew 0.6%, composed of a 2.2% higher comparable ticket less 1.3% fewer comparable transactions (2026 10-Q, MD&A). Price is doing some of the work; project traffic remains the issue.

3. The cash-flow statement: the truth-teller

Fiscal-2025 net income of $14.156 billion became operating cash flow of $16.325 billion after adding $3.514 billion depreciation/finance-lease amortization, $0.607 billion intangible amortization, and other noncash items, partly offset by working-capital uses (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement). The cash conversion check is operating cash flow $16.325 billion ÷ net income $14.156 billion = 1.15x (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement): cash exceeded reported profit, a good first signal.

Reported free cash flow was operating cash flow $16.325 billion − capital expenditures $3.679 billion = $12.646 billion (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement). That cash funded $9.152 billion dividends, $5.040 billion long-term-debt repayment, and a $5.410 billion net cash payment for businesses acquired (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement). It could not fund all of that by itself; commercial-paper and long-term-debt financing also moved. That is not a scandal—it is the consequence of buying GMS—but it raises the balance-sheet hurdle.

The statements articulate. Retained earnings moved exactly as an owner expects: beginning retained earnings $89.533 billion + net income $14.156 billion − cash dividends $9.152 billion = ending retained earnings $94.537 billion (2026 10-K, statements of stockholders’ equity). Cash also ties: beginning cash $1.659 billion + operating cash flow $16.325 billion − investing cash outflow $8.980 billion − financing cash outflow $7.714 billion + foreign-exchange effect $0.099 billion = ending cash $1.389 billion (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement and balance sheet).

Earnings-quality scan

We see an audited, internally controlled set of accounts—the auditor gave an unqualified opinion and identified the automated retail-inventory process as its critical audit matter, not a revenue-recognition exception (2026 10-K, Item 8, auditor report). We do not substitute EBITDA for cash: maintenance capex is real. Nor do we simply call acquisition amortization “free.” The specific watch items are (1) inventory/shrink estimates—the company says a 10% increase in its shrink-rate assumption would raise fiscal-2025 cost of sales by $79 million—and (2) preliminary GMS purchase-price allocation, especially customer-relationship values (2026 10-K, Item 7; Note 13). No recurring “adjusted EBITDA” legerdemain is needed to explain the core business, but the lower-margin acquired operations and recurring intangible amortization make uncritical use of adjusted EPS inappropriate.

PRINCIPLE: Statements are evidence, not conclusions. Follow profit into cash, make the balance sheet and cash-flow statement reconcile, and treat accounting goodwill as neither a value floor nor automatic proof of failure. Cash is the truth-teller.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Normalize owner earnings before capitalizing them

We need one normal owner-earnings figure, not fiscal-2022’s extraordinary demand nor a depressed housing year. We use fiscal 2023–2025, then make a transparent cautionary adjustment. First, the raw inputs:

Input, fiscal 2023–2025 Arithmetic Result
Average reported net income ($15.143b + $14.806b + $14.156b) ÷ 3 $14.702b (2026 10-K, statements of earnings)
Average D&A, including intangibles [($3.061b + $0.186b) + ($3.336b + $0.425b) + ($3.514b + $0.607b)] ÷ 3 $3.710b (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement)
Average total capex ($3.226b + $3.485b + $3.679b) ÷ 3 $3.463b (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement)
Maintenance-capex judgment average capex $3.463b × 75% $2.597b (analyst judgment anchored to 2026 10-K, Item 7: capex includes new stores, digital/supply-chain investment, and growth initiatives)
Average incremental working capital [−$2.333b + −$0.679b + $3.084b] ÷ 3 $0.024b use (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement; see calculation below)

The working-capital arithmetic is visible: fiscal-2023 cash changes in receivables, inventory, other current assets, payables/accruals, deferred revenue and taxes were $0.134b + $4.137b − $0.184b − $1.411b − $0.318b − $0.025b = $2.333b of cash release, hence −$2.333b incremental working capital; fiscal 2024 was $0.174b − $0.743b + $0.086b + $0.518b − $0.165b + $0.809b = $0.679b release, hence −$0.679b; fiscal 2025 was $0.107b − $1.498b + $0.124b − $1.058b − $0.061b − $0.698b = −$3.084b cash effect, hence a +$3.084b investment (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement). The three-year average is (−$2.333b − $0.679b + $3.084b) ÷ 3 = $0.024b investment.

Raw average owner earnings are therefore net income $14.702b + D&A $3.710b − maintenance capex $2.597b − incremental working capital $0.024b = $15.791b (inputs above). We then normalize down for the recent margin compression and the still-unproven ability of the acquired distribution assets to earn retail-like economics: $15.791b × (1 − 5%) = $15.001b, rounded to $15.0 billion. That 5% is not concealed optimism: it is a judgment based on fiscal-2025 operating margin of 12.7% versus fiscal-2024’s 13.5%, and fiscal-2026 Q1’s 11.9% (2026 10-K, statements of earnings; 2026 10-Q, statements of earnings).

This seminar uses $15.0 billion—$15.05 per 996 million year-end shares—for every valuation and every universal cross-check below. Per-share arithmetic: $15.001 billion ÷ 996 million shares = $15.06 per share (2026 10-K, Note 7; analyst normalization above). The 75% maintenance-capex split is the most contestable assumption: the company reports aggregate capex, not maintenance versus growth. We count the remaining $3.463b − $2.597b = $0.866b average annual capex as growth investment, not cash that a passive owner could withdraw (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement; analyst judgment).

Moat and returns on capital

HD’s moat is real but not mystical: local scale and convenient real estate; enormous assortment and vendor economics; brand and trust for a project that can go wrong; a dense delivery/fulfillment network; and relationships, credit, product depth and job-site delivery for Pros (2026 10-K, Item 1). About 50% of U.S. online orders in fiscal 2025 were fulfilled through a store, illustrating why a physical footprint can complement rather than merely compete with digital commerce (2026 10-K, Item 1). The moat is vulnerable to price-transparent online comparison, Lowe’s, specialty suppliers, local distributors, direct manufacturer selling, and contractors splitting orders. The industry report explicitly describes high rates, weak housing turnover, costly labor/materials, and an “industrial arms race” in which HD added SRS/GMS while Lowe’s bought FBM and Artisan Design Group (an independent research service, Retail Building Supply Industry, June 12, 2026).

The return test is favorable. HD’s own fiscal-2025 calculation gives NOPAT of $15.897 billion and average debt plus equity of $61.914 billion; ROIC = NOPAT $15.897b ÷ average debt-and-equity $61.914b = 25.7% (2026 10-K, Item 7). Our 9.0% owner hurdle is 30-year Treasury yield 4.86% + business/forecast premium 4.14% = 9.00% (an independent research service, Selection & Opinion, July 10, 2026, Selected Yields; Manual, ch. 12). Thus, the spread is ROIC 25.7% − hurdle 9.0% = 16.7 percentage points (2026 10-K, Item 7; an independent research service, July 10, 2026). This is persuasive evidence of economic goodwill.

ROE is less useful because leveraged repurchases have made book equity a distorted denominator: ROE = net income $14.156b ÷ average equity [($12.813b + $6.640b) ÷ 2] = $14.156b ÷ $9.727b = 145.5% (2026 10-K, balance sheets and statements of earnings). It is impressive but not a 145% unlevered business return. Use ROIC instead.

PRINCIPLE: Durable earning power is cash after maintaining the franchise. A moat is not a slogan; persistent returns on capital far above the owner’s hurdle, and the ability to explain why competitors cannot immediately take them away, are its evidence.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Graham: HD is not a fortress balance sheet. It is an adequate balance sheet supporting a very good cash generator. The distinction matters.

  • Net debt: total debt is $3.503b short-term + $5.178b current long-term + $44.828b long-term = $53.509b; net debt = $53.509b − cash $1.601b = $51.908b (2026 10-Q, balance sheet). Net debt ÷ normalized owner earnings = $51.908b ÷ $15.001b = 3.46 years. This is manageable for a durable retailer, but distinctly heavier than a cash-rich consumer franchise (2026 10-Q, balance sheet; analyst normalization).
  • Interest coverage: operating income $20.890b ÷ interest expense $2.412b = 8.66x for fiscal 2025 (2026 10-K, statements of earnings). Q1’s run-rate check is $4.981b ÷ $0.611b = 8.15x (2026 10-Q, statements of earnings). Good coverage, but the cushion is not infinite in a housing slump.
  • Liquidity: current ratio is $37.172b ÷ $35.580b = 1.04x, as calculated above; inventory is productive working capital, not cash (2026 10-Q, balance sheet).
  • Refinancing wall: scheduled non-lease debt maturities are $4.684 billion in fiscal 2026, $3.625 billion in 2027 and $3.115 billion in 2028 (2026 10-K, Note 5). Against this, the company had an $11.0 billion commercial-paper program supported by $11.0 billion backup facilities as of May 3, 2026 (2026 10-Q, Note 5). The maturities are material but laddered, not a single cliff.
  • Hidden obligations: operating lease liabilities are $1.484b + $8.164b = $9.648b, finance lease liabilities are $0.292b + $2.640b = $2.932b, and future definite-life intangible amortization totals $9.595b (2026 10-Q, Notes 3–4). These are not all debt, but they are fixed claims before common owners.

Verdict: adequate, not fortress. Its cash generation and investment-grade access make 3.46 years of owner earnings tolerable; its thin current ratio, acquisition debt, lease commitments, and housing sensitivity mean we demand a wider price cushion than we would for a net-cash compounder.

PRINCIPLE: Great economics do not repeal solvency. Debt must be judged against the stability of cash flow, coverage, maturities, and commitments—not waved away because a business was once excellent.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Buffett: Management’s stated sequence is sensible: reinvest first, pay the dividend second, and repurchase excess shares third (2026 10-K, Item 1). In fiscal 2025 it actually deployed cash as follows: $3.679 billion capex, $5.081 billion GMS purchase consideration, $5.040 billion long-term-debt repayment, $9.152 billion dividends, and $0 share-repurchase payments (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement; Note 13). The pause in buybacks since March 2024 while debt is reduced is an act of restraint, not a failure to “support” the stock (2026 10-K, Item 7; 2026 10-Q, Note 6).

Over the longer record, share count fell from 1.203 billion at fiscal-2016 end to 0.996 billion at fiscal-2025 end; reduction = (1.203b − 0.996b) ÷ 1.203b = 17.2% (HD XBRL facts JSON, common-shares-outstanding annual series; 2026 10-K, Note 7). But recent count went the other direction: 0.996 billion − 0.992 billion = 0.004 billion, or 0.004b ÷ 0.992b = 0.4% growth from fiscal 2023 to 2025, because buybacks stopped while employee issuance continued (2026 10-K, Note 7). Per-share value depends on this discipline.

Does retained capital pass the one-dollar test? Historical total ROIC of 25.7% exceeds the 9.0% hurdle by 16.7 points, so ordinary organic reinvestment plausibly passes (2026 10-K, Item 7; an independent research service, July 10, 2026; Manual, ch. 9). The unresolved question is the acquisitions: the “Other” segment earned only $0.316 billion ÷ $12.717 billion = 2.5% operating margin in fiscal 2025, versus Primary’s $20.574 billion ÷ $151.966 billion = 13.5% (2026 10-K, Note 2). That comparison is not a final acquisition verdict—the businesses have different models and purchase accounting—but it says the one-dollar test for SRS/GMS has not yet been demonstrated.

PRINCIPLE: We ride with management’s capital allocation. Dividends, debt repayment, acquisitions, repurchases, and share issuance are all investments; a buyback is good only below value, and a dollar retained must earn at least the owner’s hurdle.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Method choice and inputs

This is a mature, cash-generative franchise in a cyclical housing environment. A normalized owner-earnings multiple is more honest than a long DCF whose terminal growth and rate inputs would dominate the answer. The Manual’s definition remains our north star—intrinsic value = Σ OE_t/(1+r)^t—but our estimator is a full-cycle, normalized multiple of the same $15.06 per-share owner earnings derived above (Manual, chs. 10 and 14).

Where the multiple comes from. HD’s fiscal-year average P/Es from 2015 through 2025 were 22.1x, 20.3x, 21.6x, 18.9x, 20.6x, 21.1x, 21.6x, 18.3x, 20.7x, 25.1x and 25.5x; an independent research service summarizes the full-cycle median as 21.0x (an independent research service, Home Depot, June 12, 2026). The closest large peer, Lowe’s, carries a live P/E of 17.89x, versus HD’s 24.38x, in the same Retail Building Supply industry (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026). HD deserves a premium to Lowe’s for its greater scale, integrated Pro ecosystem, and demonstrated historical returns; it does not deserve an unlimited premium while mortgage rates and transactions pressure the category (2026 10-K, Item 1; an independent research service, Retail Building Supply Industry, June 12, 2026).

We reconcile the anchors this way: use 18.5x as a recession/housing-and-integration low—slightly above the peer but below HD’s historic median; 21.25x as a base—approximately its 21.0x historical median with a 0.25x quality premium; and 23.5x as a high—above median but below the present 24.38x live P/E. The chosen range is deliberately below the market’s live multiple at the base and recognizes a slower 3–5-year EPS outlook of 5.5% projected by an independent research service, rather than assuming a return to the pandemic peak (an independent research service, Home Depot, June 12, 2026; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).

Independent dividend-yield cross-check

The quarterly dividend is $2.33, so annualized dividend is $2.33 × 4 = $9.32 (2026 10-Q, Note 6). An independent research service’s long-run average dividend yield is 2.4%, while its current report showed 3.0% at the then-current quote; use a cautious normal-yield range of 3.0% to 2.6%, not a heroic 2.0% yield (an independent research service, Home Depot, June 12, 2026). That gives:

  • Conservative dividend value = $9.32 ÷ 3.0% = $310.67 (2026 10-Q, Note 6; an independent research service, June 12, 2026).
  • Central dividend value = $9.32 ÷ 2.8% = $332.86 (2026 10-Q, Note 6; an independent research service, June 12, 2026).
  • Favorable dividend value = $9.32 ÷ 2.6% = $358.46 (2026 10-Q, Note 6; an independent research service, June 12, 2026).

This check brackets the multiple method’s $280–$355 range and supports a central value around $320, but it is secondary: dividends are a management decision and currently consume $9.152 billion ÷ $14.156 billion = 64.6% of fiscal-2025 net income (2026 10-K, statements of earnings and stockholders’ equity). A dividend yield is not intrinsic value if debt service or growth investment must outrank it.

Three universal cross-checks—one owner-earnings number

  1. No-growth floor. With the same normalized owner earnings of $15.06 per share and r = 9.0%, no-growth value is $15.06 ÷ 0.09 = $167.33 per share (analyst calculation; normalized OE above; 9.0% hurdle from an independent research service, July 10, 2026). Thus, current price less no-growth floor is $343.30 − $167.33 = $175.97, or $175.97 ÷ $343.30 = 51.3% of the quote that depends on growth, persistence, or a lower required return (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).
  2. Implied multiple. Base intrinsic value implies $320 ÷ $15.06 = 21.25x normalized owner earnings (analyst calculation). That is defensible only because HD’s long historical median is 21.0x and ROIC is 25.7%, not because 21x is intrinsically cheap (an independent research service, June 12, 2026; 2026 10-K, Item 7).
  3. Owner-earnings yield. At the live quote, $15.06 ÷ $343.30 = 4.39%, below the 4.86% 30-year Treasury yield (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026; an independent research service, Selection & Opinion, July 10, 2026). Owners receive a growing, riskier cash stream rather than a bond coupon; the spread says the purchase relies on eventual growth and multiple durability.

Sanity check against Mr. Market and an independent research service

The live price is $343.30 and live P/E is 24.38x, or 24.38x ÷ 21.0x historic median = 1.16x the median (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026; an independent research service, June 12, 2026). That price is plausible—not a broken data cell—because it lies within an independent research service’s June 2026 18-month range of $270–$514, though it is higher than an independent research service’s $310.69 report-date quote and well above our base owner-earnings estimate (an independent research service, Home Depot, June 12, 2026; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026). An independent research service’s reported ranks were Timeliness 4, Safety 1, and Financial Strength A+: an informed second opinion that says good balance-sheet/earnings reliability but below-average near-term price prospects, not a valuation answer (an independent research service, June 12, 2026).

Intrinsic-value conclusion: $280 low, $320 base, $355 high per share. The methods are not fully independent—both rely on earnings durability—but they fail at different points: the multiple tests capitalization of normal owner cash, and the yield check tests the distribution claim against required return.

PRINCIPLE: Value is discounted future owner earnings, but a precise DCF can hide fragile assumptions. For a mature cyclical franchise, capitalize normalized cash, triangulate with a payout check and the universal cross-checks, and prefer a candid range to a false target price.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Graham: The quote we actually face is $343.30 (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026). Put it beside the full range:

  • Margin versus low IV = ($280 − $343.30) ÷ $280 = −22.6% (analyst calculation; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).
  • Margin versus base IV = ($320 − $343.30) ÷ $320 = −7.3% (analyst calculation; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).
  • Margin versus high IV = ($355 − $343.30) ÷ $355 = 3.3% (analyst calculation; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).

The market price is near the optimistic end, not below conservative value. A student demanding a 20% cushion to the conservative $280 low would require $280 × (1 − 20%) = $224. Round up only modestly for a durable franchise: roughly $225 or below is the price at which a genuine conservative margin begins (analyst calculation). At that price, it would still be necessary to revisit the debt and acquired-business return.

Buffett: Quality is a portion of safety: HD’s brand, scale, and high ROIC make its normalized earnings more reliable than a commodity retailer’s. But quality never converts a 3.3% optimistic-case cushion into safety. The July an independent research service backdrop reinforces patience: inflation was elevated, the Fed’s stance was cautious, and an independent research service’s asset-allocation model was 65% stocks, 10% bonds, 25% cash; it also showed Building Materials up 11.1% over the prior six weeks (an independent research service, Selection & Opinion, July 10, 2026). We do not take that allocation as an instruction to trade HD. We take it as a reminder that cash is an option when broad enthusiasm and a housing-sensitive recovery are being priced before the evidence arrives.

Mr. Market seems roughly rational-to-optimistic: he acknowledges a durable franchise, but asks the buyer to accept a 4.39% owner-earnings yield below the long bond and to underwrite housing normalization and acquisition success. The patient student need not call him crazy. Simply decline to become his partner at this price.

PRINCIPLE: Margin of safety is a purchase discipline, not a compliment to a business. Let Mr. Market offer quotations; do not let a plausible story erase the discount needed for errors, debt, and cyclicality.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Verdict: wonderful business, fairly-to-fully priced; watch, do not buy at $343.30. The answer to our opening question is conditional: the core retail/Pro ecosystem remains capable of sustaining attractive economics, as 25.7% ROIC demonstrates, but the SRS/GMS return profile and housing recovery must prove themselves before the combined business deserves the market’s premium (2026 10-K, Item 7 and Note 2; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).

What we must understand better before genuine comfort:

  1. Whether SRS/GMS can improve from fiscal-2025 Other-segment operating margin of 2.5% without consuming disproportionate capital (2026 10-K, Note 2).
  2. Whether comparable-sales recovery becomes transaction-led rather than ticket/inflation-led after Q1’s −1.3% comparable transaction change (2026 10-Q, MD&A).
  3. Whether debt reduction and integration discipline continue while $4.684 billion of fiscal-2026 debt maturities come due (2026 10-K, Note 5).

The durable lessons are: (1) a household name is not automatically a bargain—normalize cash before assigning a multiple; (2) a high ROIC tells us why quality can be worth a premium, while leverage tells us why premium pricing still needs a cushion; and (3) acquisition-led sales growth must be separated from organic earnings quality.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS

Circle of competence. The businesses we can understand well enough to forecast; HD passes because its retail/Pro economics are intelligible, though housing and acquired-distribution returns require a range (Manual, ch. 4; 2026 10-K, Item 1).

Economic moat / economic goodwill. A structural advantage that protects returns; HD’s scale, brand, locations, supply chain and Pro relationships support a fiscal-2025 25.7% ROIC, while its accounting goodwill is $22.479b and is not the same thing (2026 10-K, Item 1 and Item 7; 2026 10-Q, Note 4).

Gross, operating, and net margin. Profit at each stage divided by sales: HD’s fiscal-2025 gross margin = $54.865b ÷ $164.683b = 33.3%; operating margin = $20.890b ÷ $164.683b = 12.7%; net margin = $14.156b ÷ $164.683b = 8.6% (2026 10-K, statements of earnings).

Free cash flow (FCF). Cash from operations less all capital expenditures: HD FCF = $16.325b − $3.679b = $12.646b (2026 10-K, cash-flow statement).

Maintenance versus growth capex. Maintenance capex preserves today’s earning power; growth capex expands it. We estimate maintenance as 75% × average $3.463b capex = $2.597b, leaving $3.463b − $2.597b = $0.866b as growth capex—an explicit judgment, not a reported line item (2026 10-K, Item 7 and cash-flow statement).

Owner earnings (OE). Cash a passive owner can withdraw: net income + noncash charges − maintenance capex − incremental working capital. HD raw OE = $14.702b + $3.710b − $2.597b − $0.024b = $15.791b; normalized OE = $15.791b × 95% = $15.001b, or $15.001b ÷ 996m = $15.06/share (2026 10-K; analyst normalization).

Normalized / mid-cycle earnings. Earnings adjusted to remove peak/trough conditions; HD’s $15.001b owner earnings are the three-year raw average reduced 5% for margin and integration caution (2026 10-K; analyst normalization).

NOPAT. Net operating profit after tax, or operating income × (1 − tax rate); HD reports $15.897b NOPAT after its operating-income and tax adjustment calculation (2026 10-K, Item 7).

Invested capital. Capital committed to operations, here average debt plus equity; HD’s denominator is $61.914b (2026 10-K, Item 7).

ROIC. Return on invested capital = NOPAT ÷ invested capital; HD ROIC = $15.897b ÷ $61.914b = 25.7% (2026 10-K, Item 7).

ROE. Return on equity = net income ÷ average equity; HD ROE = $14.156b ÷ [($12.813b + $6.640b) ÷ 2] = 145.5%, a figure flattered by leverage and historical repurchases (2026 10-K, balance sheets and statements of earnings).

Net debt. Total debt less cash; HD net debt = ($3.503b + $5.178b + $44.828b) − $1.601b = $51.908b (2026 10-Q, balance sheet).

Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest expense; HD coverage = $20.890b ÷ $2.412b = 8.66x (2026 10-K, statements of earnings).

Current ratio and working capital. Current assets ÷ current liabilities, and their difference: HD = $37.172b ÷ $35.580b = 1.04x and $37.172b − $35.580b = $1.592b (2026 10-Q, balance sheet).

Cost of capital / discount rate (r). The owner’s required return and the gravity on future cash; we use 4.86% long Treasury yield + 4.14% uncertainty premium = 9.0% (an independent research service, Selection & Opinion, July 10, 2026; Manual, ch. 12).

Intrinsic value. The present value of future owner earnings, IV = Σ OE_t/(1+r)^t; HD’s multiple-based range capitalizes $15.06 OE/share at 18.5x–23.5x = $280–$355/share (Manual, ch. 10; analyst calculation).

P/E and normalized P/E. Price ÷ earnings per share; HD live P/E is 24.38x, and our base normalized OE multiple is $320 ÷ $15.06 = 21.25x (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026; analyst calculation).

Dividend yield and payout ratio. Annual dividend ÷ price, and dividends ÷ earnings: HD annualized dividend yield = $9.32 ÷ $343.30 = 2.71%; fiscal-2025 payout = $9.152b ÷ $14.156b = 64.6% (2026 10-Q, Note 6; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026; 2026 10-K).

No-growth value. Owner earnings ÷ r—the value if OE never grows: HD = $15.06 ÷ 9.0% = $167.33/share (Manual, Appendix A; analyst calculation).

Owner-earnings yield. Owner earnings per share ÷ price: HD = $15.06 ÷ $343.30 = 4.39%, versus the 4.86% long-bond yield (equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026; an independent research service, July 10, 2026).

Margin of safety. (Intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value; HD base-case margin = ($320 − $343.30) ÷ $320 = −7.3%, not a cushion (Manual, ch. 13; equity_atlas.xlsx, July 12, 2026).

One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC is at least r; HD’s historical total spread is 25.7% − 9.0% = 16.7 points, while SRS/GMS returns remain the test of incremental capital allocation (Manual, ch. 9; 2026 10-K, Item 7 and Note 2).
