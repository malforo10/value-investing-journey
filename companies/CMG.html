Chipotle Mexican Grill (CMG) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition
Case prepared July 4, 2026 · live price $35.39 (post-split basis)

A teaching note before we begin. The two of us are going to think out loud about one business, slowly, showing every division and citing the page it came from. We are not here to tell you whether to buy Chipotle. We are here to rehearse a repeatable way of thinking — one you can carry to the next business, and the one after that. When we finish, you should be able to reproduce every number and, more importantly, every question. Read the arithmetic; then read the principle in bold beneath it. That principle is the part you keep.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

BUFFETT. Let me describe Chipotle the way I'd describe it to a partner who had never eaten there. It sells burritos, bowls, tacos, quesadillas, and salads, assembled in front of you on a line, from fresh ingredients the company brands "Food with Integrity" (10-K, Item 1). As of December 31, 2025 it owned and operated 4,042 restaurants — 3,938 in the United States, 104 international — plus 14 international partner-operated units (10-K, Item 1 and Note 1). It takes your money at the counter and recognizes the revenue then and there (10-K, Note 1, Revenue Recognition). One reportable segment. No franchising of the U.S. base — every domestic restaurant is company-owned. Total revenue in FY2025 was $11,925.6 million (Consolidated Statements of Income).

GRAHAM. So the business is simple to state. That is the first gift and the first trap. A thing you can describe in one sentence invites you to believe you can forecast it. Let us be careful about what is easy and what is hard here.

BUFFETT. Exactly. What is easy: understanding how a dollar of revenue becomes a dollar of profit today. What is hard: forecasting how many restaurants there will be in ten years, and how much each one will sell. Those two numbers — unit count and same-store sales — are the entire long-run story, and both are genuinely uncertain. In fact FY2025 gave us a warning shot: comparable restaurant sales fell 1.7% for the year, driven by transactions down 2.9%, only partly offset by a 1.2% higher average check (10-K, Item 7, Sales Trends). Management guides FY2026 comps to be "about flat."

GRAHAM. So here is THE ONE QUESTION this case turns on, and I want the class to hold it in mind through every calculation:

Chipotle is plainly a wonderful business. Is $35.39 a wonderful, a fair, or a foolish price for it — and where would a real margin of safety begin?

Everything else is in service of answering that.

THE CIRCLE-OF-COMPETENCE GATE. The manual (Ch. 4) says discounting future owner earnings presupposes you can estimate them, and that "too hard" is a complete and respectable answer. Does CMG pass the gate?

  • In the circle: the product, the economics of one restaurant, the balance sheet (no debt), the cash flows — all understandable.
  • At the boundary: the ten-year unit count and same-store sales. Management talks about a very large U.S. runway; the FY2025 comp decline shows the sales-per-unit line is not a straight ruler.

PRINCIPLE. A business can be easy to understand and still be hard to forecast. The circle-of-competence gate is not "do I get the product?" — it is "can I forecast the two or three numbers that actually drive value?" For CMG we pass the gate, but with our eyes open: the hard variable is same-store sales a decade out, and we will pay for that uncertainty in the discount rate and the margin of safety, not pretend it away.

Its largest liabilities, $5.08 billion combined, are operating leases — the capitalized present value of restaurant rent (10-K, Note 1, Leases). GRAHAM. Now notice something that would alarm a novice: equity fell from $3,655.5M to $2,830.6M, and retained earnings collapsed from $1,574.2M to $619.9M (Statements of Shareholders' Equity). Did the company lose money? No — it earned $1,535.8M. Equity fell because it repurchased $2,490.7M of its own stock and retired it (Statement of Shareholders' Equity, "Repurchase of common stock"). Book value went down while the business got bigger. This is our first lesson in why we never anchor value to book.

PRINCIPLE. Book value is an accounting residue, not a measure of worth. A great, buyback-heavy business can shrink its own book value to a rounding error and be worth more than ever. The manual (Ch. 7): "book value understates a great business and overstates a poor one." Chipotle proves it — $2.8B of book value stands behind a business the market prices near $45B.

GRAHAM. Now watch the three statements articulate. Net income of $1,535.8M (income statement) is the top line of operating cash flow; add back non-cash D&A of $361.4M and stock-based comp of $119.5M, and operating cash flow reaches $2,113.9M — more than reported profit, because the big add-backs are non-cash and the company runs on negative working capital (guests pay immediately; suppliers are paid ~10 days later — 10-K, Item 7, Liquidity). Then capex of $666.3M builds next year's restaurants, and $2,425.5M of cash walks out the door to buy back stock. The balance-sheet equity decline we saw is simply this cash-flow buyback landing on the equity statement. The story closes on itself.

Earnings-quality scan (manual Ch. 7 red-flag list)

  • EBITDA masquerading as cash flow? No — the company reports GAAP operating cash flow and real capex; it does not lead with EBITDA.
  • Serial "one-time" charges? Impairment/closure costs are small and steady ($27.5M, $26.9M, $38.4M over three years) — genuinely minor for a 4,000-unit base, not a recurring cost dressed as one-time.
  • Aggressive revenue recognition? Conservative. Gift-card and loyalty balances sit in unearned revenue until redeemed (Note 3). One yellow flag worth naming: gift-card breakage revenue jumped to $27.9M from $7.8M (Note 1) — a $20.1M swing that flattered FY2025 and is an estimate, not cash from a guest. Management discloses it plainly (Item 7 bridge), which is the tell of honest accounting.
  • Stock options treated as free? No — SBC of $121.4M is expensed, and the auditor flagged its performance assumptions as the critical audit matter (Item 8, EY report). We will treat SBC as a real cost, not add it back for free.
  • Opaque disclosure? The opposite — the disclosures are clear and specific.

PRINCIPLE. Read the three statements as one circulatory system. Net income is a claim; operating cash flow tests it; the balance sheet shows where the cash went. When equity falls while the business grows, the cash-flow statement will tell you why — here, buybacks. And run the red-flag list every time: CMG passes, with the single honest caveat that a $20M gift-card-breakage estimate, not guest cash, helped FY2025 earnings.

BUFFETT. 2016 is instructive — net income of just $23M. That was the E. coli food-safety crisis year. The business nearly stalled, then compounded revenue from ~$3.9B to ~$11.9B and net income from $23M to $1.5B in nine years. That is the earnings power. Operating cash flow more than quintupled. But notice the very top-right: 2025 net income ($1,536M) was essentially flat versus 2024 ($1,534M). The compounding paused this year. A pause is not a break — but it is data, and the price should reflect that the growth is not automatic.

Owner earnings — the maintenance-vs-growth-capex split

GRAHAM. This is the most important judgment in the whole case, so we do it slowly. The manual defines:

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital

The hard term is maintenance capex — the spend to stand still — as opposed to growth capex that builds new restaurants. Accounting lumps them into one $666.3M line. We must split them by hand, and management hands us the tools to do it (10-K, Item 7): in 2025 CMG opened 334 company-owned restaurants at "about $1.5 million in development and construction costs per new restaurant." And for 2026 it explicitly guides the split — ~$531.8M new-restaurant capex vs. ~$266.9M for existing restaurants (remodels, equipment, technology).

Let me estimate maintenance capex honestly. The tempting shortcut is a residual: assume 334 units × $1.5M = ~$501M of new-store construction, then call whatever is left over — total $666.3M − $501M ≈ $165M — "maintenance." But that residual understates true upkeep. It treats every dollar not spent on brand-new stores as if it vanished, when in fact a large slice of it is genuine standing-still spend: remodels, new equipment, and technology in the existing 4,000-store base. Management tells us so directly. Its FY2026 guidance (10-K, Item 7, MD&A) breaks capital spending into ~$531.8M for new restaurants and ~$266.9M for investments in existing restaurants (remodels, equipment, technology). That second figure — call it ~$250M for FY2025 — is the honest maintenance charge, not $165M. We use ~$250M.

Now owner earnings (per the manual's formula). We do not add back stock-based compensation: SBC of $121.4M (the 10-K's critical-audit-matter, Item 8, EY report) is a real cost. It dilutes owners, and the company spends billions of buyback dollars partly to mop it up. A non-cash expense is still an expense; we let it stand.

Owner Earnings = Net income        1,535.8
               + D&A                 361.4
               − Maintenance capex   250.0
               − ΔWorking capital     ~0     (negative-WC model; no drag)
               = 1,647.2  ($M)

Per share (on 1,282.7M current shares): 1,647.2 ÷ 1,282.7 = $1.28.

BUFFETT. Compare that to reported free cash flow: operating cash flow $2,113.9M − total capex $666.3M = $1,447.6M, or $1.13/share. Owner earnings ($1.28/share) sit above FCF ($1.13/share) — and that gap is the whole point of the exercise. Most of Chipotle's capex isn't keeping the lights on; it's building new stores that don't exist yet. If Chipotle stopped growing tomorrow, it would spend only its ~$250M maintenance charge and throw off more cash than reported FCF. The growth is a choice, funded out of a fountain of cash — not an obligation. Note the discipline in the number: we did not add SBC back and we used management's own ~$250M upkeep figure rather than the flattering $165M residual, so $1.28 is deliberately the sober owner-earnings estimate — sitting between FCF at $1.13 and the inflated figure a looser hand would have produced.

One small caveat left in the base: FY2025 earnings were flattered by a ~$20.1M gift-card-breakage tailwind (Note 1) — an accounting estimate, not guest cash, worth about 1% of net income. We leave it in the base for simplicity but flag it, because it makes even $1.28 a touch generous.

PRINCIPLE. For a fast grower, free cash flow understates the owner's true earnings, because most of the capex is discretionary expansion, not upkeep — but do not overcorrect. Take maintenance capex from management's own "investments in existing restaurants" guidance, not from a residual that mislabels remodels and technology as growth. And never add stock compensation back "because it is non-cash": it is a real cost of paying employees, so it stays subtracted. One honest owner-earnings figure, conservatively built, beats a flattering headline with an asterisk.

ROIC — the star of this case

GRAHAM. The manual (Ch. 6): ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital, and the decisive comparison is ROIC against the discount rate r. Let me compute NOPAT first:

NOPAT = Operating income × (1 − tax rate)
      = 1,935.8 × (1 − 0.236)          [effective tax rate 23.6%, Item 7]
      = 1,478.9  ($M)

Now invested capital, three honest ways — because leases matter here:

  1. On reported equity (the capital-light headline): 1,478.9 ÷ 2,830.6 = 52.2%
  2. Tangible, ex-lease (PP&E net $2,679.4M plus roughly −$300M negative working capital ≈ $2,379M): 1,478.9 ÷ 2,379 = ~62%
  3. Including capitalized-lease capital (PP&E $2,679.4M + operating-lease assets $4,463.0M = $7,142.4M): 1,478.9 ÷ 7,142.4 = 20.7%

BUFFETT. Which one is "true"? All three, for different questions. The honest answer for a business that rents its real estate is #3 — you must charge Chipotle for the capital tied up in its leased land, and even then it earns ~21% on total invested capital including leases, comfortably above any discount rate we'd use. Strip the leases and the tangible store investment throws off ~60%. This is a genuinely capital-light operating model wearing a capital-heavy real-estate coat. Either way: incremental ROIC vastly exceeds the cost of capital. That single fact is what makes the growth worth having.

ROE — watch for leverage

ROE = net income ÷ average equity = 1,535.8 ÷ [(3,655.5 + 2,830.6)/2] = 1,535.8 ÷ 3,243.1 = 47.4% (an independent research service reports ~50.5%, using year-end equity). But here is the discipline the manual demands: is this ROE inflated by leverage? No financial debt — so it is not borrowed-money leverage. It is being mechanically boosted by buybacks shrinking the denominator. A 47–55% ROE on zero debt is real quality; just don't read the level as if equity were a stable base — the buybacks make ROE partly an artifact of a shrinking book.

The moat — and what could erode it in ten years

BUFFETT. The manual's test for a moat (Ch. 5) is pricing power and the three-part economic-franchise test: needed/desired, no close substitute in the customer's mind, not price-regulated. Chipotle's moat:

  • Brand + "Food with Integrity" — a genuine, hard-to-copy identity (10-K, Item 1). Registered trademarks and trade dress.
  • Throughput and real-estate density — the assembly line moves people fast; "Chipotlanes" (drive-through digital pickup) were in 257 of 334 new 2025 units (Item 7). Digital was 36.7% of food-and-beverage revenue.
  • 100% company ownership of the U.S. base — total quality control, but capital-intensive to grow (that's the trade-off we quantified above).

Now the honest counter-evidence, because a moat must be argued, not assumed. Two things in this year's filings temper the story:

  1. Comps went negative (−1.7%). A true franchise with pricing power should be able to nudge price and hold volume. Management chose to abandon its usual 3–4% annual national price increase and go to smaller local adjustments (an independent research service, 2026-05-15) — that reads as a business feeling the limit of its pricing power in a "K-shaped economy" where lower-income diners are pulling back (Restaurant Industry report).
  2. Competition is real and rising. The Restaurant Industry page names CAVA (fast-growing, company-owned, premium fast-casual — the most direct threat), Wingstop, plus the broader burger and fast-casual field. CAVA is early in its growth cycle and taking share of the "affluent, health-conscious" diner Chipotle also courts.

PRINCIPLE. A moat is a claim about the future, and it must survive its own counter-evidence. ROIC of 20–60% is the moat's fingerprint — but the moment a company voluntarily forgoes a price increase and posts negative comps, you have watched the moat's pricing-power test get harder in real time. Quality is a matter of degree, and degree changes. Rate the moat wide but note the direction of travel.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

GRAHAM. The manual (Ch. 8) lists five uses of a dollar and asks us to judge management's track record across them. Let's do it with Chipotle's real numbers.

  1. Reinvest in the business — the best use when incremental ROIC is high, and here it is (20–60%). CMG plows ~$500M+ a year into new restaurants earning far above its cost of capital. This is textbook value-creating reinvestment.
  2. Acquisitions — essentially none of consequence; small equity stakes (Tractor Beverages 13.5%, Hyphen) — Note 5. Management has resisted the "institutional imperative" to do empire-building M&A. Good.
  3. Pay down debt — not applicable; there is no debt.
  4. Dividends — none, and none expected (an independent research service). Defensible only if retained dollars clear the one-dollar test.
  5. Buybacks — the big one: $2,425.5M repurchased in 2025 (cash-flow statement), with $1.7B of authorization remaining (Item 7).

The one-dollar test (manual Ch. 8)

Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r, so that ≥ $1 of market value is created per $1 retained.

CMG retains 100% of earnings (no dividend). Does each retained dollar create a dollar of value? For the portion reinvested in new restaurants at ~20–60% ROIC against a ~10% cost of capital — emphatically yes. That part of the test passes with room to spare.

But judge the buybacks on PRICE

BUFFETT. Here I must be the skeptic, because the manual is unambiguous: buybacks create value only when the stock is bought below intrinsic value, and destroy it above. Chipotle spent $2.43 billion last year retiring shares — and against our base intrinsic value of ~$29 (derived below), that money was largely value-neutral-to-destructive. an independent research service's 2025 price range was roughly $29.8–$61.2, so the company was buying its stock in the $40s and $50s through 2025 — well above ~$29 of intrinsic value — and even the Q1-2026 repurchases at ~$36 (10-Q) were struck above base value. Buying a dollar of value for a dollar-forty destroys forty cents for the remaining owners. This was not shrewd capital allocation; it was spending $2.4B to retire shares at prices richer than the business was worth.

At 30× earnings and above intrinsic value, a buyback is not a gift to remaining owners — it is a transfer away from them. Management would have created more value banking the cash and concentrating repurchases only when the stock trades below intrinsic value (the low-to-mid $20s on our numbers). That is the one place I would press this otherwise excellent management hard: the reinvestment is superb, but the 2025–Q1-2026 buyback program bought high.

Candor

Management discloses the ugly plainly: the negative comps, the transaction declines, the margin pressure, the decision to forgo price increases, the gift-card-breakage tailwind, the tariff impact (~15 bps ongoing). No boastful EBITDA-first framing. That candor earns trust.

PRINCIPLE. Retaining every dollar is only justified if every dollar clears the one-dollar test. CMG's reinvestment passes easily; its buybacks must be judged on price, and buying back stock at 30–60× earnings is not automatically shareholder-friendly — the price paid is everything. Praise the reinvestment, question the buyback discipline, and reward the candor.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

GRAHAM. Now the arithmetic of value. The manual (Ch. 9) gives the master equation:

IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ

We will triangulate three independent roads to value — a DCF, a P/E built from CMG's own history and its peers, and a first-principles quality multiple — and then reconcile them. The business deserves a DCF as the primary method, because its cash flows are forecastable-ish and it is a compounder; the multiples are cross-checks.

The discount rate — gravity (manual Ch. 11)

The manual says r is the owner's opportunity cost — the return you require to part with your money — anchored to a normalized long-term rate. We do not build it from a beta. Beta measures share-price wobble, not the odds of being wrong about the business; risk in this framework lives in the circle of competence, in conservative inputs, and above all in the margin of safety — not in a rate we scale up and down by a stock's volatility. From Selection & Opinion (2026-06-26) today's 10-year Treasury yields 4.47%; but we do not discount at spot, because spot rates are themselves a moment in a cycle. Instead we normalize and floor the owner's hurdle:

r = normalized owner's hurdle, floored near ~10%  (base)

We use r = 10% as the base and run it up to 11% and 12% to respect the forecast uncertainty of same-store sales. There is no beta and no company-specific premium bolted on — the 10% floor already embeds a sober required return for an equity owner. When the normalized rate environment shifts, revisit this — that is gravity at work.

Road 1 — Two-stage DCF on owner earnings

Starting owner earnings per share: $1.28 (derived above). Base r = 10%.

Scenario OE growth (10 yr) Terminal g r IV / share
Low 6% 2.5% 10.0% ~$22
Base 9% 2.5% 10.0% ~$29
High 12% 3.0% 10.0% ~$37

Every input is individually reasonable. The base assumes CMG grows owner earnings at 9% — new units plus a partial recovery of same-store sales from this year's dip — which is below its historical clip and reflects a maturing U.S. base.

DCF sensitivity — the false-precision lesson (manual Ch. 12)

Vary stage-1 OE growth and the discount rate (terminal g 2.5%); values are IV/share:

r ↓ / g₁ → 6% 8% 10% 12%
10% $23 $26 $30 $35
11% $20 $23 $26 $30
12% $18 $20 $23 $27

A no-growth floor is the cleanest reality check: with zero growth, IV = OE ÷ r = $1.28 / 0.10 = ~$13. Everything above that is what you are paying for growth.

BUFFETT. Look at that spread — from ~$18 to ~$35, all from assumptions no one could call unreasonable. This is the point, not a defect. Anyone quoting you a single "target price" for Chipotle to the penny is selling false precision. The honest output is a range, and the decision comes from the margin of safety, not the decimal.

Road 2 — P/E, from history and peers

GRAHAM. Where does a multiple come from? Three honest sources:

  • CMG's own history (an independent research service, 2026-05-15): current P/E 27.8; 10-year median P/E 63.7 — but that median is elevated by the 2016–2020 window when earnings were crushed by the E. coli crisis and P/E was meaningless (an independent research service prints "NMF" for several of those years). A median distorted by near-zero denominators is not a fair anchor. An independent research service's own 2029–31 projection uses a 30× average P/E — a far more sensible normalized figure.
  • Peer set (Restaurant Industry + Selection & Opinion): McDonald's at ~21.6× (mature, franchised, slower growth); CAVA far higher (early hypergrowth); Darden ~mid-teens. CMG sits between mature MCD and hypergrowth CAVA — a 28–35× band is defensible for a still-growing, best-in-class, debt-free operator.
  • Applying the band to live EPS of $1.17: 25× = $29.25; 30× = $35.10; 35× = $40.95; 45× = $52.65.

Road 3 — quality-and-ROIC-justified multiple, from first principles

A business earning 20%+ on incremental capital, growing owner earnings ~9%, with no debt, deserves a premium multiple. The DCF is that multiple, expressed as cash flows. At the base case, IV ~$29 ÷ EPS $1.17 = an implied ~25× P/E — squarely inside the peer band rather than above it. That is a feature, not an accident: because we refused to add SBC back, used management's honest maintenance capex, and floored r at 10%, the DCF no longer floats out to an optimistic multiple. It agrees with the peers.

Reconciliation — the triangle

BUFFETT. Let me put the three roads side by side and reconcile:

  • DCF (OE₀ $1.28, r=10%): low $22 / base $29 / high $37
  • P/E (peer-anchored, 25–35× on $1.17): low $29 / base $35 / high $41
  • An independent research service's own targets: 18-month $42 midpoint ($25–$59 range); 2029–31 target $60–$85.

The disciplined DCF base ($29) now sits below the peer-P/E base ($35) — and that disagreement is informative. The DCF, built on sober owner earnings and a 10% floored hurdle, says the market's habitual 30–35× multiple for Chipotle already embeds optimism about growth and margin recovery that this year's negative comps call into question. When a cash-flow model built on honest inputs comes in under the multiple the crowd is paying, that is a signal the multiple — not the model — is stretched. Leaning on the DCF (it is the method the business deserves, and it is the more conservative road), we set:

Intrinsic value: low ~$22 · base ~$29 · high ~$37 per share.

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth (manual Ch. 10)

One more check the manual insists on: is CMG's growth the good kind? g = reinvestment rate × ROIC. Because incremental ROIC (~20–60%) hugely exceeds r (~10%), every dollar CMG reinvests in new restaurants creates well more than a dollar of value. This is a compounding machine, not a value trap that grows itself poorer. The only question was never whether the growth creates value — it does — but how much of that value the current price already claims.

PRINCIPLE. Choose the method the business deserves — a DCF for a forecastable compounder — but never trust one road. Build each multiple from a named source (own history, peers, quality logic), and when the roads disagree, the disagreement is the finding: here, the DCF's optimism collides with negative comps, so we anchor conservative. And always confirm the growth is value-creating (ROIC > r) before you pay up for it — CMG's is, decisively.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

GRAHAM. Now we place the price beside the value. Live price $35.39. The manual's formula (Ch. 12):

Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) / Intrinsic Value

PRINCIPLE. Compute the margin of safety as arithmetic against your whole range, not one number — and let the reverse-DCF tell you what the price already assumes. When the price implies growth the business must strain to deliver, Mr. Market is optimistic, not generous, and there is no bargain to be had. A margin of safety is a discount you can see, not a story you can tell; at $35.39 there is none — the price sits above base value.

Where would a real cushion begin? A one-third margin of safety to our ~$29 base would be about $19–$20; even reaching base value means waiting for the low-to-mid $20s. Two or three open questions we cannot yet answer

  1. Do same-store sales recover, or has the U.S. base matured? FY2025's −1.7% comp is the crux. If it's a pause, the DCF base holds; if it's the new normal, value is nearer our low case.
  2. Can Chipotle hold pricing power against CAVA and a K-shaped consumer? Voluntarily forgoing the annual price increase is a small crack worth watching.
  3. Will management concentrate buybacks at low prices rather than spending $2.4B near fair value? Capital-allocation discipline from here is worth a real amount of per-share value.

Universal lessons this case teaches

  1. A wonderful business is only a wonderful investment at the right price. Quality and price are two separate judgments; CMG aces the first and merely passes the second. "Price, not quality alone, makes the investment" (manual, Ch. 13).
  2. Owner earnings ≠ free cash flow for a fast grower. Split maintenance from growth capex and the true earning power appears — CMG's owner earnings exceed its FCF because most of its spending is optional expansion.
  3. The reverse-DCF is the antidote to false precision. Instead of arguing for a target price, ask what growth the current price demands, then judge whether the business can clear that bar. It converts an unknowable forecast into a testable question.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with CMG's numbers plugged in)

  • Intrinsic value. The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. CMG base ≈ $29/share (range ~$22–$37).
  • Owner earnings. Cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. OE = Net income + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. SBC is a real cost and is not added back. CMG: 1,535.8 + 361.4 − 250.0 ≈ $1,647M, or $1.28/share.
  • Maintenance vs. growth capex. Maintenance = spend to stand still; growth = expansion. CMG FY2025: total capex $666.3M; maintenance taken from management's own "investments in existing restaurants" guidance at ~$250M (not the flattering ~$165M residual), the rest growth.
  • Free cash flow. Operating cash flow − total capex. CMG: 2,113.9 − 666.3 = $1,447.6M ($1.13/share) — below owner earnings because most capex is growth.
  • ROIC. NOPAT ÷ invested capital. NOPAT = 1,935.8 × (1−0.236) = $1,478.9M. Incl. lease capital ($7,142M): 20.7%; on equity: 52.2%; tangible: ~60%.
  • NOPAT. After-tax operating profit. CMG: 1,935.8 × 0.764 = $1,478.9M.
  • ROE. Net income ÷ average equity. CMG: 1,535.8 ÷ 3,243.1 = 47.4% (VL: ~50.5% on year-end equity); flattered by buyback-shrunk equity, not by debt (debt = 0).
  • Moat / economic franchise. A durable advantage protecting high returns; signature is pricing power. CMG: brand + throughput + density; pricing power tested (forwent 2026 price hike, comps −1.7%).
  • Discount rate (r). Owner's opportunity cost, a normalized long-term hurdle floored near ~10% — no beta, no company-specific premium bolted on. CMG: base 10.0% (range 10–12%). Today's 10-yr Treasury is 4.47%, but we normalize and floor rather than discount at spot.
  • Terminal growth (g). Perpetual growth after the explicit forecast. CMG: 2.5% base (2.5–3.0%).
  • Two-stage DCF. IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. CMG base (OE₀ $1.28, 9% 10 yr, g 2.5%, r 10%) ≈ $29.
  • Reverse DCF. Solve for the growth the price implies. CMG at $35.39 (r 10%, g 2.5%) implies ~12% owner-earnings growth for 10 years (~14% at r 11%).
  • Value-creating growth. Growth adds value only when ROIC > r. CMG: ~20–60% ROIC vs. 10% r → strongly value-creating.
  • One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). CMG reinvestment: passes; buybacks in the $40s–$50s (2025) and ~$36 (Q1-2026) above ~$29 IV: value-neutral-to-destructive.
  • Margin of safety. (IV − Price) ÷ IV. CMG at $35.39 vs. ~$29 base = −22% (price above value); vs. ~$22 low = −61% (none).
  • Mr. Market. The manic partner quoting daily prices. On CMG: down 38% over the past year, recently recovering ($31.98 → $35.39) and now pricing ~12% growth for a decade — rational-to-mildly-optimistic, arguably slightly rich, not a bargain.
  • Book value (why we ignore it). CMG equity fell to $2,830.6M while the business grew, because $2,490.7M of buybacks retired stock — proof book value understates a great business.
