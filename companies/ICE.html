Valuing Intercontinental Exchange (NYSE: ICE)

A Value-Investing Seminar, In the Graham–Buffett tradition · Teaching date: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $132.99 · Method manual: How to Value a Business (rev. 2026-07-04)

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Warren: Let me tell you what Intercontinental Exchange actually is, because the name tells you almost nothing. ICE is a toll booth on the movement of financial risk. It owns the road; everyone who wants to hedge, trade, price, or originate pays to use it. There are three toll roads (10-K, Item 1 and Note 1; FY2025 revenue mix per the income statement, gross of transaction pass-throughs):

1. Exchanges — $8,120M of 2025 revenue (64%). This is the New York Stock Exchange (acquired via NYSE Euronext, 2013), plus ICE's own energy and agricultural derivatives franchise — the global benchmark for Brent crude oil, plus natural gas, softs, and interest-rate futures. The magic here is not the trading fee alone; it is open interest. When you hold a futures position, it clears at ICE's clearing house, and your margin sits there. To move it to a rival you must unwind and re-establish liquidity that does not exist anywhere else. That is a switching cost measured in basis points of slippage, and it is why a liquid contract, once dominant, stays dominant for decades. Segment operating income: $3,982M — over 80% of the whole company's operating profit (10-K, Note on segments, part 8).

2. Fixed Income & Data Services — $2,419M (19%). Bond pricing, reference data, indices (ICE built the pricing utility the fixed-income world runs on), analytics, and CDS clearing. This is largely recurring subscription revenue — customers embed ICE's data feeds into their own risk systems and cannot rip them out without re-plumbing everything. Segment operating income: $933M.

3. Mortgage Technology — $2,101M (17%). The workflow software for U.S. residential mortgage origination and servicing, assembled from Ellie Mae ($11.4B, 2020) and Black Knight ($11.8B, 2023). Loan officers originate on ICE's platform; servicers service on it. Segment operating income (GAAP): $14M — essentially zero, because nearly all of the $993M annual amortization of acquisition intangibles lands here. Read carefully: this segment throws off real cash but shows almost no accounting profit. Hold that thought; it is the crux of the whole valuation.

How each makes money, and from whom: banks, asset managers, corporations, energy majors, and mortgage lenders — the plumbing of global finance. Two revenue characters: transaction (rises with volatility and volume) and recurring (data and subscriptions, up 5% in 2025; transaction up 10% per an independent research service).

The circle-of-competence gate (manual Ch. 4). Ben: Can we forecast this business? Largely, yes — and that is the first thing that makes ICE ownable. A network-effect exchange with entrenched open interest is one of the more predictable franchises in capitalism; its long-run economics are legible. The one genuinely hard-to-forecast leg is Mortgage Technology, whose origination volumes swing with mortgage rates. So ICE sits mostly inside the circle, with one foot on the boundary. We handle the boundary the honest way — by valuing the cyclical leg conservatively, not by pretending it is smooth.

THE ONE QUESTION we must answer to value ICE: Will ICE's toll booths — exchange open interest, embedded data feeds, and mortgage-workflow lock-in — still be collecting rising tolls a decade from now, and did management overpay so badly for the mortgage road that the tolls never repay the debt?

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

Warren: Three statements, read as one story. All figures FY2025 unless noted; from the audited 10-K, Item 8.

The balance sheet — and the illusion of a $137 billion company

Total assets: $136,887M. Do not be fooled. Of that, $76,789M is "cash and cash equivalent margin deposits and guaranty funds" — customer margin held by the clearing houses — matched by an identical $76,789M liability. That is not ICE's money; it passes straight through and nets to zero. Strip the clearing pass-through and ICE is a far smaller enterprise. This is why the manual (Ch. 8, special cases) warns that a clearing/financial business must be read on different yardsticks — the gross balance sheet is mostly other people's money.

What is really ICE's:
- Goodwill $30,646M + other intangibles $15,353M = $45,999M ($81.13/share per an independent research service) — 34% of total assets, and far more than that as a share of the real business. This is the accounting scar tissue of NYSE, Ellie Mae, and Black Knight.
- Long-term debt $18,609M + short-term debt $1,035M = $19,644M total. Cash $837M. Net debt ≈ $18,807M.
- Stockholders' equity $28,915M — but note it is stuffed with acquisition goodwill. Do not anchor to book (manual Ch. 7): book value here is an artifact of purchase accounting, not a measure of the franchise.

Ben: The tangible common equity — equity minus goodwill and intangibles — is negative (28,915 − 45,999 ≈ −$17.1B). In a bank that would terrify me. In a toll-booth franchise it is merely the arithmetic of paying up for durable networks; the value is in the tolls, not the tangible assets. But it is exactly why we must scrutinize the debt (below).

The income statement — margins and trend

Revenue $12,640M; less transaction-based pass-throughs (Section 31 fees, clearing/routing) of $2,709M → net revenue $9,931M. Operating income $4,929M — a 49.6% operating margin on net revenue (52% on the an independent research service basis). Net income attributable to ICE $3,315M; diluted EPS $5.77.

The ten-year trend (XBRL): revenue $5,971M (2016) → $12,640M (2025), and net income $526M-ish operating base grown to $3,315M. Operating income has marched up every single year: $3,449M (2021) → $3,638 → $3,694 → $4,309 → $4,929M (2025). That monotonic climb — through a pandemic, a rate shock, and a mortgage-origination collapse — is the signature of a franchise, not a commodity.

Cash flow — where the truth lives

Operating cash flow $4,662M vs. net income of $3,315M — OCF exceeds NI by ~1.4×, the mark of a high-quality, non-cash-charge-heavy business (that gap is the $1,560M of D&A, most of it acquisition-intangible amortization). Capital spending: capex $373M + capitalized software $418M = $791M of cash reinvestment — only ~17% of OCF. Free cash flow ≈ $4,662 − $791 ≈ $3,871M. This is a capital-light franchise wearing a capital-heavy balance sheet: it generates cash like See's and carries debt like a leveraged buyout.

How the three articulate

The income statement's $1,560M D&A is a non-cash charge → added back on the cash flow statement, which is why OCF >> NI. That D&A traces to the $45,999M of intangibles on the balance sheet — the accounting cost of acquisitions that also put the $19,644M of debt there. One transaction (Black Knight) simultaneously depressed reported mortgage profit, inflated the balance sheet, and levered the company. You cannot understand any one statement without the other two.

Earnings-quality scan (manual Ch. 7)

  • Adjusted vs. GAAP. Management reports adjusted net income $3,993M / adjusted diluted EPS $6.95 vs. GAAP $3,315M / $5.77 (10-K MD&A reconciliation). The entire gap is dominated by +$993M "amortization of acquisition-related intangibles" (plus $66M transaction costs, small tax items). Ben: Here we must be careful, not credulous. Amortization of an acquired customer relationship or trade name is a genuinely non-cash charge — no dollar leaves the building. So adding it back for owner earnings is legitimate in substance. But — and this is the discipline — those intangibles represent competitive positions that must be maintained, and ICE spends real cash ($791M) doing so. So the honest owner-earnings figure is not the full add-back; it is the add-back net of the maintenance reinvestment. We will not simply swallow the company's $6.95.
  • Red flags? Few. ICE does not push EBITDA as if it were free cash (the manual's cardinal sin). "Acquisition-related transaction and integration costs" have run for years ($269M → $104M → $70M) and are winding down — a legitimate one-time-ish bucket that is finally shrinking, not a perpetual "restructuring" gimmick. Stock-based comp is a modest $238M (~2.4% of net revenue) and is honestly disclosed. Working capital is roughly neutral. The one auditor-flagged judgment area is uncertain tax positions ($206M) — immaterial to value.

Verdict on quality: high. The numbers are trustworthy; the only real subjectivity is how much of the $993M intangible amortization to credit back — which we resolve conservatively below.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Long-run earning power (XBRL)

Adjusted-basis EPS: $5.62 (2023) → $6.07 (2024) → $6.95 (2025), ~11% annual. Revenue compounded ~15%/yr over ten years (an independent research service), decelerating to a projected 7% as the base grows and acquisitions are digested. An independent research service projects EPS to $9.20 by 2029–31.

Owner earnings (manual Ch. 7) — the number that matters

Owner Earnings = Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWorking capital

  • Reported (attributable) net income: $3,315M
  • Add back non-cash D&A: +$1,560M (of which $993M is acquisition-intangible amortization)
  • Subtract maintenance reinvestment: I use −$791M (the full cash capex + capitalized software), which is deliberately conservative — it treats all of ICE's reinvestment as maintenance, giving zero free credit for growth spend
  • ΔWorking capital: ~$0 (operating WC roughly neutral; margin flows net out)

Owner earnings ≈ 3,315 + 1,560 − 791 ≈ $4,084M, call it ~$4,100M, or ≈ $7.13 per diluted share (575M shares).

Ben: Notice this lands almost exactly on the company's adjusted net income of $3,993M — but we got there by a stingier road (subtracting all $791M of reinvestment rather than the ~$567M of non-acquisition depreciation). That agreement is reassuring: two independent methods, one answer. I will anchor the valuation on ~$4,100M of owner earnings, not the rosier $4,308M a full-D&A-minus-maintenance-proxy calculation would give. Margin of safety begins in the inputs, not just the price.

ROIC — the heart of the ICE paradox (manual Ch. 6)

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital

NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax) = 4,929 × (1 − 0.225) = $3,822M (effective tax 22.5%).

  • On total invested capital (equity + debt − cash = $47,722M, which includes the $46B of acquisition goodwill/intangibles): ROIC = 3,822 ÷ 47,722 = 8.0%.
  • On tangible/ex-goodwill capital (~$1,723M): ROIC = ~220% — effectively infinite.

Warren: This split is the ICE story in two numbers. The underlying franchise earns astronomical returns on the tangible capital it employs — a genuine capital-light, network-effect toll booth, exactly the See's-Candy archetype the manual prizes. But ICE paid up — $46B of goodwill — to buy those franchises, and on the all-in capital an owner actually sunk in, ROIC is only ~8%, barely at the cost of capital. The manual's rule (Ch. 6, Ch. 11): value is created only when ROIC exceeds r. On tangible capital ICE laps the field; on total capital it is running even. The moat is real; the question is whether ICE overpaid to rent it. That is the one-dollar test we run on the deals below.

ROE = 3,315 ÷ 28,915 = 11.5% — respectable but not spectacular, and note (manual Ch. 6) it is held down, not flattered, by the equity being swollen with goodwill; leverage is not manufacturing a fake-high ROE here.

The moat — sources and durability

  • Network effects / open interest (Exchanges): liquidity begets liquidity; Brent and ICE's rate complex are self-reinforcing. Very wide, very durable.
  • Switching costs / data lock-in (Fixed Income & Data): pricing feeds embedded in customers' risk systems; recurring revenue that renews almost automatically.
  • Regulatory barriers: exchanges and clearing houses are licensed, supervised, systemically important — you cannot simply start a rival NYSE. This is a moat governments actively maintain.
  • Mortgage-workflow lock-in (Mortgage Tech): origination + servicing on one platform; sticky, but the volume is rate-cyclical.

Diagnostic (manual Ch. 5): pricing power. ICE raised recurring-data and exchange fees while growing volume for a decade — the textbook signature of a franchise. It passes.

Ten-year erosion risks — argued, not assumed:
1. Regulation cuts both ways — the same rules that bar competitors could cap fees or force the mortgage-data utility to open up (antitrust scrutiny of Black Knight was real).
2. Decentralization / crypto & prediction markets — new venues (Coinbase, prediction markets) nibble at trading volume; so far marginal to ICE's core, but worth watching.
3. Mortgage-tech cyclicality — a prolonged high-rate environment starves origination volumes (the boundary-of-competence leg).
4. The debt itself — a wonderful franchise financed heavily is more fragile than one financed conservatively (Ch. 8).

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY (manual Ch. 8)

Ben: This is where I earn my keep, because ICE took on serious debt for Black Knight. Five questions, answered with ICE's own numbers.

  • Leverage. Net debt $18,807M against owner earnings ~$4,100M → net debt / OE ≈ 4.6×; against operating income → net debt / EBIT ≈ 3.8×; against EBITDA ($6,489M) → 2.9×. In plain English: it would take the company under five years of owner earnings to clear its net debt. For a cyclical that would be worrying; for a business with ICE's recurring, toll-booth stability, it is a manageable load. The manual's rule is to judge the burden by the stability of the cash that services it — and ICE's cash is among the steadiest in the market (an independent research service Earnings Predictability = 100, the maximum).
  • Coverage. EBIT $4,929M ÷ interest expense $803M = 6.1× interest coverage (an independent research service computes 5.8× on LT interest). A normal year covers the interest bill six times over — comfortable, not heroic.
  • Liquidity. Setting aside the clearing pass-through, ICE holds $837M cash plus an undrawn $3.9B revolving credit facility (extended to 2029), backstopping a $1.0B commercial-paper program. Ample near-term liquidity.
  • Refinancing wall. None. The senior notes are laddered from 2027 out to 2062 at fixed coupons (1.85%–5.25%), with only staggered maturities in any year. ICE does not depend on the "continued kindness of lenders" (Ch. 8) — it controls its own fate.
  • Hidden obligations. Modest: operating leases ~$71M/yr, a small pension (assets $664M vs. obligation $595M — overfunded), $206M unrecognized tax positions. Nothing lurking.

One-line verdict: ADEQUATE-TO-STRONG — a fortress balance sheet wearing a heavy but well-laddered coat of investment-grade debt (an independent research service Financial Strength A). The leverage is real and cost ICE its financial purity, but the cash flow that services it is exceptionally stable, and the maturities are spread across four decades. This is not a balance sheet that can be broken by a single bad year — but it is not the debt-free fortress a Coca-Cola or a CME carries, and that is the price of the acquisition strategy.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS (manual Ch. 9)

Warren: Jeffrey Sprecher built ICE from a 2000 startup into a $92B franchise — one of the great capital-allocation records of the era, and the author of its one live controversy. Judge him on the six uses of a dollar.

  • Reinvest in the business — high incremental ROIC on the tangible base; ~$791M/yr, well spent on data centers, AI tooling, product.
  • Acquisitions — the double-edged sword. NYSE (2013), Ellie Mae ($11.4B, 2020), Black Knight ($11.8B, 2023). The first two look like triumphs; Black Knight is the open question.
  • Deleveraging — after Black Knight, ICE prioritized paying down debt: repayments of $2,500M in 2025, $1,600M in 2024; total debt is declining. Disciplined.
  • Dividends — raised every year; $1.92/share in 2025 (8% hike for 2026 to ~$2.08), ~28% payout. Room to grow.
  • Buybacks — paused in 2023–24 to fund deleveraging (repurchases = $0 those years), then resumed at $1,294M in 2025. Buying below intrinsic value is value-creating (Ch. 9); at today's ~$133 that is a reasonable use of cash. Good sequencing: pay down debt first, buy back second.
  • Share count — the acid test of Ch. 9. Diluted shares: 595M (2016) → 567M (2025), net down ~5% over a decade despite issuing 11M shares for Black Knight. Sprecher funded the Black Knight equity portion and still shrank the count — he respects the per-share claim.

The one-dollar test on the big deals (manual Ch. 9). Ben: NYSE and Ellie Mae clearly pass — the franchises they added throw off far more than a dollar of value per dollar spent. Black Knight is the case to interrogate. ICE paid $11.8B; the combined Mortgage Technology segment earns only $14M of GAAP operating income (crushed by amortization) but perhaps ~$700–900M of cash operating income once the $779M of segment intangible amortization is added back. On cash economics the deal earns a high-single-digit return on the $11.8B price — right around the cost of capital, not comfortably above it. Combined with the $275M of promised synergies by 2028 and any mortgage-volume recovery, it likely clears the one-dollar bar — but only modestly, and only if rates normalize. This is the Dexter-Shoe warning (Ch. 9) in mild form: a good business bought at a full price, financed with debt. The saving grace, unlike Dexter, is that Sprecher paid mostly in cash and debt, not in undervalued stock — so a merely-adequate deal did not get multiplied into a large error.

Candor: management reports adjusted and GAAP plainly, does not hide the mortgage segment's GAAP near-zero, and telegraphs its synergy targets. Fair marks.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE (manual Ch. 10–14)

Warren: Anchor: normalized owner earnings ≈ $4,100M (~$7.13/diluted share), derived conservatively above. r = 9% — the manual's gravity (Ch. 12): long Treasury 4.4% + ~4.5% equity/uncertainty premium, appropriate for a durable but leveraged, partly-cyclical franchise. ICE deserves a two-stage DCF (durable compounder) reconciled against a normalized-earnings multiple, because both the letters' method and the numbers point the same way.

Horizon = 10 years (the moat reaches that far). Owner earnings $4,100M, three scenarios:

The estimate ranges from ~$126 to ~$210 on individually reasonable assumptions — a wide band, exactly as the manual promises (Ch. 13). The spread is the point, not a defect.

Method 2 — Normalized-earnings multiple (reconciliation)

Where does a fair multiple come from? Three roads (manual Ch. 14):
- Historical median P/E. an independent research service's median P/E for ICE is 21 (2026-04-03 report); the 10-year average sits ~20–21. On adjusted EPS $6.95, 21× = $146.
- Peer/industry multiple (used only as a cross-check, never as the primary anchor — Ch. 14 excludes peer-multiple valuation from the core method). The pure-play exchange peers — CME, CBOE (Cboe Global Markets), Nasdaq (NDAQ) — and the data-franchise cousins S&P Global (SPGI) and Moody's (MCO) — trade in a ~20–28× earnings band; the highest-quality data monopolies (SPGI, MCO) command the top of it. ICE's blend of exchange + data + mortgage argues for the middle: ~19–22× on adjusted EPS.
- Quality-justified multiple from first principles. A franchise earning ~220% on tangible capital, growing owner earnings ~6–7% with a wide moat, at r = 9%, justifies well over 20× owner earnings — the DCF's own implied multiple (22.4× at base) confirms it. But the leverage and the merely-adequate Black-Knight return argue for restraint, capping the multiple rather than pushing it to the SPGI/MCO stratosphere.

Reconciled multiple range: ~18–22× adjusted EPS $6.95 = $125 – $153.

The three cross-checks, as arithmetic (manual Ch. 14)

  1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r = $4,100M ÷ 0.09 = $45,556M ≈ $79 per share. So at $133, roughly $54 of the price (~40%) is a payment for growth — a moderate, not reckless, bet on the toll booths growing. Everything above $79 must be earned by growth showing up.
  2. Implied multiple = IV ÷ OE. At the base $160/share, that is 22.4× owner earnings — squarely defensible for a wide-moat compounder at 9% and 6.5% growth. The machine is not smuggling in a runaway terminal value (a 40× output would have been the alarm; 22× is not).
  3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond. At $133, ICE yields $4,100M ÷ $75,525M market cap = 5.4%, and that coupon grows ~6–7% a year — against a static 4.4% ten-year Treasury. A growing 5.4% claim clearly beats a fixed 4.4% one. This is Chapter 12's gravity applied at the moment of decision: the yield check passes, but not by a wide margin — the stock is reasonably priced, not cheaply priced.

Triangulation → the range

The roads converge in a band: DCF low/base/high $126 / $160 / $210; historical-median-P/E $146; reconciled multiple $125–$153; an independent research service 3-to-5-year target midpoint ~$182 (their 18-month low-end $132 essentially equals today's price). Reconciling — and leaning conservative per the manual — I set:

Intrinsic value: LOW $125 · BASE $150 · HIGH $180 per share

Sanity checks: the base ($150) implies ~21.6× adjusted EPS — right at ICE's historical median and below the SPGI/MCO data-monopoly premium; the low ($125) implies ~18× — the level a skeptic pricing in mortgage cyclicality and the leverage would pay; the high ($180) aligns with an independent research service's own target zone. Live P/E on adjusted EPS is 19.1× — inside the low-to-base band, i.e., the market is pricing ICE modestly below our central estimate but above our conservative floor.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET (manual Ch. 2, 13)

Live price: $132.99. Against the range:

vs. IV Discount/(premium)

Ben: Here is the disciplined truth. At $133, ICE trades at an ~11% discount to our base intrinsic value and a ~6% premium to our conservative low case. The manual (Ch. 13) demands a wide margin against the conservative end — and by that strict test, ICE does not clear the bar today. The safety margin against the base case is real but slim; against the low case it is negative. This is the manual's "paying up for quality" tension (Ch. 13): part of ICE's safety lives in the durability itself — a conservatively projected, growing owner-earnings stream makes today's estimate tomorrow's floor — but that does not license paying any price, and $133 is only a modest discount, not the conspicuous one we insist on.

Why is it here? Warren: Mr. Market is having one of his moods. The Brokers & Exchanges group was the 2nd-worst-performing industry in the six weeks to 6/29/2026 (−13.0%, per Selection & Opinion) — geopolitical fear, mortgage-rate worries, sector rotation out of financials. ICE fell from ~$157 (its April an independent research service report price) to $133 while the business kept compounding (2025 EPS +14%). That is precisely the raw material the manual describes (Ch. 2): a falling quote on a business whose value is intact. But "improved" is not the same as "cheap enough." Mr. Market is offering a fair price on a wonderful business — he has not yet offered a foolish one.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Warren & Ben, together:

Business quality: GOOD-approaching-WONDERFUL — a genuine network-effect toll-booth franchise, held back from "wonderful" by acquisition debt and a merely-adequate return on the Black Knight price. The Exchanges and Data segments are as fine a franchise as exists in finance; the Mortgage-Tech leg and the $19.6B of debt are the asterisks.

The call: a WONDERFUL BUSINESS at a FAIR — not a bargain — PRICE. HOLD / watch, do not back up the truck. At $133 (IV base $150), the ~11% discount is enough to own ICE if you already do, and enough for a patient long-term compounder to begin accumulating — but it is not the wide margin of safety that says "buy in size." The manual's discipline (Ch. 13): below the conservative range you buy aggressively; inside it — which is where $133 sits, between our $125 low and $150 base — you buy modestly or wait. We wait for Mr. Market's next mood swing to take it toward $115–$120, where the margin of safety would become conspicuous.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION: Yes — the exchange, data, and mortgage toll booths will almost certainly still be collecting rising tolls in a decade (the moat is durable and regulatorily reinforced). And Black Knight was a full price, not a ruinous one — it likely clears the one-dollar test, but only modestly, and its verdict rests on mortgage volumes recovering. The franchise is safe; the purchase price ICE paid for part of it is the swing factor.

Two or three open questions to keep watching:
1. Does Mortgage Technology's cash profit inflect upward as mortgage origination recovers — turning Black Knight from "adequate" into "good"?
2. Does regulation stay a moat (barring rivals) or become a cap (limiting fees / forcing data access)?
3. Does net debt keep falling toward ~2× EBITDA, restoring balance-sheet purity and freeing cash for buybacks below value?

Universal lessons from the ICE case:
- A wonderful franchise financed heavily is not the same investment as a wonderful franchise financed conservatively. The moat protects the earnings; the balance sheet decides whether you get to keep collecting them through a bad year. Read both.
- Acquisitions are where great allocators go to make their one big mistake — judge them by the return on the price paid, not the quality of the thing bought. ICE bought superb franchises; whether it created value depends entirely on what it paid. Goodwill is the receipt.
- "Fair price on a wonderful business" is a real answer — and it usually means wait. The market rarely hands you a great business at a foolish price; when it merely hands you one at a fair price, admiring it and waiting is a position, not a failure.

Teaching-case disclaimer: this seminar is an educational exercise in applying a valuation method to real filings as of 2026-07-04. It is not investment advice, not a recommendation to buy or sell ICE, and every figure should be re-verified against primary sources before any capital is committed.

Post-purchase hold/sell discipline (manual Ch. 16) — for an owner: hold, favorite holding period forever, so long as (a) the thesis holds (moat intact, mortgage cash profit not deteriorating, debt falling); sell only if (b) the thesis breaks — Black Knight impairs, a rival cracks open interest, or regulation caps the tolls; or (c) Mr. Market's mood inverts and offers a price well above $180, the generous end of the range. GLOSSARY (definitions + formulas + ICE's numbers)

  • Owner earnings. Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC; the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. ICE: 3,315 + 1,560 − 791 − 0 ≈ $4,100M (~$7.13/diluted share).
  • Intrinsic value. Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ — discounted value of all future owner earnings; a per-share, estimate-not-point quantity. ICE: triangulated range $125 / $150 / $180.
  • No-growth (earning-power) value. OE ÷ r; the floor if growth stopped. ICE: 4,100 ÷ 0.09 = $45.6B ≈ $79/share; ~40% of the $133 price is a bet on growth.
  • ROIC. NOPAT ÷ invested capital; business quality vs. r. ICE: NOPAT $3,822M ÷ total invested capital $47,722M = 8.0% (goodwill-inflated); ÷ tangible capital ≈ 220%.
  • NOPAT. After-tax operating profit = EBIT × (1 − tax). ICE: 4,929 × (1 − 0.225) = $3,822M.
  • Cost of capital / discount rate (r). Owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long rates + uncertainty premium. ICE: 9% (10-yr Treasury 4.4% + ~4.6% premium).
  • Margin of safety. (IV − Price) ÷ IV; the cushion against error. ICE: vs. base $150 = +11%; vs. low $125 = −6% — too thin for a "buy in size."
  • Moat / economic franchise. Durable advantage (network, switching costs, regulation) protecting high returns; signature is pricing power. ICE: open interest, data lock-in, exchange licenses — wide.
  • Interest coverage. EBIT ÷ interest expense; solvency cushion. ICE: 4,929 ÷ 803 = 6.1×.
  • Net debt / owner earnings (and /EBITDA). (Total debt − cash) ÷ OE; years to clear net debt. ICE: 18,807 ÷ 4,100 = 4.6× (≈ 2.9× EBITDA).
  • Adjusted vs. GAAP earnings. Company non-GAAP figure excluding intangible amortization etc. ICE: adjusted NI $3,993M / $6.95 EPS vs. GAAP $3,315M / $5.77 — the gap is the $993M acquisition-intangible amortization.
  • Goodwill & acquisition intangibles. Balance-sheet cost of paying above tangible value for franchises; book value overstates the tangible business. ICE: $45,999M ($81.13/share) — 34% of assets; tangible common equity is negative.
  • One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if it creates ≥ $1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r). ICE: NYSE/Ellie Mae pass clearly; Black Knight ($11.8B) passes only modestly on cash economics ~at cost of capital.
  • EBIT / operating income. Profit before interest and tax. ICE: $4,929M (49.6% of net revenue).
  • Free cash flow. OCF − cash capex. ICE: 4,662 − 791 = ~$3,871M.
  • Mr. Market. The manic-depressive partner quoting prices by his mood; serve, don't be guided. ICE: just marked it down ~15% (sector −13%) while the business grew — a mood, not a verdict.
  • Implied multiple. IV ÷ OE, a translation-check on the DCF. ICE: base IV = 22.4× owner earnings — defensible for the quality.
  • Owner-earnings yield. OE ÷ market cap, vs. the long bond. ICE: 4,100 ÷ 75,525 = 5.4%, growing ~6–7%, vs. 4.4% Treasury — a superior but not a screaming-cheap claim.
