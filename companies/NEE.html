NextEra Energy (NEE) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition· July 4, 2026
A teaching case. Nothing here is investment advice.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

Let me tell you what you are actually buying when you buy a share of NextEra, because it is really two companies stapled together, plus a tail.

Florida Power & Light (FPL) is the crown jewel: a rate-regulated electric monopoly serving more than six million customer accounts across Florida — the largest electric utility in the United States. Here is how a regulated utility makes money, and you must understand this mechanism cold, because it is the moat. The Florida Public Service Commission (FPSC) lets FPL build power plants, wires, and substations — that pile of used-and-useful investment is called the rate base — and then sets customer rates designed to let FPL recover its costs plus an allowed return on the equity portion of that rate base. Under the 2025 rate agreement (final order January 2026, running through at least December 2029), FPL's authorized regulatory ROE is 10.95%, with a band of 9.95%–11.95%, on a regulatory capital structure of 59.6% equity (10-K Item 1, part 1). In 2025 FPL actually earned about 11.70% (MD&A part 1) — near the top of its band. That is a machine that turns capital investment into legally-sanctioned profit: every dollar of prudent rate base earns roughly eleven cents of equity return, and Florida's growth keeps feeding the machine. FPL grew its average rate base by about $5.5 billion in 2025 and another $6.3 billion year-over-year in Q1 2026 (10-Q). FPL's 2025 net income: $5,012 million, up $469 million.

NextEra Energy Resources (NEER) is the second business, and a completely different animal: the largest developer of wind, solar, and battery storage in North America, operating in merchant (competitive) wholesale markets, plus some nuclear, gas, a regulated transmission book (~$3.2B rate base), and energy trading. NEER earns money three ways: selling power under long-term contracts, harvesting federal clean-energy tax credits (production tax credits and investment tax credits), and periodically recycling assets. In 2025 NEER added ~1,604 MW of wind, ~2,859 MW of solar, and ~1,799 MW of storage, and grew its backlog to 33 GW. NEER's 2025 net income (net of noncontrolling interests): $2,975 million, up $676 million — but note that its effective tax rate was negative 343%, because tax credits, not operating margin, are doing a lot of the earning.

The tail is a ~52.5% noncontrolling interest in XPLR Infrastructure (the former NextEra Energy Partners), which has been written down repeatedly — $0.7B impairment in 2025 alone. Treat it as roughly a wash.

Corporate & Other is where the holding-company interest expense and hedge noise land; it lost $1,152 million in 2025, mostly from mark-to-market swings on interest-rate derivatives.

Consolidated 2025: net income attributable to NEE $6,835 million; GAAP diluted EPS $3.30; adjusted EPS (management's measure, stripping hedge noise and XPLR marks) $3.71.

The circle-of-competence gate. Can we forecast this? FPL — yes, with unusual confidence: a monopoly, a transparent regulatory formula, a growing and affluent service territory, and a 100-of-100 an independent research service Earnings Predictability score. NEER — approximately. Its economics hinge on tax-credit policy, interest rates (renewables are pure capital-cost plays), and merchant power prices — all forecastable within a range, but a wider range than FPL. The manual (Ch. 4) says knowing the boundary is what matters. Ours: we can value FPL like a Swiss watch and NEER like a competent-but-cyclical developer, and we should discount NEER's cash flows harder for that reason.

THE ONE QUESTION for NextEra: Will the allowed return FPL earns on its ever-growing rate base — plus NEER's tax-advantaged renewables build — keep compounding above NextEra's cost of capital, in a world where that cost of capital (long rates) has stopped falling?

Principle: For a regulated utility, the moat and the earnings engine are the same object — the regulatory compact. You are not forecasting pricing power in a free market; you are forecasting a rate base × allowed ROE arithmetic, and then asking whether the regulator and interest rates will let it hold.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The balance sheet — where a utility lives. The manual is explicit: for a capital-intensive, leveraged-by-nature business, book value and the balance sheet carry more of the valuation. Here is NEE at December 31, 2025 (10-K Item 8, part 1):

  • Property, plant & equipment, net: $156,197 million. This is the business. It is the rate base at FPL and the wind/solar fleet at NEER.
  • Total assets: $212,721 million (up from $190,144M in 2024 — the XBRL 10-year series runs $90B in 2016 → $213B in 2025, more than doubling in nine years as the company built).
  • Debt: long-term $89,556M + current portion of LTD $3,500M + commercial paper $1,955M + other short-term $608M = $95,619 million total debt. an independent research service's tally (as of 3/31/26) is $104,403M total, $93,948M long-term — a touch higher a quarter later. Cash was $2,812M, so net debt ≈ $92,807 million.
  • Regulatory assets $6,072M (costs the FPSC has agreed FPL can recover later) and regulatory liabilities $11,830M (amounts owed back to customers, chiefly deferred taxes). These line items exist only because of rate regulation — they are the accounting fingerprint of the compact.
  • Common shareholders' equity: $54,608 million → book value per share ≈ $26.19 on 2,085M shares (matches an independent research service's $26.22). Noncontrolling interests add $11,871M (mostly tax-equity investors in NEER projects — this is their money, not ours).

Notice the shape: an enormous asset base, financed roughly 60% debt / 40% equity — leverage that would terrify us in a cyclical, but which a monopoly utility's bond-like cash stream can carry. That is the manual's special-case rule in the flesh.

The income statement — regulated vs. merchant, and a warning about "net income." Operating income was $8,280M in 2025. But look at the segment split and the tax line. FPL's $5,012M is clean, regulated, predictable. NEER's $2,975M leans on tax credits (clean-energy credits rose ~$585M in 2025) and is buffeted by hedge marks. Corporate lost $1,152M almost entirely to unrealized interest-rate derivative marks. This is why management reports adjusted earnings ($3.71 vs. $3.30 GAAP): the gap is mostly non-qualifying hedge mark-to-market and XPLR write-downs. I trust the adjustment here — it removes genuine non-cash volatility from economically-hedged positions — but I note it, because the manual (Ch. 7) tells us to treat every "adjusted" number as guilty until understood. NEE's is understood: it is real economic hedging showing up as accounting noise.

The cash flow statement — the tell of a growing utility. This is where a naive investor gets NextEra badly wrong. 2025 (MD&A part 2):

  • Operating cash flow: $12,485 million (the 10-yr series: $6.4B in 2016 → $12.5B in 2025).
  • Capital expenditures & investments: $24,606 million — nearly twice operating cash flow.
  • Dividends paid: $4,680 million.

So free cash flow (OCF − capex) is roughly negative $12 billion. A pure FCF screen would scream "avoid." But this is by design, and it is the single most important lesson of this case. Of that $24.6B, FPL spent ~$8.9B (much of it growth rate base earning 10.95%) and NEER ~$15.7B building new revenue-producing assets. The company funds the gap by issuing debt ($23.4B raised in 2025), selling tax credits, taking in tax-equity ("differential membership") investors ($3.3B), and issuing some equity ($2.0B). The three statements articulate like this: the income statement's growth is manufactured on the balance sheet with external capital, and the cash flow statement shows the bill. The question the manual forces (Ch. 11) is whether that externally-funded growth earns above the cost of the capital raising it. For FPL at 11% allowed ROE vs. a ~6–7% after-tax cost of capital, the answer is a clear yes. For NEER, it is yes as long as tax credits and contract prices hold.

Earnings-quality scan. (1) Adjusted vs. GAAP — understood, above. (2) Mark-to-market on NEER/Corporate hedges — flagged, non-cash, reversing. (3) Tax credits and HLBV accounting for tax-equity partners — real cash, but it makes NEER's headline "net income" a poor proxy for owner cash; much of NEER's value is the tax shield, not operating margin. (4) Reserve amortization / the new Rate Stabilization Mechanism — FPL can pull a lever (up to ~$1.5B after-tax) to smooth earnings to hit its targeted ROE within the band. That is regulator-sanctioned smoothing, disclosed, not a red flag — but it means FPL's reported ROE is engineered to the target, so don't over-read a "beat." Verdict: earnings quality is good, with the honest caveat that NextEra is a tax-and-regulation-driven earner, not a free-market margin story.

Principle: Read the three statements as one system. For a growing utility, negative free cash flow is not weakness — it is the sound of rate base being built. What you must verify is that the return on that new rate base exceeds the cost of the debt and equity funding it. Book value here is a floor with meaning, because the regulator lets you earn a return on book.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The long record (XBRL + an independent research service). Diluted EPS (GAAP, split-adjusted) walked from about $1.19 in 2010 to $3.30 in 2025; adjusted EPS to $3.71. An independent research service's per-share history shows earnings compounding ~9.5% over 10 years and ~11.5% over 5. Dividends are the cleaner signal of durable earning power: $0.50 (2010) → $2.27 (2025), an ~11% annualized climb, raised every single year — and management guides ~10% dividend growth near-term. Book value per share grew ~8%/yr. This is a genuine, decades-long compounding record, and an independent research service rates Earnings Predictability a perfect 100.

Owner earnings — and the growth-vs-maintenance capex judgment. The manual's formula is reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. The whole game for NextEra is splitting that $24.6B of capex into maintenance (keep the existing system running) and growth (build new rate base / new projects).

  • Depreciation is a fair proxy for maintenance capex on the existing base. FPL's D&A was ~$3.8B in 2025; consolidated depreciation runs on the order of $6–7B.
  • If maintenance capex ≈ depreciation ≈ ~$6.5–7B, then the other ~$17–18B of the $24.6B is growth capex — discretionary, value-creating expansion, not a cost of standing still.
  • So owner earnings ≈ adjusted net income, roughly $7.7B (an independent research service's 2025 net profit line), or about $3.70 per share — the cash the owner could take if NextEra stopped growing. It chooses not to, because reinvesting at an 11% regulated ROE beats paying it out.

This is the crux the manual demands we make explicit: NextEra's deeply negative free cash flow is growth capex, not a leak. Strip the growth, and the business throws off ~$3.70/share of owner earnings on a bond-steady base.

ROIC / allowed ROE vs. cost of capital. FPL's allowed ROE is 10.95% and it earns ~11.7%; NEER earns high-single/low-double digits after tax credits. Consolidated return on equity is ~14% (an independent research service). Against a cost of equity we'll anchor near 9% (justified below), the spread is positive — growth creates value (manual Ch. 11: value is created only when ROIC > r). This is the mathematical license to like the reinvestment.

The moat. Four sources, in the manual's taxonomy:
1. Regulatory monopoly (FPL). No competitor may string a second set of wires to those six million homes. This is the scale-plus-regulatory-barrier moat in its purest form. The catch, per the manual's franchise test, is that FPL's returns are price-regulated — so the moat is not unlimited pricing power; it is a capped, protected, durable return. That cap is why a utility deserves a lower multiple than See's Candy, and why its value leans on book/rate base.
2. Cost advantage (FPL). FPL runs one of the lowest-cost, most efficient large utilities in the country, which keeps the regulator friendly and rates politically defensible — a virtuous circle.
3. Scale in renewables (NEER). Largest wind/solar developer in North America → supply-chain, financing, and tax-equity advantages competitors can't match. Weaker and more contestable than FPL's moat.
4. Regulatory relationships and a premium service area. Florida's demography (in-migration, no state income tax, load growth from data centers) is a structural tailwind — FPL added ~100,000 customers in Q1 2026 alone.

10-year erosion risks (the manual insists we argue durability, not assume it): (a) rate cases — a hostile future FPSC could cut the allowed ROE; the 2025 agreement locks 10.95% only through 2029; (b) interest rates — the dominant risk; higher-for-longer rates raise NEER's project costs and compress the premium the market pays for a bond-proxy; (c) subsidy/tax-credit policy — the OBBBA (2025) already modified clean-energy credits; NEE believes its pipeline through 2030 still qualifies, but this is a policy-dependent earnings stream; (d) storm risk — Florida hurricanes (Debby, Helene, Milton in 2024 drove ~$1.2B of restoration recovered via surcharge) — recoverable, but a recurring hit to cash timing.

Principle: A utility's moat is a protected but capped return on a book-value base. That makes book value and rate base the anchor of value, and makes the durability question a regulatory and interest-rate question, not a competitive-pricing one.

FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

The manual is emphatic: judge a utility on utility yardsticks, not industrial leverage rules. A steady regulated stream safely carries debt that would sink a cyclical. Let's do both the raw arithmetic and the utility interpretation.

The raw ratios (and why they look scary out of context):
- Net debt / EBITDA. Consolidated EBITDA ≈ operating income $8,280M + depreciation ~$6.5B ≈ ~$14.8B. Net debt ~$92.8B. That's ~6.3× EBITDA — a number that would be a screaming red flag for a manufacturer.
- Interest coverage (EBIT / interest). an independent research service pegs total interest coverage at 2.3× (LT interest ~$4.3B). Thin, by industrial standards.

The utility interpretation (why it's actually fine):
- Credit ratings (10-K, MD&A part 2): NEE corporate Baa1 / A− / A−; FPL A1 / A / A with first-mortgage bonds up at Aa2 / A+ / AA−; all outlooks stable. An independent research service grades Financial Strength A+. Rating agencies — who price default risk for a living — call this a strong investment-grade balance sheet.
- FFO/debt, the yardstick that actually governs a utility's rating, sits in the mid-teens for NEE (funds from operations ~$12–13B against ~$90B+ net debt), consistent with its ratings. That is the metric the manual's "quality of the liabilities" logic points to.
- FPL first-mortgage-bond coverage: the mortgage's own earnings test showed ~7.5× annual interest coverage for 2025 (10-K MD&A part 2) — a fortress at the regulated-subsidiary level, which is where the safest cash flows and the senior claims live.
- Liquidity: ~$18.7B net available liquidity; ~75 banks, none over 5% of facilities; no rating-downgrade debt-acceleration triggers.
- Refinancing wall: yes, it is enormous — NEE issued $23.4B and retired $10.3B of long-term debt in 2025, and it must roll debt continuously. It funded in 2025's high-rate market without strain, but this is the standing vulnerability: NextEra depends on open capital markets by design. The manual (Ch. 8) flags exactly this — "a business that depends on the continued kindness of lenders does not control its own fate." For an A-rated monopoly utility that dependence is normally benign; in a frozen credit market it would bite.
- Hidden obligations: pension is actually overfunded (assets $5,564M vs. obligation $2,697M — a plus); asset-retirement/nuclear-decommissioning obligations $3,669M (funded by $10,954M of special-use decommissioning funds — covered); ~$7.1B of guarantees and NEER project/tax-equity structures — real but disclosed and ring-fenced.

One-line verdict: Adequate-to-strong for a utility — a fortress at FPL, merely solid at the leveraged holding-company/NEER level. The leverage is normal for the trade and well-rated; the honest caveat is total dependence on continuous access to debt markets, which is precisely why interest-rate direction is the whole ballgame.

Principle: For a leveraged-by-nature business, the rating agencies' verdict and FFO/debt tell you more than net-debt/EBITDA. But never let "it's a utility" excuse the refinancing dependency — size the risk, don't wave it away.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

Capital allocation. Management's core act is deploying ~$24.6B/year of capital. The one-dollar test (manual Ch. 9): does each retained/raised dollar create at least a dollar of value? At FPL — reinvesting at an ~11% regulated ROE against a ~6–7% after-tax cost of capital — decisively yes. At NEER — reinvesting at tax-credit-boosted mid-teens returns — yes, conditional on policy. This is a management team whose single best use of cash is to reinvest, and it does, aggressively.

Dividends. Raised every year for decades, ~11% CAGR over 10 years, guided ~10% forward, on a ~60–61% payout of GAAP earnings. That is a shareholder-friendly, disciplined record — and the manual's point that dividends are appropriate when reinvestment can't absorb all the cash is respected here: NextEra pays out only ~60% because it can profitably redeploy the rest.

Equity issuance — the dilution question. Share count crept from 2,052M (2023) to 2,085M (Q1 2026), and NEE established a $4B at-the-market equity program on 12/31/2025. The manual (Ch. 9) is stern: issuing shares is capital allocation, and streaming out undervalued stock gives away every owner's claim. NextEra issues equity to help fund growth — modestly (a few percent of shares over years), and to build rate base earning above cost. This is value-accretive dilution if the reinvestment return holds — the Dexter Shoe warning cuts the other way only if they issue cheap stock to fund sub-cost-of-capital projects. So far the math favors them, but it is the line to watch: growth funded by external equity is only good while ROIC > r.

Candor. Management reports GAAP alongside adjusted, discloses the hedge and XPLR write-downs plainly, and lays out the rate-agreement mechanics in detail. The XPLR/NextEra Energy Partners saga — repeated impairments — is a genuine ding, but it is disclosed, not buried.

Principle: The best capital allocator is a business that can reinvest its own cash above its cost of capital — and prove it. NextEra can, at FPL especially. Judge the equity issuance not as dilution per se, but by whether the dollars raised earn above their cost. Today they do.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

The manual tells us to anchor on normalized earning power and book/rate base, and to choose the methods the business deserves. For a regulated-utility-plus-developer, that means three methods, reconciled, then triangulated with the three universal cross-checks — with special attention to interest-rate sensitivity, because a utility is a bond proxy.

Normalized owner earnings per share ≈ $4.00 — I use the 2026 forward adjusted EPS (an independent research service and management's $3.92–$4.02 guide), which cleans out 2025's hedge noise and reflects a full year of the new rate agreement. This is the input all three methods lean on; it was validated in the earnings-quality section, before any arithmetic.

The discount rate r. This is gravity, and it has changed. In June 2026 the Fed held at 3.50–3.75% and removed its easing bias; the 30-year Treasury yields 4.86%, the 10-year 4.38%, and inflation reignited (core PCE 3.4%). For a bond-proxy utility, r must reflect this. I set r = 9% as the base (long Treasury ~4.9% + an equity-risk/uncertainty premium of ~4%), and I will show the sensitivity, because utility value is hypersensitive to it.

Method A — Normalized / growth-justified earnings multiple

An independent research service's 10-year median P/E for NEE is 24×; the live multiple is 22.4×. Peers trade lower: Duke (DUK) ~15–17× (S&O shows a ~$128 price, Portfolio IV), Consolidated Edison (ED) ~15×, American Electric Power (AEP), Southern (SO), Dominion (D) cluster 14–18×. NextEra earns a premium to peers for FPL's superior service territory and NEER's growth — but a smaller premium than in the zero-rate era, because higher rates compress what any bond-proxy can command.

  • At a growth-justified 20–22× forward EPS of $4.00 → $80–$88.
  • At the historical median 24× (top of the range, hard to defend at today's rates) → $96.

I center Method A at ~$85, with the honest note that NEE's own median multiple was set in a lower-rate world.

Method B — Dividend-discount cross-check (the right tool for a 30-year dividend-grower)

Gordon growth: IV = D₁ / (r − g). D₁ = 2026 declared dividend ≈ $2.49 (an independent research service). Justify g against the record: dividends grew ~11% over 10 years but management guides ~8% forward and ~6–8% long-term for earnings; a perpetual g must sit below the economy's nominal growth, so I cap terminal dividend growth around 6.0–6.5%.

The base cell (r = 9%, g = 6.5%) gives ~$100; the more conservative (r = 9%, g = 6.0%) gives ~$83. DDM base ≈ $85–$95. Notice the violent sensitivity — a half-point of r or g swings value $15–$25. That is the utility-as-bond-proxy lesson made numerical.

Method C — Sum-of-the-parts (the book/rate-base anchor)

  • FPL. FPL earned ~11.7% on retail rate base to produce $5,012M of net income, implying regulated equity of ~$42.8B (and, at a 59.6% equity ratio, a rate base on the order of ~$72B). Premier regulated utilities trade at 2.0–2.3× regulated equity:
  • 2.0× → $85.7B → $41/share
  • 2.3× → $98.5B → $47/share
  • NEER. $2,975M net income, capitalized conservatively at 12–14× (a growth developer, but capital-hungry and tax-credit-dependent, so not a premium multiple):
  • 12× → $35.7B → $17/share
  • 14× → $41.6B → $20/share
  • XPLR stake + Corporate/holdco drag: roughly a wash to a modest negative; the holdco interest burden and impaired XPLR stake offset any residual.

SOTP ≈ $41–$47 (FPL) + $17–$20 (NEER) ≈ $58–$67/share. This is the conservative anchor — it deliberately caps NEER low and gives no credit for future growth beyond today's earnings. It sits below the earnings-power methods precisely because it is a near-static, book-anchored view. It sets the low end of my range.

The three universal cross-checks (arithmetic)

  1. No-growth floor = OE ÷ r. At OE = $4.00, r = 9%: $44.4. At r = 8.5%: $47.1. So roughly half of the $88 base value is a payment for growth — appropriate for a business whose entire thesis is rate-base and renewables expansion, but a reminder that if growth stalls (a bad rate case, a tax-credit repeal), a large chunk of the price is exposed.
  2. Implied multiple = value ÷ OE. $88 ÷ $4.00 = 22× owner earnings. Is 22× defensible for a monopoly-plus-developer growing ~8% at an above-cost return? At today's rates it is full but not absurd — it matches the live multiple and sits below the 24× historical median. If the machine had said 30×, I'd hunt for a runaway terminal value. It didn't; the translation holds.
  3. Owner-earnings / earnings yield vs. the long bond — the central check for a bond proxy. At $88.34, the 2026 earnings yield is $4.00 ÷ $88.34 = 4.5%, and the dividend yield is 2.8%, against a 30-year Treasury at 4.86%. The stock's earnings yield is below the long bond, and its dividend yield is far below it — you are paid less current yield than a Treasury, and you accept that only for the ~8% growth on top. In the zero-rate era a 3% utility yield beat a 2% bond and the stock soared; today the bond out-yields the utility. This single comparison is why the stock is no longer cheap — gravity got heavier.

Sensitivity — because utility value is hypersensitive to rates

The DDM and DCF tables above are the sensitivity: value swings from ~$71 to ~$100+ as r moves 8.5%→9.5% and g moves 6.0%→6.5%. Triangulated low $72 / base $88 / high $104. Sanity checks: An independent research service's 18-month target midpoint is $104 (my high), its low target $77 (near my low), and its 2029–31 target range $105–$140 — all consistent with my range as future values discounted back. My base ($88) essentially equals today's price, which is the whole point.

Principle: Value a bond proxy against the bond. When the long Treasury out-yields the stock's earnings and dividend yield, the entire investment case rests on growth arriving exactly as forecast — the margin of safety has been spent on optimism about rates and reinvestment.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Live price: $88.34 (equity_atlas, July 4, 2026). My base IV: $88.

Margin of Safety = (IV − Price) / IV = (88 − 88.34) / 88 ≈ −0.4%.

There is no margin of safety at today's price — the stock trades at my central estimate. Against my high case ($104) there's ~15% upside; against my low/SOTP case ($72 / high-$50s–$60s) there's meaningful downside. The manual's cornerstone (Ch. 13) is unambiguous: "If we calculate the value of a common stock to be only slightly higher than its price, we're not interested in buying." NextEra fails that test right now.

Yield check. Today's yield is 2.9%. an independent research service's own history shows NEE's yield ranged from ~1.9% (2021, the euphoric top) to ~4.0% (2010–14); the utility-group median is 3.1%. At 2.9%, NEE yields below its own historical middle and below its peer group — you are not being paid a fat yield to wait. In 2023, when the stock touched the high-$40s/low-$50s (split-adjusted), the yield pushed toward 4% and that was the moment of margin of safety. We are not there.

Where safety sits. The manual (Ch. 13) teaches that in a quality business part of the safety lives in the growing earnings stream itself — today's estimate becomes tomorrow's floor. That is real here: FPL's rate base compounds, so a patient holder's IV rises over time. But that is protection for an existing owner at a good cost, not a license to pay full price today.

Mr. Market's mood. He is not depressed about NextEra — the stock is up ~19% in 2026 (an independent research service). Utilities rallied as a defensive trade amid recession and Middle-East fears, and NEE rode it. Mr. Market is offering a fair-to-full price for a fine business. The manual says his pocketbook is useful only when his mood is foolish; right now it isn't. An independent research service, independently, reaches the same conclusion: NEE is a utility they "particularly like, but [it] doesn't meet our 11% [total-return] threshold for entry at this time… enter on a pullback."

Principle: A wonderful business at a fair price is admirable; a wonderful business at full price is a watchlist entry, not a purchase. The discount, not the quality alone, makes the investment.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Business quality: GOOD — a wonderful regulated core (FPL) bolted to a good-but-policy-dependent developer (NEER), leveraged by nature and rate-sensitive by construction. FPL alone is close to wonderful; NEER and the holdco leverage pull the blended grade to "good."

The call: a fine business at a full price — ADMIRE AND WAIT. No margin of safety at $88.34. Fairly valued to slightly rich against a $72–$104 range whose base is $88.

Answering THE ONE QUESTION — will the return on FPL's growing rate base plus NEER's tax-advantaged build keep compounding above the cost of capital, now that long rates have stopped falling? — the answer is probably yes on the business, but the market has already priced the "yes." The compounding is intact; the discount is gone. Higher-for-longer rates simultaneously (a) raise NEER's project costs and refinancing bill and (b) shrink the premium a bond proxy can command — a double squeeze that caps the upside from here.

Two or three open questions I'd want answered before buying:
1. What does the next FPL rate case (post-2029) do to the 10.95% allowed ROE if political pressure on utility bills intensifies?
2. How durable are the clean-energy tax credits underpinning NEER's earnings after further OBBBA/Treasury rulemaking — and what is NEER worth without them?

GLOSSARY (definition · formula · NextEra's numbers)

Intrinsic value — the discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. NEE: base $88/share (range $72–$104).

Owner earnings — cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔWC. NEE: ~$4.00/share normalized, because ~$17–18B of the $24.6B capex is growth, not maintenance (maintenance ≈ depreciation ≈ ~$6.5–7B).

Rate base — the pile of prudent, used-and-useful utility investment on which a regulator lets the utility earn a return. FPL: rate base on the order of ~$72B, growing ~$5.5B/yr (~$6.3B YoY in Q1 2026).

Allowed (regulatory) ROE — the return on the equity portion of rate base the regulator authorizes. FPL: 10.95% (band 9.95%–11.95%) on a 59.6% equity ratio; earned ~11.7% in 2025.

ROIC — return on invested capital; the quality gauge. NOPAT ÷ invested capital. Compare to r. NEE: FPL earns ~11.7% regulated ROE, consolidated ROE ~14%, vs. r ~9% → value-creating.

Discount rate (r) — owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long rates plus an uncertainty premium; gravity on all asset values. NEE base r = 9% (30-yr Treasury 4.86% + ~4% premium). Value is hypersensitive to it.

No-growth (earning-power) value — what it's worth if it never grows again; the floor. OE ÷ r. NEE: $4.00 ÷ 0.09 = $44.4 — about half the base value, so half the price is a bet on growth.

Dividend-discount model (DDM / Gordon growth) — values a steady dividend-grower. IV = D₁/(r − g). NEE: D₁ = $2.49; at r = 9%, g = 6.0–6.5% → $83–$100. 30-year dividend-growth record justifies the method.

Sum-of-the-parts (SOTP) — value each business on its own terms and add. NEE: FPL (2.0–2.3× regulated equity) $41–$47 + NEER (12–14× earnings) $17–$20 ≈ $58–$67/share — the conservative, book-anchored floor.

FFO/debt — funds from operations ÷ total debt; the yardstick rating agencies use for utilities. NEE: mid-teens, consistent with A−/Baa1 ratings.

Interest coverage — EBIT ÷ interest expense. NEE consolidated ~2.3× (thin industrially, normal for a utility); FPL first-mortgage-bond coverage ~7.5× (fortress at the regulated level).

Net debt / EBITDA — (total debt − cash) ÷ EBITDA. NEE: ~$92.8B ÷ ~$14.8B ≈ 6.3× — alarming for a cyclical, ordinary for a rate-regulated monopoly with bond-like cash flows.

Book value per share — common equity ÷ shares. NEE: $54,608M ÷ 2,085M ≈ $26.19. Matters more here than for an industrial, because the regulator lets FPL earn a return on book.

Margin of safety — discount of price to conservative IV; the cornerstone. (IV − Price)/IV. NEE: (88 − 88.34)/88 ≈ −0.4% → none today.

Mr. Market — the manic-depressive quoting partner. Today he is sanguine on NextEra (stock +19% in 2026), offering a fair-to-full price — useful only when foolish, and he isn't.

Non-qualifying hedge / mark-to-market — economic hedges that don't qualify for hedge accounting, so their fair-value swings hit GAAP income as non-cash noise. Drove the $3.71 adjusted vs. $3.30 GAAP EPS gap in 2025.

Tax-equity / differential membership interests — outside investors who fund NEER renewables projects in exchange for the tax credits; shown as noncontrolling interests ($11,871M) and HLBV accounting. Real cash in, but it makes NEER's headline net income a poor proxy for owner cash.
