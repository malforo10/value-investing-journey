Zoetis Inc. (ZTS) — A Value-Investing Seminar

In the Graham–Buffett tradition· Analysis date: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $74.80

A note before we begin. The two of us are going to work one company, out loud, in the order the manual prescribes: circle of competence, then moat, then returns on capital, then the cash an owner can actually take home, then the balance sheet, then the people running it — and only then the price. We are teaching a method you can repeat on any business, not delivering a verdict to memorize. Every number below is arithmetic you can check. When we reach a principle, we will stop and name it, because the principles are the part you keep.

The stock in front of us is unusual. A little over three months ago an independent research service printed Zoetis at $118.15. Today Mr. Market will sell it to us for $74.80 — down about 37%. This is a business earning 50% on equity, rated Financial Strength A, that has compounded earnings per share at 14% a year for a decade. Half of it, roughly, has been marked off. Our whole job today is to decide whether the castle's moat is draining or whether Mr. Market is simply having one of his bad days. Let us find out.

2. OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

How Zoetis makes money. Zoetis is the largest pure-play animal-health company in the world — for nearly 75 years the animal-health arm of Pfizer, spun out in 2013. It discovers, makes, and sells medicines, vaccines, and diagnostics across eight species, organized two ways that matter for our analysis:

  • By customer type: Companion animal (dogs, cats, horses) is ~70% of the $9,467M in 2025 revenue; livestock (cattle, swine, poultry, fish, sheep) is ~29%; contract manufacturing and human-health diagnostics the last ~1%.
  • By geography (the reporting segments): United States $5,097M (54%) and International $4,254M (45%).

The companion-animal side is the crown jewel — pet owners pay cash, out of pocket, and (as the 10-K puts it) reduce spending on entertainment, clothing, and household goods before they cut the dog's medicine. The livestock side is steadier than commodity inputs because the cost of a vaccine is small next to feed and improves the producer's economics. Revenue is concentrated in a handful of branded franchises: Simparica/Simparica Trio (parasiticides, ~16% of revenue), Apoquel/Apoquel Chewable (dermatology, ~12%), then Cytopoint (dermatology), Librela (osteoarthritis-pain monoclonal antibody), and the ceftiofur line — the top five are ~42% of revenue, the top ten ~57%.

The ONE question. From $118 to $74.80 in a quarter, from a 30× historical median multiple to roughly 12× today. What did the market learn? Read the filings and it is not a mystery: growth has decelerated. Q1 2026 revenue was $2,262M, up just 2.9% over the prior year. In the U.S. 10-K results, companion-animal growth was "partially offset by lower sales of Librela" — the very osteoarthritis-pain franchise the bulls were counting on. Softening U.S. vet-visit trends and "increased competitive pressures on certain products" are named as near-term headwinds. And the industry itself is out of favor (Drug Industry Timeliness 61 of 94), with the market's imagination consumed by human GLP-1 weight-loss drugs — raising the speculative question of whether an obesity-for-pets or a next-generation competitor could someday assault a key franchise.

So the question that organizes everything below: Is the moat actually cracking — a durable franchise in permanent decline — or is this Mr. Market panicking over a slowdown from spectacular growth to merely good growth? We will not answer it by intuition. We will answer it with the pipeline.

CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE gate. Can we forecast this business? The manual's first filter is not price but predictability (Ch. 4). Animal health is, for a patient analyst, a forecastable business: recurring purchases of prescription-and-vaccine products, cash-pay customers, no third-party reimbursement bureaucracy, product lives measured in decades, and demand that has historically been more stable than the broad economy. The unknowables — will a specific mAb hold share against a specific new entrant? — are real but bounded; they touch individual products, not the existence of the franchise. This clears the gate. What would put it outside the circle is if the entire category faced a technological cliff we could not size; we will test that skepticism in Section 4. This is inside our circle — we proceed, but we forecast conservatively.

PRINCIPLE. Price tells you what someone will pay today; it is not evidence about value (Ch. 2). A 37% drawdown is a fact about Mr. Market's mood, not yet a fact about the business. Before we let a falling quote frighten or tempt us, we must value the business independently — and to do that, we first confirm we can forecast it at all.

3. READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The three statements are one story told three ways. We read them as evidence, compute the ratios as arithmetic, and check that they articulate — that the income statement's profits show up as cash and reconcile to what the balance sheet says.

The income statement (FY2025, $millions). Revenue 9,467; cost of sales 2,666 → gross profit 6,801, a 71.8% gross margin. Subtract SG&A 2,378, R&D 698, intangible amortization 128, and restructuring 51, and operating income is about 3,546 — a 37.5% operating margin (an independent research service, using its adjusted convention, shows ~45%; the gap is amortization and restructuring, which we keep in). Below the line: interest expense 222, other income (36), pre-tax income 3,360, tax 687 (20.5% rate), net income $2,673M. Diluted EPS $6.02 GAAP; an independent research service's adjusted figure is $6.41.

The cash-flow statement — the honesty check. Net income 2,673 converts to operating cash flow of $2,904M. Depreciation and amortization add back 487; share-based compensation 83; and there was a working-capital build (receivables −236, inventories −199) as the business grew. Capital expenditures were $621M. This is the articulation test the manual demands: profits are showing up as cash. OCF ($2,904M) modestly exceeds net income ($2,673M) — a drug company's signature: real earnings, light on physical assets.

Ratios as arithmetic.
- Gross margin 6,801 ÷ 9,467 = 71.8%.
- Operating margin 3,546 ÷ 9,467 = 37.5%.
- Net margin 2,673 ÷ 9,467 = 28.2%.
- Cash conversion: OCF ÷ net income = 2,904 ÷ 2,673 = 1.09×.

Earnings-quality scan (Ch. 7 red-flag list). We look for the tricks and find few. No EBITDA-as-cash-flow theatrics in the financials. Restructuring is small ($51M, ~1.5% of operating income) and disclosed. Share-based compensation is $83M — under 1% of revenue, genuinely modest; Zoetis expenses it, and it is a real cost we leave in. Revenue recognition carries the usual rebate/return estimates, plainly described. One item to flag honestly: in 2026 Zoetis is eliminating a one-month reporting lag for foreign subsidiaries, which pulled ~2.5–3.5% of extra sales into reported Q4 2025 — a timing artifact management explicitly says "we do not expect to recur." A careful analyst removes that tailwind from the run-rate; we do. The accounts are, on balance, clean and candidly presented.

PRINCIPLE. Reported earnings are an accounting construct; the cash-flow statement is where you find out whether they are real (Ch. 7). Here they are — OCF exceeds net income, SBC is small, one-time items are minor and disclosed. That earns us the right to trust the earnings we are about to capitalize. Note the timing tailwind and strip it; the margin of safety protects against honest error, not against an input we knew was flattered.

4. EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

The XBRL trend — what earning power has actually done. Here is the ten-year record, pulled from the filings:

Year Revenue ($M) Net income ($M) Op. cash flow ($M) Diluted shares (M)
2019 6,260 1,500* 1,795 ~487
2020 6,675 1,638 2,126 ~475
2021 7,776 2,037 2,213 ~473
2022 8,080 2,114 1,912 ~466
2023 8,544 2,344 2,353 462
2024 9,256 2,486 2,953 455
2025 9,467 2,673 2,904 444

(2019 net income shown as reported operating level; the XBRL "attributable" figure of $384M that year reflects one-time items.)

Read it plainly: revenue up ~51% over six years, net income up ~63%, share count down from ~487M to 444M. Earnings power grew and the pie was cut into fewer slices. But — and this is the news the market is reacting to — look at the last leg: revenue growth slowed from +8.7% (2024) to +2.2% (2025) to +2.9% (Q1'26). The compounding machine has downshifted. Not stalled. Downshifted.

Owner earnings (Ch. 7) — the cash we could actually withdraw. Reported earnings are the starting point, not the answer:

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital

Start with net income $2,673M. Add back non-cash D&A $487M. Now the hard term — maintenance vs. growth capex. Total capex was $621M, but PP&E depreciation was only $330M. A drug company is not capital-hungry; the gap between $621M spent and $330M consumed is largely growth capex — new capacity, not treadmill spending. Maintenance capex is closer to the $330–350M depreciation level; we use $350M to be conservative. Working capital consumed roughly $600M in 2025 as the business grew and pulled sales forward — an unusually heavy year; a normal growing-year build is closer to $150M, and we use that.

Owner earnings ≈ 2,673 + 487 − 350 − 150 = $2,660M, or ≈$6.26 per share on 424.9M shares.

Cross-check with the crude free-cash-flow number: OCF $2,904M − total capex $621M = $2,283M ($5.37/sh). Owner earnings sit sensibly between crude FCF (which over-charges by counting growth capex) and net income. Call normalized owner earnings ~$2,600M / ~$6.15–6.25 per share. This is a high-conversion, low-capex earnings stream — exactly the capital-light profile the manual prizes (Ch. 6).

The moat — and honest skepticism about it. Animal health has a genuinely unusual set of structural advantages, and we should name each rather than assert "moat" and move on:

  1. No third-party reimbursement. Owners pay cash. There is no insurer or government formulary squeezing price — the single biggest difference from human pharma, and the reason pricing power is real.
  2. No generic cliff like human pharma. The 10-K makes the key point: "there is no large, well-capitalized company focused on generic animal health products." Markets per product are small, distribution and veterinarian education matter, and — critically — "significant brand loyalty to products often continues after the loss of patent-based and regulatory exclusivity." Generics erode share slowly, not catastrophically.
  3. Fragmented, relationship-based prescribers reached by Zoetis's own direct sales force — a distribution moat a new entrant must rebuild from scratch.
  4. R&D and regulatory barriers, plus a portfolio of ~300 product lines and lifecycle innovation (Simparica → Simparica Trio; Apoquel tablet → chewable) that keeps refreshing exclusivity.

Now the skepticism the manual demands ("durability must be argued, not assumed" — Ch. 5). The bear case is specific and we take it seriously:

  • Librela (OA pain mAb) is softening in the U.S. — the 10-K says so outright. If a flagship new franchise is losing momentum against competition or safety perception, that is a real dent.
  • Generics do bite the older livestock lines. The cautionary tale is in the filings: Draxxin lost 66% of U.S. sales since its 2021 first generic entry. Excede/Naxcel and Cerenia formulations are rolling off patent 2025–2028. This is why livestock (and some legacy pharma) is the lower-quality half of the business.
  • The GLP-1/obesity-for-pets question is a genuine unknown-unknown. We cannot size it, and we refuse to pretend we can.

But weigh it correctly. The dermatology and parasiticide franchises (Apoquel, Cytopoint, Simparica) — the ~28% of revenue that is the core — show no such crack; internationally Librela and Solensia are still growing. Draxxin at 66% decline is one product in the 29% livestock bucket, not the companion-animal engine. The moat is not cracking across the franchise; one new product decelerated, some old products face slow generic erosion, and growth normalized. That is a business maturing, not a castle falling.

ROIC — the arithmetic of quality (Ch. 6).

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital

NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax) = 3,546 × (1 − 0.205) = $2,819M. Invested capital = debt + equity − cash = 9,150 + 3,331 − 2,312 = $10,169M. So:

ROIC = 2,819 ÷ 10,169 = 27.7%

Against our discount rate r = 9%, ROIC of ~28% is roughly three times the cost of capital. This is the signature of a real franchise (manual: "the single best measure of business quality"). When ROIC so exceeds r, growth creates value and retention is justified (Ch. 11) — a point we bank for Section 6.

ROE — with the leverage caveat. an independent research service shows ROE of 50.5%; on 2025's numbers, net income $2,673M ÷ equity $3,331M = 80.2%. Before we cheer, the caveat the manual insists on (Ch. 8): that gaudy number is manufactured partly by shrinking the denominator. Equity fell from $4,997M (2023) to $3,331M (2025) because Zoetis bought back $3.2B of stock in 2025 and the convertible-note mechanics reduced paid-in capital. Buybacks lower book equity, which inflates ROE. The ROIC of 28% — which counts all the capital, debt included — is the cleaner, un-flattered read on business quality, and it is excellent on its own.

PRINCIPLE. The width of the moat governs the two inputs that matter most — how high returns are and how long they last (Ch. 5–6). ROIC of ~28% versus a 9% cost of capital is not an accounting artifact; it is a business that earns far more than the capital it uses, protected by cash-pay customers, a direct sales force, and brand loyalty that outlives patents. A decelerating growth rate does not repeal that. Skepticism is a tool, not a verdict: we named the real threats (Librela, livestock generics, GLP-1) and found them bounded, not existential.

5. FINANCIAL STRENGTH & SOLVENCY

Can the business survive a bad year and a shut credit market without becoming a forced seller (Ch. 8)? Five questions, each answered with Zoetis's own numbers.

1. Leverage. Total debt is $9,150M (principal); cash $2,312M; so net debt ≈ $6,838M. Against owner earnings of ~$2,660M:

Net debt ÷ owner earnings = 6,838 ÷ 2,660 = 2.6×

Against operating cash flow, 6,838 ÷ 2,904 = 2.4×. A conservative business could clear its net debt from roughly two-and-a-half years of its own cash. For a stable, cash-pay, recession-resilient franchise, that is comfortably prudent — well inside the credit facility's own 3.5× net-debt/EBITDA covenant.

One honest note: gross debt jumped in 2025 (from $6.65B to $9.15B) because Zoetis issued $2.0B of 0.250% convertible notes in December and $1.85B of senior notes, using the proceeds largely to buy back stock. This is management deliberately swapping cheap debt for equity at what it evidently considered a low share price — aggressive, but the net leverage stayed manageable at 2.6×.

2. Coverage.

Interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest expense = 3,546 ÷ 222 = 16.0×

A normal year's operating income covers the interest bill sixteen times over. It would take a catastrophic, sustained collapse in earnings — not a routine downturn — to threaten the interest payment. This is a wide cushion.

3. Liquidity. Current assets $6,768M vs. current liabilities $2,235M — a current ratio of 3.0×. Cash $2,312M. There is no near-term squeeze; Graham's precondition of ample working capital is met with room to spare.

4. Refinancing and maturities. The debt is well-laddered across senior notes maturing 2027, 2028, 2030, 2032, 2035, 2043, 2047, 2048, 2050 — plus the $2.0B convertible due 2029. No wall of maturities forces a refinancing into a shut market. Zoetis also has an untapped $1.25B revolving facility (to 2030) and a $1.0B commercial-paper program with nothing outstanding. It does not depend on the continued kindness of lenders.

5. Hidden / off-balance-sheet obligations. We looked. Operating-lease rentals are trivial (~$60M/yr). Pension is essentially fully funded (assets $103M vs. obligation $145M — a $42M gap, immaterial). Purchase commitments total $427M, disclosed and ordinary. The convertible note deserves attention: it converts at $148.20 per share — nearly double today's $74.80. It is deeply out-of-the-money and will not dilute owners at anything near the current price; management further bought capped calls (cap $211.72) to blunt dilution even if the stock doubles. No time bombs.

Verdict: Financial Strength A is earned — a fortress balance sheet with modest net leverage (2.6×), 16× interest coverage, 3× liquidity, laddered maturities, and no hidden obligations.

PRINCIPLE. The balance sheet is the foundation the earnings rest on (Ch. 8): it is the denominator of ROIC, the guarantee we are never the forced seller of Chapter 3, and the first thing to verify before trusting any earnings stream to persist. Zoetis passes — which means the downshift in growth is a business question, not a survival question. That distinction is everything.

6. MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

We judge management on the six uses of a dollar (Ch. 9) and on candor.

The six uses, and Zoetis's record.
1. Reinvest in the business — at ~28% incremental ROIC, this is the best use, and Zoetis does it: R&D $698M (7.4% of revenue), lifecycle innovation, growth capex.
2. Acquire — Zoetis has been disciplined and small here (Basepaws, PetMedix, adivo, a UK diagnostics lab) — bolt-ons, not empire-building. No Dexter-scale blunder in evidence.
3. Pay down debt — used sparingly; it has instead added leverage to fund buybacks.
4. Dividends — grown aggressively: from $0.38/share (2016) to $2.00 (2025), a ~19.5% ten-year growth rate. Payout is still modest at ~31% of earnings.
5. Repurchase shares — the big one, discussed below.
6. Issue / refuse to issue shares — the convertible is structured (with capped calls, $148.20 strike) specifically to avoid dilution. Management respects the share count.

Share-count trend (the discipline that matters). From the XBRL:

2016 2018 2020 2022 2024 2025
492.9M 479.6M 475.3M 463.8M 448.5M 424.9M

Shares outstanding fell ~14% over nine years — and the pace accelerated in 2025 (23.9M shares, $3.2B). Every remaining owner's slice of the franchise grew.

Buybacks on price — the crucial test. The manual is emphatic: buybacks create value only below intrinsic value, destroy it above (Ch. 9). In 2025 Zoetis repurchased at an average around $130–135 (the stock's range) — arguably near or above our conservative intrinsic value, which is a fair criticism of the timing. But the December raise-and-buyback, and the 2026 program, are being executed with the stock in the $70s–$90s — far below any reasonable estimate of value. At $74.80, every dollar Zoetis spends buying its own stock is a dollar creating well over a dollar of value for the owners who stay. This is exactly the condition under which buybacks are the correct use of cash. Management is, at today's price, allocating capital superbly.

The one-dollar test — does retention pass?

Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r.

With incremental ROIC around 28% and r at 9%, every dollar Zoetis retains and reinvests produces far more than a dollar of value. Retention is decisively justified here — this is the capital-light, high-return case where holding earnings back to compound beats paying them out. Contrast with a utility earning 8% on capital: there, the dollar should go out as dividends. Zoetis is the opposite kind of business, and its ~31% payout / heavy-reinvestment mix is the right answer.

Candor. Management reports its problems plainly: the 10-K names the Librela U.S. softening, the Draxxin −66% generic history, the competitive pressures, and even flags the one-time reporting-lag tailwind and tells you it won't recur. That is the candor the manual looks for.

PRINCIPLE. Share count is capital allocation, and intrinsic value is a per-share quantity (Ch. 9). A management that shrinks the count ~14% while earning 28% on capital, and that is now buying stock hand-over-fist at a price far below value, is compounding your ownership on your behalf. The one-dollar test passes emphatically — retention here builds value, it does not bury it.

7. WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

Now, and only now, the arithmetic of value (Ch. 10). Our estimator, per the manual, for a durable high-ROIC compounder: a two-stage DCF of normalized owner earnings as primary, cross-checked three ways, with an explicit reconciliation of the multiple.

The discount rate as gravity (Ch. 12). With the 30-year Treasury near 5%, we set r = 9% — the long bond plus a ~4% premium for the (real but bounded) uncertainty in the cash flows. Rates are the gravity that pulls all asset values; at today's 5% long bond, 9% is appropriate for a wide-moat, A-rated franchise.

Inputs. Normalized owner earnings $2,660M (Section 4). Growth: this is where we apply hard-won conservatism. An independent research service projects 7% EPS growth to 2029–31; the ten-year history is 14%. But Q1'26 revenue grew just 2.9%, Librela is soft in the U.S., and generics nibble the livestock lines. We therefore do not use the historical 14% or even the full 7%. Our stages:
- Base: 5% for 10 years, then 2.5% terminal.
- Low: 3% for 10 years, then 2.0% terminal (barely above inflation — a "growth is largely done" scenario).
- High: 7% for 10 years, then 3.0% terminal (an independent research service's growth path holds).

Two-stage DCF results (r = 9%):

Scenario Stage-1 growth Terminal g Intrinsic value Per share
Low 3% 2.0% $41,700M ~$98
Base 5% 2.5% $50,600M ~$119
High 7% 3.0% $62,000M ~$146

Deriving and reconciling the multiple — the honest adjustment from 30×. an independent research service's historical median P/E is 30×. We must adjust that DOWN, and say exactly why:
- Slower growth: the franchise has downshifted from mid-teens to low-single-digit revenue growth. A 30× multiple was a bet on 10%+ growth that is no longer the base case. Adjust down.
- Higher rates: 30× was earned in a near-zero-rate world; at a 5% long bond, gravity pulls multiples down for everyone. Adjust down.
- Competition: Librela softening and generic erosion of legacy lines argue for a haircut. Adjust down.
- But quality: a 28%-ROIC, A-rated, cash-pay franchise still deserves a premium to the market's 18× median — it is far better than the average business.

Netting these: a quality-justified multiple of ~18–20× owner earnings is defensible — market-multiple-plus, not the old 30×. On owner earnings of $6.26/share, that is $113–$125. This reconciles cleanly with the base-case DCF (~$119) — the DCF and the multiple are the same statement in two languages, and here they agree. (We deliberately do not value off peer drug multiples as the primary method; the manual excludes peer-multiple pricing because it anchors to Mr. Market's opinion of other businesses.)

Triangulated range. Because the honest doubt is about growth and competition, we lean toward the cautious estimators and set:

Intrinsic value: Low ~$98 · Base ~$115 · High ~$140 per share.

The three cross-checks (Ch. 14).

1. No-growth floor (checks the growth assumption). Value Zoetis as if it never grows another dollar:

$2,660M ÷ 0.09 = $29,556M → ~$70 per share (≈$78 at r = 8%).

This is the arithmetic that should make you sit up. At $74.80, the market is paying essentially the no-growth floor. You are being asked to pay for the earning power as it stands today and getting the entire future of a 28%-ROIC franchise — every new product, every price increase, every share buyback — for close to nothing.

2. Implied multiple (checks the model's output). Base IV $50,600M ÷ owner earnings $2,660M = ~19× owner earnings. Is 19× defensible for a 28%-ROIC franchise growing 5% then 2.5%? Yes — comfortably; it is a modest premium to the market's 18× median for a far-above-average business. No runaway terminal value hiding here (terminal is ~57% of the base-case value — high but within bounds for a durable franchise, and we capped g conservatively).

3. Owner-earnings yield vs. the long bond (checks the anchor to rates). At $74.80:

owner-earnings yield = $2,660M ÷ $31,785M market cap = 8.4%.

Against a ~5% long bond whose coupon never grows, an 8.4% yield that does grow (even at a conservative 5%) is a plainly superior claim. This is Chapter 12's gravity applied at the moment of decision — and gravity is on our side.

The reverse-DCF — the closer. Run the model backward on the price. What growth does $74.80 silently assume? Solving the two-stage model (r = 9%, terminal 2.5%) for the price of $31,785M market cap:

The market is implying roughly −1% owner-earnings growth for a decade.

Read that again. Mr. Market is pricing a 50%-ROE, 28%-ROIC, Financial-Strength-A franchise — one that grew EPS 14% a year for ten years — as if its owner earnings will shrink about 1% annually for the next ten. Even an independent research service's cautious 7% and the observed 2.9% Q1 growth are worlds above that. The market has done our negative analysis for us: the price can only be justified by assuming the franchise is in outright decline.

Sensitivity table — intrinsic value per share, varying r and terminal g (base owner earnings $2,660M, 5% stage-1 growth):

Discount rate r g = 2.0% g = 2.5% g = 3.0%
8% $126 $137 $151
9% $107 $115 $124
10% $93 $99 $106

Across every reasonable combination of rate and terminal growth, intrinsic value lands $93–$151 — and the entire table sits above today's $74.80. Even the harshest corner (r = 10%, g = 2%) is $93, a ~20% premium to the price.

Sanity vs. An independent research service. VL's 18-month target midpoint is $138 and its 2029–31 target range is $165–$225. Our base of ~$115 is deliberately below VL — we are more conservative than the analyst on growth — yet still ~50% above the price. When the cautious analyst and the even-more-cautious value investor both land far above the quote, that agreement is worth something.

PRINCIPLE. Value is a range, not a point, and the honesty is in the range (Ch. 10, 13). Our range is $98–$140, base ~$115. Every cross-check that could break differently — the no-growth floor ($70), the yield (8.4% vs. 5%), the reverse-DCF (−1% implied) — arrives by a different road and points the same way. When the roads cannot all be wrong the same way and they agree, we act with confidence.

8. MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

The manual's cornerstone (Ch. 13):

Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value

At the live price of $74.80 against our triangulated range:

  • vs. Low $98: (98 − 74.80) ÷ 98 = 24% margin of safety.
  • vs. Base $115: (115 − 74.80) ÷ 115 = 35%.
  • vs. High $140: (140 − 74.80) ÷ 140 = 47%.

Even against the conservative end of our range — the "growth is mostly done" case — there is a ~24% cushion. Against the base case, ~35%. This is a genuine margin of safety, not a sliver.

Where does the safety sit — in the discount, or in the quality? The manual (Ch. 13, "Paying up for quality") teaches that in a durable franchise, part of the safety lives in the quality itself: a conservatively projected earnings stream that keeps growing makes today's estimate the floor of tomorrow's. Here we have both kinds of safety at once — a rare combination:
- Discount safety: the ~35% gap between price and base value.
- Quality safety: a 28%-ROIC franchise that, even growing at just 3%, compounds the floor upward while we wait; and management buying back stock below value, mechanically raising per-share value.

Is Mr. Market depressed or rational? He has a reason — growth genuinely decelerated, Librela softened, the sector is out of favor, GLP-1 mania has drawn attention elsewhere. That is not nothing; a rational observer would mark the multiple down from 30×. But he has overshot. Marking a franchise from 30× to 12× — pricing in negative growth for a decade — is not rational repricing; it is extrapolating one soft year into permanent decline. That is Mr. Market at his most manic-depressive: he is offering to sell us a wonderful business, at close to its no-growth floor, because it merely grew well instead of spectacularly.

PRINCIPLE. Volatility is opportunity, not risk (Ch. 3). The 37% fall did not make Zoetis riskier — the business strengthened its per-share economics through the decline (buybacks below value). It made the stock cheaper, and therefore safer in the only sense that matters: the probability of permanent loss fell as the price fell below a rising, conservatively-estimated value. It is his pocketbook we use, not his wisdom.

9. THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Quality tier. Zoetis sits in the top tier — a wonderful business in the manual's sense (Ch. 5): a durable, wide-moat, capital-light franchise earning ~28% on invested capital, protected by cash-pay customers, a direct sales force, brand loyalty that survives patents, and the absence of a human-pharma-style generic cliff. Financial Strength A, fortress balance sheet, owner-aligned capital allocation. It is not flawless — the livestock half faces slow generic erosion and Librela has stumbled in the U.S. — so it is a wonderful companion-animal franchise stapled to a merely-good livestock business. But the blended enterprise is high-quality by any honest measure.

The ONE question, answered. Is the moat cracking, or is this Mr. Market panic? The evidence says panic on a durable compounder. The moat is intact where it matters — dermatology and parasiticides, the core companion-animal engine, are not cracking; ROIC is ~28%; the balance sheet is a fortress; management is compounding per-share value. What actually happened is that growth normalized from spectacular to good, one new product decelerated, and the market extrapolated that into a decade of decline (the reverse-DCF's −1% implied growth). That is a valuation panic, not a franchise collapse. The castle's moat is not draining; Mr. Market is simply in a foul mood.

Open questions (intellectual honesty demands we name them).
- Librela's trajectory: is the U.S. softening a temporary competitive/perception issue or a structural ceiling on the OA-pain mAb franchise? Watch quarterly.
- GLP-1 / obesity-for-pets: an unknown we cannot size. If a weight-loss blockbuster for companion animals emerged from a competitor, it could reshape the category. Low-probability, high-consequence — monitor.
- Livestock generics: Excede, Cerenia, and others roll off patent 2025–2028; expect Draxxin-style slow erosion in that 29% bucket.
- Buyback discipline: management bought high in 2024–early 2025; we want to see the discipline it is showing now (buying in the $70s) continue.

Universal lessons from this case:
1. A great business at a fair price beats a fair business at a great price — and here we get a great business at a cheap price, the rare best-of-both.
2. The reverse-DCF is the antidote to panic. When a stock crashes, ask what the new price assumes. If it assumes absurdity (here, negative growth from a 14%-grower), the market has done your work for you.
3. Distinguish deceleration from decline. Growth slowing from 14% to 5% is not the same as a business shrinking; conflating them is how Mr. Market manufactures bargains.
4. The no-growth floor tells you what you're paying for growth. At $74.80 ≈ the $70 floor, you pay almost nothing for the future of a compounder — the definition of a margin of safety.

11. GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (in order of appearance, with ZTS's numbers)

  1. Circle of competence. The set of businesses you can forecast; boundary matters more than size. ZTS: inside — recurring, cash-pay animal health with bounded unknowns.
  2. Mr. Market / price ≠ value. The market quotes a mood, not a worth. ZTS: $118 → $74.80 in one quarter is mood, not a business fact.
  3. Gross margin = gross profit ÷ revenue. ZTS: 6,801 ÷ 9,467 = 71.8%.
  4. Operating margin = operating income ÷ revenue. ZTS: 3,546 ÷ 9,467 = 37.5%.
  5. Net margin = net income ÷ revenue. ZTS: 2,673 ÷ 9,467 = 28.2%.
  6. Cash conversion = operating cash flow ÷ net income. ZTS: 2,904 ÷ 2,673 = 1.09× (earnings are real cash).
  7. Owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − Δworking capital. ZTS: 2,673 + 487 − 350 − 150 = $2,660M ≈ $6.26/sh.
  8. Maintenance vs. growth capex. Spending to stand still vs. to expand. ZTS: total capex $621M but PP&E depreciation only $330M → maintenance ~$350M; the rest is growth.
  9. ROIC = NOPAT ÷ invested capital. ZTS: 2,819 ÷ 10,169 = 27.7% (vs r = 9%).
  10. NOPAT = operating income × (1 − tax rate). ZTS: 3,546 × (1 − 0.205) = $2,819M.
  11. ROE = net income ÷ equity. ZTS: 2,673 ÷ 3,331 = 80.2% — flattered by buyback-shrunk equity; ROIC is the cleaner read.
  12. Net debt / owner earnings = (total debt − cash) ÷ owner earnings. ZTS: (9,150 − 2,312) ÷ 2,660 = 2.6×.
  13. Interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest expense. ZTS: 3,546 ÷ 222 = 16.0×.
  14. Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. ZTS: 6,768 ÷ 2,235 = 3.0×.
  15. One-dollar test. Retain a dollar only if incremental ROIC ≥ r. ZTS: 28% ≫ 9% — retention decisively justified.
  16. Discount rate (r). Opportunity cost = long bond + risk premium. ZTS: ~5% Treasury + ~4% = 9%.
  17. Two-stage DCF = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. ZTS base (5%→2.5%, r=9%): ~$119/sh.
  18. No-growth (earning-power) value = owner earnings ÷ r. ZTS: 2,660 ÷ 0.09 = $29,556M ≈ $70/sh — the floor the price nearly matches.
  19. Implied multiple = intrinsic value ÷ owner earnings. ZTS: 50,600 ÷ 2,660 = ~19× (a fair premium to the 18× market median).
  20. Owner-earnings yield = owner earnings ÷ market cap. ZTS: 2,660 ÷ 31,785 = 8.4% (vs. ~5% long bond).
  21. Reverse DCF. Solve the price for implied growth. ZTS: $74.80 implies ~−1% owner-earnings growth for a decade — the market pricing decline into a 14%-historic-grower.
  22. Margin of safety = (intrinsic value − price) ÷ intrinsic value. ZTS: vs. base $115 → 35%; vs. low $98 → 24%.
  23. Risk = probability of permanent capital loss, not volatility. ZTS: the 37% drop lowered risk (price fell below a rising value), it did not raise it.
  24. Quality-vs-price doctrine. A great business at a fair price beats a fair one at a great price. ZTS: a great business at a cheap price — both at once.
