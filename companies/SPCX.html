Valuing SPCX — Space Exploration Technologies Corp (SpaceX)

A Value-Investing Seminar, In the Graham–Buffett tradition · Teaching date: July 4, 2026 · Live price used: $162.00 (equity_atlas.xlsx, 07/04/2026 11:45)

A teaching case, not investment advice. We are two long-dead teachers walking a class through a live, freshly-IPO'd security to show the method — the sequence of questions — not to issue a recommendation. Where we are uncertain, we will say so plainly, because on this particular company uncertainty is the main event.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

BUFFETT. Let's start where we always start: what does this thing actually do, and can we understand it? On the surface SpaceX is the most famous company on Earth — reusable rockets, Starlink dishes on farmhouse roofs, a founder who is a household name. But the prospectus contains a surprise that changes everything, and I want you to sit with it before you look at a single multiple.

The company that IPO'd on June 12, 2026 is not the SpaceX of popular imagination. It is a merged entity with three reportable segments (Note 19 / Note 18, item8_financial_statements.txt):

  1. Space — Falcon 9 / Falcon Heavy / Dragon / Starship. Designs, builds, launches, and increasingly reuses rockets. FY2025 revenue $4,086M.
  2. Connectivity (Starlink) — a worldwide low-earth-orbit broadband and satellite-to-mobile network. FY2025 revenue $11,387M. This is the giant.
  3. AI (xAI) — a vertically integrated AI platform: advertising (the X/Twitter business) plus Grok and its data-center "AI Solutions & Infrastructure." FY2025 revenue $3,201M.

The prospectus states the AI segment is "built on our founder Elon Musk's mission" and is the former xAI/X business, folded in via the xAI merger. All prior periods have been restated as-combined.

GRAHAM. So we are handed a conglomerate wearing a rocket's clothing. This is not pedantry. When we compute a per-share number, consolidated GAAP figures blend three utterly different economic animals: a roughly break-even rocket business, a superbly profitable and fast-growing broadband utility, and a spectacularly loss-making AI venture. Segment income from operations, FY2025 (Note 19):

Segment FY2025 revenue FY2025 operating income
Space $4,086M $(657)M
Connectivity (Starlink) $11,387M $4,423M
AI (xAI) $3,201M $(6,355)M
Consolidated $18,674M $(2,589)M

The deciding question. Everything below turns on one question: Are we buying Starlink — a capital-intensive but wide-moat broadband compounder throwing off a 39% segment operating margin — or are we buying that jewel welded to xAI, a $6.4-billion-a-year operating-loss machine whose ultimate economics no honest person can yet forecast? The price you pay buys the whole bundle; you cannot buy the segment you like.

The circle-of-competence gate. Chapter 4 of the manual: "confine analysis to businesses whose long-term economics you can understand well enough to forecast within a sensible range… 'Too hard — I cannot forecast this' is a complete and respectable answer."

Let us be honest, as the manual demands. Starlink, considered alone, sits close to the edge of a competent circle: a subscription broadband utility with real numbers, comprehensible unit economics, and a decade-out demand curve one can argue about sensibly. xAI does not sit inside any prudent investor's circle in 2026. Its revenue is $3.2B, its operating loss is twice its revenue, its capex ($12.7B in FY2025) is four times its revenue, and the return on that AI capital is — as Selection & Opinion itself notes about the whole AI-capex wave — "largely unproven." Forecasting xAI's owner earnings a decade out is not analysis; it is a guess dressed as one.

GENERAL PRINCIPLE. A conglomerate is only as forecastable as its least-forecastable material segment. When a business staples a jewel to a black box, the black box sets your circle-of-competence verdict for the whole — because you must buy both.

GRAHAM. Two things. First, PP&E more than doubled in fifteen months, from $21B to $54B — this is a business pouring concrete and launching metal at a furious rate. Second, on a pro-forma basis (capitalization table, giving effect to the IPO and preferred conversion): cash & equivalents rise to $90.3B after the $135/share raise, redeemable preferred converts to common, and total shareholders' equity becomes $116.0B. Post-IPO the balance sheet is fortress-strong on cash — but it also now carries, after the June 26 8-K, roughly $29–30B of long-term debt plus a further $25.5B senior-notes issue and a $20B bridge loan. This is a capital-hungry enterprise, and the manual is explicit that capital-hunger is a warning, not a virtue.

Never anchor to book. The $116B pro-forma equity is a bookkeeping artifact of the IPO cash and paid-in capital; against a $41.3B accumulated deficit it tells you almost nothing about intrinsic value. As the manual says (Ch.7): book value understates a great business and overstates a poor one. We note book and move on.

BUFFETT. This is the statement that matters, and it is brutal in its clarity. Operating cash flow is real and rising — $4.5B → $5.8B → $6.8B. But capex went from $4.4B to $20.7B, so free cash flow swung from roughly breakeven to minus $14 billion. And the segment capex tells you exactly who ate it: FY2025 capex was Space $3.8B, Connectivity $4.2B, and AI/xAI $12.7B — data centers for Grok. Q1 2026 alone: $10.1B capex against $1.0B CFO, with $7.7B of it AI. There is also a $7.1B non-cash "capex in accounts payable," so the true capital commitment is even larger than the cash line.

How the three articulate. The income statement shows a growth-investment-driven GAAP loss. The cash-flow statement shows the base business (Space + Starlink) generating billions of real operating cash, all of it — and $14B more — being redeployed into growth capex, the majority into an AI venture of unproven return. The balance sheet shows the result: a PP&E base that doubled, funded by a mountain of new debt and IPO equity. The story is internally consistent. It is the story of a business consuming enormous capital to grow, where whether that growth creates or destroys value is the entire question (manual Ch.10).

GENERAL PRINCIPLE. When operating cash flow is strong but free cash flow is deeply negative, the difference is a bet management is making with your money. Your job is to judge the return on that bet — not to admire the cash flow before the bet is placed.

EARNINGS POWER & THE MOAT

Isolating the earning power — the recent-IPO normalization

GRAHAM. The manual (Ch.7) tells us to compute owner earnings = reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔworkingÂ capital, and warns that "the hardest and most important term is maintenance capital expenditure." For a two-year-old public conglomerate mid-mega-investment, separating maintenance from growth capex is genuinely hard — but we must try, and we must do it segment by segment, because the consolidated number is meaningless.

Consolidated is unusable. Consolidated FY2025 owner earnings would be: net loss $(4,937) + D&A $6,701 + SBC add-back debatable − capex $20,737 = a large negative number. That merely restates "FCF is −$14B." It values nothing. So we do what the case demands: value the good business, and treat the rest explicitly.

Starlink (Connectivity) normalized owner earnings — the estimate that matters.
- Segment operating income FY2025: $4,423M (on $11,387M revenue → 38.8% operating margin).
- Add back segment D&A: $2,376M (much of it satellite depreciation).
- Segment capex FY2025: $4,178M. Here is the maintenance-vs-growth judgment. Starlink is still expanding rapidly (subscribers +105% YoY), so a large share of that $4.2B is growth capex — new satellites and ground stations to serve new subscribers, not to hold the existing base. Satellites have a ~5-year orbital life, so maintenance (replacement) capex is real but well below the total. Judgment: call maintenance capex ~$2.4B (roughly equal to D&A, a conservative-to-fair proxy for a fleet being continuously replenished), leaving ~$1.8B as growth capex.
- Apply a normalized cash tax at ~21% to segment operating income (the segment is pre-corporate-overhead and pre-interest; this is deliberately rough).

Rough Starlink owner earnings ≈ (operating income $4,423 × (1 − 0.21)) + D&A $2,376 − maintenance capex ~$2,400 ≈ $3,495 + $2,376 − $2,400 ≈ ~$3.5B, before allocating any corporate overhead or the interest on the debt that partly funds it. Even after a generous haircut for shared corporate cost and interest, Starlink alone plausibly earns on the order of $2.5–3.5B of owner earnings today, growing fast. That is the engine.

Space: roughly break-even at the operating line ($(657)M in FY2025, but that is after $3.0B of Starship R&D — an option, not a maintenance cost). Normalized for the growth R&D, the legacy Falcon launch franchise is modestly profitable. We treat Space as approximately zero owner earnings today, with a valuable free option on Starship.

AI (xAI): negative owner earnings — a cash consumer. $(6,355)M operating loss and $12.7B capex. On the manual's arithmetic this segment is destroying value at present (ROIC deeply negative < r). We assign it zero or negative value in the base case and treat any positive outcome as speculative upside outside our circle.

The moat

BUFFETT. Chapter 5: the diagnostic test of a moat is pricing power, and a franchise is a product (1) needed/desired, (2) with no close substitute, (3) not price-regulated. Let's grade each business.

  • Starlink — a genuine, wide, and rare moat. It is a low-earth-orbit network with scale, capital, spectrum, and launch advantages that compound. Consider: SpaceX launches its own satellites on its own reusable rockets (122 Starlink launches in 2025; SpaceX put >80% of the world's mass to orbit in 2025 at >99% Falcon mission success, ~165 Falcon 9 launches, >2,200 tonnes), so its cost to deploy capacity is a fraction of any rival who must buy launches. That is a structural low-cost position AND a scale/network advantage AND vertical integration — three moat sources at once. Rivals are years and tens of billions behind, and must launch on someone else's rockets. Subscriber growth of +105% (5.0M → 10.3M) with the base still profitable is the fingerprint of a moat. The one crack: ARPU fell from ~$86 to ~$66/month year-over-year as growth shifted to lower-priced international consumers — so Starlink has scale pricing power but not yet premium pricing power, and the MD&A frames the decline as a deliberate share-grab. Still, this is as close to a wide moat as a five-year-old business gets.
  • Space: a strong, arguably widening cost moat in launch (reusability nobody else has matched at scale), but it is a lumpy, contract-driven, government-heavy business — a good business, not a compounding franchise on its own.
  • AI (xAI): no demonstrated moat. The AI-model field is a capital arms race with many well-funded entrants and, per an independent research service's own macro commentary, unproven returns. Spending $12.7B/yr does not by itself create a moat; it may simply be table stakes in a game with no durable winner yet. (Scale markers from the MD&A: ~1.3B lifetime accounts, ~550M monthly active users, 6.3M paying subscribers, ~1.0 GW of compute — impressive reach, but reach is not yet a franchise.)

A note on rivals: the prospectus discusses competition only generically ("established and emerging companies," "foreign competitors that may benefit from government support"). It does not name Blue Origin, Amazon's Kuiper, or specific Chinese constellations. We reference them as the real-world competitive set, but they are our characterization, not the filing's.

ROIC vs cost of capital — the decisive comparison

Manual Ch.6: value is created only when ROIC > r. We set r below (~9–10%). Consolidated ROIC is negative because of xAI. But that is the wrong lens. The honest read:
- Starlink: ROIC comfortably above r. ~$3.5B pre-corporate owner earnings against a satellite/ground asset base of very roughly $15–20B implies a high-teens-plus return — well above cost of capital. Value-creating growth.
- Space: ROIC roughly at or slightly below r, distorted by Starship investment. Neutral.
- AI/xAI: ROIC deeply negative. Value-destroying growth today, by the manual's arithmetic, unless it becomes something it is not yet.

GENERAL PRINCIPLE. A single wonderful business inside a conglomerate does not make the conglomerate wonderful. You must earn the jewel's high multiple by subtracting, at honest cost, everything stapled to it.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

GRAHAM. The manual (Ch.8) judges management on capital allocation across the five uses, the one-dollar test, buybacks-on-price, and candor. Here the analysis is unusually sharp because one man, Elon Musk, controls it all.

Control. Post-IPO, Musk beneficially owns 849.5M Class A and ~5,569M Class B shares (incl. 350M options). Class B carries 10 votes per share; Musk holds >50% of total voting power, can elect all directors (Class B alone elects 51% of the board), and can only be removed by Class B holders — himself. SpaceX is a "controlled company" exempt from key Nasdaq governance rules. Layer on Texas incorporation: the charter/bylaws set a 3%-ownership threshold (≈ $53 billion at this valuation) before a holder may bring a derivative suit or a shareholder proposal, codify the business-judgment rule, and impose forum-selection and mandatory-arbitration provisions. Translation: retail Class A holders have essentially no governance recourse. Musk is also CEO and CTO, carries no key-person life insurance, and splits his time across Tesla, Neuralink, and The Boring Company. As minority Class A holders, you are passengers, seat-belted in. That is not automatically disqualifying — we happily rode with owner-operators — but it removes every external check the manual prizes.

The five uses of capital, as practiced here:
1. Reinvest in the business — done at enormous scale ($20.7B capex). Value-creating where it is Starlink (ROIC > r); value-destroying where it is xAI (ROIC < r). Management is making a giant, undiversified bet that AI will eventually clear its cost of capital.
2. Acquisitions — the xAI merger (Feb 2026, shown retrospectively as a common-control combination), folding a huge loss-maker into the crown-jewel cash flows, and the ~$19.6B EchoStar spectrum acquisition (AWS-4 / H-block, plus up to 15 MHz of AWS-3): ~$11.1B in Class A stock (261.8M shares at a fixed $42.40) plus up to ~$8.5B of EchoStar debt payoff; FCC-approved 5/12/2026, transfer closing 5/22/2026 into a Nevada trust, acquisition closing expected ~Nov 2027. The spectrum deal is strategically coherent (Starlink Mobile needs spectrum) but large; the xAI merger is precisely the kind of empire-expanding combination the manual warns about under the "institutional imperative."
3. Pay down debt — no; the company is adding debt aggressively ($20B bridge loan, $25.5B notes).
4. Dividends — none, appropriately, given the reinvestment runway.
5. Buybacks — historically repurchased stock/preferred ($1.1B in FY2025, $4.3B in Q1 2026 around the IPO), but at private-round prices; not a price-disciplined public buyback we can score against intrinsic value.

The one-dollar test (manual Ch.8): retain a dollar only if it produces ≥ $1 of market value, i.e., incremental ROIC ≥ r. Starlink passes emphatically. xAI fails today — dollars poured in are, on current economics, returning less than a dollar of value. Management is retaining everything and cross-subsidizing the failing test with the passing one. A value investor must price that.

Candor. Mixed. The prospectus is, to its credit, unusually transparent in its segment disclosure — it lets us see xAI's losses cleanly, which many promoters would bury. That is a point for candor. Against it: the whole structure is built around a single founder's vision and control, with related-party debt (~$9.0B owed to Musk-affiliated entities) threaded through the balance sheet.

GENERAL PRINCIPLE. An owner-operator with total control is a double-edged sword: superb when he compounds within his circle, dangerous when the institutional imperative leads him to annex a value-destroying venture that minority holders cannot veto.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

BUFFETT. The master equation never changes: IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. But the manual's discipline is to choose the estimator the business deserves. This business deserves a sum-of-the-parts, because its three parts have wildly different economics and stapling them into one multiple would be malpractice. We triangulate, we sensitize, and — because it is a two-year-old public conglomerate with an unforecastable segment — we widen the margin of safety far beyond normal.

Post-IPO share count and the price the market is quoting

  • IPO price: $135.00/share; 555,555,555 Class A shares sold.
  • Pro-forma-as-adjusted fully-diluted shares (capitalization table): ~7,380M Class A + 5,696M Class B + 494M Class C ≈ ~13,570M shares (13.57 billion). (We use the fully-diluted, post-conversion count — the manual's rule for an IPO. Using the pre-IPO ~2.9B weighted-average share count would overstate per-share value roughly 4–5×; that is the classic new-issue trap.)
  • Live price $162 × 13.57B shares ≈ ~$2.20 trillion equity market capitalization.
  • Add ~$30B net debt (post-IPO cash largely offsets gross debt) → enterprise value on the order of $2.2 trillion. (Order-of-magnitude; the exact figure depends on over-allotment and final share count, but the scale is what matters.)

GRAHAM. Hold that number. The market is pricing this enterprise at over two trillion dollars. Now let us build value from the parts and see what must be true to justify it.

Part 1 — Starlink (the jewel), valued as a growing broadband franchise

Two independent methods, reconciled:

(a) DCF on Starlink owner earnings. Start ~$3.0B owner earnings (mid of our $2.5–3.5B range, after a haircut for corporate overhead and interest). Assume the moat supports 20%/yr growth for 10 years (below the current +50% revenue pace — deliberately conservative, and reflecting ARPU pressure), then 4% terminal. Discount at r = 9% (manual Ch.11: long rate ~4.5–5% + premium; we use a franchise-appropriate 9%).
- Two-stage IV ≈ explicit-period PV + discounted terminal. At these inputs, Starlink alone is worth on the order of $300–500B. (Sensitivity: at 15% growth / r=10%, ~$220B; at 25% growth / r=8%, ~$650B. The spread is the point.)

(b) Multiple cross-check. ~$3.0B owner earnings growing 20%+ with a wide moat plausibly earns a 30–50× multiple → $90–150B on today's earnings, or, on the segment operating income of $4.4B growing fast, a 40–60× operating multiple → ~$180–260B. Reconciling the DCF and the multiple, and giving weight to the extraordinary growth runway (only 10.3M of a 3.3-billion-person addressable market), we anchor Starlink at roughly $300–500B, base ~$400B.

Part 2 — Space (Falcon franchise + Starship option)

Near-break-even legacy launch business with a widening cost moat, plus a genuine but unpriceable option on Starship (which could be transformational or a money pit). We value the base launch franchise conservatively at $50–150B (a strategic, high-barrier, government-anchored asset) and treat Starship as option value, base ~$0, upside large. Base ~$100B.

Part 3 — AI (xAI)

A $3.2B-revenue business losing $6.4B/yr at the operating line with $12.7B/yr capex and no demonstrated moat. Mr. Market clearly assigns it a large positive number (it is why the private "AI" narrative commands hundreds of billions). We, following the manual, refuse to. In our base case we value xAI at $0 — its owner earnings are negative and its future is outside our circle. In a bear case we value it below zero (a call on the crown-jewel cash flows, since it consumes them). In a bull case, if you insist AI clears its cost of capital someday, you might assign $200–400B — but that is a speculation, not a valuation, and the margin of safety must absorb the possibility it is worth nothing.

Sanity check vs an independent research service / live multiple. We have no an independent research service report and no live P/E to check against — the atlas fields are blank, the company has never earned a GAAP profit ex-mark-to-market. That absence is itself a red flag: there is no external, independent estimate of earning power to triangulate our own. The only market datapoint is the price, and the price ($162, ~$2.2T EV) sits 3–7× above even our high-case intrinsic value of ~$66/share. For the price to be right, xAI must be worth well over a trillion dollars on its own — i.e., the market is paying, in full and today, for an AI outcome that on current economics is destroying capital.

Value-creating vs capital-consuming growth (manual Ch.10), restated as the verdict on price: the market is capitalizing all of SpaceX's growth — including the xAI growth that currently earns a return below its cost of capital — as though it were Starlink-quality growth. That is the error the manual is built to prevent.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

GRAHAM. The arithmetic, done coldly.

  • Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) / Intrinsic Value.
  • Base IV ~$37; price $162 → (37 − 162)/37 = −338%. The price is not at a discount to conservative value; it is at a large premium to even our optimistic value.
  • Against the high case ($66): (66 − 162)/66 = −145%. Still no margin of safety — a negative one.
  • The margin-of-safety buy price, demanding the manual's wide cushion given the xAI uncertainty, would be perhaps 30–50% below our low case — i.e., somewhere around $11–15/share. We are being offered $162.

Is Mr. Market euphoric, depressed, or rational? Euphoric. And the context confirms it: Selection & Opinion shows the S&P 500 up 24.5% in twelve months, the market near an all-time high (last top 6/2/2026), the an independent research service Asset Allocation Model at 65% stocks, and explicit commentary that the AI-capex trade is "the primary engine for the U.S. economy right now" with returns "largely unproven." SpaceX IPO'd into the hottest possible tape for exactly its story, and the stock is already +20% above its $135 IPO price in its first weeks. This is Mr. Market at his most manic — offering to sell you a great story at a two-trillion-dollar quote.

One clarification on solvency, in fairness. Despite the negative free cash flow and $41.3B accumulated deficit, the auditors issued no going-concern qualification — the filing states liquidity is sufficient for at least twelve months (post-IPO cash of ~$90B pro-forma, plus a $5B revolver). So this is a capital-intensity and capital-allocation risk, not an imminent-insolvency risk. We frame it as: the company can afford to keep making its xAI bet for years — which is precisely why the market gets to keep paying for it, and why the discipline to not pay falls entirely on us.

IPO caveats & the lock-up (manual's recent-IPO rule).
- Early post-listing prices are the least reliable Mr. Market quotes — thin float, momentum, index-inclusion speculation.
- Lock-up / supply overhang: ~12.2 billion shares become eligible for resale under Rule 144 starting 90 days after the offering, subject to lock-up, and holders can force registration of ~9.2 billion shares. Musk is under no obligation to keep his stake after lock-up. That is an enormous latent supply against a small IPO float — a classic setup for post-lock-up pressure.

GENERAL PRINCIPLE. A brand-new issue, priced into a euphoric market, on the decade's hottest narrative, with a huge lock-up overhang, is the single most dangerous configuration in which Mr. Market operates. The correct posture is patience, not participation.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Rating: AVOID (at $162). Not because SpaceX is a bad business — Starlink is one of the most impressive franchises we have ever examined, and Space is a formidable, moated enterprise. We avoid the security at this price for three converging reasons: (1) a material segment (xAI) sits outside any prudent circle of competence and is currently destroying capital; (2) there is no margin of safety — the ~$2.2T price is a large premium to even our optimistic sum-of-the-parts; and (3) it is a two-year-old public conglomerate with negative free cash flow, no earnings history to normalize, no independent an independent research service check, and a huge lock-up overhang, priced into a euphoric market.

Answering the one deciding question: Are we buying Starlink, or Starlink welded to a black box? At $162 we are being asked to pay full price for the black box and then some. The wonderful part (Starlink) is real; the price makes the whole un-buyable.

Business quality vs. the security: the Starlink business is wonderful; Space is good; xAI is too-hard/avoid; the combined security at $162 is an avoid. The manual's "too new to forecast a decade out" verdict applies with full force — and here it is not a cop-out but the correct answer.

Two or three open questions we cannot yet answer (and neither can Mr. Market):
1. Will xAI ever earn a return above its cost of capital, or is it a permanent drain that the market is nonetheless capitalizing at a premium? This single question drives most of the price.
2. How durable is Starlink's pricing power as ARPU falls (−22.9% YoY) and Kuiper/Chinese constellations mature? Is it a wide moat or a widening one — or a Roman candle?
3. What is normalized free cash flow once the Starship and xAI mega-investments mature — does the base business's real cash-generating power emerge, or does capital-hunger persist indefinitely? And relatedly: does Starship work? The growth strategy is "highly dependent" on it; it has flown 12 flight tests (through May 2026) without yet delivering a payload, still needs rapid full reusability, upper-stage catch, and in-orbit cryogenic refueling that has never been demonstrated by anyone. SpaceX does not insure its own vehicles or satellites. Starship is a genuine option — but an option, not a certainty, and the price treats it as nearly done.

Universal lessons from this case:
- Sum-of-the-parts is a discipline, not a decoration. When segments have opposite economics, one blended multiple hides the very thing you must see. Read the segment table before the headline.
- A jewel welded to a black box is priced as two jewels by an excited market. The value investor's edge is refusing to pay for the box — and being willing to walk away from the jewel when the box comes attached at full price.
- Use the post-IPO diluted share count, always. Here it is the difference between ~$2.9B and ~13.57B shares — a 4–5× swing in per-share value and the most common way new-issue investors fool themselves.
- Price, not quality alone, makes the investment (manual Ch.13). The greatest business on Earth is a poor purchase at a price that already assumes the best of everything.

Teaching case only — not advice. If Mr. Market ever offers this jewel-and-box bundle at, say, $15–25/share in a future panic, with xAI's fate clearer, the analysis reopens. At $162, we do nothing and wait. Doing nothing, well, is itself a discipline.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (with SpaceX's numbers plugged in)

  • Intrinsic Value (IV). The discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ. SpaceX SOTP base ≈ $500B equity ≈ $37/share.
  • Owner Earnings. Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔworkingÂ capital. Consolidated SpaceX is not usefully computable (deeply negative); Starlink segment ≈ $2.5–3.5B after maintenance capex.
  • Maintenance capex. Capex needed to preserve competitive position and unit volume, vs. growth capex for expansion. SpaceX FY2025 total capex $20,737M; we judge only a minority is maintenance (Starlink maint. ~$2.4B of its $4.2B; most of xAI's $12.7B is growth).
  • Free cash flow (CFO − capex). SpaceX FY2025: $6,785M − $20,737M = −$13,952M. Deeply negative.
  • ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital. Quality measure; create value only if ROIC > r. Starlink > r (high-teens+); consolidated negative (dragged by xAI).
  • r (discount rate). Opportunity cost ≈ long rate + risk premium. Here long Treasury 4.47% (10-yr) / 4.97% (30-yr); we use r = 9–10% for the franchise, higher for speculative segments.
  • g (sustainable growth) = Reinvestment Rate × ROIC. Value-creating only if ROIC > r. Starlink's high-ROIC growth is value-creating; xAI's is value-destroying.
  • Margin of Safety = (IV − Price)/IV. Base: (37 − 162)/37 = −338% — a negative margin; no cushion.
  • Two-stage DCF. IV = Σₜ₌₁ⁿ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ. Applied to Starlink: ~$3.0B start, 20% for 10 yrs, 4% terminal, r=9% → order of $300–500B.
  • Moat / economic franchise. Durable advantage whose signature is pricing power. Starlink: scale + low-cost launch + spectrum = wide; xAI: none demonstrated.
  • Mr. Market. The manic-depressive partner who quotes a price, not a value. Today: euphoric (S&P +24.5% YoY, AI-capex mania, IPO at +20% over issue).
  • Controlled company / dual-class. Class B = 10 votes/share; Musk holds >50% voting power, elects all directors; Nasdaq governance exemptions apply. Class A holders are passengers.
  • Lock-up. Contractual post-IPO no-sell window. ~12.2B shares eligible under Rule 144 90 days post-offering; ~9.2B shares with registration rights; Musk not obligated to hold.
  • Enterprise Value (EV). Equity value + net debt. SpaceX ≈ $2.2 trillion ($162 × ~13.57B shares + ~$30B net debt).
  • Diluted share count (post-IPO). ~13.57 billion (Class A ~7.38B + Class B ~5.70B + Class C ~0.49B), pro-forma-as-adjusted — the correct denominator, ~4–5× the pre-IPO weighted average.
  • Segment operating margin. Operating income ÷ segment revenue. Starlink FY2025: $4,423M/$11,387M = 38.8%.
  • Backlog / deferred revenue. Contracted future revenue. SpaceX backlog $28,377M (YE2025); deferred revenue $13,236M (Q1 2026).
  • Customer concentration. U.S. government ("Customer A") = 20.9% of FY2025 revenue (down from 25.2% in 2023 as Starlink Consumer scales).
