Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMY) — A Value-Investing Seminar

Co-taught by Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett
Case-study date: July 4, 2026 · Live price: $58.13

A teaching case, not a recommendation. We are going to think out loud, show every
figure as arithmetic, cite the statement behind it, and pull out the transferable
principle each time. Our aim is not to tell you whether to own Bristol-Myers Squibb.
It is to hand you a repeatable way of thinking that you can carry to the next company,
and the one after that.

OPENING THE CASE — THE BUSINESS

WARREN: Let's start where we always start. What are we actually buying? Not a ticker — a fractional claim on the cash a business will hand its owners over its remaining life (Manual, Ch. 1). So: what does Bristol-Myers Squibb do, and can we forecast that cash?

BMY is a global biopharmaceutical company. It reports as a single operating segment (10-K, Item 8, Note 1) — the CEO manages the whole enterprise on consolidated net income, not by drug. But for our purposes the business splits cleanly into two buckets that management itself uses (10-K Item 8, Note 2, revenue by product):

FY2025 revenue = $48,194M, made of:

Bucket FY2023 FY2024 FY2025 Trajectory
Growth Portfolio $19,366M $22,563M $26,409M Rising ~$3.5–3.8B/yr
Legacy Portfolio $25,640M $25,737M $21,785M Now eroding
Total Revenues $45,006M $48,300M $48,194M Flat

The individual drugs (10-K Item 8, Note 2):

Legacy (the wasting assets):
- Eliquis (blood thinner) — $14,443M, still growing, but the single largest concentration of risk.
- Revlimid (multiple myeloma) — $2,951M, already collapsing from $6,097M in 2023 as generics arrive.
- Pomalyst $2,733M, Sprycel $493M (was $1,930M in 2023), Abraxane $368M (was $1,004M).

Growth (the replacements):
- Opdivo (immuno-oncology) — $10,049M, the biggest single drug, but itself a Legacy-in-waiting.
- Orencia $3,705M, Yervoy $2,900M, Reblozyl $2,327M, Camzyos $1,068M, Breyanzi $1,358M, Opdualag $1,185M, Sotyktu $291M, Krazati $205M, and the newest bet, Cobenfy (schizophrenia, from the $14B Karuna deal) — just $155M.

Customers are not patients; they are three wholesalers — McKesson (36% of U.S. gross), Cencora (29%), Cardinal (22%) — and behind them the payers: Medicare, Medicaid, the 340B program, and commercial insurers who extract enormous rebates (we'll show that shortly).

BEN: Now the gate the Manual insists we walk through before any arithmetic — the circle of competence (Ch. 4). Discounting future owner earnings presupposes you can estimate them. So the honest question: can we forecast this business?

Let me be candid, because candor is the whole discipline here. Big pharma sits near the edge of the circle — and this particular company sits right on the line. Its value hinges on three things none of us can forecast with precision:
1. Patent-expiry timing — when exactly does each drug lose exclusivity, and how fast does revenue fall?
2. Pipeline success probabilities — will Cobenfy, Camzyos, Breyanzi and the acquired assets actually replace the losses?
3. Drug-pricing politics — the Inflation Reduction Act, "most-favored-nation" pricing, the December 2025 U.S. Government Agreement (10-K Item 1).

Principle (Ch. 4): "Too hard — I cannot forecast this" is a complete and respectable answer, and using it often is a competitive advantage. We will not pretend to precision we don't have. Instead we will do what an honest analyst can do: bound the range widely, and let the margin of safety — not false confidence — make the decision.

THE ONE QUESTION that this entire case reduces to:

Does the Growth Portfolio plus the pipeline replace the Legacy patent-cliff losses fast enough, and at high enough returns, to keep owner earnings roughly intact through the trough?

Everything that follows is an attempt to bound the answer.

READING THE STATEMENTS TOGETHER

The three statements are three views of the same year. Read alone, each misleads; read together, they articulate and tell the truth. Nowhere is this more important than at BMY, where the income statement is a hall of mirrors and the cash-flow statement is the honest witness. This is the star section of the case.

The income statement (10-K Item 8, Consolidated Statements of Earnings)

WARREN: Look at 2024. A GAAP loss of $8.9 billion — negative $4.41 a share. A newcomer would flinch. Now hold that next to the cash-flow statement for the very same year: operating cash flow of $15,190M. A business cannot lose nine billion dollars and generate fifteen billion of cash unless the "loss" is largely fictional. It was. The 2024 loss was almost entirely a single non-cash / non-recurring accounting event: a $13,373M Acquired IPRD charge (10-K Item 8, Note 1), of which $12,122M was the one-time, non-tax-deductible write-off from acquiring Karuna Therapeutics (Cobenfy). Buy a drug in development, and accounting makes you expense the whole purchase price at once as if you'd set the cash on fire — even though you got a real asset.

Principle (Ch. 7): reported (GAAP) earnings are an accounting construct, not the cash an owner can extract. When the income statement and the cash-flow statement disagree violently, believe the cash.

The GAAP-vs-cash gap — the earnings-quality scan

Two recurring, non-cash items mangle BMY's GAAP earnings:

(1) Amortization of acquired intangibles. When BMY bought Celgene for ~$74B in 2019, most of the price landed on the balance sheet as "intangible assets" (drug rights), which then amortize through the income statement year after year — a real accounting expense, but no cash leaves the building. Watch it wind down as the Celgene intangibles run off:

  • 2023: $9,047M → 2024: $8,872M → 2025: $3,317M

That $5.6B year-over-year drop in a single non-cash line is a big reason 2025 GAAP EPS ($3.46) looks so much better than 2024. It is an accounting tailwind, not an operating one.

(2) Acquired IPRD — lumpy write-offs of drugs bought in development ($3,721M in 2025, $13,373M in 2024). These are real cash costs of M&A, but they hit in one violent lump and distort year-to-year comparability.

Management's own reconciliation (10-K Item 7, MD&A) lays the gap bare:

($M, except per share) 2024 2025
GAAP net earnings/(loss) (8,948) 7,054
Specified items (add-back) 11,288 5,491
Non-GAAP net earnings 2,340 12,545
GAAP diluted EPS (4.41) 3.46
Non-GAAP diluted EPS 1.15 6.15

BEN: Here is the discipline, Warren. Non-GAAP is not automatically the truth either. Management would like us to add back all $5,491M of "specified items" in 2025. We should not do so uncritically. Notice what's in that bucket (MD&A, Note on non-GAAP): amortization "of product rights that generate a significant portion of our ongoing revenue and will recur until the intangible assets are fully amortized." Read that twice. Those product rights earn the revenue. Adding back their entire amortization while keeping the revenue is a mild flattery. The honest owner-earnings number sits between GAAP's $3.46 and non-GAAP's $6.15 — closer to non-GAAP because the amortization is genuinely non-cash, but with a haircut because it stands in for a wasting asset that must be replaced.

Principle (Ch. 7, red flags): "one-time" or "specified" items that recur every year are, in truth, recurring costs of doing business. Add back the non-cash ones; scrutinize the ones management wishes were invisible.

The one place cash tells the truth cleanly: rebates

Buried in Note 2 is one of the most instructive tables in the filing — the bridge from list price to what BMY actually keeps:

BMY bills $88.1B and keeps $46.8B — it gives back $41.3B, or 47%, in rebates and discounts, and that give-back is growing faster than gross sales (up 12% in 2025 vs. gross up 5%). Principle: pricing power is the signature of a moat (Ch. 5). A business handing back 47% of its list price and rising is telling you the payers, not the seller, increasingly hold the pen.

The balance sheet — and why we will NOT anchor to book

(10-K Item 8, Consolidated Balance Sheets, Dec 31, 2025)

  • Total assets $90,038M; total liabilities $71,533M; total BMS equity $18,473M.
  • Of that asset base: Goodwill $21,754M + Other intangibles $19,103M = $40,857M — 45% of all assets are acquisition accounting entries, much of it Celgene, some already impaired ($1,098M of impairment charges in 2025 alone).
  • Equity has collapsed: XBRL shows $51,598M (2019) → $18,473M (2025) — a two-thirds destruction, driven by buybacks (treasury stock now –$43,579M) and by absorbing the goodwill impairments and the Karuna write-off.

Principle (Ch. 7): book value understates a great franchise and overstates a poor one; never anchor value to book — especially here, where the equity is a residual battered by buybacks and impaired goodwill. We will value the cash, not the book.

Ratios as arithmetic (FY2025)

  • Gross margin = (48,194 − 13,936) ÷ 48,194 = 34,258 ÷ 48,194 = 71.1% — very high, the pharma signature.
  • Operating cash margin = OCF ÷ revenue = 14,156 ÷ 48,194 = 29.4% — a cash gusher.
  • Net debt = (LT debt 42,850 + ST debt 2,261) − (cash & securities 11,069) = $34,043M (MD&A confirms).
  • Debt / OCF = 45,111 ÷ 14,156 = 3.19× — meaningful leverage, but serviceable.
  • Interest coverage (an independent research service) = ~1.4× on a pretax basis after amortization — thin on GAAP, comfortable on cash.
  • Dividend coverage by cash = FCF ÷ dividends = (14,156 − 1,311) ÷ 5,045 = 12,845 ÷ 5,045 = 2.55× covered.

The statements articulate: high margins → strong cash → covers a heavy debt load and a rich dividend, while GAAP earnings whipsaw on non-cash M&A accounting.

Read the concentration: Eliquis ($14.4B) + Opdivo ($10.0B) = $24.4B — over half of total revenue — both hit U.S. loss-of-exclusivity in 2028. The 10-K is blunt about what that means (Item 1): "Upon the expiration or loss of market exclusivity on a product, we can lose the major portion of that product's revenue in a very short period of time."

And the politics arrived early. Eliquis was in the first round of IRA Medicare price-setting — its "maximum fair price" takes effect January 1, 2026 (10-K Item 1). Under the December 2025 U.S. Government Agreement, BMY agreed to provide Eliquis free to Medicaid from Jan 1, 2026 and to donate API to a strategic reserve. Orencia was selected for 2028 negotiation; Pomalyst's max fair price starts 2027. The cliff is being pulled forward by policy.

Against this, the bridge: the Growth Portfolio has been adding ~$3.5–3.8B of revenue per year ($19.4B → $22.6B → $26.4B). But the drugs meant to carry the future — Camzyos, Breyanzi, Reblozyl, Cobenfy — are still only ~17% of the Growth Portfolio (an independent research service, June 2026). The biggest Growth drug, Opdivo, is itself going off-patent in 2028. So the honest picture: the Growth Portfolio is growing, but its two biggest members (Opdivo, and arguably the whole immuno-oncology franchise) are wasting assets too, and the genuinely new engines are still small.

Principle (Ch. 5): a moat must be argued, not assumed, and its width governs how long high returns persist. Here every drug's moat is a patent — a real barrier with an expiry date stamped on it. This is a moat as a wasting asset: durable in aggregate (pharma majors persist for decades on R&D scale, commercial infrastructure, and manufacturing know-how), but wasting drug-by-drug.

The Owner-Earnings ladder (FY2025)

Manual (Ch. 7): Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Non-cash charges − Maintenance Capex − ΔWorking Capital.

Step $M Per share (÷2,036M)

+ Depreciation & amortization (non-cash) 4,011
= subtotal 11,065
− Maintenance capex (1,311)

Memo: FCF = OCF 14,156 − capex 1,311 12,845 $6.31
Memo: Non-GAAP net income 12,545 $6.15

The capital-light point, in arithmetic: capex $1,311M vs. D&A $4,011M — capex is one-third of depreciation. Pharma is not capital-intensive; the plant maintains itself cheaply. That is exactly why owner earnings ($4.79/sh) sit far above GAAP EPS ($3.46) — most of the D&A add-back is real cash freed up, not a hole that maintenance capex must refill.

BEN: For valuation we won't use the flattering top ($6.31 FCF) or the depressed bottom ($3.46 GAAP). We'll normalize a base owner-earnings power of ~$5.25/share — below FCF and non-GAAP (haircut for the wasting-asset amortization that stands in for drugs we'll have to replace), above the amortization-mangled GAAP figure. We'll carry a range of $4.50 (low) to $6.15 (high) because the pipeline uncertainty is genuine. Principle (Ch. 12): value the business on what it is likely to earn, not its best case — so the margin of safety is not quietly spent on optimism.

ROIC vs. the misleading ROE — the acquisition-diluted-returns lesson

An independent research service prints ROE of 38.2% for 2025. Take that at face value and you'd call BMY one of the great businesses on earth. Don't.

WARREN: ROE of 38% on an equity base of only $18.5B that has been shrunk two-thirds by buybacks and impairments is a mirage. Divide any profit by a small enough denominator and you get a big percentage. The honest question the Manual asks (Ch. 6) is: what does the business earn on all the capital deployed in it — the debt and the goodwill from the deals?

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital.

  • Normalized EBIT: pretax earnings 9,328 + interest 2,389 + lumpy IPRD 3,721 + impairment 1,098 = 16,536.
  • NOPAT at an 18% tax rate = 16,536 × (1 − 0.18) = $13,560M.
  • Invested capital = equity 18,473 + net debt 34,043 = $52,516M.
  • ROIC = 13,560 ÷ 52,516 = 25.8% (≈21% if you use gross rather than net debt).

BEN: On its face 26% is excellent — comfortably above any discount rate. But here is the subtle and important lesson. That ratio is high partly because impairments have already written down the failed capital, shrinking the denominator. The truer test of management's stewardship is the one-dollar test on the money actually spent (Ch. 8): BMY poured ~$74B into Celgene (2019) + ~$14B into Karuna + ~$5B RayzeBio/Mirati — roughly $93B of acquisitions — and the result is revenue flat at $45–48B for five years and equity down from $51.6B to $18.5B. The incremental return on the price paid for that empire-building has been mediocre at best. The high reported ROIC and the poor incremental return on acquisition dollars are both true — and telling them apart is the whole art.

Principle (Ch. 6 & 8): a headline return ratio flatters management when the denominator has already been impaired down. Judge capital allocation by the value created per dollar spent, not by the ratio left standing after the failures are written off.

MANAGEMENT AS PARTNERS

The five uses of a dollar of free cash (Ch. 8), and how BMY's management (CEO Christopher Boerner, since 2024) has allocated:

1. Reinvest in the business — R&D of $9,951M in 2025 (21% of revenue). Necessary and real. But note: BMY's own commentary concedes in-house R&D has been "prohibitively expensive" and it has leaned on acquisitions instead (an independent research service). A franchise that must buy its pipeline rather than grow it is telling you something about its internal moat.

2. Acquisitions — the dominant use, and the one to judge hardest. Apply the one-dollar test (Ch. 8): did $1 spent create ≥$1 of value?
- Celgene (~$74B, 2019): brought Revlimid (now collapsing on schedule), Pomalyst, Reblozyl, Abraxane. Revlimid alone has fallen from ~$12B peak to $2,951M. An independent research service's verdict is that Celgene "underperformed." On the evidence — five years of flat revenue and two-thirds of equity gone — this deal did not clearly create $74B of value. A fail-to-unsure on the one-dollar test.
- Karuna (~$14B, 2024): Cobenfy (schizophrenia) — at $155M of sales it is a bet, not yet a payoff. Unsure.
- RayzeBio ($4.1B), Mirati ($4.8B), Orbital, 2seventy bio — smaller, early-stage, unproven.

Principle (Ch. 8): the institutional imperative pushes managers to expand for its own sake and overpay; acquisitions are frequently value-destroying. Debt-funded serial M&A that leaves revenue flat is the pattern to watch for.

3. Pay down debt — genuinely creditable in 2025. Net debt fell $4.4B (from $38.5B to $34.0B); they retired $8.7B of principal via tender offers (taking a $356M redemption loss) and repaid $10.9B of long-term debt. Debt/cap improved 72% → 68% across the year. Credit ratings solid: Moody's A2, S&P A, stable (MD&A). This is rational, safety-raising allocation.

4. Dividends — an aristocrat. Raised again to $0.62/quarter ($2.48/yr) in 2025; $5,045M paid. Not cut even in the 2024 "loss" year. Covered 2.55× by FCF. Yield ~4.5% live.

5. Buybacks — $5.0B authorization sits entirely unused; zero repurchased in 2025. Principle (Ch. 8): buybacks create value only below intrinsic value; the price paid is everything. Pausing buybacks to pay down debt while the stock is arguably cheap is defensible but not obviously optimal — a judgment call that reflects balance-sheet caution over opportunism.

Candor: management does report its problems plainly (the exclusivity table, the IRA impacts, the Celgene underperformance implicitly). We credit that.

WHAT IS IT WORTH? — INTRINSIC VALUE

BEN: Remember the Manual's warning (Ch. 9): the output is a range, not a point, and for a patent-cliff business the range must be wide. We will triangulate three independent methods, then reconcile.

Discount rate (Ch. 11 — interest rates as gravity)

The 10-year Treasury is 4.47%, the 30-year ~5.0% (Selection & Opinion, June 2026). Onto the long risk-free rate we add a premium for the genuine forecast uncertainty of a cliff-facing pharma. We will run r = 8%, 9%, 10%, and treat 9% as base — a reasonable owner's opportunity cost for a business this hard to forecast.

Method 1 — Normalized owner-earnings multiple, haircut for cliff risk

We must anchor the multiple from somewhere and justify it:

  • (a) BMY's own history: an independent research service 10-yr median P/E = 23. But that median reflects a pre-cliff franchise. A business staring at a 2028 loss of half its revenue does not deserve its historical multiple. Haircut hard.
  • (b) Peers (Drug Industry report): Eli Lilly commands a premium multiple (>30×) because it has real growth (GLP-1). J&J, Amgen trade mid-to-high teens. BMY is the out-of-favor cheap large-cap — industry Timeliness is weak (61 of 94). BMY should sit at the bottom of the peer range, because it has the clearest terminal-decline risk.
  • (c) Quality-justified: a mature cash-cow facing a real cliff, with capital-light economics and a solid balance sheet, reasonably earns 8–12× owner earnings — cheap, reflecting the wasting-asset risk, not the historical 23×.

Method 2 — Two-stage DCF modeling the trough, then recovery (Ch. 9)

We explicitly model owner earnings declining through the 2028–29 cliff, then recovering as the Growth Portfolio and pipeline take over. Base path (10 explicit years): –8%, –10%, –6%, –2%, +2%, +4%, +5%, +4%, +3%, +3%, then terminal growth g. Starting OE = $5.25/sh.

Sensitivity table — IV per share (base trough-recovery path):

g = 0% g = 1% g = 2%
r = 8% $58.11 $62.51 $68.37
r = 9% $51.41 $54.56 $58.61
r = 10% $46.07 $48.39 $51.29

Scenario paths:
- Pessimistic (pipeline fails, terminal decline; OE₀ $4.50; r=10%; g=0%): ~$31
- Optimistic (pipeline replaces cliff faster; OE₀ $6.15; r=9%; g=2%): ~$80

Principle (Ch. 11): terminal growth g is especially fraught for a patent-cliff business — a mature pharma may realistically face g near zero, or even a permanent slow decline. We anchor the base at r=9%, g=1% → $54.56, and note how a single point of g or r moves the answer $5–7/share. That sensitivity is the point, not a defect.

Method 3 — Dividend cross-check

Dividend $2.48/sh. At what yield does the market value it?
- 4.5% → $2.48 ÷ 0.045 = $55.11
- 5.0% → $49.60
- 5.5% → $45.09

BMY's own history shows a yield that has swung from ~2.4% (2016, when growth was expected) to 4.9% (2024–25). A 4.5–5.0% yield is elevated versus its past — the yield is signalling value, but only if the payout is safe (it is 2.55× covered by cash — see below).

Reconciliation and triangulation

  • Method 1 (multiple): $36 / $52.50 / $73.80
  • Method 2 (DCF): $31 (pessimistic) / $54.56 (base) / $80 (optimistic)
  • Method 3 (dividend): $45–$55

The three methods converge on a base near $52–$55 and, crucially, agree on a wide range:

Intrinsic value: LOW ~$40 · BASE ~$54 · HIGH ~$72

The width — a factor of nearly 2× from low to high — is honest, not sloppy. It is the direct reflection of the one thing we admitted at the gate: we cannot forecast the pipeline.

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth (Ch. 10)

g = Reinvestment Rate × ROIC. BMY's reported ROIC (~26%) exceeds r (9%), which would normally make growth value-creating. But the incremental capital — the $93B of acquisitions — has produced flat revenue. That is the fingerprint of value-consuming growth (Ch. 10: reinvestment creates value only when the return on the capital that funds it exceeds r). BMY has been spending heavily to stand still. The market's low multiple is, in part, a correct verdict on that.

MARGIN OF SAFETY & MR. MARKET

Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value − Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value (Ch. 12).

Live price $58.13. Against our range:

  • vs. base $54: (54 − 58.13) ÷ 54 = −7.6% → trading at a slight premium to base.
  • vs. low $40: (40 − 58.13) ÷ 40 = −45% → well above the pessimistic case; if the cliff is worse than modeled, real downside exists.
  • vs. high $72: (72 − 58.13) ÷ 72 = +19% → if the pipeline delivers, meaningful upside.

The cushion price: to buy with a genuine Graham margin of safety — say a 25–30% discount to our base $54 — you'd want to pay around $38–$40. That is, not coincidentally, the bottom of an independent research service's own 18-month target range ($38–$67). At $58.13, there is essentially no margin of safety against the base case.

WARREN: So what is Mr. Market doing at $58.13? He is offering the business at roughly 9–10× owner earnings (58.13 ÷ ~$6 FCF) with a 4.5% dividend yield. That is statistically cheap — cheaper than the market (~18× median), far cheaper than Lilly. Mr. Market is clearly pessimistic. The question the Manual forces (Ch. 2) is: is his pessimism about the cliff overdone, or correct?

Our honest answer: it is close to correct, not wildly overdone. The 2028 cliff on Eliquis + Opdivo ($24B) is real, dated, and being pulled forward by IRA politics. The replacements are growing but still small, and the biggest "growth" drug is itself expiring. A low multiple on a business whose revenue may genuinely trough is not a mispricing — it may be Mr. Market pricing a wasting asset correctly. The cheapness is real; so is the catch.

Principle (Ch. 2 & 3): a low price is only opportunity if the business's value is intact. Volatility is opportunity; a permanent impairment of earning power is not. Here the risk is the second kind — so the low multiple demands respect, not reflexive contrarianism.

THE VERDICT & THE LESSON

Verdict: FAIR — statistically cheap, but with a genuine terminal-decline risk. A Graham "cigar-butt-meets-franchise": priced for pessimism that is largely justified, offering no margin of safety at today's price.

Not "Wonderful" — the moat is a wasting asset and the incremental returns on $93B of M&A have been poor. Not "Avoid" — this is a cash-gushing, investment-grade, dividend-secure franchise at 9–10× cash earnings, not a value trap headed for zero. It sits in the honest middle: Fair. And for many disciplined investors, the correct verdict is the one the Manual blesses at the gate — "too hard": the pipeline outcome that determines everything is genuinely unforecastable.

THE ONE QUESTION, answered: Does the Growth Portfolio + pipeline replace the cliff fast enough? Our best honest answer: partially, and probably not fully by the trough. The Growth Portfolio is real and rising (~$3.7B/yr), but Opdivo's own 2028 expiry and the still-small new drugs (Cobenfy $155M) mean the 2028–2030 window likely sees owner earnings dip before any recovery. The base case is a trough, not a collapse — but not a smooth hand-off either.

Two or three open questions we cannot resolve (and neither can anyone honestly):
1. Pipeline probabilities — will Cobenfy, Camzyos, and the acquired assets scale to multi-billion-dollar drugs, or disappoint like so many before them?
2. Drug-pricing policy — how much further will the IRA, "most-favored-nation" pricing, and government agreements erode margins before patents even expire?
3. Dividend safety through the trough — covered 2.55× by cash today, but if 2028–29 owner earnings fall 25–30%, does the aristocrat streak survive? (Our read: probably yes, given the cash cushion — but it is the number to watch.)

Universal lessons to carry to the next company:
1. Cash beats GAAP when they disagree. BMY lost $8.9B on paper in 2024 while generating $15.2B of cash. Non-cash amortization and lumpy IPR&D write-offs make GAAP EPS nearly useless here. Always build the owner-earnings ladder and let the cash-flow statement be your witness.
2. A moat with an expiry date is a wasting asset. Patents are genuine barriers — but each one runs out on a scheduled day. Value such a business as a stream that declines and must be replaced, not as a perpetuity. Model the trough explicitly.
3. "Cheap with a catch" is the hardest call in investing. A 9× multiple and a 4.5% yield scream value — but a low multiple can be Mr. Market correctly pricing a decline. The discipline is to separate a temporary panic (buy) from a permanent impairment (respect the price). Demand a wide margin of safety precisely when the range is wide — and here, at $58, there isn't one.

GLOSSARY — TERMS & FORMULAS (in order of appearance)

Circle of competence — the set of businesses you can understand well enough to forecast; its boundary matters more than its size (Ch. 4). BMY: near/over the edge — pipeline and drug-pricing outcomes are not reliably forecastable.

Intrinsic value — the discounted value of all owner earnings a business will produce over its life. Formula: IV = Σ OEₜ ÷ (1+r)ᵗ (Ch. 1, 9). BMY: ~$54/share base, range $40–$72.

Mr. Market — the manic-depressive partner who quotes a price daily; there to serve you, not guide you (Ch. 2). BMY: quoting $58.13, pessimistic — and here largely correctly so.

Owner earnings — the cash an owner can withdraw without weakening the business. Formula: Reported earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex − ΔworkingC (Ch. 7). BMY FY2025: 7,054 + 4,011 − 1,311 = $9,754M = $4.79/sh; FCF proxy $6.31/sh.

GTN adjustments — gross-to-net: rebates, chargebacks, discounts subtracted from list price. BMY: $88,085M gross − $41,329M (47%) = $46,756M net product sales.

Non-GAAP / specified items — management's adjusted earnings, adding back amortization, IPR&D, restructuring, etc. Treat critically — recurring "one-time" items are real costs (Ch. 7 red flags). BMY FY2025: GAAP $3.46 → Non-GAAP $6.15/sh; honest number ~$5.25.

Amortization of acquired intangibles — non-cash write-down of acquired drug rights (mostly Celgene). BMY: $9,047M (2023) → $8,872M (2024) → $3,317M (2025), running off.

Acquired IPRD — in-process R&D from acquisitions, expensed at once. BMY: $13,373M (2024, incl. $12,122M Karuna) drove the GAAP loss; $3,721M (2025).

Book value — accounting equity; understates great franchises, overstates poor ones — never anchor to it (Ch. 7). BMY: equity $18,473M, collapsed from $51,598M (2019); 45% of assets are goodwill + intangibles.

Moat / economic franchise — durable competitive advantage; signature is pricing power (Ch. 5). BMY: patents — a moat with an expiry date; a wasting asset.

Loss of exclusivity (LOE/LOB) — the patent/regulatory expiry after which generics/biosimilars enter and revenue falls fast. BMY: Eliquis & Opdivo, ~$24B combined, U.S. LOE 2028.

Maintenance capex — capital spending to preserve competitive position and unit volume, vs. growth capex (Ch. 7). BMY: capex $1,311M ≈ ⅓ of D&A $4,011M — capital-light.

ROIC — return on invested capital. Formula: NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital (Ch. 6). BMY: 13,560 ÷ 52,516 = 25.8% (headline) — but incremental return on M&A capital poor.

ROE — return on equity; here misleading because the equity base is buyback-shrunk and impairment-battered (Ch. 6). BMY: reported 38.2% — a mirage.

Discount rate (r) — owner's opportunity cost, anchored to long rates plus an uncertainty premium; "gravity" on asset values (Ch. 11). BMY: base r = 9% (10-yr Treasury 4.47%, 30-yr ~5.0%, plus premium).

One-dollar test — retain a dollar only if it creates ≥$1 of value (incremental ROIC ≥ r) (Ch. 8). BMY: ~$93B of acquisitions left revenue flat — fails/unsure on Celgene.

Value-creating vs. value-consuming growth — reinvestment adds value only when ROIC > r on the capital that funds it (Ch. 10). BMY: incremental M&A capital produced flat revenue → value-consuming.

Two-stage DCF — explicit forecast years + terminal value. Formula: IV = Σ OEₜ/(1+r)ᵗ + [OEₙ₊₁/(r−g)]/(1+r)ⁿ (Ch. 9). BMY: base (trough-then-recovery, r=9%, g=1%) = $54.56/sh.

Terminal growth (g) — perpetual growth after the explicit period; especially fraught for a cliff business (Ch. 11). BMY: modeled 0–2%; a mature pharma may face g≈0.

Margin of safety — the discount of price to conservative IV; the cornerstone. Formula: (IV − Price) ÷ IV (Ch. 12). BMY: (54 − 58.13) ÷ 54 = −7.6% — none at today's price; cushion price ~$38–40.

Risk — the probability of permanent loss of capital, not price volatility (Ch. 3). BMY: the real risk is a genuine terminal decline in earning power, not the beta of 0.70.
